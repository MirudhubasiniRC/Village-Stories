# Character Camera Rules

## Purpose

This document defines how recurring characters should be visually presented throughout the Village Stories universe.

It governs character presentation rather than cinematography.

Technical definitions for shot types, camera movement, lenses, composition, lighting, depth of field, and visual rhythm are maintained separately in:

```text
05_Cinematography/
```

All character documentation inherits these rules.

---

# Philosophy

The camera should observe characters with quiet respect.

Characters are not performers.

They are ordinary people living ordinary lives.

The audience should feel like a silent guest witnessing genuine moments rather than watching staged performances.

---

# Character Presentation

Characters should always appear:

* Natural.
* Comfortable.
* Unaware of the camera.
* Connected to their surroundings.
* Engaged in meaningful activity.

Avoid poses that appear designed for photographs.

---

# Environmental Connection

Characters should rarely appear isolated.

Whenever appropriate, frame them together with their environment.

Examples:

Mother

* Kitchen
* Garden Window
* Clay Stove
* Traditional tools

Father

* Vegetable Garden
* Paddy fields
* Canal
* Backyard

Daughter

* Learning beside parents
* Courtyard
* Garden
* Village pathways

Family Dog

* Near family members
* Backyard
* Kitchen entrance
* Courtyard

The environment should help define the character.

---

# Eye Contact

Characters normally focus on:

* Their work.
* Family members.
* Nature.
* Animals.
* Traditional tools.

Direct eye contact with the camera should be extremely rare.

---

# Expressions

Maintain subtle, authentic expressions.

Common expressions include:

* Gentle smile.
* Quiet concentration.
* Curiosity.
* Gratitude.
* Thoughtfulness.
* Relief.
* Calm concern.

Avoid exaggerated facial acting unless required by a major story event.

---

# Hands

Hands are an important storytelling element.

Frequently show characters:

* Cooking.
* Grinding.
* Harvesting.
* Serving food.
* Gardening.
* Weaving.
* Caring for animals.
* Teaching children.

Experienced hands should communicate years of practice.

---

# Body Language

Movement should feel:

* Relaxed.
* Confident.
* Familiar.
* Purposeful.

Characters should move as people who know their home and village intimately.

Avoid unnecessary gestures.

---

# Character Interaction

When multiple characters appear together:

Maintain:

* Natural spacing.
* Comfortable posture.
* Relaxed interaction.
* Shared attention on the activity.

Avoid symmetrical posing or artificial arrangements.

---

# Emotional Presentation

Emotion should emerge through:

* Body language.
* Facial expression.
* Shared activity.
* Silence.
* Environment.

Avoid relying on dramatic gestures.

---

# Relationship With Nature

Whenever appropriate, allow characters to naturally interact with:

* Sunlight.
* Rain.
* Wind.
* Gardens.
* Trees.
* Birds.
* Butterflies.
* Steam.
* Firelight.

Nature should become part of character presentation.

---

# Background Characters

Background characters should:

* Continue their own activities.
* Never compete for attention.
* Support the realism of the scene.

Their presence should reinforce the feeling of a living village.

---

# Character Identity

Every character should remain visually recognizable through:

* Posture.
* Movement.
* Habits.
* Typical activities.
* Clothing.
* Hairstyle.

Recognition should come from consistency rather than repeated explanation.

---

# Relationship With Cinematography

This document defines **how characters should be presented**.

Technical filmmaking standards—including shot definitions, camera movements, lens language, composition, lighting, depth of field, colour language, visual rhythm, and cinematic continuity—are defined in:

```text
05_Cinematography/
```

All character scenes should follow those global cinematography standards.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Natural presentation.
* Environmental context.
* Subtle emotion.
* Purposeful movement.
* Consistent visual identity.

Never:

* Pose characters unnaturally.
* Make characters perform for the camera.
* Separate characters from their environment without narrative reason.
* Contradict established appearance or behaviour.

# Canon Authority

This document permanently defines how recurring characters are visually presented throughout the Village Stories universe.

All future image prompts, storyboards, animations, and production assets should follow these presentation principles together with the global cinematography standards.

---

# Guiding Principle

The camera should quietly accompany the characters rather than direct them.

Their expressions, movements, relationships, and connection to the village should feel so natural that the audience forgets the camera exists and simply experiences life alongside them.

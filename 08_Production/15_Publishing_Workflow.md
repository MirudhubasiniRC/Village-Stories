# Publishing Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines the permanent workflow for preparing, scheduling,
publishing, verifying, maintaining, and learning from Village Stories releases
on YouTube.

Publishing is the final production stage, not a button press. It is where the
finished episode, thumbnail, metadata, captions, playlists, links, and public
promise are assembled into one reliable viewer experience.

## Philosophy

Village Stories should arrive with the same care visible inside its episodes.
The viewer should receive an accurate title, truthful thumbnail, complete
description, accessible captions, calm ending, and a useful next choice—not a
rushed upload designed to exploit attention.

Consistency matters, but a schedule never outranks truth, canon, technical
readiness, or dignity. Delay an incomplete release rather than publish a
misleading or defective one.

## Objectives

The publishing workflow must:

1. Release only quality-approved episodes.
2. Deliver a coherent and honest audience promise across all public surfaces.
3. Protect canon, continuity, accessibility, rights, and technical quality.
4. Schedule releases reliably without making unverified promises.
5. Link viewers to genuinely relevant episodes and playlists.
6. Verify the live experience after YouTube processing.
7. Turn audience evidence into careful improvements without trend-chasing.

## Roles

### Release Manager

Owns the release calendar, readiness decision, scheduling, release record, and
post-publish verification. May delay a release when a gate is incomplete.

### Episode Director

Confirms the final episode represents its intended story and authorizes any
creative update after picture or sound lock.

### Quality Lead

Issues the final release authorization described in `14_Quality_Control.md`.
Can block publication for unresolved defects.

### Metadata Lead

Supplies approved title, description, playlist, captions, language, card,
end-screen, and link fields.

### Thumbnail Owner

Supplies the approved thumbnail, source record, and final version identifier.

### Publisher

Uploads the approved master, applies the release package, configures YouTube
settings, and completes live verification.

### Community Steward

Reviews comments and audience signals according to the series tone, escalating
corrections, safety issues, or recurring confusion to the Release Manager.

## Publishing Principles

1. Publish the episode that was approved, not a last-minute substitute.
2. Keep one source of truth for each final asset and field.
3. Never let urgency bypass a failed quality gate.
4. Make every viewer-facing claim traceable to the finished episode.
5. Keep continuation links truthful and optional.
6. Preserve emotional rest at the ending; do not cover it with aggressive
   promotion.
7. Treat post-release corrections transparently and proportionately.

## Release Package

The Release Manager must receive a complete package before upload:

* Approved platform master and version identifier.
* Approved thumbnail and version identifier.
* Final title, description, chapters, search terms, playlists, and language
  fields.
* Captions and accessibility review status.
* Cards, end screens, pinned comment, and destination links if used.
* Rights, credits, disclosures, and age-suitability status.
* Quality-control release authorization and unresolved-waiver record.
* Episode ID, format, duration, target publish time, and owner.

If any required item is missing, the episode is not release-ready.

## Publication States

Use clear states so no draft is mistaken for an approved release:

| State | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Planned | Premise exists; no release commitment is public. |
| In production | Episode is being made; schedule may change. |
| In review | Quality gates are active; no upload authorization. |
| Release-ready | All assets and approvals are complete. |
| Uploaded private | Exact YouTube upload exists for technical verification. |
| Scheduled | Public time and settings are locked, subject to final check. |
| Published | Viewers can access the approved release. |
| Corrected | A logged post-release change has been applied. |
| Paused | Release is withheld or removed while a material issue is reviewed. |

Do not announce a state externally unless it is true.

## Step-by-Step Workflow

### 1. Plan the release window

Select a release window that supports the channel's sustainable rhythm and the
audience's likely availability. Use historical audience data as information,
not as a command to produce more frequently than quality permits.

Record the target time internally. Do not promise viewers a specific date or
hour until the episode is release-ready and the schedule is confirmed.

### 2. Confirm release readiness

Before upload, verify the package against the release checklist:

* Quality authorization is signed.
* Master, thumbnail, metadata, captions, links, and credits are final.
* The release title and thumbnail match the actual opening.
* Relevant playlists already exist or have been approved.
* No unresolved blocker or unapproved waiver exists.

If a creative change is made after authorization, determine which earlier
gates it invalidates and rerun them.

### 3. Upload privately

Upload the approved master to YouTube with private visibility first. Enter the
episode ID in the internal release record and confirm that the platform has
recognized the intended duration and file.

Do not upload alternate masters, test thumbnails, or provisional captions to a
shared public state where a viewer may encounter them.

### 4. Apply approved metadata

Enter only the approved field set:

* Title and description.
* Thumbnail.
* Playlist placement.
* Chapters, where appropriate.
* Captions and language fields.
* Audience and rights settings.
* Cards and end screens.
* Relevant links and approved pinned comment.

Read every field after entry. Autocomplete, old templates, copied links, and
saved defaults can create inaccurate public information.

### 5. Verify the processed private upload

After YouTube processing completes, view the exact upload from the audience
perspective.

Check:

* Thumbnail rendering and title truncation.
* First 30 seconds, a middle scene, and final minute.
* Video clarity, framing, colour, synchronization, and audio level.
* Caption availability, timing, spelling, and language label.
* Chapter points, cards, end screens, and playlist links.
* Description links, credits, and public-facing wording.
* The ending remains emotionally complete before any navigation prompt appears.

This check is for the platform transcode, not merely the local master.

### 6. Schedule with integrity

Set the approved visibility and publish time. Confirm timezone, date, title,
thumbnail, playlist, audience setting, and release state before saving.

Schedule premieres only when a premiere genuinely suits the episode and the
team can support it. Do not use a premiere countdown to imply a major event or
manufacture urgency around an ordinary release.

### 7. Final pre-publication gate

Immediately before the scheduled release, confirm:

1. The exact approved upload is selected.
2. The episode has not been replaced by a later unreviewed version.
3. Thumbnail and metadata match the release record.
4. Captions and links still function.
5. There are no new canon, rights, safety, or technical concerns.

If the gate fails, pause the release and notify the responsible owners.

### 8. Verify the public release

After publication, open the public URL without relying on creator-only views.
Check that visibility, thumbnail, title, description, captions, chapters,
playlist placement, cards, end screens, and intended links render correctly.

Record:

* Publication timestamp and URL.
* Final public title and thumbnail version.
* Verification time and reviewer.
* Any discrepancy and its resolution.

### 9. Steward the first audience response

Observe comments and early signals without refreshing compulsively or making
reactive creative changes. Respond only where a response is useful, kind,
accurate, and consistent with the channel's voice.

Thank viewers for specific observations. Correct factual misunderstandings
calmly. Do not argue, bait debate, pressure engagement, or promise a sequel
that is not planned.

### 10. Review and archive

At the planned review interval, examine the episode alongside its package and
store lessons in the release record. Archive final assets, public URL,
metadata, captions, approvals, analytics observations, and post-release
changes.

## Release Decision Framework

### Publish now when

* All applicable quality gates pass.
* The technical platform version is verified.
* The package makes one honest promise.
* Captions, rights, and audience settings are correct.
* The release supports the channel's sustainable plan.

### Delay when

* A canon or continuity issue remains unresolved.
* The thumbnail or title overpromises.
* Captions, rights, technical processing, or links are incomplete.
* A material change after review requires re-approval.
* The team cannot verify the live version responsibly.

### Correct after release when

* A factual description, link, chapter, caption, or packaging error can be
  fixed without changing the episode's truth.
* A technical platform issue appears after publishing.
* Accessibility needs a timely correction.

Log what changed, why, who approved it, and whether viewers need a clear
public clarification.

### Pause or remove when

* The episode contains a material canon, rights, safety, dignity, or
  accessibility failure that cannot be corrected safely while public.
* The uploaded media is not the approved episode.
* Public packaging materially misleads viewers and cannot be promptly fixed.

Escalate promptly. Do not hide a serious error by quietly changing surrounding
metadata while leaving the harmful core untouched.

## YouTube Considerations

### Honest engagement

Use YouTube features to help viewers choose and continue fitting content:

* Clear thumbnails and titles.
* Relevant playlists.
* Accurate chapters.
* Optional end-screen recommendations.
* Captions and language support.
* Calm, useful community conversation.

Do not use false countdowns, misleading community teasers, fabricated
milestones, clickbait, comment bait, artificial scarcity, or off-topic trend
references.

### Short-form and long-form

Shorts may need a concise title and a selected cover, but they still require a
truthful first frame and self-contained experience. Long-form releases may use
chapters, playlists, and end screens more fully, provided they do not fragment
the story or spoil its resolution.

### Analytics review

Review analytics in combination:

* Impressions and click-through rate show whether packaging is understandable.
* Early retention tests whether the opening fulfills the promise.
* Completion and returning viewers indicate whether the experience satisfies.
* Traffic sources and search terms reveal whether discovery language is
  attracting a fitting audience.
* Comments reveal connection, confusion, and accessibility needs.

Do not change a thumbnail solely because a single early metric is lower than
expected. First verify delivery, audience match, topic clarity, and sufficient
data. Test only honest alternatives.

## Community and Corrections

The public voice should mirror the series: warm, specific, modest, and
truthful.

### Respond when

* A viewer asks a factual question that can be answered from the episode or
  approved canon.
* A caption, link, or context error needs acknowledgment.
* A viewer shares a respectful connection to a task, place, or feeling.

### Do not respond by

* Claiming historical authority beyond documented research.
* Defending an error without checking it.
* Encouraging arguments about culture, poverty, family, or rural life.
* Turning criticism into promotional drama.
* Naming or exposing private contributors without consent.

For a substantive correction, update the relevant public field where possible,
log it, and consider a pinned clarification only when it helps viewers
understand a material issue.

## Continuity Across Releases

Before scheduling, check the relationship to nearby uploads:

* Is this episode's season plausible after the previous episode?
* Does its clothing, object state, weather, and day progression match any
  explicit continuation?
* Is “Part,” “next,” “tomorrow,” or “continued” language supported?
* Does playlist ordering create a false chronology?
* Are end-screen recommendations genuinely related?

If episodes are released out of production order, preserve truth through
standalone descriptions and correct playlist sequencing. Do not imply a
timeline the channel has not established.

## Example Release Scenarios

### Correct release

An episode about rain slowing a kitchen task is released with a thumbnail of
Mother assessing damp wood, a title naming rainy-day cooking, captions that
identify rain on tile, and an end screen to a related monsoon playlist. The
opening immediately shows the weather and task.

### Incorrect release

The same episode is promoted as `A Terrifying Storm Hit Our Village!` with a
thumbnail of a frightened dog and a description claiming the family was in
danger. Even if it receives clicks, this is a release failure.

### Correct correction

After publication, a chapter timecode is found to be six seconds early. Update
the chapter, verify it publicly, and log the change.

### Pause scenario

A generated background contains a modern branded sign. If it is visible and
materially compromises the period setting, pause or replace the episode after
quality review rather than pretending it is insignificant.

## Canon Authority

Publishing cannot revise canon through a title, caption, description, playlist,
community post, or correction. When a public field conflicts with canon,
correct the field or pause the release; do not redefine the world around it.

## Guiding Principle

Publish with enough care that every viewer receives the episode they were
truthfully invited to watch.

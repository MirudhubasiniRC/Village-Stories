# Script Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories writers and directors develop a
canon-safe premise into an approved, production-ready script. It applies to
YouTube Shorts and long-form episodes and connects story design to research,
pre-production, Google Flow shot generation, sound, edit, and release.

A Village Stories script is not dialogue alone. It is a truthful plan for
visible action, place, time, practical work, sound, and emotional recognition.

## Philosophy

The series finds story in ordinary life already made meaningful by attention:
a meal, a repair, a school departure, rain changing a task, animal care, a
shared walk, or an unspoken act of help. Scriptwriting protects that meaning by
making every action belong to known people in a known world.

The writer does not enlarge a small premise with spectacle. The writer reveals
why it matters to this family today. A script succeeds when the viewer can
watch a practical task change a relationship feeling without a lecture telling
them what to feel.

## Guiding Principle

Write only what this established village can show, and let a small truthful
choice carry the episode's feeling.

## Authority and Scope

Read `08_Production/01_Production_Philosophy.md` before using this workflow.
Specific canon for characters, locations, objects, world, and culture controls
all factual claims. Research verifies or constrains a choice; it never
overrides factual canon. `06_Story_Engine/` controls story construction, and
`07_Episode_System/` controls format and viewer-facing organisation.

This workflow cannot create a character trait, object, location, route,
seasonal event, cultural practice, past event, family rule, or new continuity
fact. If a scene requires one, replace the scene with a documented alternative
or request an explicit canon decision outside the script workflow.

## Script Objectives

Every approved script must:

* have one clear practical and emotional centre;
* name the season, time, weather, locations, and cast;
* use only canon-safe people, places, objects, and behaviour;
* show cause, effort, obstacle, response, result, and emotional settling;
* give each scene visual, narrative, and emotional purpose;
* make routes, object states, clothing condition, and elapsed time legible;
* leave room for sourced sound, silence, and Google Flow continuity;
* meet the appropriate Short or long-form episode requirements;
* honestly support title, thumbnail, and release planning.

## Roles

### Writer

Owns the premise, beat sheet, action lines, dialogue intention, and revisions.
The writer must identify assumptions and cannot conceal unknown facts in poetic
language.

### Director

Ensures the script can be shown through performance, staging, camera purpose,
geography, and paced action. The director protects the difference between a
written explanation and a visible beat.

### Canon steward

Checks the source map and stops contradictions. They resolve which existing
document controls a disputed fact but do not add new canon to save a draft.

### Researcher

Verifies narrow factual questions using `03_Research_Workflow.md`, records
limitations, and returns production constraints.

### AI artist / Google Flow operator

Reviews scripts for promptable actions, reference needs, visual ambiguity, and
continuity risk. They cannot rewrite character or world facts through prompt
interpretation.

### Sound lead and editor

Check whether action supports sound and whether scenes contain usable
transitions, coverage, pace, and breathing room.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Select a canon-safe episode seed

Start with the Story Engine operating formula:

```text
Season + location + character need or skill + gentle conflict
+ practical action + honest emotion = episode seed
```

Name each variable with established canon. For example, monsoon, Family
Kitchen, Mother's meal preparation, damp firewood, patient family action, and
warmth are usable only after the applicable source documents confirm them.

Do not begin from a twist, a lesson, a villain, a thumbnail concept, or a
generic “Tamil village” mood. Begin from an ordinary situation with an
observable human need.

### Step 2: Write the one-sentence story promise

State:

> When [practical condition] affects [character's task or need], [character
> and relationship] respond through [canon-safe action], arriving at
> [grounded emotion].

Example:

> When monsoon dampness weakens Mother's cooking fire, the family responds with
> patient practical help, arriving at the calm warmth of a completed meal.

If the sentence needs danger, shame, coincidence, magic, or a new fact to feel
interesting, choose another seed.

### Step 3: Create a source and question list

Before beats, list the exact canon documents needed for characters, locations,
objects, season, research, Story Engine, and Episode System. Mark all factual
questions as Pass, Verify, or Stop.

Use `03_Research_Workflow.md` only when the existing sources do not answer a
narrow production-relevant question. Do not research after writing a detailed
scene that already depends on unverified assumptions.

### Step 4: Define the episode centre

Write four concise statements:

* **Need:** What must be done, understood, or cared for today?
* **Obstacle:** What gentle, visible condition complicates it?
* **Action:** What plausible effort or cooperation responds?
* **Feeling:** What changes in the family or shared space by the end?

The obstacle must complicate work, not make viewers fear for safety. The
resolution must come from attention, skill, patience, cooperation, or a
plausible change in circumstance.

### Step 5: Choose episode format honestly

Use the relevant `07_Episode_System/` manuals.

For a Short:

* establish the meaningful clue immediately;
* retain visible cause and response;
* avoid exposition that replaces action;
* use one task and one emotional turn;
* finish with an earned settling image.

For long-form:

* let preparation, travel, observation, and work develop naturally;
* allow a location or relationship to reveal a fuller pattern of care;
* use connected actions only when they share the same practical/emotional
  centre;
* avoid padding with repeated work, extra conflict, or unrelated scenic shots.

The format changes duration and compression, not the moral or factual rules.

### Step 6: Build the beat sheet

Use this durable rhythm:

1. **Open:** sensory, spatial, or practical clue.
2. **Notice:** a person sees the need or condition.
3. **Prepare:** the task begins with understandable materials and skill.
4. **Complicate:** gentle obstacle or relationship recognition changes effort.
5. **Respond:** a plausible action, help, or patient adjustment.
6. **Receive:** the visible result becomes lived feeling.
7. **Settle:** a quiet final image gives the emotion room.

For every beat, answer:

* What does the viewer learn visually?
* What changes in the task or feeling?
* What canon fact must remain true?
* What sound or silence belongs here?
* What state must hand off to the next beat?

Remove a beat that repeats information without deepening task, relationship,
place, or emotional progress.

### Step 7: Assign character purpose

Each present character needs a documented, natural reason to be there. Use
character canon to write a choice, not a label.

Mother may carry care and household skill through measured work and observation.
Father may show steadiness through checking, repair, planning, or making room
for another's contribution. Elder Brother may show responsibility through
movement and practical help. Younger Daughter must have agency through
curiosity, care, observation, or careful attempts. The Family Dog remains a
living family presence, not a comic prop or miraculous solver.

Do not insert a family member merely to create a reaction shot, forced joke, or
speech that the action already communicates.

### Step 8: Write scenes as observable units

Every scene heading and action block must establish:

* exact established location and relevant zone;
* time of day, season, weather, light, and ground condition;
* character positions and action;
* featured objects and their visible state;
* what changes before the scene ends;
* route, time, or sound bridge if the next scene changes place.

Prefer concrete action:

> Mother tests the damp wood beside the weak fire. The flame catches briefly,
> then settles into smoke.

Avoid unfilmable summary:

> Mother feels that the rain has made everything difficult.

The latter can be inferred from the former and may be supported by a measured
look or a necessary line of dialogue.

### Step 9: Write dialogue after action

Dialogue should clarify context, make a decision together, respond
specifically to another person, or reveal a relationship feeling that cannot
be shown fully. It must not narrate visible action, explain every emotion, or
deliver a moral lesson.

Before retaining a line, ask:

1. Can a look, hand action, object, or pause communicate this?
2. Does the line sound like this documented person?
3. Does it move a practical or emotional decision forward?
4. Does it preserve dignity for everyone present?
5. Can it be heard above the planned sound bed?

If not, remove or rewrite it.

### Step 10: Script place, route, and time

Characters do not appear at a new location because the next image is more
attractive. Establish departure, route, arrival, or believable elapsed time
when necessary. Carry objects, weather, mud, wetness, clothing condition, and
emotional state across the move.

Public places such as the school, Tea Stall, Grocery Store, River Ghat,
Vinayagar Shrine, artisan areas, and Festival Ground require an established
reason for the visit. Do not use them as interchangeable village scenery.

### Step 11: Script visual and sound handoffs

At each scene end, state enough for pre-production to build continuity:

* where people are, face, or leave;
* what they carry or have placed;
* object state and task progress;
* weather, light, and surface change;
* intended visual transition;
* foreground action sound, ambience, dialogue window, or quiet.

Use `06_Story_Engine/13_Visual_Storytelling.md` and
`14_ASMR_Storytelling.md`. The script should create a visible sound source; it
must not demand unmotivated effects to create interest.

### Step 12: Review for Google Flow feasibility

With the AI artist, test each scene:

* Can the action be divided into clear, continuity-safe shots?
* Does the script identify the established location, people, object, state,
  time, weather, and camera purpose?
* Is one prompt being asked to show too many events?
* Will a prop, route, animal placement, or costume state need tracking?
* Could a vague line invite a modern, generic, or invented image?

Revise the script, not canon, when the answer reveals ambiguity. Google Flow
is most reliable when each shot has one clear action and inherited continuity
facts.

### Step 13: Plan honest YouTube presentation

Identify an opening that immediately shows a truthful clue, a title direction
that describes the actual task or feeling, and a thumbnail candidate rooted in
an approved image. Do not write a moment of threat or surprise only to create
clickability.

The episode must be satisfying alone. A continuing thread may be mentioned
only when it is a real next-day task, ongoing work, or relationship feeling,
never a false cliffhanger.

### Step 14: Lock the production script

The locked script includes:

* episode promise and format;
* source map and resolved research notes;
* named season, time, weather, locations, cast, and featured objects;
* beat sheet and scenes;
* dialogue with purpose;
* action, state, route, and sound handoffs;
* potential generation risks and exclusions;
* quality-gate result and named approvers.

After lock, any change to cast, location, object, task, outcome, season, or
continuity state requires rechecking the affected story, research, and
pre-production gates.

## Script Format Guidance

Use a simple readable format:

```text
SCENE / ESTABLISHED LOCATION — TIME, SEASON, WEATHER
Visual condition and character positions.
Observable action and object state.
Necessary dialogue.
Sound and continuity handoff.
```

The format is flexible, but the information is mandatory. Avoid literary prose
that obscures staging and avoid technical shot lists so rigid that they prevent
the director from preserving a natural performance.

## Example: From Weak to Production-Ready

**Weak:** “A storm threatens dinner, so Father heroically saves the family.”

This creates exaggerated stakes, does not name a practical cause, and
misrepresents both the series tone and character use.

**Production-ready:** “During monsoon rain, damp firewood weakens Mother's
cooking fire. The family responds through patient, established practical help;
the meal's completion lets the household settle together.”

The later script must still verify every object, storage source, kitchen zone,
route, weather state, and character action before production.

## Quality Gates

### Gate 1: Premise

Pass when the story is canon-safe, ordinary but meaningful, emotionally
grounded, and solvable without spectacle or invention.

### Gate 2: Character and period

Pass when every character behaves according to documentation; the setting,
objects, clothing, language behaviour, technology, and social context remain
early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

### Gate 3: Story movement

Pass when cause, need, effort, obstacle, response, result, and quiet emotional
resolution are visible and proportionate.

### Gate 4: Production clarity

Pass when scenes provide location, time, weather, objects, action, states,
routes, sound intent, and Google Flow handoff information.

### Gate 5: Format and release integrity

Pass when the episode meets the relevant Episode System requirements and can
be honestly presented to viewers.

Use `06_Story_Engine/15_Story_Checklist.md` as the full operating checklist.
Any Stop or unresolved Verify item fails script lock.

## Revision Framework

When a draft fails, identify the earliest cause:

* **Canon failure:** replace the fact, action, or premise with a documented
  alternative.
* **Character failure:** revise the choice, not the character.
* **Period failure:** remove modern or generic detail and recheck all related
  sounds, objects, and backgrounds.
* **Story failure:** clarify need, cause, effort, or emotional result.
* **Pacing failure:** combine repetition, compress established travel, or let
  the right action breathe; do not add spectacle.
* **Production failure:** make state, staging, prompts, and transitions more
  explicit.
* **YouTube failure:** rewrite packaging around what the episode actually is.

Re-run all later gates affected by the repair. Do not fix a foundational
premise with extra dialogue, music, or a decorative shot.

## Common Mistakes

* Beginning with a lesson instead of a lived need.
* Making weather a visual filter rather than a condition that alters action.
* Using a character out of personality to make a plot move.
* Giving Younger Daughter no agency or making the Family Dog a comic device.
* Introducing an undocumented object to solve a task.
* Jumping between locations without route and time logic.
* Writing dialogue that repeats visible information.
* Resolving a conflict through luck, rescue, or a miracle.
* Cutting away the moment an action succeeds.
* Using generic “village” imagery, sound, or nostalgia language.
* Writing a clickbait hook, title, or ending that does not belong to the
  episode.
* Assuming an attractive Google Flow result can repair a vague script.

## Final Rule

Before approving a script, ask: can every important image, sound, action, and
feeling happen here because the established world makes it true? If not,
rewrite the earliest beat that made it false.

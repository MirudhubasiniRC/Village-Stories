# Production Philosophy

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories is made from approved canon into
finished animated episodes. It gives writers, directors, AI artists, sound
artists, editors, and release reviewers one shared production standard.

It is a permanent operating manual, not a source of new lore. It explains how
to make choices faithfully when an episode moves from an idea to Google Flow
generation, edit, review, and YouTube release.

## Production Promise

Village Stories is a canon-locked animated series set in early-1990s rural
Tamil Nadu. Its production must make ordinary life feel specific, lived, and
emotionally valuable without making it falsely dramatic, picturesque, poor, or
old-fashioned.

The work is successful when a viewer can understand:

* where the family is;
* what practical action matters today;
* how season and weather shape that action;
* what each person notices, needs, or offers;
* why the final quiet moment was earned.

## Philosophy

Production is not the stage at which a correct story becomes a collection of
attractive images. It is the stage at which the story proves it belongs to this
family, this village, this period, and this day.

An episode may be modest in event but never careless in observation. A vessel,
a wet threshold, a school bag, a rope, a waiting dog, a changing patch of
light, or an unfinished task can carry meaning when it has a practical owner,
place, state, and consequence.

Use technology to preserve this attention. Google Flow can create images, but
it cannot decide what is canon, what a character would do, why an object is
there, or whether one shot truthfully follows another. Those remain human
creative responsibilities.

## Core Objectives

Every production must:

1. Protect established canon over convenience, novelty, or visual polish.
2. Preserve early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu period truth in action and detail.
3. Make character feeling visible through practical choices.
4. Keep geography, time, objects, weather, costume, and sound continuous.
5. Use Google Flow as a controlled shot-production tool, not an author.
6. Deliver a self-contained episode whose YouTube presentation promises only
   what the episode genuinely provides.
7. Leave release records that make future episodes easier to keep consistent.

## Guiding Principle

Make every production choice serve a visible act of ordinary life, and let
canon decide what that act can truthfully be.

## Canon Authority

No production document may create, revise, or quietly imply canon. When
documents differ in scope, use the most specific established source:

1. Character files control identity, appearance, relationships, behaviour, and
   documented costume facts.
2. Location files control geography, layout, routes, use, atmosphere, and
   location-specific objects.
3. Object files control design, materials, handling, condition, and period use.
4. World and culture files control setting, season, village context, and
   historical truth.
5. `05_Research/` supports verification and flags risks; it does not override
   established specific canon.
6. `06_Story_Engine/` controls story construction within that factual canon.
7. `07_Episode_System/` controls episode format, pacing, hook, ending, and
   viewer-facing organisation.
8. This folder controls the operational process used to execute approved work.

If a source is absent, unclear, or contradictory, mark the item **Verify** and
do not invent a solution. Revise the scene to use a documented alternative or
obtain a canon decision outside production. A beautiful render is never
evidence that an undocumented choice is acceptable.

## Non-Negotiable Principles

### 1. Truth before spectacle

The camera observes work, care, weather, waiting, and shared space. It does not
manufacture feeling through exaggerated poses, threatening sound, impossible
camera drama, comic humiliation, or generic “village beauty” imagery.

### 2. Cause before response

Show the damp wood before the weak fire, the forgotten item before the return,
or the loose rope before the repair. A viewer should see why a choice matters
before being told how to feel about it.

### 3. Practicality carries emotion

Tasks are not filler between dialogue scenes. Cooking, carrying, tending,
repairing, walking, waiting, and sharing give the episode its emotional
structure. The task must be understandable, physically plausible, and allowed
to progress on screen.

### 4. Specificity protects dignity

Do not make rural life a decorative nostalgia set or a hardship display.
People, animals, labour, homes, and traditions have practical agency. Avoid
stereotype, clutter, exoticised sound, and unmotivated crowds.

### 5. Continuity begins before generation

Continuity is a design input, not an editor's final repair. Before a shot is
made, the team must know what was true immediately before it: location zone,
time, weather, clothing condition, object state, character position, carry,
screen direction, and emotional state.

### 6. Restraint creates room

Not every action needs dialogue, music, a close-up, an ASMR accent, or a cut.
Choose one primary visual or sound idea per moment. Let a finished action and
its result remain visible long enough for recognition.

### 7. Audience respect includes accuracy

YouTube packaging may invite attention, but must not promise danger, a mystery,
or a revelation the episode does not contain. Retention data can reveal
confusion or pacing weakness; it cannot authorize abandoning the series'
truthful, calm promise.

## Production Roles

One person may perform several roles in a small production, but every role's
responsibility must still be consciously checked.

### Canon steward

Maintains the list of controlling documents, resolves which source governs a
question, records confirmed episode handoffs, and stops undocumented additions.
The canon steward does not alter canon merely because a production need exists.

### Writer

Builds an episode from established people, places, objects, season, and
relationships. The writer documents need, gentle conflict, practical action,
emotional result, dialogue purpose, and all assumptions needing verification.

### Director

Turns an approved script into readable scenes and shots. The director protects
performance, geography, visual cause-and-effect, pace, and the emotional
purpose of every camera choice.

### AI artist / Google Flow operator

Generates only approved, prompt-ready shots; compares outputs to source canon;
rejects drift; and records accepted-frame continuity facts. The operator must
not solve a difficult generation by adding an unapproved prop, person, layout,
or period detail.

### Sound and music lead

Builds sound from visible, canonical sources. They preserve location, material,
weather, distance, dialogue windows, and silence. Music, if used, supports
rather than dictates emotion.

### Editor

Builds a legible sequence from approved shots. The editor checks travel,
screen direction, object state, light, sound bridges, task progress, and
breathing room after resolution.

### Release reviewer

Runs final story, factual, technical, and YouTube checks. This role has the
authority to hold release when a known issue remains unresolved.

## Production Lifecycle

Use the following lifecycle for every episode:

1. Select a canon-safe premise.
2. Verify its factual foundations.
3. Write and approve a production brief.
4. Draft and lock the script and beat sequence.
5. Build the scene, asset, continuity, sound, and prompt plans.
6. Generate and accept shots one continuity-aware sequence at a time.
7. Assemble picture and sound.
8. Review the whole episode, not only individual shots.
9. Prepare truthful YouTube packaging.
10. Release, record the episode's final state, and learn without changing
   creative principles for short-term metrics.

Do not begin a later stage merely because an earlier deliverable exists. Begin
only after its quality gate passes.

## Decision Framework

When a proposed choice arises, ask in this order:

1. Is it established in specific canon?
2. Does it fit the named season, time, location zone, and route?
3. Does this character plausibly make this choice?
4. Does it make the task or emotional turn clearer?
5. Can Google Flow produce it without creating continuity risk?
6. Does it fit the required episode format and truthful viewer promise?

If the answer to 1, 2, or 3 is no or unknown, stop. If the answer to 4, 5, or
6 is no, revise or remove the choice. Do not use visual attractiveness as a
tiebreaker against canon.

## Quality Gates

### Gate A — premise readiness

Pass only when the story has a documented practical need, gentle obstacle,
emotion, season, location, cast reason, and canon-safe solution.

### Gate B — script readiness

Pass only when each scene has a purpose, plausible transition, observable
action, emotional progression, and no unresolved factual assumption.

### Gate C — production readiness

Pass only when continuity logs, prompt packets, asset references, sound
intentions, and scene handoffs are prepared.

### Gate D — shot acceptance

Pass only when each accepted Google Flow output matches relevant canon and the
previous accepted shot. Reject drift rather than planning to hide it in edit.

### Gate E — episode readiness

Pass only when the assembled episode meets `06_Story_Engine/15_Story_Checklist.md`
and the relevant `07_Episode_System/` checks.

### Gate F — release readiness

Pass only when title, thumbnail, description, captions, metadata, and any
series connection represent the actual episode without spoilers, false stakes,
or modern visual contamination.

## Example: Correct Production Reasoning

An approved monsoon kitchen episode concerns damp firewood delaying a family
meal. Production begins by confirming the established kitchen zone, family
members, storage logic, season, rain condition, and objects. The director
plans rain on tile, a damp wood test, weak smoke, careful responses, a
canon-supported dry alternative, steady cooking, and the shared meal.

The Google Flow operator records that Mother remains at the fire, the wood is
first damp, the rain is steady, Younger Daughter is nearby but away from smoke,
and the Family Dog rests plausibly outside the active work space. The editor
does not cut immediately on the restored flame; the completed meal is allowed
to settle. The title promises a rain-day family task, not a disaster.

## Common Production Mistakes

* Treating a script approval as permission to invent visual detail.
* Generating isolated “pretty” shots and trying to connect them afterwards.
* Replacing an unavailable canonical object with a modern or generic one.
* Allowing character faces, age, clothing, house geometry, or dog markings to
  drift between accepted shots.
* Using a new location or route because it creates variety.
* Treating rain, harvest, or festival colour as a filter instead of a condition
  that changes behaviour.
* Adding loud effects, music, or cutting to compensate for a weak scene.
* Publishing a click-oriented title or thumbnail that changes the episode's
  emotional contract.
* Recording an issue but releasing anyway because it seems small in one shot.

## Final Rule

When production pressure and canon truth conflict, protect canon truth. An
episode can be simplified, regenerated, delayed, or redesigned; the permanent
world must not be bent to rescue a temporary production choice.

# Project-Wide Production Checklist

## Purpose

This manual provides the permanent project-wide gate system for taking a Village Stories episode from an early idea to a verified YouTube upload.

It answers:

> Is this episode ready to move forward, who decides, and what evidence proves it?

This is a decision framework, not a promise that every item will be relevant to every Short or long-form episode. Mark non-applicable items with a reason; never silently skip them.

---

# Philosophy

Quality is built at each handoff, not rescued at upload.

Village Stories protects a living world, gentle emotional truth, early-1990s rural South Indian authenticity, visual continuity, and calm ASMR-aware pacing. A project gate turns those values into observable decisions before an error becomes costly.

No gate exists to slow honest work. Each gate prevents a specific kind of avoidable failure: invented canon, weak story, unsafe generation, confusing handoff, broken continuity, unlicensed audio, or an unverified public release.

Passing a checklist does not replace responsible judgment. Failing to record a decision does not make it unnecessary.

---

# Objectives

Use this manual to:

* provide a shared definition of readiness at every stage;
* identify responsible roles and required handoffs;
* protect canon, continuity, research accuracy, and rights;
* ensure Google Flow output serves the planned episode rather than steering it randomly;
* ensure YouTube-facing metadata and files match the approved release;
* retain clear QA and approval evidence;
* expose open questions early enough to resolve safely;
* make a released episode reconstructible and reviewable.

# Gate Operating Rules

Every gate has five parts:

```text
Entry condition
Required evidence
Questions to answer
Decision owner
Exit decision
```

Possible exit decisions:

* `pass` — work may proceed.
* `pass_with_conditions` — work may proceed only with named conditions and owner.
* `hold` — an unresolved issue blocks the next stage.
* `rework` — material must be revised and resubmitted.
* `cancel` — the episode or work item should not continue.

Record the decision, date, revision, decision owner, and open items. A verbal “looks good” is not enough for a milestone.

---

# Roles and Handoffs

One person may fill multiple roles on a small production, but the decisions must remain distinct.

## Story Lead

Owns the premise, emotional truth, and Story Engine alignment.

## Canon/Continuity Owner

Protects established facts, chronology, character identity, location logic, objects, and world consistency.

## Research Owner

Validates evidence, historical caution, and unresolved factual questions.

## Production Lead

Owns schedule, scope, records, risk escalation, and stage readiness.

## Visual/Generation Lead

Owns reference use, storyboard feasibility, Google Flow generation records, and selected-shot continuity.

## Audio Lead

Owns dialogue, ambient sound, Foley, music rights, intelligibility, and audio continuity.

## Editor

Owns the picture/audio assembly, subtitles, editorial continuity, and review export.

## QA Reviewer

Checks the work against documented requirements and verifies issue resolution.

## Release Owner

Owns the approved master, YouTube metadata, upload settings, publication, and post-upload verification.

# Gate 0: Intake and Idea Qualification

## Entry condition

An idea exists but has not been promised to production.

## Required evidence

* one-sentence premise;
* intended format and approximate duration;
* proposed season, location, and characters;
* practical activity or relationship need;
* initial source references;
* statement of the viewer feeling or learning value.

## Questions

* Does the idea belong to Village Stories rather than another genre?
* Is its interest grounded in ordinary life, relationship, craft, weather, routine, or memory?
* Does it avoid false danger, cruelty, spectacle, and modern intrusion?
* Can it be supported by established canon or a clearly identified research task?
* Is it distinct from recently completed episodes in action, emotional movement, or setting?
* Is the intended format proportionate to the idea?

## Decision

The story lead and production lead accept, defer, research, or decline the idea.

## Exit

The project has an episode identifier, accountable owner, and no hidden assumption that an unverified detail is canon.

# Gate 1: Canon and Research Baseline

## Entry condition

The idea is accepted for development.

## Required evidence

Create an episode reference manifest containing:

* relevant canon documents;
* character, location, world, and object sources;
* research sources and cautions;
* season, time of day, chronology, and social context;
* approved reusable asset references;
* facts that must remain consistent;
* open factual questions with owner and deadline.

## Questions

* Are all characters available, appropriately aged, and behaving within established identity?
* Is the location layout, access, weather, and time of day possible?
* Are every object, tool, food, garment, vehicle, and utility period-appropriate?
* Does the activity fit the season, household routine, and village context?
* Are research claims evidenced rather than inferred from a convenient image?
* Would the idea create a new persistent fact, location, character, or relationship?

## Decision framework

If an essential fact is not documented:

1. search the existing authority and research records;
2. distinguish absent documentation from permission to invent;
3. assign research or canon review;
4. postpone depiction until it is approved;
5. record the resolution in the manifest.

## Exit

The baseline is approved, or unresolved facts are explicitly marked as blockers.

# Gate 2: Story Development

## Entry condition

The canon/research baseline supports the premise.

## Required evidence

* premise and theme;
* character need or skill;
* gentle conflict or practical obstacle;
* beginning, development, and emotional rest;
* scene list;
* planned educational, humour, ASMR, and visual beats when relevant;
* intended ending and return reason.

## Questions

* Does the episode show meaningful action rather than explaining it?
* Is the conflict proportionate, kind, and solvable?
* Do characters make choices that reveal their established personalities?
* Does the story let the location, weather, objects, and sounds matter?
* Is any lesson discovered through action rather than delivered as a lecture?
* Is humour dignified and never based on humiliation, stereotypes, or unnatural behaviour?
* Does the ending settle emotionally without manufactured cliffhangers?

## Google Flow relevance

Write actions that can be shown clearly. Replace abstract direction such as “feel nostalgic” with observable direction such as “the child pauses beside the repaired lantern and watches its warm light reach the doorway.”

## Exit

Story lead approves the outline; continuity owner confirms that planned actions remain within baseline.

---

# Gate 3: Episode Structure and Script

## Entry condition

The story outline is approved.

## Required evidence

* script or beat sheet;
* scene timing;
* hook, opening, middle, and ending plan;
* dialogue/narration plan;
* visual action plan;
* ASMR, ambience, music, and silence plan;
* subtitle and language requirements.

## Questions

* Does the opening earn attention with truthful sensory, emotional, or practical interest?
* Does the pacing match the intended Short or long-form format?
* Is the story understandable without excessive explanation?
* Does dialogue sound appropriate to character, place, and period?
* Does each scene advance action, relationship, atmosphere, or understanding?
* Are silence and natural sound given space where they carry meaning?
* Does the ending fulfill the episode rather than merely stop it?

## Handoff

Story lead hands the approved script/beat sheet, reference manifest, and unresolved constraints to production, visual, and audio leads.

## Exit

The production team can plan every scene without inventing missing story or canon decisions.

---

# Gate 4: Production Design and Storyboard

## Entry condition

The script or beat sheet is structurally approved.

## Required evidence

* scene and shot list;
* storyboard or visual plan;
* character, clothing, prop, location, weather, and lighting references;
* camera and transition notes;
* continuity map for entrances, exits, object positions, and time progression;
* estimated asset and generation needs.

## Questions

* Does each shot have a reason to exist?
* Are screen direction, scale, props, clothing, light, and weather consistent across the scene?
* Can the selected framing communicate the planned action without disorienting the viewer?
* Does visual rhythm support calm attention rather than constant movement?
* Does the plan avoid generic or culturally inaccurate visual shortcuts?
* Are recurring items referenced from approved sources rather than remade from memory?

## Decision framework

Choose between a new visual asset and reuse by asking:

1. Is a reusable approved asset accurate for this exact use?
2. Can it preserve continuity without visible mismatch?
3. Does creating new material add story value or only novelty?
4. Will reuse create incorrect weather, costume, lighting, or object state?

## Exit

Visual/generation lead and continuity owner approve a storyboard baseline. Any scope changes return to the relevant earlier gate.

---

# Gate 5: Generation and Asset Selection

## Entry condition

Storyboard and visual references are approved.

## Required evidence

For each selected visual or generated shot:

* episode, scene, and shot identifier;
* exact prompt or prompt record;
* source image/input references;
* candidate output reference;
* selected output reference;
* duration and technical notes;
* continuity notes;
* reviewer and selection decision.

For all assets:

* clear status: draft, candidate, review, approved, selected, locked, or superseded;
* provenance and rights record where needed;
* file location complying with `16_File_Organization.md`.

## Google Flow checklist

* Prompt describes the approved character, location, object, action, time, mood, and camera need.
* Input references are approved and correctly identified.
* Generated output has no unexplained modern object, clothing, architecture, utility, or text.
* Character appearance, age, expression, and movement match approved references.
* Props remain physically plausible and consistent with previous/next shots.
* Weather, light direction, time of day, and object state match the continuity map.
* A candidate is never treated as selected solely because it is the most recent run.
* The selected prompt and output are preserved outside the platform.

## Exit

Only selected, traceable assets are handed to editorial. Defects are regenerated or returned to storyboard, not patched by undocumented assumption.

---

# Gate 6: Audio Production

## Entry condition

The picture plan is sufficiently stable for sound work.

## Required evidence

* dialogue/voice record and approvals;
* ambience, Foley, music, and silence plan;
* source and rights records for non-original audio;
* preliminary mix;
* audio continuity notes;
* intelligibility and accessibility notes.

## Questions

* Does sound support the village environment and story action truthfully?
* Are ASMR moments natural and placed with intention?
* Is dialogue intelligible without over-compression or unnatural loudness?
* Does music support rather than force emotion?
* Are there enough rests for environmental sound and silence?
* Are all music, recordings, and sound effects authorized for the intended use?
* Do recurring sound cues remain consistent with prior established usage?

## Exit

Audio lead approves the sound package for edit, with unresolved mix items documented.

---

# Gate 7: Editorial Assembly

## Entry condition

Selected picture and sound assets are available with source records.

## Required evidence

* edit project reference;
* review export;
* versioned edit manifest;
* subtitles/captions;
* audio mix state;
* list of intentional deviations from storyboard;
* source asset list.

## Questions

* Does the edit preserve story logic, geography, object state, and character action?
* Is the pace calm but purposeful for its format?
* Are visual cuts, sound transitions, and silences intentional?
* Is there an accidental jump in costume, hand position, object amount, weather, or time?
* Are captions accurate, timed, readable, and faithful to dialogue/narration?
* Does the episode still satisfy the approved story and episode-system requirements?

## Handoff

Editor sends the review export, asset manifest, subtitle state, issue list, and deviation list to QA.

## Exit

The review cut is identifiable, accessible to reviewers, and traceable to approved sources.

# Gate 8: Continuity, Quality, and Rights QA

## Entry condition

A review cut and its records are ready.

## Required evidence

* completed QA checklist;
* continuity review;
* research/canon exceptions review;
* visual and audio defect log;
* subtitle/caption review;
* rights verification;
* issue owner, severity, and resolution record.

## QA checklist

### Canon and continuity

* Characters, relationships, ages, clothing, and behaviour match authority.
* Locations, objects, tools, food, architecture, and utilities are accurate.
* Season, weather, time, chronology, and travel are plausible.
* No scene accidentally creates a new permanent fact.

### Story and emotion

* The conflict remains gentle and truthful.
* Emotional beats are earned through action and relationship.
* The episode does not use artificial danger, humiliation, or false cliffhangers.
* The ending provides emotional rest.

### Visual and audio

* Selected shots are technically usable and visually coherent.
* No generation artifact, unwanted text, or modern intrusion remains.
* Audio is intelligible, balanced, and rights-cleared.
* ASMR elements are purposeful and not overused.

### Delivery

* Duration, aspect ratio, frame rate, resolution, and audio format match release requirements.
* Thumbnail and metadata accurately represent the episode.
* Captions and disclosures meet the intended audience and platform needs.

## Severity framework

* `blocker` — cannot release; affects rights, safety, core canon, broken file, or materially misleading delivery.
* `major` — must resolve unless release owner documents an exceptional decision; affects comprehension, continuity, or quality substantially.
* `minor` — correct when practical; does not change the episode’s truth or usability.
* `note` — retained for learning; no action required.

## Exit

QA either approves the release candidate or returns a specific, prioritized issue list. “No time” is not a resolution for a blocker.

# Gate 9: Release Readiness

## Entry condition

QA has approved the release candidate and all blockers are resolved.

## Required evidence

* exact approved master reference;
* release approval record;
* thumbnail and metadata approvals;
* caption/subtitle files;
* rights and credit confirmation;
* release archive draft;
* backup confirmation;
* upload checklist.

## YouTube checklist

* Correct account/channel and intended visibility are selected.
* Upload master is the exact approved release file.
* Title, description, credits, links, chapters, and disclosures match approval.
* Thumbnail is the approved final image and meets platform requirements.
* Audience setting is correct.
* Captions/subtitles are attached and reviewed.
* Playlist, language, category, and any other selected settings are intentional.
* Copyright, music, sponsorship, altered-content, and other disclosures are reviewed where applicable.
* Scheduled date/time and time zone are correct.

## Decision framework

If a late issue appears, ask:

1. Is it a blocker, major, minor, or note?
2. Does correcting it alter a locked master, metadata, rights status, or public expectation?
3. Is re-QA required after correction?
4. Does postponement protect quality more than schedule pressure?

## Exit

Release owner signs off on the exact master and upload package. Create the release archive before publication.

---

# Gate 10: Upload and Publication Verification

## Entry condition

The release package is approved and archived.

## Required evidence

* upload record;
* YouTube video ID and URL;
* publication date/time;
* post-upload viewing check;
* final live metadata record;
* any platform processing, copyright, or visibility result;
* release archive completion.

## Verify after processing

* The live video is the intended master, without unexpected truncation or corruption.
* Picture, audio, captions, and thumbnail display correctly.
* Title, description, links, credits, chapters, and disclosures are correct.
* Visibility, audience, playlist, and scheduled time are correct.
* No unresolved platform claim or restriction prevents the intended release.
* The public page accurately represents the calm, authentic series promise.

## Exit

Record `released` only after live verification. If a material discrepancy is found, follow the correction and rollback controls in `17_Version_Control.md`.

---

# Gate 11: Post-Release Archive and Learning

## Entry condition

The episode is publicly available and verified.

## Required evidence

* protected release archive;
* final master and checksum when feasible;
* upload metadata record and URL;
* selected prompt/asset manifest;
* final QA and approval records;
* backup confirmation;
* concise retrospective.

## Retrospective questions

* What strengthened authenticity, emotion, ASMR, clarity, or viewer understanding?
* Which continuity controls caught a real issue?
* Which workflow friction should be improved without weakening controls?
* Did a reusable asset, prompt pattern, or checklist item prove valuable?
* Does any new lesson require an update to a production manual, research record, or canon proposal?

Do not edit canon merely because an episode has been released. Propose an explicit change through the relevant authority if a durable update is necessary.

## Exit

The episode is recoverable, its decisions are traceable, and its lessons are assigned to the proper authoritative document.

# Stop-the-Line Conditions

Pause the current gate and notify the accountable owner when any of the following occurs:

* a canon contradiction or unapproved new permanent fact;
* uncertain historical detail that is visible or narratively important;
* a potentially unlicensed visual, music, voice, or sound asset;
* a selected Google Flow output with unexplained modern or culturally inaccurate content;
* missing source records for an approved asset;
* lost or inaccessible release master;
* unresolved blocker QA issue;
* uncertainty about the exact file being uploaded;
* an already published episode requiring material correction.

Stopping is a quality action, not a failure. Resume only after the resolution is recorded.

# Common Mistakes

## Starting generation before canon review

An attractive image can make an unsupported assumption feel real. Establish the reference baseline first.

## Treating a generated output as research

Google Flow can visualize a direction; it does not verify historical truth or establish canon.

## Passing a gate without evidence

An unchecked memory cannot support a later handoff. Link the actual file, revision, or approval record.

## Letting schedule pressure override a blocker

Late uploads can be corrected. Public continuity, rights, and trust damage are harder to repair.

## Checking only visual quality

Audio, captions, metadata, rights, and emotional honesty are part of the episode experience.

## Calling an upload complete before watching it

Platform processing can change the delivered result. Verify the live page and playback.

---

# Continuity and Canon Authority

This gate system catches and routes contradictions; it does not resolve them by itself.

When an episode plan conflicts with a permanent source:

1. preserve the conflicting evidence;
2. stop the affected stage;
3. consult the source authority and accountable owner;
4. revise the episode or formally propose a canon change;
5. document the approved resolution;
6. re-run all impacted gates.

The most convenient production choice never outranks established canon.

---

# Guiding Principle

Move an episode forward only when the next team member can trust the work, understand its sources, and protect the Village Stories world without guessing.

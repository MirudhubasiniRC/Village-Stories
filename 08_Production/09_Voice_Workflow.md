# Voice Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines the permanent process for recording, generating, editing, reviewing, and approving narration and spoken performance for Village Stories.

The voice must make the viewer feel welcomed into a remembered, lived-in village. It must never flatten the work into a lecture, sell the emotion too hard, or speak over the natural life of a scene.

---

## Philosophy

Narration is a companion, not an announcer.

It notices what the image cannot fully carry, gives emotional context when needed, and then makes room for hands, weather, animals, work, and silence. Its warmth comes from attention and restraint, not from exaggerated sweetness or constant explanation.

The most effective line is often the shortest truthful line—or no line at all.

---

## Objectives

Every approved voice track must:

* Sound warm, clear, calm, and emotionally honest.
* Support, rather than duplicate, the visual storytelling.
* Respect the established character, culture, and early-1990s village setting.
* Preserve meaningful ASMR, environmental sound, and silence.
* Follow the approved scene timing and YouTube delivery format.
* Be technically clean, intelligible, and consistently balanced.

---

## Scope and Boundary

This document governs narration and any scripted spoken voice used in an episode.

It does not establish character biography, invent dialogue, decide the story's emotional meaning, replace audio sound-design rules, or change the approved episode structure. Voice production must serve the relevant canon, story, episode, animation, and audio documents.

Do not add spoken explanation merely because an image takes time to understand. First ask whether the image, edit, or a small sound cue can carry the meaning.

---

## Roles

### Writer or Episode Lead

Supplies the approved script, scene intent, pronunciation notes, and clear reason for every spoken line.

### Voice Director

Guides warmth, pace, emphasis, breathing, and emotional restraint. Protects the series voice from promotional, theatrical, or generic AI delivery.

### Narrator or Voice Performer

Performs the approved text faithfully while offering natural variation within the stated intention.

### Dialogue Editor

Removes technical defects, selects takes, manages timing, and prepares clean stems without damaging human rhythm.

### Audio Reviewer

Reviews intelligibility, continuity, tone, cultural care, and space for environmental sound.

---

## Canon Authority

Follow authority in this order:

1. Character, world, location, object, and culture documents determine facts and pronunciation context.
2. Series Style Lock, especially `Audio_Identity.md` and `Storytelling_Identity.md`, determines series expression.
3. Story Engine determines emotional honesty and narrative function.
4. Episode System determines pacing, hook, opening, ending, and format requirements.
5. This manual determines voice-production procedure.

A polished read of an incorrect line is still incorrect. Revise the script or regenerate the take; do not use delivery to disguise a canon conflict.

---

## Voice Identity

The approved narration voice should be:

* Warm without being syrupy.
* Patient without being slow or sleepy by default.
* Clear without sounding like a commercial.
* Familiar without pretending to know more than the scene shows.
* Gentle with children, elders, animals, work, faith, and everyday difficulty.
* Capable of quiet delight, concern, humour, and reflection without melodrama.

Avoid the rhythm of a trailer, tourism documentary, motivational speech, news report, influencer video, or lecture.

---

## Script Readiness

Do not record an unreviewed script.

Before production, confirm for every line:

* What new information, feeling, or transition does it provide?
* Could the image already communicate this without words?
* Does it state an established fact accurately?
* Is its vocabulary natural, respectful, and understandable for the intended audience?
* Does it leave enough time for the viewer to see the action?
* Does it leave a window for featured natural sound or silence?

Delete lines that merely describe what is plainly visible: “She is stirring the pot” over a clear shot of stirring. Keep a line only when it gives meaning, memory, relationship, or a necessary orientation.

---

## Script Markup

Prepare a marked narration script before recording.

For each line, note:

* Episode, scene, and shot range.
* Purpose: orient, connect, reflect, reveal, transition, or close.
* Delivery temperature: observant, tender, playful, thoughtful, or quiet concern.
* Pace and planned pause.
* Word or phrase requiring accurate pronunciation.
* What visual or sound must remain audible beneath or after the line.
* Whether the line may be shortened in the edit.

Use slashes for small breaths and brackets for intentional silence. Do not mark every word for emphasis; a crowded page produces an overperformed read.

---

## Pronunciation and Language Care

Use the actual names, terms, and pronunciation guidance established by canon or verified research. When uncertain:

1. Check the relevant canon document.
2. Check approved research and prior episode records.
3. Ask a qualified cultural or language reviewer when the term affects meaning or respect.
4. Record a pronunciation reference before final performance.

Do not replace an unfamiliar local word with a generic modern term only because it seems easier. Equally, do not use untranslated terminology as decoration when the audience cannot infer meaning from context.

## Detailed Workflow

### 1. Receive the Locked Scene Packet

Collect the approved edit or animatic, script, continuity ledger, featured-sound plan, and applicable character/location references.

### 2. Read the Scene Without Recording

Watch once with scratch audio muted. Identify what the picture already says. Watch again with the natural sound plan. Mark only the gaps that narration should fill.

### 3. Verify Facts and Language

Check names, relationships, customs, tools, food, time of day, season, and period detail against source documentation. Flag uncertainty before the recording session.

### 4. Mark Rhythm

Place breaths and pauses around action completion, looks, transitions, and natural sounds. Do not force the narration to fill every second.

### 5. Record a Calibration Passage

Record several lines representing neutral observation, small warmth, quiet humour, and reflection. The Voice Director approves the voice temperature before the full session.

### 6. Record Full Takes

Record at least one faithful, unhurried take before exploring small alternatives. Keep pre-roll and post-roll silence; do not cut breaths at capture.

### 7. Select by Context

Choose takes while watching the scene. A beautiful isolated sentence may step on a visual beat or end too late for the next shot.

### 8. Edit Transparently

Remove noise, accidental clicks, and obvious unwanted breaths. Preserve purposeful breaths, human timing, and the small imperfections that make the voice feel present.

### 9. Check Against Natural Audio

Lay the narration over the planned room tone, work sound, rain, birds, or silence. Re-time or shorten the line if it masks the featured sound.

### 10. Obtain Approval

The Voice Director approves performance; the Episode Lead approves meaning; the Audio Reviewer approves context and technical quality.

### 11. Deliver Organized Stems

Provide the approved narration stem, optional alternates, script version, timecode notes, pronunciation record, and edit notes to music/SFX and post-production.

## Direction Method

Direct from action and relationship rather than abstract intensity.

Instead of:

> Make it deeply emotional.

Use:

> She has been waiting for the rain to ease before sending him out. Let the concern be present, but do not announce it. Leave a small pause after “wait.”

Instead of:

> Sound happy.

Use:

> He has noticed the first ripe guava. This is a private, ordinary delight; let the smile arrive late in the line.

The listener should feel that the narrator is seeing the moment, not performing a label for it.

---

## Pace and Silence

Narration pace must follow comprehension and emotional space, not a fixed words-per-minute target.

Use silence when:

* A hand action has an important tactile sound.
* A character's look changes the emotional meaning.
* Rain, wind, a kettle, a bird, a bicycle bell, or a work rhythm establishes place.
* The episode needs rest after information or emotion.
* The viewer benefits from simply being in the location.

Do not leave silence because the story has stopped. Leave it because the village is speaking.

---

## Narration and Character Dialogue

Narration should not explain a character's feeling immediately after the character expresses it through action or dialogue.

When character dialogue is used:

* Keep it age-appropriate and consistent with the profile.
* Allow natural overlap only when it remains understandable.
* Do not make every character speak in the narrator's poetic language.
* Preserve local social respect and family dynamics without caricature.
* Let a practical task remain practical; do not turn ordinary conversation into a speech.

If a dialogue line changes a relationship, object ownership, plan, or event, update the continuity record.

---

## AI Voice Use

If a synthetic voice is used, it must be directed and reviewed to the same standard as a human recording.

* Use only an approved, rights-cleared voice identity.
* Never imitate a real person without explicit permission and lawful authorization.
* Build a tested pronunciation lexicon for recurring names and Tamil words.
* Generate short, scene-specific units rather than one uncontrolled episode-long read.
* Inspect sentence joins for changed pitch, pace, breath, or emotional temperature.
* Avoid excessively smooth, breathless, or emotionally inflated output.
* Preserve an audit record of voice source, script version, and approvals.

AI convenience never permits an uncanny, generic, or culturally careless performance.

---

## Google Flow Integration

Google Flow clips must be reviewed before the voice is locked. Watch the approved generation, not a text description, and mark:

* The exact action and reaction that narration must not describe redundantly.
* The featured natural-sound and silence windows created by the animation.
* Any visible mouth movement that requires dialogue sync or makes narration-only treatment preferable.
* Shot handles and camera moves that affect phrase length or entry timing.
* Visual facts—object state, weather, time, and character behaviour—that the script may reference.

If Google Flow introduces a continuity error or an ambiguous action, return the shot to animation. Do not use narration to explain away a visual contradiction.

---

## Technical Recording and Editing Standard

Capture and maintain voice at the highest practical clean resolution for post-production. Use a controlled, quiet recording environment and retain an unprocessed original.

During edit:

* Remove persistent noise without making the voice watery or metallic.
* Correct only distracting breaths, mouth noises, or clicks.
* Keep volume and tonal character consistent across a scene.
* Do not apply aggressive compression that eliminates the series' intimate dynamic range.
* Preserve head and tail handles for re-timing.
* Name files by episode, scene, script revision, performer/source, and take.

Technical polish must make the voice easier to hear, not make it sound detached from the world.

---

## Continuity Requirements

Maintain a voice continuity note across the episode:

* Narrative tense and point of view.
* Pronunciation of names, places, objects, and terms.
* Emotional temperature after each turning point.
* Character vocal state when dialogue continues across scenes.
* Any line that references time, weather, unfinished work, or object state.
* Pauses reserved for recurring sound motifs.

Review the complete episode after individual scene approval. Narration drift is often audible only when several scenes play together.

---

## Quality Checks

### Meaning

* Does each line earn its place?
* Is every factual statement canonically correct?
* Does the wording honour the culture and characters?

### Performance

* Is the voice warm, clear, restrained, and specific to the moment?
* Is emphasis natural rather than promotional?
* Are pauses intentional?

### Picture Relationship

* Does the line arrive after the viewer has enough visual orientation?
* Does it avoid describing obvious action?
* Does it leave key looks and tactile actions audible?

### Audio Relationship

* Can the featured natural sound still be heard?
* Is silence protected where planned?
* Does the voice sit naturally with the scene's acoustic space?

### Continuity and Delivery

* Are names and terms pronounced consistently?
* Are file names, revisions, stems, and timecodes complete?
* Does the track remain intelligible on modest speakers and phone playback?

## Common Mistakes and Corrections

### Mistake: Narrating Every Image

The script reports each action as it occurs.

**Correction:** remove literal description. Keep only context the image cannot carry, then let sound and visuals breathe.

### Mistake: Emotional Advertising Voice

The delivery rises into dramatic emphasis for a small domestic moment.

**Correction:** lower intensity, shorten the line, and locate the emotion in the character's action rather than the narrator's performance.

### Mistake: Filling Every Pause

Silence is treated as an editing error.

**Correction:** identify the place sound or visual beat that owns the silence. Hold it deliberately.

### Mistake: Inconsistent Tamil Pronunciation

A recurring name changes between scenes or is pronounced from spelling rather than an approved reference.

**Correction:** use the lexicon and re-record the affected line. Do not normalize the error in the mix.

### Mistake: Audio Cleanup Removes Humanity

Heavy processing turns breaths and room presence into a sterile synthetic sound.

**Correction:** return to the clean source and use lighter restoration. Intimacy needs controlled natural detail.

---

## Decision Framework

When deciding whether to keep a narration line, ask in order:

1. Is it true to canon and this scene?
2. Does it add meaning that image, action, or sound cannot provide?
3. Does it preserve the character's dignity and the episode's emotional honesty?
4. Does it leave room for natural audio and silence?
5. Does its pace serve the viewer and the platform format?
6. Is the delivery warm and unforced?

If the answer to the first four questions is no, remove or rewrite the line before refining performance.

---

## YouTube Considerations

For YouTube, voice must orient the viewer without becoming a retention trick.

* In a Short, the opening line may provide quick context, but the first image and sound must remain strong.
* Keep spoken ideas compact enough to be understood on phone speakers.
* Do not shout, tease false danger, or repeat a hook to manufacture urgency.
* In long-form episodes, vary narration density and preserve full quiet passages.
* Captions must match the approved final script and retain names and cultural terms accurately.
* If a line is essential for understanding, ensure the visual edit gives it a readable home.

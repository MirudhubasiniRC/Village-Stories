# Music and SFX Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines the permanent workflow for selecting, creating, editing, mixing, reviewing, and approving music, environmental sound, foley, ASMR, and silence for Village Stories.

Sound is not a layer added after the picture is complete. It is part of the village's continuity: rain arrives on roofs and leaves, a stove has a particular breath, tools answer the hands that use them, and rooms have distinct quiet.

---

## Philosophy

The soundscape should make the audience feel present, not impressed by production.

Natural sound, restrained music, and meaningful silence are equal storytelling tools. A quiet pot simmering may matter more than a score. A music cue may gently connect a memory or change in feeling, but it must never instruct the audience to feel what the picture has not earned.

Sound should be specific to place, material, weather, time, and action.

---

## Objectives

Every approved mix must:

* Preserve a credible early-1990s South Indian village sound world.
* Match the visual continuity of place, weather, time, material, and action.
* Give natural audio and ASMR a clear narrative role.
* Use music sparingly and emotionally honestly.
* Keep narration and essential action intelligible.
* Respect the viewer's need for rest, not constant stimulation.
* Meet the technical requirements of YouTube delivery without crushing dynamic range.

---

## Scope and Boundary

This manual governs music and sound-effects production through the pre-master mix.

It does not redefine the series' audio identity, write story content, change visual canon, or perform final video assembly. Canon sources define what the village contains; animation and edit decisions define what is seen; this manual defines how it is heard.

Never add a sound simply because the timeline feels empty. First determine whether the scene calls for room tone, a real action detail, music, or silence.

---

## Roles

### Episode Lead

Defines the scene's emotional function and approves whether sound supports the story.

### Sound Designer

Builds location beds, action sounds, foley, transitions, and ASMR details consistent with picture and canon.

### Music Lead or Composer

Selects or creates music cues with appropriate emotional restraint, instrumentation, and continuity.

### Dialogue and Mix Editor

Balances voice, music, SFX, room tone, and silence; protects clarity and natural dynamics.

### Continuity Lead

Checks that audible facts match the visual ledger: weather, location, material, tool, time, and off-screen activity.

### Audio Reviewer

Approves technical readiness, cultural authenticity, series identity, and YouTube playback suitability.

---

## Canon Authority

Apply the following order:

1. World, location, object, character, and research documents define the sound sources that can truthfully exist.
2. `Audio_Identity.md` and other Series Style Lock documents define sonic character.
3. Story Engine and Episode System define the emotional and structural use of sound.
4. The approved edit and continuity ledger define what must align with picture.
5. This manual defines the sound-production process.

If a compelling sound contradicts the image, period, place, or story, it is a defect. Do not retain it for energy or polish.

---

## Sound Categories

### Location Bed

The low-level, sustained acoustic identity of a place: courtyard air, a quiet kitchen, schoolyard distance, tea-stall presence, field wind, or rain under a roof.

### Environmental Detail

Intermittent living-world sounds: birds, insects, distant voices, leaves, animals, distant work, a cycle passing, or a temple sound where canonically and spatially appropriate.

### Action SFX and Foley

Sounds attached to visible contact: a vessel placed down, rice washed, sandals on earth, a door latch, grinding, sweeping, chopping, cloth folded, or a tool used properly.

### ASMR Feature

A deliberately heard tactile or natural action that invites attention: water poured into metal, rain on tile, firewood catching, grain shifting, or a broom across a courtyard.

### Music

A restrained emotional or structural layer. Music can connect, soften, open, close, or reflect; it must not replace story truth.

### Silence

An intentional absence of foreground sound that makes room for visual attention, a real room tone, a look, or a transition.

---

## Audio Continuity Ledger

Maintain an audio entry alongside the visual scene ledger.

For every scene, record:

* Location, time of day, season, weather, and interior/exterior condition.
* Base ambience and its perspective.
* Visible action sounds and materials.
* Off-screen sounds that are logically audible.
* Music cue name, entrance, exit, and emotional purpose.
* Narration windows and protected silence.
* Sound bridges across scene changes.
* Any intentional change in acoustic point of view.

Do not use the same generic bird loop, wind bed, or room tone in every scene. Repetition becomes a false world signal.

---

## Required Inputs

Before sound work begins, obtain:

1. Approved or near-locked picture edit with handles.
2. Scene objective and emotional progression.
3. Visual continuity ledger and shot notes.
4. Relevant location, object, season, and research documents.
5. Narration script or approved voice stem with timecodes.
6. Notes identifying featured ASMR moments and intended silences.
7. Existing recurring music and sound motif library, if any.

If picture timing is still changing rapidly, build only a flexible temp pass. Do not over-detail foley that will be destroyed by later edits.

---

## Detailed Workflow

### 1. Spot the Episode

Watch the full edit with no added score or effects. Mark story turns, tactile actions, location changes, narration windows, sound bridges, and true silent beats.

### 2. Confirm the Sound Reality

For each scene, ask what the place would actually sound like at that time, in that weather, from that camera distance. Consult canon and research before choosing assets.

### 3. Build the Location Bed

Lay in a restrained, stable base ambience for each location. Use variation and perspective so it remains alive without drawing attention to looping.

### 4. Place Essential Action Sounds

Add sounds that make the visible action physically legible. Match material, force, distance, hand timing, and object scale.

### 5. Choose One or Two ASMR Priorities

Do not make every action a featured sound. Choose the tactile sounds that carry process, calm, transition, or memory, then give them space.

### 6. Create Silence Intentionally

Remove competing layers where a look, action, or room needs to settle. Silence may still contain a very low truthful room presence.

### 7. Add Music Only After the Natural Pass

If the scene works with ambience, action, and silence, it may need no score. Add music only for a defined emotional or structural reason.

### 8. Integrate Narration

Make room for words rather than pushing narration above a crowded mix. Thin or pause background layers first, then use gentle level control only as needed.

### 9. Mix in Context

Review every transition, cut, and sound bridge across the full episode. Individual scenes cannot be approved in isolation.

### 10. Test Delivery

Check on studio monitoring, ordinary speakers, headphones, and phone playback. Confirm that important voice and action remain clear without eliminating all dynamic contrast.

### 11. Approve and Archive

Deliver organized stems, cue sheet, source/license records, mix revision, and audio continuity ledger to post-production.

## Google Flow Integration

Google Flow generation is the source of visible sound truth, not a substitute for sound design.

For every approved clip, inspect the actual action frame by frame when necessary:

* Identify which contacts, materials, weather conditions, and movements need audible support.
* Match foley timing to the generated hand, foot, tool, cloth, water, or object movement.
* Use only the acoustic perspective implied by the camera and location.
* Preserve the clip's useful natural-sound window; do not blanket it with generic ambience or score.
* Flag physically impossible or continuity-breaking motion for animation correction rather than inventing a sound that makes it seem plausible.

The sound pass should make Google Flow animation feel more materially grounded while keeping the village's established acoustic identity.

---

## Natural Audio and ASMR Method

Treat natural sound as action with texture and consequence.

For a featured sound:

1. Identify the visible source and material.
2. Confirm the action's exact start and end frame.
3. Choose or record a sound with appropriate weight, resonance, and distance.
4. Remove nearby competing sound only if the scene benefits.
5. Let the sound decay naturally when the image permits.
6. Check that it has not become exaggerated, comic, or unnaturally close.

For example, washing rice should include water, grain, vessel contact, and hand rhythm—but not a glossy, oversized splash that belongs to an advertisement.

---

## Music Decision Framework

Before using music, answer:

* What exact story function does this cue serve?
* Could natural sound or silence serve that function better?
* Does the cue match the scene's cultural and emotional setting?
* Does it preserve the dignity of ordinary work and feeling?
* Can it enter and leave without announcing itself?

Use music for:

* A gentle opening orientation.
* A modest emotional connection after the scene has earned it.
* A memory, seasonal shift, or closing rest.
* A continuity motif used consistently and sparingly.

Do not use music to:

* Tell the viewer that a small event is monumental.
* Cover weak animation, unclear editing, or absent ambience.
* Make rural life sound exotic, tragic, or falsely quaint.
* Force retention through sudden drops, impacts, or suspense pulses.

---

## Music Palette and Restraint

Follow the Series Style Lock for the approved music identity. Until a specific recurring palette is canonically defined, prefer music that is:

* Warm, textural, and modest in scale.
* Rhythmically unhurried when the scene is unhurried.
* Supportive of local emotional texture without reducing culture to a stock “ethnic” sound.
* Sparse enough for spoken words and natural details to remain present.
* Free of contemporary trailer impacts, EDM-style drops, aggressive risers, or synthetic spectacle unless specifically established by canon.

Maintain a cue log. If a cue recurs, record its intended association and do not assign it a contradictory meaning later.

---

## Location and Perspective Rules

Sound must honour where the viewer is.

* A kitchen has contained reflections, nearby utensils, and household texture; an open field has air, distance, and less hard reflection.
* Rain under a tiled roof differs from rain in a courtyard.
* A sound behind a wall, at a road, or in another room should not play as if it is beside the camera.
* An off-screen animal or worker must be plausible for the location, time, and activity.
* A cut from exterior to interior should carry a deliberate acoustic change, not retain the same full-bright ambience.

When the camera changes distance, adjust sound perspective gradually or cut with intention.

---

## Object and Material Sound Integrity

Use object documentation to identify material and handling.

Check:

* Metal vessels ring differently from clay, wood, stone, and steel.
* A small tool cannot produce the weight of a large one.
* Wet earth, dry earth, tile, woven mat, and wood require different footsteps.
* Cloth, leaves, grain, firewood, water, and cooking all have distinct textures.
* Repeated objects should retain recognizable sonic character when heard again.

Do not substitute generic plastic, glass, electronic, or modern appliance sounds for traditional materials.

---

## Early-1990s Authenticity

Reject or question:

* Modern ringtones, notification sounds, digital-payment cues, and contemporary vehicle signals.
* Modern appliance hums or electronic ambience not established by canon.
* Pop-culture music choices that break the setting or date the series incorrectly.
* Generic “jungle,” “exotic market,” or exaggerated poverty sound libraries.
* Constant crowd noise in places that should be quiet.

Authenticity is not silence or hardship. It is specific, period-appropriate human life.

---

## Mixing Principles

Build the mix around story attention:

1. Essential spoken meaning.
2. Featured visual action and ASMR.
3. Emotional truth of the scene.
4. Credible location bed.
5. Supporting detail.
6. Music, when justified.

Use level, frequency balance, and arrangement to make space. Avoid making every layer loud. Loudness is not presence.

Preserve dynamic contrast: a room can grow quieter before rain; a small vessel sound can be intimate; a music entrance can remain modest. Do not normalize every moment to the same intensity.

---

## Quality Checks

### Authenticity

* Are all sound sources possible in this place, era, season, and time?
* Do materials and activities sound credible?

### Picture Sync

* Do contact sounds align with hands, tools, footsteps, and object weight?
* Does sound perspective match framing and location?

### Story

* Does each layer serve action, emotion, orientation, or transition?
* Has music earned its use?

### ASMR and Silence

* Is there room for the selected tactile sounds?
* Are meaningful quiet beats protected from filler?

### Continuity

* Do ambience, weather, recurring motifs, and object sounds carry correctly across scenes?
* Are transitions deliberate rather than abrupt accidents?

### Technical Delivery

* Is narration intelligible without pushing the rest of the mix flat?
* Are there no clicks, bad loops, unwanted clipping, or abrupt tails?
* Does the mix translate to phone playback while retaining natural range?

## Common Mistakes and Corrections

### Mistake: Wallpaper Ambience

The same birds and breeze play under every scene.

**Correction:** build location- and time-specific beds. Reduce layers where the location should feel still.

### Mistake: Music Tells the Audience What to Feel

A heavy sentimental cue begins before the character's action has meaning.

**Correction:** delay, thin, or remove the cue. Let the story action establish emotion first.

### Mistake: Foley Is Too Clean or Loud

Every vessel clink and footstep sounds close, glossy, and identical.

**Correction:** match distance, material, force, and room. Let some actions remain understated.

### Mistake: Silence Is Filled With a Drone

An obvious pad or constant score hides a necessary pause.

**Correction:** retain only the truthful room presence, or use genuine silence if the edit calls for it.

### Mistake: Modern Sound Enters Unnoticed

A familiar contemporary effect slips into a period scene.

**Correction:** reject it against the authenticity checklist and replace it with a verified period-appropriate source.

---

## YouTube Considerations

YouTube playback conditions vary, but the answer is not constant loudness.

* Make narration and essential actions understandable on a phone speaker.
* Preserve enough contrast for ASMR and quiet reflection to remain meaningful on headphones.
* Start a Short with a truthful, recognizable sound or image; avoid loud stingers.
* Avoid sudden level changes that punish listeners who are using headphones.
* Provide captions for speech, but do not rely on captions to repair an unintelligible mix.
* Keep music rights, source records, and licenses organized before upload.

Platform delivery should make the episode accessible without changing its sonic identity.

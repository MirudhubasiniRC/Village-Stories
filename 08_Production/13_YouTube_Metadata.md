# YouTube Metadata

## Purpose

This manual establishes the permanent metadata system for Village Stories
uploads. Metadata helps the right viewer understand, find, and return to an
episode without distorting its story.

It governs titles, descriptions, tags where useful, chapters, playlists,
captions, language fields, cards, end screens, pinned comments, and metadata
review. It does not replace the episode, thumbnail, or canon.

## Philosophy

Metadata is a clear label on a truthful experience. It should name the people,
place, task, season, feeling, and series context that the episode actually
contains. Searchability is valuable only when it leads viewers to a fitting
video.

Village Stories earns engagement through clarity, emotional honesty, and a
dependable world—not sensational claims, keyword stuffing, or artificial
urgency.

## Objectives

Metadata must:

1. State the episode's real subject and format.
2. Help viewers discover relevant village-life, family, craft, food, season,
   and gentle-story content honestly.
3. Set expectations fulfilled by the thumbnail and opening.
4. Provide accurate accessibility and language support.
5. Connect episodes into useful playlists and truthful continuities.
6. Preserve the early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu **setting** in all public language,
   while titles may use familiar pan-Indian dish search names (e.g. kheer, poori,
   samosa) when the episode truly shows that food — see
   `02_World/Culture/Food_Repertoire_And_Audience_Bridge.md`.
7. Create a durable release record that can be audited or updated safely.

## Truth Rules

Never:

* Claim an event, lesson, tradition, recipe, repair, or outcome not shown.
* Use “shocking,” “danger,” “emergency,” “viral,” “must watch,” or equivalent
  escalation for ordinary gentle stakes.
* Call a reconstructed or stylized scene “real footage” or “documentary”
  unless that classification is accurate and approved.
* Use present-day village assumptions, modern conveniences, or anachronistic
  cultural labels in the copy.
* Add unrelated trending names, current events, brands, celebrities, or
  high-volume keyword lists to attract mismatched audiences.
* Promise a sequel, reveal, giveaway, or upload date without a verified plan.
* Disclose private production information, individual personal data, or
  unlicensed claims about music, images, or sources.

If a line would disappoint a viewer who watches the full episode carefully,
rewrite or remove it.

## Roles

### Metadata Lead

Creates the release brief, drafts all fields, manages versions, and confirms
that every field has a source in the locked episode.

### Episode Director

Approves the episode summary, title direction, educational wording, and links
to connected episodes.

### Canon Reviewer

Confirms names, relationships, locations, objects, seasonal language, cultural
detail, and historical framing.

### Accessibility Reviewer

Checks captions, language labels, chapter language, links, and descriptions
for clarity and accuracy.

### Publisher

Enters approved fields in YouTube, verifies the rendered result, and records
the published URL and metadata version.

## Metadata Source Brief

Create a source brief after picture and sound lock. It contains:

* Episode ID, format, duration, and release status.
* Final story premise in one sentence.
* Main characters, established location, season, and time of day.
* Visible task, gentle obstacle, and earned resolution.
* Educational or cultural context that is actually demonstrated.
* Relevant series, playlist, and previous/next episode links.
* Approved thumbnail concept and exact opening promise.
* Verified languages, captions, credits, and rights notes.

Do not draft from an early outline once the final cut differs from it.

## Title System

### Title purpose

A title should make a real episode worth choosing by naming its meaningful
action, object, person, place, or season. It is not a summary of every beat.

### Title patterns

Use the pattern that describes the actual entry point:

* **Task + condition:** `Cooking with Damp Firewood | Village Stories`
* **Character + practical action:** `Father Repairs the Old Rope | Village Stories`
* **Object + question:** `The Forgotten School Notebook | Village Stories`
* **Season + lived activity:** `A Rainy Morning in the Village | Village Stories`
* **Relationship + action:** `Elder Brother Waits for His Sister | Village Stories`

The series suffix is useful when it improves recognition, but it must not
crowd out the specific episode subject.

### Title decision framework

Choose a title only if it answers:

1. What will the viewer actually see or feel?
2. Which concrete noun or action makes it distinct?
3. Does the wording match the thumbnail and first moments?
4. Is it understandable without exaggeration?
5. Does it preserve the dignity of the people represented?

### Title checks

Avoid titles that are:

* Vague: `A Special Day`
* Inflated: `The Most Emotional Village Story Ever`
* Misleading: `The Storm That Changed Everything` for ordinary rain
* Spoiling: `Father Fixes It Perfectly` when the repair is the episode journey
* Modernizing: `Village Life Hack` or `No Phone Challenge`

Prefer `Father Tests a Careful Repair` when that is the genuine story.

## Description System

### Required structure

Use this order unless a platform field requires another:

1. A one- or two-sentence truthful episode summary.
2. A brief series context line.
3. Relevant playlist or previous/next episode links.
4. Credits, rights notices, and accessibility information as applicable.
5. A restrained invitation to subscribe or continue watching.

The first lines carry the main responsibility because they appear first in
many YouTube surfaces.

### Description writing rules

Write in present tense unless historical context genuinely needs another
tense. Use specific nouns and actions. Describe feelings through the practical
situation rather than declaring them as advertising.

Good:

> Rain slows Mother's cooking fire, and the family finds a patient way to
> finish the morning meal together.

Poor:

> What happens next will make you cry! This unbelievable village survival
> story is a must-watch.

### Educational and cultural wording

Name a skill, routine, or tradition only when it is visible and contextually
accurate. Avoid claims that one family represents every village, every Tamil
household, or a timeless past.

When context needs qualification, use plain language such as:

> This episode is set in the established early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu world of
> Village Stories.

Do not turn the description into a lecture or make unsupported historical
claims.

## Search Terms, Tags, and Hashtags

Use search language to clarify the episode, not to game discovery.

### Eligible terms

Eligible terms come directly from the source brief:

* Village Stories
* rural Tamil Nadu when the setting is relevant
* family life, village cooking, monsoon, school morning, repair, farming, or
  another visible activity
* established character or location names, where useful
* the episode's accurate format: animated story, family story, or short film,
  only if correct

### Ineligible terms

Do not use:

* Names of unrelated trending creators, films, brands, games, or news.
* “Real village” when the work is fictional or generated.
* Generic traffic bait such as `#viral`, `#fyp`, `#shocking`, or a long block
  of unrelated hashtags.
* Terms implying modern technologies absent from canon.
* Repeated variants inserted solely to manipulate search.

Use only a small, relevant set of hashtags if the channel's current publishing
strategy supports them. Omit them when they add no clarity.

## Chapters

Create chapters for long-form episodes when they improve navigation without
spoiling the story or fragmenting its calm flow.

Chapter labels should name visible stages:

* `The Morning Rain`
* `The Damp Wood`
* `Finding a Dry Piece`
* `A Warm Meal`

Avoid labels such as `THE SHOCKING TWIST`, `Will It Work?`, or a label that
reveals an ending before the viewer reaches it.

Check each timecode against the final cut after export. Do not use chapters for
Shorts unless the platform and format make them useful.

## Playlists and Series Links

Place every upload in the most truthful existing playlist:

* Season or weather sequence.
* Family and relationship sequence.
* Location or craft sequence.
* Short-form collection.
* Long-form collection.

Do not force an episode into a theme it only touches briefly. A playlist order
may support a natural continuing thread, but every video must remain
satisfying alone.

Link a previous or next episode only when the relationship is real: a continued
task, same day, same season, shared object, or meaningful character arc. Never
label an unrelated upload as “Part 2” to manufacture a chain.

## Captions and Language Fields

Captions are part of the episode, not a last-minute upload field.

### Caption workflow

1. Create a transcript from the locked audio.
2. Correct names, place names, culturally specific terms, dialogue, and
   meaningful ambient cues.
3. Time captions to spoken language and necessary sound information.
4. Identify non-speech sound only when it contributes to understanding, such
   as `[rain on tile]` or `[rope tightens]`.
5. Review on the final YouTube player for timing, line breaks, and overlap.

Never use fabricated dialogue, simplify a person's meaning into a stereotype,
or label ordinary sound as danger for drama.

Select language and translation fields accurately. Human review is required
where a translation changes cultural meaning, tone, family relationship, or
historical detail.

## Cards, End Screens, and Pinned Comments

### Cards

Use cards sparingly and only when a related video helps the viewer understand
or continue a genuine interest. Place them away from a key emotional line,
close sound, or visual action.

### End screens

Offer one or two fitting next choices, such as the next episode in a real
sequence or a relevant playlist. The end-screen image and wording must not
interrupt the ending's emotional rest.

### Pinned comments

Use a pinned comment for useful context, an accurate playlist path, a gentle
question about the visible episode experience, or a correction if required.

Good question:

> Which small part of the morning routine stayed with you?

Do not demand engagement, create false debate, ask leading emotional questions,
or imply that viewers must comment to receive a continuation.

## Step-by-Step Workflow

### 1. Receive the locked production package

Confirm final cut, title brief, thumbnail brief, canon references, captions
status, rights status, and connected-episode information. Return incomplete
packages to production.

### 2. Draft the title and opening description

Use the source brief. Write two to four truthful title candidates and one
description. Check them beside the approved thumbnail and first 30 seconds.

### 3. Build the full field set

Add accurate playlist, chapters if needed, language, captions, credits, links,
cards, end screens, and eligible search terms.

### 4. Run the promise test

Ask a reviewer who has not written the copy:

* What episode do you expect from this page?
* Does the first minute fulfill that expectation?
* Does any word imply a larger stake, different setting, or outcome?

Revise any mismatch.

### 5. Run canon and accessibility review

Verify all proper nouns and cultural language. Review captions and links.
Confirm no metadata references a modern object or circumstance absent from the
world.

### 6. Enter and preview on YouTube

Use the approved release checklist. Preview title truncation, first description
lines, chapters, captions, cards, end screens, playlist placement, and
thumbnail rendering on relevant device sizes.

### 7. Archive

Save the final metadata text, upload settings, URL, publication time, and
approvals with the episode ID. Record later changes with their reason and date.

## Quality Gates

### Gate A: Factual match

Every named person, object, action, place, season, and outcome exists in the
final episode.

### Gate B: Audience promise

Thumbnail, title, description, and opening describe the same experience.

### Gate C: Canon and tone

Copy protects early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu accuracy, human dignity, and the
series' calm emotional scale.

### Gate D: Accessibility and rights

Captions, languages, credits, links, and disclosures are complete and correct.

### Gate E: Technical publishing

All fields render correctly and point to intended public destinations.

## YouTube Learning Loop

After release, examine audience retention, click-through rate, traffic source,
search terms, captions feedback, comments, and returning viewers in context.

Interpret patterns carefully:

* Low discovery may mean the topic is unclear, not that the story needs false
  stakes.
* Strong clicks with early exits may mean packaging overpromised.
* Strong completion with low clicks may mean title or thumbnail clarity needs
  improvement.
* Repeated questions may reveal a caption, context, or sequencing gap.

Use evidence to improve usefulness. Do not let a short-term metric overrule
canon or the episode's honest promise.

## Canon Authority

Metadata may explain canon but cannot extend, reinterpret, or override it. If
the public wording needs a fact absent from the production package, remove the
claim or obtain approved canon clarification before publishing.

## Guiding Principle

Help the right viewer find the real episode, understand its promise, and feel
that their attention was respected.

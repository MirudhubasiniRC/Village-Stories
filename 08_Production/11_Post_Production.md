# Post-Production

## Purpose

This manual defines the permanent workflow for assembling approved animation, narration, music, sound, captions, titles, and delivery files into a finished Village Stories episode.

Post-production is where individual assets become one continuous experience. Its purpose is not to add speed, shine, or drama after the fact. Its purpose is to preserve continuity, clarify the story, protect natural rhythm, and deliver the series faithfully to YouTube.

---

## Philosophy

Editing is the art of deciding what the viewer has time to notice.

Village Stories needs cuts that follow attention: a hand completes its task, a face receives a feeling, rain settles into a courtyard, a sound carries into the next room. The editor must resist the assumption that every second needs a new image, sound, word, or effect.

The finished episode should feel observed rather than assembled.

---

## Objectives

Every locked episode must:

* Tell the approved story clearly and truthfully.
* Preserve locked character, location, object, camera, lighting, costume, and seasonal continuity.
* Maintain a warm narration relationship with natural sound, ASMR, music, and silence.
* Retain early-1990s village authenticity in every visible and audible frame.
* Use visual and audio rhythm suited to the intended YouTube format without chasing false urgency.
* Include accurate, accessible titles, captions, metadata, and delivery files.
* Leave an organized record that supports future episodes and continuity review.

---

## Scope and Boundary

This manual governs picture edit, sound assembly, finishing, titles, captions, export, upload preparation, and archival handoff.

Post-production cannot overrule canon or repair a flawed story by hiding its problems with speed, music, or effects. If a source animation, voice line, or factual detail is wrong, return it to the responsible production stage and document the issue.

---

## Roles

### Episode Lead

Owns final story intent and approves that the episode remains faithful to its premise.

### Picture Editor

Builds the cut, protects visual rhythm, maintains screen direction, and identifies missing or contradictory coverage.

### Post-Production Supervisor

Maintains version control, schedules approvals, confirms source readiness, and controls the final master.

### Continuity Lead

Reviews visual and audio continuity against the episode ledger and the authoritative source documents.

### Sound and Music Lead

Delivers approved stems, reviews the final integrated mix, and protects narration, natural audio, ASMR, music, and silence.

### Finishing Editor

Handles color, cleanup, titles, captions, graphics, export settings, and platform packaging without breaking the series style.

### Quality Reviewer

Performs independent full-episode review before release.

---

## Canon Authority

Use this authority order when a post-production choice is uncertain:

1. Specific world, character, location, object, culture, and research documents define factual reality.
2. Series Style Lock defines visual, camera, animation, audio, and production identity.
3. Story Engine defines narrative and emotional truth.
4. Episode System defines format, pacing, opening, ending, and viewer structure.
5. Approved scene packets and continuity ledger define current episode state.
6. This manual defines post-production procedure.

Editing convenience never outranks established fact. A beautiful cut that places a character, prop, season, or sound in the wrong state must be corrected.

---

## Required Handoff Package

Do not begin the final assembly without:

* Approved animation masters and reference stills.
* Shot list, storyboard, and scene objective notes.
* Current visual and audio continuity ledgers.
* Approved narration and dialogue stems with script version.
* Approved music, ambience, SFX, ASMR, and cue-sheet information.
* Relevant canon and style references.
* Source versions, file naming rules, aspect ratio, target duration, and intended YouTube format.
* A clear list of unresolved issues, if any.

If a source is provisional, label it clearly. Never allow a temp asset to become final by accident.

---

## Project Organization and Version Control

Create a stable episode structure containing:

* Source animation and reference images.
* Audio stems and sound-source records.
* Edit project files.
* Exports and review versions.
* Captions, title copy, description copy, and thumbnail source.
* Continuity ledger and approval log.

Use a consistent naming pattern such as:

```text
EP##_SC##_SH##_asset-purpose_v##_
```

Keep the source name intact when possible. Every review note must identify the episode version and timecode. Do not overwrite an approved master; create a new revision with a reason recorded.

---

## Detailed Workflow

### 1. Verify Source Readiness

Confirm that animation, narration, music, SFX, and titles are the intended versions. Check technical integrity before creative editing begins.

### 2. Build the Assembly Cut

Place shots in storyboard order with only essential timing adjustments. Preserve source handles and avoid adding polish before the story is readable.

### 3. Check Story Clarity

Watch the assembly with minimal sound. Confirm who wants what, what changes, what practical action happens, and where the emotional rest belongs.

### 4. Check Visual Continuity

Compare every cut against the ledger: character identity, costume, prop state, location zone, screen direction, weather, light, and time of day.

### 5. Set Picture Rhythm

Adjust shot lengths according to action completion, viewer comprehension, emotional response, and the intended episode format. Hold a shot because it matters, not because the timeline needs padding.

### 6. Integrate Voice and Natural Sound

Add approved narration and dialogue, then build the natural sound, ASMR, and silence around the cut. Do not cover an important action with explanatory speech.

### 7. Add Music With Restraint

Use only approved cues with a clear story function. Review cue entrances and exits against narration, ASMR, and quiet visual beats.

### 8. Finish Picture

Apply color and cleanup only within the Series Style Lock. Preserve the hand-painted feel, material texture, lighting family, and established camera identity.

### 9. Add Text Elements

Create titles, captions, end cards, and any on-screen identifiers with accessible, unobtrusive design. They must not introduce modern visual language that clashes with the series world.

### 10. Run Full Quality Review

Watch the complete episode at normal speed, then in targeted passes for story, continuity, sound, historical detail, and technical delivery.

### 11. Export and Validate

Export platform-specific masters, inspect them after export, and confirm audio sync, crop, captions, and metadata before scheduling upload.

### 12. Archive and Update Records

Save final masters, source versions, captions, cue sheet, approvals, and the completed continuity ledger. Record new permanent facts only through the canon process.

## Google Flow Source Review

Treat every Google Flow clip as a versioned source asset with known limits.

Before a clip enters a locked edit:

* Compare it with its approved reference, prompt, and continuity-ledger entry.
* Check character identity, clothing, props, location anchors, camera behaviour, weather, and action state.
* Review the first and final frames against neighbouring shots.
* Reject visual artifacts, unexplained people or objects, unwanted modern details, and unmotivated camera movement.
* Preserve the original approved file and identify all crops, retimes, cleanups, or replacements in the edit record.

Post-production may select and time a valid Google Flow take; it may not make an invalid take canonical through editing.

## Picture Editing Principles

### Cut on Understanding

Cut after the viewer can read the action or emotion—not simply at the first moment it becomes visible.

### Preserve Cause and Effect

If a character looks toward a sound, show enough of the sound source or consequence to make the connection legible.

### Protect the Action

Do not cut away from the contact that gives an activity meaning: the vessel being set down, the knot being tied, the rain reaching the courtyard, or the child handing over an object.

### Use Transitions Sparingly

Straight cuts, sound bridges, and natural visual rests are usually sufficient. Avoid decorative transitions, speed ramps, flash frames, and effects that make the village feel like a promotional montage.

### Respect Quiet

Slow does not mean unedited. A quiet passage still needs intention, progression, and truthful attention. Remove only what the viewer genuinely does not need.

## Camera and Visual Continuity

Before locking picture, verify:

* Character entrance and exit directions remain coherent.
* Eye lines, hand positions, and object transfers make sense across cuts.
* Shot sizes and camera movement follow the established camera language.
* The same location retains its architecture, landmark objects, light direction, weather, and degree of use.
* Costumes, hair, dirt, wetness, footwear, and carried items remain in the right state.
* Background figures, animals, and vehicles do not appear, disappear, or change activity without cause.
* Seasonal conditions remain coherent through the episode.

Use a continuity cutaway only when it is truthfully motivated by the location or action. Do not hide a mismatch under a reaction shot and leave the source defect unrecorded.

---

## Emotional and Narrative Rhythm

The edit must support the episode's gentle conflict and honest emotional progression.

Check:

* The opening creates curiosity through a real sensory, practical, or emotional point of interest.
* The middle develops one understandable task, need, or relationship movement.
* The resolution arrives through action, care, learning, or acceptance rather than manufactured spectacle.
* The ending gives emotional rest and a truthful reason to remember the episode.
* Comedy, if present, is kind and character-based.
* No moment is inflated into crisis just to maintain attention.

If a scene feels flat, first ask whether the story beat, action, or performance is unclear. Do not default to faster cutting or louder music.

---

## Narration, Music, SFX, ASMR, and Silence

Use the final mix as a relationship, not a stack of tracks.

### Narration

Place narration where it adds orientation, memory, or feeling. Recut or shorten a line that narrates an already readable image.

### Natural Sound

Maintain credible room tone, environment, and material detail. Let visible actions retain their sound consequences.

### ASMR

Protect selected tactile moments with enough picture time and audio space for the viewer to notice them.

### Music

Use music to support a deserved transition or feeling. Keep it below the truth of the moment.

### Silence

Retain intentional quiet after a look, task, or change. Silence may contain low natural ambience, but it should never be filled automatically.

When these elements compete, prioritize story clarity, natural action, and emotional honesty over decorative density.

---

## Color, Cleanup, and Graphic Restraint

Follow the Series Style Lock for visual finish.

Do:

* Match exposure, color family, texture, and atmospheric depth between shots.
* Preserve natural material detail in clay, wood, fabric, metal, foliage, water, and skin.
* Correct visible technical artifacts that distract from story or continuity.
* Use titles and captions that remain readable without overwhelming the frame.

Do not:

* Add glossy modern contrast, neon grading, artificial lens effects, or excessive sharpening.
* Remove texture until the hand-painted world becomes sterile.
* Use contemporary meme graphics, aggressive animated text, or branded visual clutter.
* Crop or reframe a shot in a way that breaks its camera intention or removes the story action.

---

## Early-1990s Authenticity Review

Review both image and sound for:

* Modern technology, logos, packaging, vehicles, appliances, fashion, or lighting.
* Period-inappropriate music or sound effects.
* Generic rural stereotypes, false dirtiness, or “museum village” presentation.
* Incorrect treatment of work, worship, school, family, animals, or social relationships.
* Captions or metadata that use wording inconsistent with the episode's actual context.

If a detail is uncertain, return to canon and research. A late discovery is a reason to correct the source, not a reason to normalize the error.

---

## Captions, Titles, and Accessibility

Captions are part of the finished story.

* Transcribe approved narration and dialogue accurately.
* Preserve correct names, local terms, and meaning; do not replace them with vague approximations.
* Time captions to natural speech without covering important visual details where placement is controllable.
* Describe essential non-speech sound only when it matters to understanding or atmosphere.
* Check readability on mobile screens.
* Keep titles concise, truthful, and emotionally aligned with the episode.

Avoid clickbait titles, false danger, all-caps urgency, and thumbnail text that promises a different kind of story.

---

## YouTube Delivery Workflow

### Format Decision

Confirm whether the episode is a Short or long-form release before final crop and timing decisions.

### Shorts

* Maintain a clear central action in vertical framing.
* Establish a truthful sensory or practical hook immediately.
* Keep captions clear in the vertical safe area.
* Do not sacrifice a complete emotional ending for an abrupt loop or false cliffhanger.

### Long-Form Episodes

* Allow locations, actions, and quiet transitions to breathe.
* Use chapter-like shifts only when they support the episode's real structure.
* Keep the opening honest and the ending restful.

### Upload Package

Before upload, verify:

* Correct master file, crop, duration, and audio sync.
* Accurate title, description, thumbnail, captions, and audience setting.
* Music and asset rights records.
* Playlist and related-episode links, if applicable.
* No placeholder text, temp slate, unapproved watermark, or wrong revision.

The upload page is a delivery step, not a place to rewrite the episode's promise.

## Quality Checks

### Story Check

* Is the episode understandable, emotionally honest, and complete?
* Does every scene serve the approved episode intent?

### Visual Check

* Are character, location, object, costume, weather, camera, and lighting locks intact?
* Are cuts clean and screen direction clear?

### Audio Check

* Is narration warm and intelligible?
* Do natural sound, ASMR, music, and silence have purposeful space?
* Are audio transitions and sync clean?

### Authenticity Check

* Does each visual and sound belong in this early-1990s village?
* Is culture represented with specificity and dignity?

### Platform Check

* Does the episode read at its intended YouTube format and crop?
* Are captions, titles, thumbnails, and metadata accurate and truthful?

### Archive Check

* Are final versions, source logs, approvals, and continuity records complete?

## Common Mistakes and Corrections

### Mistake: Fixing Pace With Fast Cuts

An unclear sequence is chopped into short shots until the viewer cannot follow the work.

**Correction:** clarify the cause-and-effect action, add or restore the needed shot, then use cuts that follow attention.

### Mistake: Covering a Continuity Error

A prop changes hands, so the edit hides it behind an unrelated insert.

**Correction:** repair or replace the faulty source where possible; otherwise use a truthful transition and record the limitation for review.

### Mistake: Music Covers the Scene

Score begins early and flattens rain, tools, narration, and emotional nuance.

**Correction:** lower, delay, simplify, or remove the cue. Let the scene's actual sound and action lead.

### Mistake: Modern Packaging in the Final Frame

An AI-generated logo, appliance, or contemporary text remains in a background corner.

**Correction:** return the shot for correction or crop only if the crop preserves camera intent and does not create another continuity issue.

### Mistake: Clickbait Packaging

The title or thumbnail frames a gentle household problem as danger or scandal.

**Correction:** rewrite it to name the real activity, season, place, or feeling.

---

## Decision Framework

When a post-production choice has competing benefits, decide in this order:

1. Canon and historical accuracy.
2. Story clarity and emotional honesty.
3. Character, location, object, camera, and audio continuity.
4. Natural rhythm, ASMR, and meaningful silence.
5. Accessibility and platform readability.
6. Surface polish or novelty.

If a proposed effect, cut, cue, caption, crop, or title fails a higher criterion, reject it even when it appears more dramatic or marketable.

---

## Release Approval

The episode may be released only when the Episode Lead, Continuity Lead, Sound/Music Lead, and Quality Reviewer have approved their relevant checks.

Record:

* Final master version and export date.
* Final title, description, thumbnail, and captions version.
* Approval names or roles and outstanding known limitations.
* Canon implications, if any.
* Source and rights records for music and external assets.

No final approval may create new permanent canon silently. New recurring facts must be documented through the appropriate authority process.

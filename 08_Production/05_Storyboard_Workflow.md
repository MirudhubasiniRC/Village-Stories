# Storyboard Workflow

## Purpose

This is the permanent production workflow for translating an approved Village Stories episode into a clear, filmable storyboard.

It turns story intent into a controlled plan for images, animation, sound, editing, and YouTube delivery before image generation begins.

Use it for every Short, long-form episode, trailer, opening, and reusable sequence.

---

# Philosophy

A Village Stories storyboard is not a gallery of attractive pictures.

It is a continuity contract.

It records what the audience sees, where they are, when the moment occurs, who is present, what changes, and how one shot leads honestly into the next.

The board should make the village feel observed rather than staged.

Choose the quiet, specific action that reveals life over the busy shot that merely proves production scale.

---

# Scope And Definitions

## Storyboard

A numbered visual and timing plan for the final edit.

Each panel represents one shot or a deliberately linked shot unit.

## Shot

One uninterrupted camera view.

Do not describe several cuts as one shot.

## Scene

A continuous story unit in one time, location, and dramatic purpose.

One scene can contain many shots.

## Continuity Packet

The approved references and written facts that a board must preserve.

It includes the episode brief, character references, location references, prop references, timeline facts, seasonal facts, and prior-shot handoff notes.

## Locked Board

A board approved for image-prompt and Google Flow production.

Changes after lock must be recorded, reviewed, and propagated to affected shots.

---

# Canon Authority

This workflow governs production process; it does not create or overrule story canon.

When sources conflict, use this order:

```text
01_Canon/ Series Style Lock and timeline
↓
02_World/ geography, culture, time, nature, and locations
↓
03_Characters/ Character_Rules and individual character documents
↓
04_Objects/ recurring object definitions
↓
06_Story_Engine/ and 07_Episode_System/
↓
Approved episode brief and continuity packet
↓
This storyboard and its shot notes
↓
Image prompts, generated frames, animation clips, and edit decisions
```

When a source is missing or ambiguous, pause the shot and research or escalate it.

Never solve uncertainty by inventing a permanent visual fact inside a prompt.

---

# Guiding Principle

Plan every shot so that it could only belong to this family, this village, this day, and this moment.

---

# Roles

## Director

Owns emotional clarity, visual rhythm, and final creative approval.

The Director ensures that every cut serves the story instead of displaying a camera trick.

## Story Editor

Supplies the approved beat sheet, episode objective, learning element, emotional turn, and ending.

The Story Editor confirms that the board has not accidentally changed meaning.

## Canon And Continuity Lead

Builds the continuity packet, verifies facts, records decisions, and approves all canonical details before board lock.

This role can veto an attractive shot that contradicts established truth.

## Storyboard Artist

Designs shot order, composition, screen direction, action, and transitions from approved facts.

The artist writes enough information for another production member to reproduce the intended shot.

## Image Prompt Lead

Reviews the board for promptability.

They flag crowded action, underspecified identity, untestable camera moves, and frames that require incompatible visual states.

## Google Flow Operator

Reviews each planned animation beat for viable generation, reference use, motion scope, and reroll strategy.

They do not silently redesign the board to accommodate a generation result.

## Editor And Sound Lead

Checks timing, cut logic, ambience handoffs, ASMR opportunities, caption-safe areas, and thumbnail opportunities.

---

# Required Inputs

Do not board from an idea alone.

Before drawing panel 1, assemble:

* Approved episode premise and one-sentence viewer promise.
* Episode type, target format, duration, and release goal.
* Beat sheet or scene structure.
* Date, season, weather, time progression, and relevant timeline facts.
* Character roster, current clothing state, emotional state, and temporary conditions.
* Locations in story order and their geographical relationship.
* Recurring props, their condition, owner, starting location, and final location.
* Required learning moment, ASMR moment, hook, and resolution.
* Existing visual references for recurring characters and locations.
* A list of any deliberate new canon awaiting approval.

If any item is unknown, mark it `OPEN` and resolve it before lock.

# Production Naming

Use one stable production identifier:

```text
EP###_ShortTitle
```

Use shot identifiers in story order:

```text
EP###_S01_SH001
EP###_S01_SH002
EP###_S02_SH003
```

Use a revision suffix only when the shot meaning changes:

```text
EP###_S01_SH002_R02
```

Never rename a shot merely because its generated image was rerolled.

The identifier describes the editorial shot, not a particular AI output.

# Storyboard Workflow

## Step 1: Read For Meaning

Read the episode brief without designing shots.

Write one sentence each for:

* What changes by the end.
* What the viewer should feel.
* What practical activity carries the episode.
* What the audience must understand visually.
* What must remain quiet or unsaid.

If the episode depends on dialogue to explain basic action, add a visual action or object that communicates it.

## Step 2: Build The Continuity Packet

Create a one-page scene ledger before thumbnailing.

For every scene, record:

* Location and exact sub-area.
* Time of day and weather.
* Characters and their entrance positions.
* Clothing, footwear, hair, tools, and carried items.
* Prop start state and end state.
* Direction of travel.
* Emotional state at scene start and finish.
* Ambient sound bed.
* Canon references consulted.

Use `same as prior shot` only when the prior shot is named.

## Step 3: Divide Into Visual Beats

Turn each story beat into an observable event.

Use verbs such as notices, carries, washes, waits, listens, shares, repairs, teaches, gathers, or returns.

Avoid abstract board directions such as “show warmth” or “make it emotional.”

Instead, show the Daughter holding a basket steady while Mother ties the banana-leaf bundle.

## Step 4: Choose The Shot Purpose

Every shot must have one primary job:

* Establish place.
* Orient geography.
* Reveal character.
* Clarify action.
* Mark a discovery.
* Hold emotion.
* Transition time or place.
* Provide rhythm or breath.
* Deliver a YouTube hook.
* Create a clean ending image.

If a shot has no job, remove it.

If it has three jobs, split it.

## Step 5: Plan Geography And Screen Direction

Draw a simple top-down location map for every scene with more than one shot.

Mark:

* Doorways, paths, work surfaces, water, animals, and hazards.
* Character start positions.
* Camera zone.
* Primary movement line.
* Screen-left and screen-right travel.

Maintain the 180-degree relationship unless the board deliberately reorients the audience with an establishing or neutral shot.

Do not cut from Father walking screen right to an unexplained reverse-angle arrival that makes him appear to turn back.

## Step 6: Thumbnail Before Detail

Make fast, small compositions.

At this stage evaluate:

* Is the focal action readable at phone size?
* Is the character placed in a believable part of the location?
* Is there a clear foreground, story plane, and depth?
* Does the camera observe rather than pose?
* Does the shot vary visual distance from its neighbours?

Do not spend time rendering a composition that fails at thumbnail scale.

## Step 7: Specify The Panel

For every shot, document:

```text
Shot ID:
Duration target:
Format and safe area:
Scene / location / sub-area:
Time, weather, and light:
Camera framing, height, angle, and movement:
Screen direction:
Characters and exact visual state:
Props and exact state:
Primary action:
Start frame:
End frame:
Sound / silence:
Transition in and out:
Continuity handoff:
Prompt references required:
Approval status:
```

Describe the start and end frame even when the camera is static.

Google Flow needs a bounded action, and the editor needs a usable cut point.

## Step 8: Design The Opening

For YouTube, establish a concrete reason to watch within the first moments.

Open on an image or action that is immediately legible without volume:

* Rain beginning to patter on an unfinished drying mat.
* A child noticing a missing seed packet beside the garden bed.
* Steam lifting as Mother uncovers a pot.
* Father pausing at a canal gate that will not open.

Do not begin with a generic aerial view, slow title card, or unrelated scenic montage.

An establishing shot may follow the hook if it clarifies the situation.

## Step 9: Plan Rhythm

Alternate shot scale, stillness, and activity with purpose.

Use wider shots to locate people in the village.

Use medium shots for relationships and physical work.

Use close details for hands, material texture, small discoveries, and ASMR.

Do not use rapid cutting to manufacture energy.

Do not hold a shot after the meaningful action has ended simply because the image is beautiful.

## Step 10: Mark Image And Animation Boundaries

Classify each planned shot:

* `I` — still image or held frame.
* `A-S` — simple animation from one controlled starting reference.
* `A-T` — transition requiring a matched end state.
* `C` — composited or editorial solution required.

Keep one animation clip focused on one dominant change.

Examples: steam rises, a hand places a brass tumbler down, a child turns her head, leaves move in wind.

Do not combine walking, handoff, animal interaction, a camera orbit, and a changing weather effect in one generated clip.

## Step 11: Add Sound Intent

Every scene needs an ambience decision.

Record the foreground sound only when it matters:

* stone grinding,
* water pouring,
* bicycle bell,
* ladle against brass,
* paddy rustle,
* dog paws on packed earth.

Silence is a direction, not an omission.

Leave visual space for sound-led moments.

## Step 12: Run Continuity Passes

Perform separate passes rather than one vague review:

1. Canon and history.
2. Character identity and behaviour.
3. Geography and screen direction.
4. Props and costume state.
5. Light, weather, and time.
6. Emotional progression.
7. Promptability and Google Flow feasibility.
8. YouTube readability and caption safety.

Correct the board before creating production images.

## Step 13: Lock And Hand Off

Lock only when every shot has an approved state, reference needs, and a defined endpoint.

Deliver:

* Locked board PDF or image sequence.
* Shot list and timing sheet.
* Continuity packet.
* Prompt-ready shot descriptions.
* Reference assignment list.
* Open-risk list, if any.

# Decision Frameworks

## When To Use A Wide Shot

Use it when the viewer must understand place, weather, distance, group arrangement, or a meaningful relationship between person and environment.

Do not use it merely because a location is pretty.

## When To Use A Detail Shot

Use it when the material, hand skill, object state, or small emotional clue carries story information.

Examples:

* Mother’s fingers testing whether rice is clean.
* A wet bicycle pedal catching morning light.
* Elder Brother’s muddy hand opening a canal latch.

Do not use a detail shot if it hides essential spatial action.

## When To Move The Camera

Move only if the movement reveals, follows, connects, or changes feeling.

Prefer a static observation, gentle pan, restrained track, or slow push when justified.

Avoid orbiting, racing, crane-like spectacle, whip pans, and motion that makes ordinary work look like an advertisement.

## When To Cut

Cut on:

* completed action,
* shift of attention,
* meaningful reaction,
* new information,
* rhythm change,
* sound handoff,
* or a clear geography reset.

Never cut merely to conceal an unclear plan.

## When AI Output Cannot Match The Board

Ask in this order:

1. Is the board's story purpose still correct?
2. Can the same purpose be achieved with a simpler, canonical shot?
3. Can a still, insert, cutaway, or edit bridge solve it?
4. Does the board need a formally approved change?

Do not accept a visually impressive generation that alters continuity, character identity, or story meaning.

# Continuity Rules

## Characters

Use the documented appearance, age, body proportions, clothing logic, habits, relationships, and camera presentation.

The family remains Father, Mother, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and their German Shepherd family dog.

The dog is a gentle family companion and guardian, never a police, military, or attack-dog image.

Keep characters connected to meaningful activity and environment.

## Locations

Use named locations and their documented architecture, material logic, map position, entry points, and surrounding landscape.

Do not relocate a kitchen window, path, tree, canal, shop counter, or shrine because another composition is easier.

## Props

Track ownership, orientation, fill level, cleanliness, damage, and handoff.

A clay pot cannot be full in a later close-up if it was emptied in the previous shot.

A basket cannot move from Mother’s left hand to Father’s right hand without a shown or motivated handoff.

## Light And Weather

Keep sun angle, cloud cover, wetness, shadow direction, smoke behavior, and clothing response coherent inside a scene.

Time shifts need an explicit transition or a visible environmental cue.

## Period

This is early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

Do not board smartphones, digital payment, modern delivery culture, LED or decorative lighting, contemporary appliances, routine modern vehicles, branded packaging, logos, modern fashion, or plastic-dominant dressing.

Authenticity does not mean poverty, dirt, or stereotype.

# Positive Examples

## Example: Clear Hook

`EP014_S01_SH001` is a tight shot of raindrops darkening a line of sun-drying chillies.

The first drop lands; the Daughter’s hand enters frame and gathers the nearest tray.

The audience immediately understands weather, urgency, activity, and period-appropriate household life.

## Example: Honest Geography

Father exits the backyard gate screen right carrying a bamboo basket.

The next shot is a wide path view where he enters screen left and continues toward the garden.

The camera position changes, but his world direction remains clear.

## Example: Promptable Action

Mother kneels beside the clay stove, places one split log into the fire, and watches the flame catch.

The action has one subject, one prop, one hand movement, a stable environment, and a natural endpoint.

## Example: Emotional Ending

At dusk, the children place the repaired lantern on the thinnai.

Father sees it from the courtyard and smiles gently before returning to his work.

The held wide frame lets the house, family, and evening ambience complete the feeling without explanation.

# Common Mistakes

## Mistake: Decorative Boards

Problem: every panel is a beautiful wide view and the action is unclear.

Correction: give each shot one narrative job and include readable hands, props, and reactions.

## Mistake: Generic “Village” Images

Problem: the board could occur anywhere in South Asia or any century.

Correction: use the documented location, materials, recurring people, local routines, and early-1990s object logic.

## Mistake: Character Drift

Problem: a board labels a figure “Mother” but omits clothing, hair, posture, or activity references.

Correction: reference the individual character packet and state the active visual conditions.

## Mistake: Impossible Action

Problem: a single clip asks for a child to run, catch a basket, laugh, turn, and reveal a new location while the camera circles.

Correction: divide the beat into simple shots with clean endpoints.

## Mistake: Continuity By Memory

Problem: the team assumes everyone remembers which hand held the pot.

Correction: record start and end states in every panel.

## Mistake: Modern Convenience

Problem: a visually clean generated shot includes a plastic water dispenser, bright LED light, motorbike, or printed branding.

Correction: reject it and reinforce material, period, and negative constraints in the downstream prompt.

## Mistake: Shorts Framing As An Afterthought

Problem: faces, hands, subtitles, or essential props fall outside the vertical-safe crop.

Correction: board in the intended delivery aspect ratio from the first thumbnail.

# Storyboard QA Checklist

## Story

* Is each shot necessary?
* Is the practical activity understandable without explanatory dialogue?
* Does the ending complete an emotional beat?

## Canon

* Are period, season, village culture, and geography correct?
* Are recurring characters, locations, and objects sourced from canon?
* Are proposed changes documented rather than implied?

## Continuity

* Does each shot state its handoff from the prior shot?
* Are screen direction, costume, props, light, and weather coherent?
* Are time changes motivated?

## Image And Animation Readiness

* Is every character identity sufficiently specified?
* Is the main action simple and observable?
* Does each moving shot have a clear start and end state?
* Are required reference images assigned?

## YouTube Readiness

* Does the opening work without sound?
* Is the focal action readable at phone size?
* Is essential action clear within the intended crop?
* Are caption and interface-safe zones protected?

# Final Approval

The storyboard is approved only when it tells the episode clearly, protects canon, and can be produced without asking the generation tool to invent missing decisions.

The board is the source of truth for the shot plan until a formally recorded revision replaces it.

# Pre-Production

## Purpose

This manual defines the mandatory preparation work before Village Stories shots
are generated, sound is built, or an episode is assembled. It converts an
approved story intention into a canon-safe, technically usable production
packet.

Pre-production prevents a familiar failure: discovering only after generation
that the wrong location, season, object, route, costume condition, emotional
state, or sound source was assumed.

## Philosophy

Good preparation does not make a gentle episode rigid. It gives small moments
the precision needed to feel true. When the team knows why a character carries
an object, where they stand, what changed in the weather, and what the viewer
must notice, Google Flow generation can serve the story instead of improvising
the world.

Pre-production is complete when another qualified team member can make the
next approved shot without guessing.

## Objectives

Before production begins, establish:

* the episode's story and emotional promise;
* controlling canon sources and every fact to verify;
* scene geography, route, time, season, weather, and task progress;
* character purpose, performance state, clothing, and carrying logic;
* exact approved objects and their state changes;
* visual, sound, and edit intentions;
* a prompt-ready handoff for every generated shot;
* honest YouTube considerations without allowing platform incentives to alter
  the episode.

## Guiding Principle

Prepare the truth of the scene before asking a tool to picture it.

## Authority and Scope

Read `08_Production/01_Production_Philosophy.md` first. Specific character,
location, object, world, culture, and research documents remain factual
authorities. `06_Story_Engine/` controls story design; `07_Episode_System/`
controls format and audience-facing structure.

This manual does not authorize:

* a new character, animal, location, business, object, festival, profession,
  route, house feature, or local custom;
* a change to appearance, costume, relationships, geography, period, or known
  episode state;
* a modern convenience introduced to simplify action or improve a prompt.

Mark unknowns **Verify**. A verified absence is not permission to fill a gap
with invention.

## Required Inputs

Do not open pre-production until these inputs exist:

1. A premise that has passed the relevant Story Engine checks.
2. The intended format: Short or long-form episode.
3. The named season, time span, weather condition, and primary location.
4. A clear practical need, obstacle, action, and emotional result.
5. The planned cast and documented reason for each presence.
6. The controlling canon document list.

If any input is missing, return to story development. Prompt experimentation is
not a substitute for a premise.

## Roles

### Writer

Provides the approved premise, beat sheet, dialogue draft, visible cause and
effect, and an explicit emotional turn. The writer identifies questions; they
do not answer factual gaps by assumption.

### Director

Builds scene intent, geography, shot roles, transitions, performance actions,
and emotional breathing room. The director ensures visual action leads
explanation.

### Canon steward

Checks each factual assertion against the controlling source. They maintain
the verification list and identify when a choice must be changed rather than
interpreted.

### AI artist / Google Flow operator

Assesses prompt feasibility, creates stable reference packets from approved
facts, identifies likely generation drift, and plans controlled variation
rather than broad, ambiguous requests.

### Sound lead

Maps visible sound sources, ambient beds, dialogue windows, silence, and
material conditions before visual production locks.

### Editor

Reviews the plan for legible scene joins, screen direction, elapsed time,
repetition, and cut points. Editing needs shape planning; it must not become
the rescue system for missing coverage.

## Pre-Production Workflow

### Step 1: Open the episode record

Create one episode record containing:

* working episode identifier and proposed format;
* one-sentence premise;
* season, weather, day period, and estimated story duration;
* primary emotional outcome;
* primary task and gentle complication;
* responsible people and approval status;
* links to all source documents used.

The record is a continuity tool, not public-facing metadata. Do not present a
working identifier as a canonical title unless title canon exists elsewhere.

### Step 2: Build a canon source map

List every source document that governs the episode. Include character files
for each cast member, the exact location files, featured object files, relevant
world/culture files, applicable research, Story Engine documents, and Episode
System documents.

For each planned fact, record:

| Fact | Source | Status | Production consequence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rain condition | seasonal/world source | Pass | wet surfaces and sound bed |
| Kitchen zone | location source | Pass | camera and object placement |
| Featured vessel | object source | Verify/Pass | prompt reference and handling |

Use the table as a production record, not as an excuse to generalise sources.
When a source says less than the scene needs, simplify the scene.

### Step 3: Write the production brief

The brief must state:

* What ordinary moment becomes meaningful?
* Whose need or skill anchors the episode?
* What is the visible practical task?
* What changes or complicates it?
* What established resource, skill, patience, or cooperation resolves it?
* What does the last image allow the viewer to feel?
* Why are these people in this location at this time?
* What must never be shown or implied?

Example:

> In monsoon weather, Mother needs to continue preparing a meal when damp
> firewood weakens the cooking fire. The family responds through patient,
> established practical help. The ending is the felt steadiness of the finished
> meal, not a spoken lesson.

This is a production brief, not a complete script. It makes the story testable
before detailed labour begins.

### Step 4: Run premise and period checks

Apply `06_Story_Engine/15_Story_Checklist.md`, especially the Episode Promise,
Canon and Period, Character Truth, Place/Route/Time, and Season/Weather gates.
Also consult `05_Research/Common_Historical_Mistakes.md`.

Stop or revise when the plan contains:

* modern devices, vehicles, visual branding, packaging, lighting, or behaviour;
* a generic location masquerading as an established one;
* a route that skips time or geography;
* weather used only as mood styling;
* a child, elder, worker, woman, or animal reduced to a stereotype or plot
  device;
* forced danger, humiliation, miracle, or coincidence.

### Step 5: Lock the beat sequence

Use the Story Engine and relevant Episode System structure to order the beats:

1. sensory or spatial opening;
2. clue to need or task;
3. preparation and purposeful action;
4. gentle obstacle, discovery, or relationship recognition;
5. practical response;
6. visible result;
7. quiet emotional settling image.

For a Short, compress without removing cause and effect. For long-form work,
allow tasks, transitions, observation, and emotional recognition to breathe.
Never add artificial subplots simply to fill duration.

### Step 6: Create scene cards

Make a scene card for each location-time unit. Each card records:

* scene purpose;
* established location and exact usable zone;
* entry condition and exit condition;
* time, season, weather, light, surface, and ambient activity;
* present characters and reason they are present;
* required objects and their beginning/end state;
* action, dialogue purpose, and emotional change;
* route or transition into and out of the scene;
* primary visual idea and primary sound source.

If a scene has no practical, emotional, geographical, or transition purpose,
remove or combine it.

### Step 7: Make the continuity ledger

The continuity ledger is mandatory before generation. Record the beginning and
end of every shot or continuous action:

* character position, orientation, posture, clothing condition, and emotion;
* all carries, placements, and exchanges;
* object state: full/empty, wet/dry, tied/loose, whole/used, hot/cool,
  prepared/unprepared;
* animal position and plausible movement;
* weather intensity, light direction, shadows, smoke, mud, and water;
* route, screen direction, elapsed time, and camera relationship;
* foreground sound state and ambient transition.

Example handoff:

> Mother remains beside the kitchen fire, facing the vessel. The tested wood is
> damp and set beside the fire. Rain remains steady on the tile roof. Younger
> Daughter stands clear of the smoke, watching Mother. The Family Dog rests at
> the threshold. The next action is Father bringing the approved dry option
> from an established source.

Do not write “same as before.” State the facts that must survive the cut.

### Step 8: Build the asset and reference packet

For every recurring character, location, object, and animal, collect the
approved source references and concise stable identifiers. Include only facts
that are documented and visible in the intended shot.

The packet should specify:

* approved visual identity and scale;
* clothing and condition for this episode;
* relevant location zone and fixed spatial landmarks;
* exact object form, material, condition, and handling;
* season, weather, ground, light, and environmental state;
* exclusions such as modern materials, brands, extra people, or altered
  architecture.

Do not overload prompts with unrelated decorative detail. Detail is useful only
when it protects a factual or narrative requirement.

### Step 9: Design shots by purpose

Use `06_Story_Engine/13_Visual_Storytelling.md`. For every shot, name one
primary job:

* establish place, route, time, or weather;
* reveal a clue, need, object, or practical state;
* show a character's deliberate action;
* show a relationship response;
* mark the result of action;
* transition plausibly between scenes;
* hold the final emotional residue.

Plan a shot only if it changes the viewer's understanding. A pleasing texture
or a dramatic angle is not a shot purpose.

### Step 10: Plan sound before image lock

Use `06_Story_Engine/14_ASMR_Storytelling.md`. Each scene needs:

* primary visible action sound;
* supporting environment sound;
* human presence and dialogue window;
* intentional quiet or musical restraint;
* conditions affecting material and distance;
* sound handoff to the next shot.

If an image will not visibly support a planned sound, adjust the shot plan
before generation. Never add arbitrary “village ambience” in post.

### Step 11: Create Google Flow prompt packets

Each prompt packet has one shot objective and includes:

1. the exact established location and its active zone;
2. time, season, weather, light direction, and surface condition;
3. named character(s), approved appearance, wardrobe state, position, and
   action;
4. approved featured object(s), state, and relationship to hands or space;
5. camera distance, framing, and movement only where story-purposeful;
6. screen direction and what enters, leaves, or remains off-screen;
7. continuity facts inherited from the previous accepted shot;
8. exclusions required to prevent period or canon drift.

Avoid prompts such as “beautiful traditional village scene” or “emotional
family moment.” They invite generic invention. Request observable action in an
established space instead.

### Step 12: Plan editorial and YouTube intent

Identify the truthful opening image, the image or moment that could represent
the episode for a thumbnail, and the ending image. These choices must emerge
from the approved story, not alter it.

For YouTube, confirm:

* the opening makes a genuine sensory, practical, or emotional promise;
* the title can describe the real task or feeling without false danger;
* the thumbnail image is clear, canon-safe, and does not misrepresent the
  action;
* any connection to another episode is an honest continuing thread, not an
  unresolved artificial cliffhanger;
* captions and descriptions will not add unsupported factual claims.

### Step 13: Conduct the production readiness meeting

Review the brief, source map, beats, scene cards, continuity ledger, reference
packet, sound plan, prompt packets, and risks as one connected plan.

Use one of these decisions:

* **Pass** — all relevant factual and execution requirements are ready.
* **Revise** — correction is clear and does not change the approved premise.
* **Verify** — controlling source must be consulted before work continues.
* **Stop** — a premise or plan conflicts with canon or series rules.

Document the decision, owner, and next review point. Do not begin generation
with Verify or Stop items open.

## Prompt Design Standards for Google Flow

### Describe action, not mood alone

Instead of “Mother looks worried in a cinematic kitchen,” describe the visible
cause and action: Mother tests a documented damp piece of wood near the
established cooking fire while rain is audible on the tiled roof. Her concern
is carried by the action and posture.

### Preserve identity through stable references

Use the approved character, location, and object descriptions consistently.
Do not casually rename, simplify, or reimagine recurring elements between
prompts. Stable identity is more important than variety.

### Limit each prompt to one readable beat

One prompt should normally show one action or one reaction. Do not request
multiple events, changing weather, a travel jump, and a complex camera move in
a single generated shot. Split the beat and use the continuity ledger.

### Treat generations as candidates

No generated output becomes production truth just because it is useful-looking.
Compare every candidate with its prompt packet and controlling source. Reject
shots that introduce altered faces, costume, age, architecture, tool design,
animal markings, location crowding, modern objects, or unsupported activity.

## Pre-Production Quality Gates

### Gate 1: Canon foundation

Pass when all cast, locations, objects, seasonal conditions, and research
claims have a controlling source and no source conflict remains unresolved.

### Gate 2: Story readiness

Pass when the need, gentle obstacle, practical response, emotional turn, and
ending are visible and proportionate. A premise depending on spectacle,
villainy, danger, or coincidence fails.

### Gate 3: Scene readiness

Pass when every scene has purpose, entry/exit logic, route/time continuity,
seasonal consequence, and a clear cast reason.

### Gate 4: Generation readiness

Pass when each planned shot has a reference packet, exact action, continuity
handoff, exclusions, and acceptance criteria.

### Gate 5: Sound readiness

Pass when each important sound has a visible source, correct material and
distance, and no conflict with dialogue, weather, or the planned edit.

## Acceptance Criteria Template

Before generating a shot, define what must be true for acceptance:

* the correct documented characters are present;
* their appearance, costume, scale, posture, and emotional state fit canon;
* the exact location zone has not changed;
* featured objects match approved design and state;
* season, weather, surface, light, and background activity are plausible;
* action begins and ends as required for the next handoff;
* no modern, branded, generic, or undocumented element appears;
* the frame has the planned story purpose.

The operator may accept a natural visual variation only if it changes none of
these requirements and does not create a later continuity problem.

## Example: Scene Card

**Scene purpose:** Show that the fire is unreliable because the wood is damp.

**Place and time:** Established Family Kitchen, monsoon daytime; rain on tiled
roof; damp threshold; limited smoke moving away from nearby people.

**Characters:** Mother prepares the meal; Younger Daughter notices the problem
from a safe, plausible position; Family Dog rests where it does not obstruct
work.

**Action:** Mother tests damp wood; the flame weakens; she pauses to assess it.

**Object state:** Wood begins damp; vessel remains in progress; meal is not
yet ready.

**Visual and sound:** Detail of the wood test and weak flame, with rain,
muted crackle, and a purposeful vessel sound. No dramatic thunder or
unexplained sorrowful music.

**Exit:** Mother remains at the fire; the next scene must account for the
arrival of a documented practical response.

## Common Mistakes

* Starting visual prompts before naming the season and period constraints.
* Copying a location name into a prompt without checking the documented zone
  and route.
* Creating a long script with no object, costume, or weather state log.
* Planning a solution that needs an unestablished tool or storage place.
* Treating a character's canon as a mood board rather than behavioural fact.
* Asking Google Flow for a generic rural setting, then accepting invented
  architecture and background activity.
* Writing sound only after visual generation has made the source ambiguous.
* Calling a prompt “continuity-aware” while it omits carries, exits, wetness,
  light, or screen direction.
* Selecting a thumbnail moment that depicts more excitement than the episode.
* Passing pre-production because the team assumes editing will solve gaps.

## Final Rule

If a production plan cannot clearly state what was true before a shot and what
will be true after it, the shot is not ready to generate.

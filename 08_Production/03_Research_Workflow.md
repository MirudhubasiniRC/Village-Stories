# Research Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories researches a production question without
inventing, changing, or weakening canon. It is for writers, directors, AI
artists, canon stewards, and reviewers who need to verify a period detail,
practical action, object use, seasonal condition, sound, route, or production
assumption.

Research makes an episode more truthful only when the result is used within
established canon. It is not a license to fill an undocumented gap with a
plausible-looking fact.

## Philosophy

Village Stories represents early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu through useful,
specific life rather than a collage of nostalgic signs. Good research asks:

* Would this action, object, sound, or convenience belong in this village on
  this day?
* What practical reason would make it visible?
* Does it support, contradict, or lie outside the established world?

Research serves the story's human truth. It must never turn village life into
spectacle, poverty imagery, a timeless fantasy, or an overly crowded archive
of details.

## Guiding Principle

Verify what the episode needs, then use only what canon permits.

## Canon Authority

The authority order is mandatory:

1. Specific character, location, object, world, and culture canon.
2. Established continuity records from approved previous episodes.
3. Story Engine and Episode System rules.
4. Research documents and verified sources.
5. Production interpretation.

Research may:

* confirm that an already-established choice is period-plausible;
* clarify handling, sequence, material, seasonal effect, or sound;
* identify a risk, ambiguity, or anachronism;
* suggest that the current premise should be simplified or replaced.

Research may not:

* override a specific canon document;
* establish a new permanent fact by implication;
* add a vehicle, tool, animal, building, profession, ritual, garment, food,
  technology, or social practice merely because it existed historically;
* rewrite an existing location layout or character behaviour;
* use an uncertain reference as a visible production fact.

If verified information conflicts with existing canon, preserve canon during
production and flag the discrepancy for an explicit canon decision outside
this workflow. Do not silently “correct” a permanent file or current episode.

## Research Questions

Open a research task only for a production-relevant question. A good question
is narrow, observable, and tied to a scene.

Good:

* What period-appropriate sound and handling could accompany the approved
  vessel in this documented kitchen action?
* How should rain plausibly affect the established path and clothing condition
  during the named season?
* Does a proposed visual detail introduce a modern material or convenience?

Too broad:

* What was village life like?
* What did all Tamil families do?
* What would look authentic?

Broad questions encourage stereotype. Narrow questions let the team verify a
visible choice.

## Roles

### Requester

Usually the writer, director, or AI artist. States the exact production
question, scene purpose, controlling canon source, deadline, and decision that
the answer will inform.

### Researcher

Collects relevant evidence, separates observation from inference, cites
sources, identifies uncertainty, and never presents a guess as canon.

### Canon steward

Classifies findings against existing authority and decides whether the outcome
is Pass, Revise, Verify, Stop, or Requires Canon Decision.

### Production lead

Uses accepted findings to revise a brief, script, prompt, sound plan, or
continuity record. They must record the production consequence, not merely
archive links.

## Research Workflow

### Step 1: State the decision

Begin with a decision statement:

> We need to decide whether this visible action, object condition, sound, or
> route is safe to use in Scene __, and what exact production constraint it
> creates.

Name the episode, scene, format, location, season, day period, characters,
objects, and source files already consulted. If no scene or decision exists,
do not research yet.

### Step 2: Read controlling canon first

Read the specific character, location, object, world, culture, continuity,
Story Engine, and Episode System documents that govern the question. Existing
canon may already answer it.

Record:

* confirmed facts;
* explicit restrictions;
* unknown details;
* terms that need precise verification;
* potential conflict with the proposed scene.

Do not research around an inconvenient canon fact in hope of finding an
exception.

### Step 3: Classify the question

Mark it as one or more of:

* period and technology;
* object material, use, or state;
* household, work, food, or transport practice;
* location and route plausibility;
* weather, season, light, surface, or clothing effect;
* animal care and natural behaviour;
* social interaction, language, or dignity risk;
* visual reference;
* sound and ASMR source;
* YouTube presentation or accessibility requirement.

Classification tells the team which existing documents must be checked and
which production discipline will use the finding.

### Step 4: Gather suitable evidence

Prefer sources that are specific to the period, region, material practice, or
established context. Use multiple relevant sources when a detail is important,
ambiguous, or easy to stereotype.

Possible evidence includes reliable historical material, regionally grounded
documentary reference, direct factual documentation, established archive
material, and internally approved research notes. Visual reference alone can
show appearance but may not explain date, location, ownership, use, or
frequency.

Avoid treating search results, undated photographs, generic AI imagery,
contemporary village media, film styling, tourism imagery, or one person's
memory as conclusive proof without context.

### Step 5: Record evidence, not mood

For each source, record:

* source identity and access date;
* relevant period and regional fit;
* exact observation;
* what it does not establish;
* confidence level;
* possible production use;
* canon compatibility.

Write “this source shows a material or practice under stated conditions,” not
“this is what all village families used.” Do not universalise households,
gender roles, income, language, caste, occupation, rituals, or animal care.

### Step 6: Assess canon compatibility

Classify every finding:

* **Compatible** — supports a documented fact or a constrained production
  interpretation.
* **Useful but non-canonical** — historically plausible but not authorized for
  visible use; keep only as background context.
* **Conflict** — contradicts existing canon or established continuity.
* **Insufficient** — evidence is vague, modern, undated, or unsupported.
* **Risk** — likely to create a stereotype, anachronism, or visual ambiguity.

Only Compatible findings can move directly into production, and only within
the scope canon already permits.

### Step 7: Turn evidence into a production constraint

Translate accepted evidence into an observable instruction:

* “The approved rain condition must make the threshold and footsteps visibly
  damp; do not retain dry-dust sound.”
* “Keep the featured object in the documented material and use it only in the
  established action.”
* “Remove the proposed branded packaging because it creates a period risk.”

Avoid vague directions such as “make it authentic.” A production constraint
must tell a writer, director, AI artist, sound lead, or editor what to do or
avoid.

### Step 8: Update the correct production record

Add the result to the research log, source map, scene card, continuity ledger,
prompt packet, sound plan, or script note as applicable. Do not modify canon
documents through this workflow.

If the finding affects multiple scenes, identify every affected scene and
re-run relevant quality gates. Period detail is often connected to clothing,
weather, materials, sound, routes, and practical action.

### Step 9: Close with a decision

Close every task as:

* **Pass** — documented and ready to use in its stated scope.
* **Revise** — current scene needs an identified adjustment.
* **Verify** — evidence or controlling canon remains incomplete.
* **Stop** — the proposed choice cannot be used.
* **Requires Canon Decision** — production cannot resolve the conflict.

No open question may be hidden inside a final prompt.

Use this structure:

```text
Episode / scene:
Decision needed:
Controlling canon:
Question classification:
Known canon facts:

Evidence and limitations:
Canon compatibility:

Date reviewed:
```

## Historical and Period Safety Check

Before approving a researched visual or sound element, test it against
`05_Research/Common_Historical_Mistakes.md`.

Reject modern internet, smartphones, social media, digital payment,
contemporary delivery culture, LED or decorative modern lighting, modern
appliance-heavy interiors, routine modern transport, branded packaging, logos,
modern fashion, plastic-dominant staging, supermarket behaviour, café culture,
and modern school technology.

Also reject the opposite error: treating early-1990s rural life as frozen,
uniformly poor, visually dirty, or without practical adaptation and dignity.

## Research for Google Flow

Google Flow reference work needs special discipline. A generated image can
combine periods, regions, materials, and architecture in a visually convincing
but factually false way.

For a research-backed prompt:

1. State only documented or accepted visible facts.
2. Anchor the shot in the exact established location and active zone.
3. Specify the action, object state, season, weather, and time.
4. Carry character and object facts from approved canon rather than generic
   descriptors.
5. Include exclusions for known anachronism or drift risks.
6. Compare generated candidates with source facts before acceptance.

Never use a generated candidate as research evidence. It is an output that
must be checked, not a source that validates itself.

## Research for Sound

Sound needs the same evidence standard as picture. Verify the source, material,
distance, weather condition, and purpose of each close sound. A pleasant
effect is not suitable if the frame cannot show what creates it.

Questions to ask:

* Is this sound made by an approved object, animal, surface, or activity?
* Does its material and condition match the visual?
* Is its distance plausible from the camera and established geography?
* Does it imply a modern object, dense urban scale, threat, or cartoonishness?
* Does it help the viewer understand work, weather, or feeling?

## Research for YouTube

Research may help improve title clarity, captions, discoverability, and
accessibility, but it cannot make packaging more dramatic than the episode.
Use platform learning to understand whether viewers understood the opening,
task, and ending. Do not use trend research to impose danger, arguments,
shocking thumbnails, modern memes, or false cliffhangers on the series.

## Example: Period-Risk Finding

The director proposes a visible packaged item to clarify a Grocery Store visit.
The researcher checks the location and object canon first, then checks the
period-risk list. If no documented item authorizes it and the proposed form
risks modern branding, the result is Stop, not “add a vintage-looking label.”
The scene must use an established object or be rewritten.

## Example: Seasonal Sound Finding

The sound lead needs footsteps for a monsoon path. Existing season and location
canon establish rain and surface conditions. Research may clarify how wet
ground changes the sound, but the production result is narrow: footsteps and
movement must agree with the visible damp path. It does not permit inventing a
new route or seasonal event.

## Common Mistakes

* Researching a broad aesthetic instead of a scene decision.
* Looking at external reference before reading specific canon.
* Treating “historically possible” as “canonically approved.”
* Turning one visual reference into a universal social claim.
* Using generic Indian or contemporary rural reference for early-1990s Tamil
  Nadu without checking period and regional relevance.
* Adding researched facts directly to prompts without an authority check.
* Letting research justify caricature, hardship display, or decorative clutter.
* Saving links but failing to write a production consequence.
* Accepting a Google Flow image because it feels authentic.
* Modifying canon files to close an episode deadline.

## Quality Gates

### Gate 1: Question readiness

Pass when the request names a production decision, scene context, controlling
canon, and intended use.

### Gate 2: Evidence quality

Pass when sources are relevant enough to support the stated narrow conclusion
and their limits are recorded.

### Gate 3: Canon compatibility

Pass only when the finding is compatible with specific established canon.

### Gate 4: Production usability

Pass when the result becomes a clear visual, sound, script, continuity, or
prompt constraint.

### Gate 5: Closure

Pass when a decision is recorded and every affected production record has been
updated. A research folder full of unclassified material is not a completed
workflow.

## Final Rule

When research makes a scene more complicated than canon can support, choose a
smaller truthful scene rather than a larger uncertain one.

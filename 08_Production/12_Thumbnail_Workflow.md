# Thumbnail Workflow

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories thumbnails are planned, created,
reviewed, versioned, and released. A thumbnail is an accurate invitation to a
specific episode, not a separate piece of drama.

It applies to long-form episodes, Shorts where a selectable cover is used,
playlists, and any public episode image. It does not authorize changes to
character, location, object, season, or story canon.

## Philosophy

The strongest Village Stories thumbnail makes an ordinary, meaningful action
easy to notice at a small size. It earns curiosity by showing a real question:
what is Mother noticing, why is Father repairing this rope, or will the
siblings solve their small practical problem?

Clarity is more valuable than noise. Warmth is more valuable than shock.
Recognition is more valuable than a promise the episode cannot keep.

## Objectives

Every approved thumbnail must:

1. Identify the episode's real subject within one glance.
2. Make the central character, object, or action readable on a phone screen.
3. Reflect the actual setting, season, mood, and stakes of the episode.
4. Preserve early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu period accuracy.
5. Respect each character's documented appearance and dignity.
6. Create a useful contrast with adjacent channel uploads without abandoning
   the series visual language.
7. Match the title and opening rather than compensate for a weak one.

## Non-Negotiable Rules

Never use:

* An expression, action, location, or outcome absent from the episode.
* Fear, injury, crying, danger, conflict, or urgency exaggerated beyond the
  real story.
* A fabricated broken object, animal threat, weather event, crowd, or visitor.
* Modern text styling, mobile interfaces, logos, packaging, vehicles, fashion,
  LED lighting, or modern appliance imagery.
* A misleading before/after composite that implies a transformation not shown.
* Sexualized, humiliating, stereotyped, or caricatured depictions of people.
* The Family Dog as a reaction prop, mascot, or false source of danger.
* More than one visual question competing for attention.

If the best-performing-looking concept is dishonest, it is not an eligible
concept.

## Roles

### Episode Director

Defines the true emotional and practical promise, selects the thumbnail moment,
and confirms that the image represents the finished episode.

### Canon Reviewer

Checks characters, clothing, objects, locations, weather, season, architecture,
and period details against the permanent source documents.

### Thumbnail Designer

Builds readable, canon-safe layouts from approved stills or generated assets;
does not invent story information to improve click appeal.

### Google Flow Operator

Generates or selects visual material only from the locked shot and continuity
records. Flags generation drift rather than retouching it into acceptance.

### Metadata Publisher

Ensures the final image, title, description, playlist, and opening make the
same promise. Publishes only the approved version.

## Authority and Inputs

Before concept work, gather:

1. The locked episode premise and final cut.
2. The episode's opening, central task, gentle conflict, and resolution.
3. Relevant character, location, object, culture, and season canon.
4. The visual continuity record and approved Google Flow frames.
5. `06_Story_Engine/13_Visual_Storytelling.md`.
6. `07_Episode_System/04_Hook_System.md`.
7. The current upload's planned title and metadata brief.

Specific canon always wins. This manual governs packaging only within those
facts.

## Thumbnail Decision Framework

Choose the image around one truthful viewer question:

| Episode truth | Honest thumbnail question |
| --- | --- |
| Damp wood delays cooking | Can the family get the fire going? |
| A child forgot a school item | What did she leave behind? |
| Father repairs a useful object | Will this careful repair hold? |
| Rain changes an outdoor plan | How will the work continue in rain? |
| A neighbour needs help | What small need has brought them here? |

Reject a concept if its question depends on a secret, threat, or twist that the
episode does not actually deliver.

### Select the focal point

Use one of these, in priority order:

1. A visible meaningful action.
2. A character noticing a practical problem.
3. A useful object whose condition establishes the problem.
4. A calm result that expresses the episode's earned feeling.

Do not lead with a generic scenic wide shot, a posed portrait, or an unrelated
beautiful detail unless it is the episode's real point.

### Decide whether text is needed

Start without text. Add only short, legible text when the image alone cannot
identify a specific, truthful subject.

Text must:

* Be understandable at small size.
* Describe rather than exaggerate.
* Use one short phrase or fewer.
* Avoid all-caps shouting, clickbait punctuation, and claims.
* Remain visually secondary to the human action.

Prefer “Rainy Day Cooking” to “DISASTER IN THE KITCHEN!” and “The Missing
Notebook” to “YOU WON'T BELIEVE THIS!”

## Step-by-Step Workflow

### 1. Create the thumbnail brief

Record:

* Episode working title and format.
* One-sentence truthful premise.
* Primary character and their visible need.
* Location, time of day, season, and weather.
* Important object and its exact state.
* Emotional tone: curiosity, care, patience, relief, or quiet delight.
* The episode opening visual.
* The outcome that must not be revealed, if preserving it is honest.

The brief must describe what exists in the final cut, not what the team wishes
the episode had been.

### 2. Identify three honest concepts

Draft up to three concepts, each with one focal point and one question. For
each, state the exact timestamp or planned shot that supports it.

Example:

* **Concept A:** Mother testing damp firewood in the kitchen; “Will the fire
  catch?” is supported by the opening task.
* **Concept B:** A close view of the wet wood and weak smoke; useful only if
  Mother remains visibly present or the title supplies the human context.
* **Concept C:** A finished meal; warm but weaker if it hides the episode's
  initial problem.

Do not create ten minor variations before deciding what the episode genuinely
promises.

### 3. Check canon before asset creation

Verify:

* Character face, age, hair, clothing, and body language.
* Location geometry, working zone, material, and route logic.
* Object material, period, condition, and appropriate handling.
* Seasonal light, ground condition, rain, smoke, and work routine.
* Absence of prohibited modern visual contamination.

If information is not documented, use a safer established detail or escalate
for canon documentation. Do not guess.

### 4. Source the visual

Prefer a clear frame from the approved final episode when it communicates the
truth cleanly. If a dedicated asset is necessary, create it from the same
locked visual state.

An asset may improve framing, crop, and legibility. It may not add an event,
emotion, object, character, or outcome.

### 5. Use Google Flow safely

For a generated thumbnail asset, hand off:

* Canonical character identifiers and approved appearance references.
* Exact location zone and background geometry.
* Season, time, weather, light direction, and surface condition.
* The object, its state, and precise hand action.
* Camera distance, framing, and the single intended visual question.
* Explicit exclusions: no text, logo, modern object, extra crowd, stylized
  drama, or altered costume.

Review the result at full size and thumbnail size. Regenerate when it changes
facts; do not hide a continuity failure with a tight crop.

### 6. Compose for small-screen reading

Place the focal action where it remains clear after platform cropping. Maintain
enough separation between face, hands, object, and background to read the
relationship. Keep the setting useful but uncluttered.

Use natural colour and contrast from the scene. Do not apply artificial neon,
heavy orange-and-teal grading, spotlighting, comic outlines, arrows, circles,
or alarm symbols.

### 7. Align title and opening

Read the title, view the thumbnail, then watch the first 15–30 seconds.

The viewer should recognize the thumbnail's subject quickly. The opening may
add context; it must not reveal that the image implied a different story.

### 8. Run quality gates

Pass the visual, truth, canon, and technical gates below. Log the selected
version and the rejected reason for any near-final alternative.

### 9. Export and hand off

Export the approved platform-ready image according to the current YouTube
technical requirements. Store the source, final file, version name, episode
identifier, approval date, and approver with the episode package.

The publisher receives only the approved final asset and matching metadata
brief.

## Quality Gates

### Gate A: Story truth

* Does this occur in the episode?
* Does it represent the central action or honest entry point?
* Would a viewer reasonably feel the episode delivered what it suggested?
* Does it avoid spoiling a resolution that should unfold in the episode?

### Gate B: Canon and period

* Are all people, clothes, objects, locations, animals, and weather correct?
* Is the village represented as lived-in and dignified rather than exotic,
  impoverished, or decorative?
* Is every visible convenience plausible for the established early-1990s world?

### Gate C: Readability

* Can a viewer identify the focal point at small size?
* Is the face/action/object relationship clear?
* Is text, if any, brief and truthful?
* Does the composition remain useful when cropped for platform surfaces?

### Gate D: Platform alignment

* Does the title describe the same episode?
* Does the opening fulfill the visual promise?
* Does the image distinguish the upload without becoming visually loud?
* Is it appropriate for a broad audience and YouTube policy?

## Examples and Mistakes

### Good: Rainy cooking episode

Show Mother at the established kitchen fire, assessing a visibly damp piece of
wood while rain is naturally visible or implied by the setting. The title may
name rainy-day cooking. The image promises a real practical difficulty and
careful response.

### Bad: Rainy cooking episode

Show flames engulfing the kitchen, Mother crying, or a frightened Family Dog.
None belongs to a gentle damp-firewood story; all create a false emergency.

### Good: Forgotten school item

Show Younger Daughter looking into her established bag while Elder Brother
notices the concern on the village path. The object is real, the relationship
is real, and the question is small but understandable.

### Bad: Forgotten school item

Show a huge red “MISSING!” label, a tearful close-up, or a school gate scene
that never occurs. These manufacture stakes and misrepresent the child.

## YouTube Considerations

YouTube performance data can reveal whether the image is clear to the intended
audience. Review impressions, click-through rate, first-minute retention,
comments about expectation, and satisfaction signals together.

Do not judge a thumbnail by click-through rate alone. A high click rate paired
with early departure can indicate a mismatched promise. Improve clarity,
subject selection, title alignment, or opening pace before increasing drama.

If testing alternate thumbnails is available, test only honest variants drawn
from the same episode promise. Keep a record of what changed and why. Never
use testing to determine which false implication produces more clicks.

## Continuity Record

For every final thumbnail, record:

* Episode ID and final title.
* Final-cut timestamp or dedicated asset source.
* Canon references consulted.
* Character wardrobe and object state.
* Location, season, time, and weather.
* Version history and approver.
* Metadata version linked to the release.

This record prevents a later re-upload, playlist cover, or translated title
from separating the image from its episode truth.

## Canon Authority

Character, world, location, object, culture, seasonal, Story Engine, and
Episode System documents are authoritative. When a packaging idea conflicts
with any of them, discard the packaging idea.

## Guiding Principle

Invite the viewer into the episode that truly exists, and make its small human
meaning clear enough to choose.

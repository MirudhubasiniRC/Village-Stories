# Quality Control

## Purpose

This manual defines the permanent quality-control system for Village Stories.
It protects the series from factual drift, weak storytelling, continuity
breaks, technical defects, misleading packaging, and avoidable publishing
errors before they reach viewers.

Quality control is not a cosmetic final pass. It is the evidence-based process
that confirms each episode remains a truthful part of the Village Stories
world from premise through public release.

## Philosophy

A polished image cannot repair a broken promise. A high-resolution frame cannot
make an inaccurate object canonical. A strong retention graph cannot justify
false suspense. Quality means the story, world, sound, picture, packaging, and
viewer expectation agree.

The work should feel calm because the process is disciplined. Reviewers protect
the small details that allow viewers to trust the village: where a tool was
placed, how wet the path is, what a character knows, and whether the rain,
sound, and light belong to the same moment.

## Objectives

Quality control must:

1. Verify compliance with the full canon hierarchy.
2. Confirm that story, pacing, emotion, visual language, and sound serve the
   series promise.
3. Detect continuity failures before final export.
4. Detect modern visual or sonic contamination.
5. Validate technical delivery, captions, accessibility, rights, and metadata.
6. Reject misleading audience promises.
7. Produce clear, actionable feedback and an auditable approval record.

## Authority

The governing sources are, in order of specificity:

1. Character documentation for identity, appearance, relationship, behaviour,
   and voice/body language.
2. Location documentation for geography, layout, atmosphere, and use.
3. Object documentation for material, period, handling, and state.
4. World, culture, seasonal, and research documentation for setting truth.
5. Story Engine documents for story construction and emotional boundaries.
6. Episode System documents for format, hooks, pacing, openings, endings, and
   review.
7. Production manuals for execution and release process.

When sources appear inconsistent, pause approval and escalate the conflict.
Never resolve it by silently inventing a compromise.

## Roles

### Quality Lead

Owns the review plan, assigns gates, records findings, and makes the final
pass/fail recommendation.

### Canon Reviewer

Reviews factual and historical accuracy. Has authority to block a scene,
asset, sound, thumbnail, or metadata claim that contradicts canon.

### Story Reviewer

Checks premise, stakes, character choices, emotional truth, pace, opening, and
ending against the Story Engine and Episode System.

### Picture and Continuity Reviewer

Checks image quality, generated-image drift, screen direction, geography,
wardrobe, props, weather, time, action progress, and edit logic.

### Sound and Accessibility Reviewer

Checks dialogue, ambience, ASMR, music, silence, captions, language fields,
and hearing-accessible meaning.

### Packaging Reviewer

Checks thumbnail, title, description, playlists, cards, end screens, and
promotional language against the locked episode.

### Publisher

Completes technical platform checks and confirms that the approved assets—not
drafts—are the ones scheduled or released.

No reviewer should approve their own unresolved defect.

## Severity and Decision Rules

### Blocker

A blocker prevents release. Examples:

* Canon contradiction or modern contamination.
* Misleading thumbnail, title, description, or hook.
* Missing legal/rights clearance where required.
* Broken audio, missing essential captions, corrupt export, or wrong episode.
* Character, animal, child, cultural, or safety portrayal that violates series
  dignity.

### Major defect

A major defect materially weakens comprehension, continuity, trust, or
technical quality. It must be fixed or formally waived by the Quality Lead and
Episode Director with a documented reason.

### Minor defect

A minor defect is visible but does not change story meaning, canon, viewer
trust, accessibility, or technical usability. Fix when practical; log if
deferred.

### Observation

An improvement idea that does not block the current version. Keep separate
from defects so revision notes remain actionable.

The default decision is **not approved** until each applicable gate passes.
“No one noticed” is not evidence of quality.

## Review Artifacts

Every episode receives a quality package containing:

* Episode ID, format, duration, version, and review date.
* Source premise, script or beat sheet, and approved canon references.
* Shot list, visual continuity log, and Google Flow prompt/asset record.
* Sound plan, music/rights record, and captions.
* Final master export and platform export.
* Thumbnail, metadata brief, title, description, and public links.
* Defect log, approvals, waivers, and final release decision.

Keep version names unambiguous. Never review an asset whose relation to the
final cut is unclear.

## Quality Gates

### Gate 0: Premise and pre-production

Run before substantial production.

Check:

* The premise arises from established canon.
* The practical need, gentle conflict, action, emotion, and ending follow the
  Story Engine.
* Characters belong in the selected place and task.
* Season, weather, time, routes, objects, and animal care are plausible.
* No new person, profession, object, location, cultural practice, or
  convenience has been assumed without canon.
* The format and first-seconds hook are truthful.

Output: approved brief, revision request, or canon escalation.

### Gate 1: Script and storyboard

Run before image generation or filming.

Check:

* Cause appears before response.
* Every scene has a story function.
* Dialogue does not explain what action can show.
* The emotional turn is proportionate and earned.
* The ending provides rest rather than forced suspense.
* Each transition has a plausible route and elapsed time.
* The planned shots meet the Visual Storytelling rules.
* Each planned sound has a visible purposeful source.

Output: locked plan with continuity handoff fields.

### Gate 2: Google Flow and asset review

Run on every generated asset before it enters the edit.

Check:

* Character identity, age, clothing, hair, body language, and relative scale.
* Location architecture, zones, materials, background activity, and geography.
* Object identity, material, orientation, state, and safe/appropriate use.
* Period accuracy: no smartphone, modern payment cue, LED fixture, contemporary
  packaging, vehicle, fashion, appliance, urban signage, or generic stock
  imagery.
* Season, rain, mud, smoke, food, daylight, and shadow continuity.
* Natural animal presence and welfare.
* Camera purpose and absence of spectacle-only visual treatment.

Reject assets that merely look attractive if they are not factually reliable.
Do not patch drift by hiding it in blur, crops, or rapid edits.

### Gate 3: Rough cut review

Run after the intended structure is visible.

Check:

* The first moments introduce a true point of interest.
* Viewer can understand where they are, what is needed, and why it matters.
* Pacing gives work, weather, sound, and recognition room to register.
* The task progresses visibly from need through response to result.
* Characters act consistently with documented personality and relationships.
* The edit does not manufacture peril, shame, melodrama, or a moral lecture.
* Visual and sound transitions describe the same place and time.

Record precise timestamps for every defect.

### Gate 4: Picture lock

Run after visual revisions are complete.

Check frame by frame where needed:

* Wardrobe, hair, footwear, carried items, and mud/wetness states.
* Hand use, screen direction, eye-line, object placement, and object progress.
* Food, fire, rope, water, repair, animal, and weather states.
* Routes between locations and time-of-day progression.
* Color consistency without artificial modern grading.
* No flicker, broken anatomy, warped text, unwanted logo, duplicate object,
  impossible reflection, or generated artifact.
* Canon-safe end frame and transition out.

Output: picture-lock version identifier.

### Gate 5: Sound lock and accessibility

Check:

* Dialogue is intelligible and belongs to the shown speaker and moment.
* Foreground sound has a visible, proportionate source.
* Ambient bed matches location, weather, time, and distance.
* ASMR supports action rather than performing rural life as spectacle.
* Music does not force emotion, hide story information, or introduce an
  incompatible modern feeling.
* Silence retains truthful room tone where appropriate.
* No modern vehicle, device, urban traffic, or electronic cue exists without
  canon support.
* Captions are accurate, timed, readable, and include essential sound context.
* Audio levels and transitions are stable across the episode.

### Gate 6: Packaging and platform readiness

Check:

* Thumbnail is factual, readable, period-safe, and approved under
  `12_Thumbnail_Workflow.md`.
* Title, description, tags, chapters, links, and playlists are approved under
  `13_YouTube_Metadata.md`.
* Thumbnail, title, and opening make one matching promise.
* Cards and end screens point to relevant, truthful destinations.
* Channel and playlist placement are correct.
* Required rights, disclosures, age suitability, language fields, and captions
  are complete.

### Gate 7: Final release verification

Run on the exact platform upload before scheduling or publishing.

Check:

* Correct master, title, thumbnail, captions, metadata, visibility, date, and
  playlist have been selected.
* The platform transcode plays correctly at the beginning, middle, and end.
* Captions, chapters, end screens, and cards render as intended.
* Public-facing links work and lead to the right destinations.
* No draft notes, placeholders, internal links, or unapproved assets remain.

Output: publish authorization or release block.

## Detailed Review Procedure

### 1. Prepare independently

Read the source brief and relevant canon before watching. Do not let an
attractive first impression replace fact checking.

### 2. Watch once as a viewer

Note what the episode promises, where attention naturally goes, and what
confuses or overstates the story. Do not pause except for technical failure.

### 3. Review against evidence

Watch again with the canon and continuity log. Record timestamp, issue,
severity, evidence, required correction, owner, and status.

### 4. Verify the revision

Review the changed asset and every adjacent cut, sound, caption, title, and
thumbnail dependency. A fix that changes an object state can create a later
continuity defect.

### 5. Make the decision

Approve only when all applicable gates pass. A waiver must name the exact
defect, why it cannot be fixed, viewer impact, approvers, and whether future
use of the asset is prohibited.

## Continuity Matrix

Maintain these fields across all shots:

| Category | Record |
| --- | --- |
| Character | clothing, hair, condition, emotion, entry/exit, carried items |
| Object | owner, location, orientation, full/empty, wet/dry, tied/loose, progress |
| Location | zone, route, background, surface, weather exposure |
| Time | time of day, light, season, weather intensity, elapsed action |
| Sound | foreground source, ambience, distance, dialogue, silence |
| Story | need, action completed, remaining task, emotional change |

No cut may contradict the recorded state without a visible or audible cause.

## Common Failures

### “The image looks cinematic”

Cinematic appearance is not approval. Reject a dramatic low-angle pose,
unnatural close-up, glowing light, or picturesque hardship image if it has no
story role or contradicts the series' observed-life approach.

### “It is only in the background”

A background phone, plastic-heavy brand display, modern motorcycle, LED strip,
or contemporary sign can break the period. Backgrounds require review.

### “The audience will not notice”

Viewers may not identify the defect, but repeated inconsistency weakens trust.
Correct it when it is visible or foundational.

### “The thumbnail needs stronger emotion”

Use the actual moment of attention, care, practical uncertainty, or relief.
Do not add tears, fear, flames, danger, or a false reaction.

### “The sound makes it feel bigger”

Remove alarm-like thunder, impacts, music swells, animal distress, or loud
effects that make routine work feel dangerous.

## Post-Release Quality Learning

Review performance after a meaningful period without treating analytics as a
replacement for craft review.

Look for:

* Whether the opening fulfilled the package promise.
* Completion and retention points that reveal confusion or rushed pacing.
* Comments indicating delight, misunderstanding, or expectation mismatch.
* Caption, language, and accessibility feedback.
* Repeated production defects that need a process change.

Log lessons as process improvements. Do not alter canon or chase a trend merely
because one upload underperformed.

## Canon Authority

Quality control cannot approve a canon conflict for convenience, schedule, or
performance. Escalate ambiguity; reject contradiction.

## Guiding Principle

Release only the version in which the village, the story, and the viewer's
expectation all tell the same truth.

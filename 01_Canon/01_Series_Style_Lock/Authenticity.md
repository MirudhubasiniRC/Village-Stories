# Authenticity

## Purpose

This document defines the historical, cultural, and lifestyle authenticity standards for Village Stories.

The goal is to faithfully represent everyday life in a 1990s South Indian village while respecting local traditions, occupations, architecture, materials, customs, and community life.

Authenticity is one of the defining strengths of the series.

Whenever authenticity and convenience conflict, authenticity should be preferred.

---

# Authenticity Philosophy

Village Stories is inspired by real life.

The village should never feel generic or fictional.

Every location, object, routine, and interaction should have a believable reason to exist within the world.

The audience should feel that this village could have existed exactly as shown.

---

# Historical Period

The universe takes place during the early-to-mid 1990s.

All creative decisions should respect this period.

Technology, transportation, communication, occupations, architecture, household items, clothing, and daily routines should remain appropriate for this era.

---

# Everyday Life

Stories should naturally include authentic village activities.

Examples include:

* Cooking with firewood.
* Farming.
* Water collection.
* Washing clothes at the River Ghat.
* Bathing in the river.
* Caring for livestock.
* Drawing kolams.
* Visiting neighbours.
* Seasonal farming.
* Weekly markets.
* Temple visits.
* School routines.
* Delivering letters.
* Repairing household items.

Daily life should feel productive and connected to the community.

---

# Traditional Materials

Prefer naturally available materials.

Examples include:

* Clay.
* Terracotta.
* Wood.
* Stone.
* Bamboo.
* Coir.
* Cotton.
* Brass.
* Copper.
* Palm leaves.

Modern synthetic materials should only appear if they are historically appropriate for the period and setting.

---

# Architecture

Buildings should reflect traditional rural South Indian construction.

Examples include:

* Whitewashed walls.
* Terracotta roof tiles.
* Wooden doors and windows.
* Red oxide floors.
* Open courtyards.
* Front thinnai.
* Clay kitchens.
* Verandas.
* Stone wells.

Homes should appear practical, lived-in, and well maintained.

---

# Clothing

Clothing should reflect everyday village life.

Examples include:

* Cotton sarees.
* Veshtis.
* Lungis.
* Pavadai and blouse.
* Cotton shirts.
* Simple towels.
* Traditional festival clothing.

Clothing should be functional, comfortable, and culturally appropriate.

---

# Food

Food should reflect local ingredients, seasons, and traditional cooking methods.

Meals should be prepared using authentic utensils, ingredients, and techniques.

Cooking should naturally become part of storytelling rather than simply serving as a background activity.

---

# Occupations

Village life depends on many different people.

Examples include:

* Farmers.
* Teachers.
* Postman.
* Carpenter.
* Blacksmith.
* Potter.
* Washerman.
* Barber.
* Shopkeeper.
* Temple priest.
* Fisherman.
* Basket weaver.

These occupations should appear naturally as the universe expands.

---

# Community Life

Village life is shared.

People naturally:

* Help neighbours.
* Exchange food.
* Share news.
* Gather beneath the banyan tree.
* Meet at the River Ghat.
* Visit festivals.
* Attend ceremonies.
* Work together during harvest.

The village should always feel socially connected.

---

# Cultural Traditions

Traditions should appear through observation rather than explanation.

Examples include:

* Lighting the evening oil lamp.
* Morning kolams.
* Festival decorations.
* Temple rituals.
* Harvest celebrations.
* Respect for elders.
* Community meals.
* Seasonal customs.

Traditions should feel like ordinary parts of life.

---

# Forgotten Details

Whenever appropriate, preserve authentic details that are becoming less common.

Examples include:

* Inland letters.
* Postcards.
* Money orders.
* Public telephone booths.
* Bicycle repair shops.
* Hand-operated grinding stones.
* Palm-leaf baskets.
* Traditional toys.
* Village storytellers.
* Manual farming tools.

These details should appear naturally within stories.

---

# Research Before Assumption

If historical accuracy is uncertain:

* Research first.
* Prefer documented information.
* Do not guess.
* Update the Research library when new knowledge is discovered.

Authenticity should improve continuously as the project grows.

---

# Avoid

Avoid introducing elements that conflict with the established period or setting.

Examples include:

* Smartphones.
* Modern appliances.
* Contemporary fashion.
* PVC-heavy architecture where inappropriate.
* LED lighting.
* Modern vehicles dominating village life.
* Contemporary branding.
* Modern packaging.

If an item existed during the 1990s but was uncommon in rural villages, include it only when it makes sense for the story.

---

# Guiding Principle

Authenticity is not about making the past feel old.

It is about respectfully portraying a way of life as it genuinely existed.

Every episode should preserve a small piece of living cultural memory while telling an emotionally engaging story.

# Story Quality Checklist

## Purpose

This is the final quality gate for every Village Stories episode.

Use it after an idea has been selected, after the beat sheet is written,
and once more before visuals, sound, or publication are approved.

It protects the series promise:

* early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu is a lived place, not a decorative backdrop;
* Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and Family Dog remain
  recognizable people and animal companions;
* small practical problems can carry real feeling;
* calm, dignity, and emotional truth matter more than plot size.

This document checks quality. It does not replace detailed world, location,
object, culture, or character canon.

## Philosophy

A good episode is not merely free of mistakes. It makes the viewer feel that
this family could only have lived this moment in this village, season, and
home.

Quality comes from connection:

```text
Specific place + truthful character choice + useful action + earned feeling
= a memorable ordinary moment
```

Do not polish an episode with extra events when the emotional centre is weak.
Find the meaningful detail, practical need, or quiet choice that is already
inside the premise.

## Quality Principles

1. **Canon before convenience.** A useful idea must fit the established world.
2. **Action before explanation.** Let care appear in hands, work, food,
   posture, timing, and small choices.
3. **Proportion before intensity.** A minor problem may matter deeply without
   becoming a crisis.
4. **Dignity before spectacle.** Work, age, money limits, sadness, and village
   routines are never displayed for pity or amusement.
5. **Resolution before perfection.** The ending needs emotional completion,
   not a magically perfect practical outcome.
6. **Specificity before decoration.** Seasonal weather and locations must
   affect what characters do.
7. **Silence before speeches.** Use dialogue only when it adds something the
   image, sound, and action cannot.

## How to Use This Checklist

Mark every item as:

* **Pass** — visible, accurate, and useful.
* **Revise** — present but vague, unsupported, or emotionally weak.
* **Stop** — contradicts canon or the series promise.

A single Stop prevents production. Correct the premise, not only the final
line or individual shot. A cluster of Revise marks means return to the beat
sheet before visual generation.

## Gate 1: Premise

Before planning scenes, confirm:

- [ ] The premise can be stated in one calm sentence.
- [ ] It begins with an ordinary need, observation, task, or relationship.
- [ ] The practical situation belongs naturally to the selected location.
- [ ] The conflict is manageable and has no villain or threat.
- [ ] The chosen emotional destination is one or two compatible feelings.
- [ ] The episode does not require a coincidence to begin or end.
- [ ] The story can remain meaningful if told quietly.

### Premise Decision Framework

Ask these questions in order:

1. What practical thing needs attention?
2. Why does it matter to this character today?
3. What small obstacle changes the routine?
4. What does another family member notice, offer, or learn through action?
5. What final image proves the feeling has changed?

If question 4 cannot be answered without a lecture, surprise twist, or new
outside event, the premise is not ready.

## Gate 2: Canon and Period

- [ ] The episode is plausible in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.
- [ ] Clothing, food, tools, transport, communication, and household objects
  are period-correct and documented where required.
- [ ] No modern device, internet behaviour, modern consumer culture, or
  unexplained convenience enters the story.
- [ ] Travel follows the established village geography and sensible time.
- [ ] The selected location's documented use, access, and atmosphere are
  respected.
- [ ] The time of day supports the work, weather, light, and sound described.
- [ ] Any festival, custom, school activity, or visitor is supported by canon.

### Canon Check

When an idea conflicts with a general story-engine instruction, follow the
more specific source:

```text
Specific character/world/location/object canon
        >
Story-engine operating rules
        >
This quality checklist
        >
An attractive new idea
```

Record the source document used for any detail that could otherwise drift
between episodes.

## Gate 3: Character Truth

For every speaking or visually important character, confirm:

- [ ] Their goal grows from their documented personality or current need.
- [ ] Their voice, patience, humour, skill, and body language remain familiar.
- [ ] Their relationship with the other family members remains respectful.
- [ ] They have a practical reason to be in the scene.
- [ ] They contribute through a choice, reaction, or useful absence.
- [ ] They are not reduced to a helper, obstacle, joke, or moral lesson.

### Family Application

**Mother** may make care visible through preparation, observation, restraint,
or capable action. Do not make her endlessly self-sacrificing for easy tears.

**Father** may bring steadiness, work knowledge, dry warmth, or quiet concern.
Do not make him distant or harsh merely to create tension.

**Elder Brother** may carry responsibility, pride, impatience, skill, or a
gentle teasing impulse within canon. Do not turn ordinary sibling friction
into cruelty.

**Younger Daughter** may notice, wonder, ask, help imperfectly, or form a
small connection with people, plants, and animals. Do not make her wiser than
every adult for a forced lesson.

**Family Dog** may notice changes, wait, follow, protect a routine, or create
gentle humour. Do not give the dog impossible understanding, danger, or an
unexplained role in solving everything.

## Gate 4: Conflict and Emotion

- [ ] The obstacle is practical, human, seasonal, or relational in a gentle
  way.
- [ ] Its scale matches the episode length and tone.
- [ ] Nobody is humiliated, threatened, abandoned, or made into a villain.
- [ ] Sadness, if present, is quiet, safe, and held by care or understanding.
- [ ] The emotional shift follows visible events rather than music or speech
  alone.
- [ ] The practical outcome may remain modest, but the feeling reaches rest.

### Safe Sadness Test

Use sadness only when it reveals memory, absence, tiredness, disappointment,
or care without making suffering the attraction.

Pass example: Father quietly saves the first ripe guava because Mother once
said it would suit Younger Daughter's school snack; the family discovers the
small act at supper.

Stop example: A family member is publicly shamed, severely ill, endangered,
or deprived so the ending can demand tears.

## Gate 5: Season and Location

- [ ] The season changes a need, task, texture, or decision.
- [ ] Weather changes clothing, light, mud, water, shade, animals, or pace.
- [ ] The location is used for what people actually do there.
- [ ] Objects are handled as useful, worn, cared-for parts of life.
- [ ] Background activity supports the village without becoming generic noise.

### Seasonal Applications

**Summer:** test water, shade, early work, tired animals, cooling food, and
waiting for rain.

**Monsoon:** test shelter, wet paths, protected fires, drainage, drying,
rain-pauses, and renewed attention to fields.

**Post-monsoon:** test repaired routes, green growth, clearer air, outdoor
work, and thankful observation.

**Harvest:** test cooperation, stored grain, shared meals, measured effort,
and practical celebration.

**Festival periods:** test preparation, custom, food, and community only when
supported by existing culture and festival canon.

## Gate 6: Scene and Visual Storytelling

- [ ] Every scene changes information, feeling, or practical progress.
- [ ] The first useful image establishes place, task, or concern.
- [ ] The emotional turn can be understood with dialogue removed.
- [ ] At least one meaningful detail carries the episode's feeling.
- [ ] Camera attention honours hands, faces, materials, animals, weather, and
  working rhythms without making them ornamental.
- [ ] Dialogue does not repeat visible action.
- [ ] Sound supports the place: utensils, cloth, footsteps, birds, rain,
  cooking, animals, or village distance as appropriate.

### Positive Visual Example

Younger Daughter holds a dry school notebook above her head while crossing a
monsoon yard. Elder Brother silently stretches his folded towel between her
and the rain. Their hurried feet, the towel, and Mother warming the notebook
near a safe fire later tell the whole care story.

### Negative Visual Example

The same situation is explained by a long speech about family unity, followed
by an impossible sudden clear sky and a congratulatory group pose.

## Gate 7: Ending

- [ ] The final action answers the episode's emotional question.
- [ ] The closing image is calm, concrete, and earned.
- [ ] The episode does not state a moral it has already shown.
- [ ] No last-minute miracle, outsider rescue, or surprise danger is needed.
- [ ] Hope is modest and believable.

Useful ending images include a repaired object returned to use, food shared
without announcement, a dry garment on a line, a child looking again at a
saved plant, or Family Dog settling near the family after the routine returns.

## Google Flow Quality Check

Before generating or approving Google Flow shots:

- [ ] Build a continuity sheet for people, clothing, props, weather, time,
  location state, and animal position.
- [ ] Write each shot prompt from the approved scene plan, not from a new
  improvised idea.
- [ ] Preserve the same practical action across cuts; a vessel, bag, tool, or
  wet garment cannot change state without a shown reason.
- [ ] Keep faces, ages, proportions, and documented appearance consistent.
- [ ] Ask the image to show an action or response, not a vague emotion label.
- [ ] Reject visuals that glamorize hardship, make the village look generic,
  or introduce unapproved modern objects.
- [ ] Verify the final sequence still makes sense with sound muted.

Google Flow supports continuity; it does not decide canon, emotional meaning,
or story logic.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails | Revision |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adding a larger emergency | Replaces meaning with urgency | Return to the original small need |
| Making everyone agree instantly | Removes character texture | Let care appear through different useful responses |
| Treating weather as scenery | Wastes the seasonal engine | Make it alter a task or choice |
| Explaining every feeling | Flattens visual storytelling | Replace one speech with an observable action |
| Using sadness as a climax | Breaks safety and dignity | Let concern resolve through companionship or care |
| Making the dog a plot device | Breaks animal truth | Use ordinary attention, waiting, or gentle behaviour |
| Ending with a slogan | Forces a lesson | End on an image that carries the insight |

## Continuity Handoff

After approval, hand the next production stage:

1. the one-sentence premise;
2. selected season, time, and location;
3. character goals and documented canon references;
4. object, costume, weather, and prop states;
5. the emotional turn and final image;
6. any continuity facts that must remain true in a later episode.

Do not hand off unresolved questions as though they are choices for visual
production. Resolve them against canon first.

## Final Decision

Approve only when all three statements are true:

- This could genuinely happen to this family in this village at this time.
- The emotion is visible, proportional, and earned through action.
- The final image leaves warmth, calm, curiosity, gratitude, quiet pride, or
  small hope.

If any statement is false, revise the premise before scenes are locked.

## Canon Authority

Specific approved character, world, location, object, culture, and episode
continuity documents overrule this manual. This manual is a quality gate, not
permission to invent around missing canon.

## Guiding Principle

Quality in Village Stories means that an ordinary act feels truthful, specific,
and quietly important because this particular family shares it in this
particular village.

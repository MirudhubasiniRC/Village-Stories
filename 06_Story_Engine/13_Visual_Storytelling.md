# Visual Storytelling

## Purpose

This manual turns Village Stories emotion into clear, calm images.

The viewer should understand where the family is, what they need, and how they
feel before dialogue explains it.

It applies to shot selection, scene progression, visual prompts, and final
Google Flow review. It does not replace character, location, object, or season
canon.

## Philosophy

Village Stories is observed life, not visual spectacle.

Meaning lives in an ordinary action done with care: Mother testing a cooking
fire, Father checking a repaired knot, Elder Brother slowing his walk for
Younger Daughter, or the Family Dog waiting beside a familiar person.

The camera notices work, weather, texture, and small choices. It never treats
rural life as a decorative backdrop or a hardship display.

## Core Principles

1. Show the cause before the response.
2. Let a useful object carry story information.
3. Let hands, posture, and attention reveal feeling.
4. Make season visible in labour, light, surfaces, and clothing.
5. Earn close-ups with a clear dramatic reason.
6. Use wide shots to establish geography, not merely beauty.
7. Hold on the completed action long enough for its emotion to register.
8. Prefer one readable visual idea per shot.

## Show Before Explaining

Story information should first appear through:

* Eyes, posture, hands, and small expressions.
* A prepared vessel, repaired tool, packed school bag, or waiting animal.
* Weather changing light, fabric, mud, water, or smoke.
* A character choosing to help before being asked.
* An unfinished task that establishes what remains to be done.

Dialogue may clarify what an image cannot show. It must not repeat what the
image already says.

## Visual Grammar

### Establish

Start a new location with a useful orientation image: the Family Kitchen fire,
the Front Thinnai before a visitor arrives, the courtyard route to the
Livestock Shelter, or the school path leading away from home.

Establish time through light and activity. Morning may include preparation,
school departure, or animal care; dusk may include return, cooling light, and
the settling of a finished task.

### Notice

Move from place to the detail that matters. A damp bundle of firewood, mud at
the threshold, an empty water vessel, a loose cart rope, or a forgotten school
item is a story clue, not filler.

### Act

Show the practical response in a sequence the viewer can follow:

1. Character notices the need.
2. Character chooses a sensible action.
3. Hands prepare or use the established object.
4. A gentle obstacle changes the effort.
5. Another character responds, helps, or understands.

### Receive

After the task, show the result in lived space: a protected fire, a dry path,
a settled animal, food shared, or a child relieved. Do not cut away the moment
the action succeeds.

## Camera Focus

Use meaningful details: steam from food, a hand tying coir rope, rain on tile,
a child watching a seedling, or a parent noticing a quiet worry.

Favour:

* Hands performing known work.
* Faces only at a change of thought or feeling.
* Objects before they become important.
* A character's line of sight, followed by what they see.
* The Family Dog's natural attention or resting place when it supports the
  scene.

Avoid collecting attractive details that do not advance place, task, emotion,
or transition.

## Shot Roles

| Shot role | What it must communicate |
| --- | --- |
| Establishing wide | Location, time, weather, route, and available activity |
| Medium action | Who is doing the work and how others relate |
| Detail close-up | Useful object, skilled hand movement, or emotional clue |
| Reaction | A quiet recognition, concern, relief, pride, or warmth |
| Transition | A motivated move between established locations |
| Ending image | The emotional residue after resolution |

Do not use a close-up merely to make a moment feel important. Importance comes
from the story context that precedes it.

## Character Applications

### Mother

Frame Mother through attentive household skill and emotional observation.
Her glance toward a child, measured movement at the fire, or quiet adjustment
to a practical problem can carry care without an explanatory speech.

### Father

Show Father's steadiness through checking, repairing, lifting, planning, or
quietly making room for another person's contribution. Avoid heroic poses or
sudden emotional displays that conflict with his documented character.

### Elder Brother

Use Elder Brother's movement to show responsibility, outdoor confidence, and
sibling protection. A slowed pace, returned item, or practical help should be
visible before it is praised.

### Younger Daughter

Use Younger Daughter's curiosity through observation, questions shown in her
face, careful attempts, and small acts of care. Do not reduce her to constant
reaction shots.

### Family Dog

The Family Dog is a living family presence, not a comic prop. Show it near
plausible activity: waiting on the thinnai, following a compound route, resting
beside work, or responding gently to familiar movement.

## Seasonal Applications

### Summer

Show shade, early work, dry surfaces, thirst-conscious routines, tired animals,
and cooling food. Do not apply a generic warm filter while keeping monsoon
behaviour.

### Monsoon

Show wet thresholds, tile rain, careful footing, damp cloth, protected
firewood, drainage attention, and renewed field edges. Rain must affect what
characters can carry, where they stand, and how they travel.

### Post-Monsoon

Show cleaner air, green growth, repaired paths, resumed outdoor work, and
relief in the pace of the village.

### Harvest

Show cooperation, stored grain, field labour, practical meals, and work-worn
objects. Treat labour with dignity; never turn people into a scenic montage.

### Festivals

Use only established culture and Festival Ground canon. Preparation, shared
work, and familiar ritual detail should outweigh crowd spectacle.

## Location Applications

Use locations according to `09_Location_Usage.md`.

* Family Kitchen: fire, food, vessels, preparation, waiting, and family care.
* Courtyard: shared work, departures, arrivals, washing, animal movement, and
  visible weather.
* Front Thinnai: resting, conversation, waiting, and received visitors.
* Backyard and garden: observation, tending, seasonal change, and quiet play.
* Livestock Shelter: care, repair, feeding, and responsible routine.
* Village paths and paddy fields: motivated travel, farm work, and changing
  weather; always establish where the route begins and ends.
* Primary School, Tea Stall, Grocery Store, River Ghat, Vinayagar Shrine, and
  artisan areas: use only when their documented activity gives the episode a
  reason to go there.

## Decision Framework: Is This Shot Necessary?

Keep a proposed shot only if it does at least one of the following:

1. Establishes a canon-safe place, time, or route.
2. Reveals a character's need, skill, or relationship.
3. Makes practical work understandable.
4. Shows the season changing behaviour.
5. Marks a genuine emotional turn.
6. Prepares or pays off a visual detail.

If it does none, remove it or combine it with a useful shot.

## Decision Framework: Show or Say?

Use an image when the information is concrete, emotional, or physical:

* the firewood is damp;
* Younger Daughter has noticed something;
* Father has found the loose rope;
* Elder Brother is choosing to help;
* Mother understands a quiet worry.

Use dialogue when a character must share context not visible in the frame, make
a decision together, or offer a relationship-specific response.

## Example: Damp Firewood

**Premise:** During monsoon, Mother needs to finish a meal while damp firewood
makes the kitchen fire unreliable.

1. Rain marks the tiled roof; a covered wood bundle rests near the kitchen.
2. Mother's hand tests a damp piece; the fire gives weak smoke.
3. Younger Daughter watches, concerned, while the Family Dog settles away from
   the smoke.
4. Father returns with a dry piece from the sensible established storage place.
5. Mother restarts the fire; steam rises steadily from the vessel.
6. The family receives the meal without announcing a moral.

The episode is visible because each image changes the viewer's understanding.

## Example: Forgotten School Item

**Premise:** On a school morning, Elder Brother notices Younger Daughter's
forgotten item after they have started along the village path.

Show the packed bag first. Show Younger Daughter searching, then Elder Brother
recognising the clue. Establish his return route before cutting to it. End with
the siblings continuing together, not with a lecture about responsibility.

## Google Flow Considerations

Google Flow generation must protect continuity across every visual beat.

For each shot prompt, state:

* exact established location and its relevant zone;
* time of day, season, weather, and ground condition;
* the named character or characters present;
* character appearance, clothing, scale, and emotional state from canon;
* the established object being handled;
* the precise action, screen direction, and relationship in frame;
* camera distance and purpose;
* environmental continuity from the preceding shot.

Carry forward stable identifiers rather than re-describing a character loosely.
Never allow a prompt to invent a new house layout, vehicle, tool, garment,
animal marking, or background crowd for convenience.

### Prompt Handoff

Before generating the next shot, record:

1. Where each character stands or exits.
2. What each character carries.
3. Object state: dry/wet, full/empty, tied/loose, prepared/unprepared.
4. Weather intensity and light direction.
5. The action completed and the action about to begin.

## Visual Continuity Rules

* A character cannot appear at a new location without a shown or established
  route and plausible elapsed time.
* An object remains where it was last placed unless a character moves it.
* Wetness, mud, smoke, food progress, and daylight evolve consistently.
* Do not reverse hand use, screen direction, or object condition between cuts
  unless the action visibly motivates it.
* Keep the Family Dog's presence spatially plausible; it cannot appear and
  disappear as a decorative reaction device.
* Recheck all generated frames against appearance and location canon before
  accepting them.

## Common Mistakes

* Explaining feelings that a face, action, or object already shows.
* Using slow motion, dramatic angles, or beauty shots to manufacture emotion.
* Cutting from a wide location image to an unrelated close-up with no story
  bridge.
* Treating rain, harvest, or festival colour as interchangeable decoration.
* Making a character stare sadly without a visible cause.
* Showing undocumented tools, modern technology, or generic rural imagery.
* Overcrowding the frame with people who have no relationship or task.
* Letting Google Flow alter character appearance, costume, house geometry, or
  object state between shots.

## Canon Authority

When a visual idea conflicts with documented character identity, location
layout, object use, period detail, route, weather, or season, the canon wins.
Rewrite the image; do not bend the world to preserve a shot.

## Guiding Principle

Show ordinary life with enough precision that a small act of care becomes
emotionally unmistakable.

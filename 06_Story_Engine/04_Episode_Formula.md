# Episode Formula

## Purpose

This document provides reusable, canon-safe patterns for developing individual Village Stories episodes.

An episode formula is a starting shape, not a finished plot. It helps turn a practical daily activity into a story with a clear need, a gentle turn, shared action, and a quiet emotional conclusion.

Use a formula only after checking the relevant character, location, season, and world canon. Then adapt it to the people and conditions of the specific episode.

---

# Philosophy

Repetition is welcome when it reflects the rhythms of rural family life. Meals, school mornings, repairs, rain, field work, visits, and animal care can return many times because no two days are emotionally identical.

The formula must never become a machine that produces interchangeable stories. The changing element is not simply the obstacle. It is the relationship, responsibility, observation, or seasonal condition that gives the familiar activity new meaning.

---

# Core Principles

* Start with an ordinary, visible activity.
* Give the day one practical need.
* Choose an obstacle or discovery that belongs to that activity.
* Let the available family members contribute truthfully.
* Resolve through local knowledge, patience, and cooperation.
* Leave the audience with a modest emotional gain.
* Do not use a formula to bypass canon or manufacture drama.

---

# Formula Anatomy

Every episode formula should contain these five parts:

1. **Everyday base:** What routine, task, or obligation begins the day?
2. **Specific need:** What must be prepared, protected, completed, understood, or adjusted?
3. **Gentle turn:** What shortage, weather shift, misunderstanding, timing issue, or observation changes the plan?
4. **Human response:** How do character-specific actions create a believable solution?
5. **Settled image:** What final action, sound, or image proves that the day has found its balance?

The formula is complete only when it contains an emotional thread. “The family makes food” is an activity. “The family adjusts a meal so a child’s small contribution can be included” is an episode premise.

---

# Formula Selection Framework

## Choose From Daily Truth

Select a formula from something the village family would naturally encounter:

* Household work.
* Food preparation.
* School routine.
* Seasonal weather.
* Field, garden, or animal care.
* Maintenance and repair.
* A neighbourly exchange.
* Festival or temple preparation.

Do not begin with a genre label such as “adventure,” “mystery,” or “rescue.” Begin with the day’s real work.

## Match Formula to Season

Ask whether the proposed activity makes sense in the established weather and agricultural rhythm. Rain stories require real rain conditions; drying, water, field, or festival stories require the correct seasonal context.

## Match Formula to Cast

Choose the family member whose established nature most naturally carries the emotional thread. A Father-led repair and a Younger Daughter-led school observation should not use identical emotional beats.

## Match Formula to Place

Use an established location because its familiar tools, routes, sounds, and social rules will make the episode more specific.

# Reusable Formulas

## Cooking Formula

**Ingredient need → careful preparation → small shortage or interruption → practical adjustment → shared meal**

Cooking episodes are about nourishment, planning, memory, hospitality, and the care hidden inside routine work.

Useful gentle turns:

* A needed ingredient is less plentiful than expected.
* Rain, heat, or a visitor changes the timing.
* A child wants to help but must learn a careful step.
* A utensil, fuel, or work space needs a practical adjustment.

Positive application:

Mother prepares a familiar meal while Younger Daughter wants to include something she has helped gather. The quantity is limited, so Father and Elder Brother help adapt the serving plan without making the child feel dismissed. The final meal carries her contribution.

Avoid:

* Treating cooking as a frantic competition.
* Creating waste, shouting, or humiliation for comedy.
* Adding modern packaged convenience to solve the shortage.

## Rain Formula

**Weather change → protect people, animals, food, or work → adapt together → rain-settled calm**

Rain episodes are about attentiveness, timing, shelter, and the way a household responds before inconvenience grows.

Useful gentle turns:

* Rain begins earlier than expected.
* A drying, carrying, or travel plan needs changing.
* The Family Dog needs a dry familiar place.
* A child is disappointed that an outdoor plan must wait.

Positive application:

Father notices rain gathering while household work remains outside. The family brings in what matters most, then finds a quieter indoor task. The ending values the rain’s sound after the effort rather than celebrating an extreme storm.

Avoid:

* Flood, lightning danger, trapped characters, or disaster rescue.
* Making rain a villain.
* Ignoring wet clothing, paths, tools, and changed timing.

## School Formula

**Child’s task or uncertainty → household preparation → school-related moment → steady support → quiet confidence**

School stories are about growth within the safety of family routine.

Useful gentle turns:

* A child has forgotten a small required item.
* A handmade task needs protecting or finishing.
* The route, weather, or timing requires a calmer plan.
* The child worries about doing a familiar thing independently.

Positive application:

Elder Brother prepares for a responsibility connected to school and wants to manage it alone. Father respects that wish while offering one practical check. Later, Elder Brother uses the preparation successfully and returns with restrained pride.

Avoid:

* Competitive humiliation, bullying, severe punishment, or exam panic.
* Making teachers or classmates generic villains.
* Treating school achievement as the only measure of a child’s worth.

## Farm or Garden Formula

**Seasonal task → patient observation and work → natural limitation → useful progress or learning → gratitude**

Farm and garden episodes are about labour, weather, patience, and respect for processes that cannot be hurried.

Useful gentle turns:

* Soil, water, shade, or timing needs closer observation.
* A tool or container requires repair.
* A child learns that a plant or task cannot be forced.
* The family must choose what can realistically be done today.

Positive application:

Father and Elder Brother begin a modest seasonal task. Younger Daughter asks why they wait before the next step. The answer appears through observation rather than a lecture, and the work ends with useful progress rather than an instant result.

Avoid:

* Unrealistic harvests.
* Sudden miracles or implausible expert knowledge.
* Turning farm work into a dangerous survival struggle.

## Animal-Care Formula

**Animal’s routine changes → family observes carefully → gentle care → restored comfort or routine**

Animal-care stories teach attention without turning animals into plot devices.

Useful gentle turns:

* The Family Dog avoids a usual resting place.
* Water, shade, bedding, or a familiar object needs attention.
* The dog’s behaviour helps someone notice a harmless household issue.
* A child learns the difference between affection and responsible care.

Positive application:

Younger Daughter notices that the Family Dog is seeking shade earlier than usual. The family adjusts water and resting arrangements during the heat. The ending shows the dog comfortable near them as the day continues.

Avoid:

* Injury, cruelty, abandonment, or peril for emotional leverage.
* Human-like dialogue or impossible understanding.
* Treating the dog as a joke machine.

## Household Repair Formula

**Useful object needs attention → assess available materials → patient repair → small correction or second attempt → restored use**

Repair stories value thrift, competence, and the quiet reassurance of maintaining what the household depends on.

Useful gentle turns:

* The first repair is not quite right.
* A necessary material must be found locally.
* Elder Brother rushes and learns to slow down.
* The repaired item has a small history or emotional association.

Positive application:

Father notices a worn household item before it fails. Elder Brother offers assistance and learns why careful measurement matters. Mother later uses the repaired item naturally, which becomes the episode’s quiet proof of success.

Avoid:

* Making the object’s failure dangerous.
* Treating older objects as disposable.
* Using a sudden purchase or modern replacement as the answer.

## Visitor Formula

**Familiar arrival → household adjustment → shared conversation or small help → warm departure**

Visitor episodes are about hospitality, flexibility, and how a household makes room without losing its daily truth.

Useful gentle turns:

* The visitor arrives during active work.
* Food or seating must be adjusted modestly.
* A visitor brings news or a small need that can be met locally.
* A child learns the rhythm of receiving someone with respect.

Positive application:

A known neighbour arrives while Mother is preparing the household. The family does not perform abundance; they share what is sensible, adjust the work, and send the neighbour away with genuine warmth.

Avoid:

* A suspicious stranger, secret, threat, or melodramatic revelation.
* Treating hospitality as a burden that fuels resentment.
* Sudden wealth or lavish display.

## Festival or Temple Formula

**Preparation → family and community contribution → modest complication → shared completion → reverent or joyful calm**

Festival and temple episodes are about participation, preparation, respect, and community rhythm.

Useful gentle turns:

* A household responsibility needs finishing on time.
* Weather or a missing small material requires adaptation.
* A child learns why a preparation is done carefully.
* Several people coordinate without one person becoming a hero.

Positive application:

The family prepares a modest contribution within its established means. A small timing issue requires Father and Mother to divide the work differently, while the children contribute appropriately. The ending rests on gathered community feeling rather than spectacle.

Avoid:

* Treating belief or ritual as exotic decoration.
* Inventing rites without research or canon support.
* Using crowds, noise, or miracle-like events as a replacement for story.

## Neighbourly Help Formula

**Familiar need nearby → family offers practical help → shared work reveals relationship → routine resumes warmer**

This formula should preserve the dignity of both giver and receiver.

Useful gentle turns:

* Help must fit around the family’s own responsibilities.
* The neighbour has useful knowledge in return.
* A child learns that help can be quiet and ordinary.

Positive application:

Father pauses to assist a neighbour with a manageable task on the established route. The delay changes the family’s schedule only slightly; Mother adjusts the next household step without complaint. The episode ends with routine restored and the relationship deepened.

Avoid:

* Making help a public display of superiority.
* Creating a crisis that requires a heroic rescue.
* Presenting the neighbour as helpless or comic.

# Formula Variation Rules

Vary a repeated formula by changing one or two meaningful elements:

* The season or time of day.
* The family member whose perspective leads.
* The emotional question.
* The practical limitation.
* The location within established canon.
* The kind of quiet ending.

Do not vary by simply making the obstacle bigger. The formula remains fresh when the observation is fresh.

---

# Decision Frameworks

## The Formula Fit Check

Before approving an outline, confirm:

1. The formula arises from a canonically available activity.
2. The season and location make practical sense.
3. The central character has a truthful reason to care.
4. The turn is modest and connected to the task.
5. The solution uses visible effort or knowledge.
6. The ending contains emotional settlement.

## The Distinction Check

When two planned episodes use the same formula, identify what makes them emotionally different.

For example, two cooking episodes can differ because one is about Younger Daughter being included and the other is about Mother receiving quiet support after a tiring day. If the answer is only “the ingredient is different,” develop the premise further.

## The Simplicity Check

If the formula needs multiple twists, unfamiliar locations, a large cast, or extensive explanation, reduce it. The strongest Village Stories premise can usually be understood in one clear sentence.

# Story Applications

## Building an Episode Brief

Record:

* Formula name.
* Everyday base.
* Season and time of day.
* Primary location.
* Featured family members.
* Practical need.
* Gentle turn.
* Resolution.
* Final image.

This information should be enough to check the premise before a full beat sheet is written.

## Building a Beat Sheet

Map the formula to the structural rhythm in **03_Story_Structure.md**. Add only beats that establish, complicate, solve, or emotionally settle the chosen premise.

## Building Scenes

Choose actions, props, and sensory details from the relevant location and character canon. A formula becomes believable through these specifics.

# Character Applications

## Mother-Led Formulas

Cooking, household adjustment, visitor, and seasonal preparation formulas often reveal Mother’s attention and care. Do not reduce her to constant service; include her judgement, skill, and quiet humour where canonically appropriate.

## Father-Led Formulas

Repair, field, route, weather, and neighbourly-help formulas often reveal Father’s steadiness. His solution should be practical and measured, not a dramatic rescue.

## Elder Brother-Led Formulas

School, repair assistance, errands, and growing-responsibility formulas can show Elder Brother’s learning. Let pride be balanced by patient correction and real contribution.

## Younger Daughter-Led Formulas

School, animal care, small household responsibility, and discovery formulas can show Younger Daughter’s wonder and direct feeling. Keep her contribution age-appropriate.

## Family Dog-Led Formulas

Animal-care and routine-observation formulas may begin with the Family Dog, but the dog remains an animal within the family’s life. The episode’s emotional point belongs to care, not anthropomorphism.

# Seasonal Variations

Use seasons to choose rather than merely decorate a formula.

* Heat supports shade, water, early work, animal comfort, and timing stories.
* Rain supports protection, indoor adjustment, wet paths, stored goods, and waiting stories.
* Productive agricultural periods support patient work, tools, food, and household coordination.
* Festival seasons support preparation, modest contribution, and community connection.

Confirm exact practices and timing with the relevant research, world, and seasonal canon documents before finalising.

---

# Google Flow Considerations

Formula names can guide clear, truthful episode metadata:

* A rain-adjusted meal.
* A school-morning preparation.
* A careful household repair.
* Looking after the Family Dog in the heat.
* A small village festival preparation.

Use the real activity and feeling to support discovery. Do not label a gentle adjustment as a “crisis,” “shocking rescue,” “secret,” or “survival challenge.”

The opening and episode description should make the formula legible without giving away every small turn.

---

# Common Mistakes

* Treating a formula as a script.
* Reusing the same obstacle with different objects.
* Forgetting to choose a central relationship.
* Adding a major plot to make a routine seem important.
* Selecting a season only after writing the episode.
* Making every solution equally easy.
* Repeating the same final meal or sunset image without emotional reason.
* Forgetting that a community formula still needs a family point of view.
* Using an unverified cultural practice as a decorative detail.

---

# Continuity Rules

When reusing a formula:

* Do not repeat an already resolved lesson as though the character learned nothing.
* Track the condition and history of recurring objects.
* Preserve season and weather continuity across nearby episodes.
* Keep locations, travel routes, and household routines consistent.
* Do not give a character new skills, access, or responsibilities without supporting canon.
* Do not reuse a formula so soon that the family appears trapped in the same day.

Formula repetition should create familiarity, not continuity errors.

This document is the authority for reusable episode-premise patterns.

It does not override specific world, character, location, research, seasonal, or story-rule canon. A formula is invalid when it requires a fact that canon does not support.

# Guiding Principle

Use familiar daily patterns to reveal a new small truth about how this family lives, works, and cares for one another.

# ASMR Storytelling

## Purpose

This manual uses close, natural sound to make Village Stories work, weather,
and care emotionally present.

ASMR is not a separate attraction. It is a storytelling layer that lets a
viewer feel the weight of a vessel, the patience of a repair, the shelter of
rain on tile, or the calm after a family task is complete.

## Philosophy

The village should sound inhabited, useful, and specific to the moment.

A small sound earns attention when it comes from a real action performed by a
known person in an established place. A ladle touching a vessel can show that
Mother has finished cooking. A rope tightening can show Father's careful
repair. Soft footsteps can show Elder Brother waiting for Younger Daughter
instead of leaving her behind.

Sound supports dignity. It must never turn labour, animals, or rural life into
an exotic texture collection.

## Core Principles

1. Record or design sound from a visible, purposeful source.
2. Let one foreground action lead each quiet moment.
3. Preserve the location's natural sound bed.
4. Match the sound's intensity to distance, material, and weather.
5. Use silence as a living pause, never a technical void.
6. Allow dialogue and emotional recognition to remain understandable.
7. Repeat a sound only when repetition reveals rhythm, effort, or change.
8. Never use sound to create danger larger than the story's real stakes.

## Suitable Moments

* Grinding stone, chopping, stirring, pouring, and vessel placement.
* Rain on tile, water in a pot, steam, wood-fire crackle, and leaves moving.
* Bangles during cooking, coir rope pull, basket rustle, and bicycle bell.
* Birds, distant cattle, footsteps, and gentle animal breathing.
* Seeds, grain, fabric, mud, broom fibres, and a wooden door in practical use.

## Rule

ASMR belongs inside purposeful action. Do not isolate sounds as empty spectacle
or use them where they interrupt dialogue, danger awareness, or emotional
clarity.

## Sound Hierarchy

Every scene should identify its levels before mixing.

### Primary sound

The most meaningful action in the frame: stirring, tying, pouring, sweeping,
repairing, feeding, or placing an object down.

### Supporting sound

The sound that establishes the working environment: rain on tile, the kitchen
fire, courtyard birds, field wind, schoolyard distance, or livestock movement.

### Human presence

Breath, cloth movement, footsteps, small laughter, a short spoken exchange,
and natural pauses.

### Emotional accent

A brief sound at a change of understanding: a vessel settling after a careful
pour, the knot pulling tight, a bag being lifted again, or rain easing.

Do not make every level equally loud. The listener needs a clear point of
attention.

## Sound-to-Story Method

For each intended ASMR beat, answer:

1. Who makes the sound?
2. What established object, surface, or animal makes it?
3. Where in the established location does it occur?
4. What action causes it?
5. What does the sound make the viewer understand or feel?
6. What sound was heard just before it?
7. What sound follows when the action changes?

If the beat answers only “it sounds pleasant,” it is not yet a story sound.

## Natural Sound Families

### Kitchen and household work

Use the scrape of grinding, a knife against a board where appropriate, ladle
movement, a vessel set down, firewood placed carefully, cooking fire crackle,
water poured, and bangles moving with Mother's hands.

Keep the sequence practical. A cooking sound should reflect preparation,
heat, waiting, or completion rather than become an unrelated montage.

### Courtyard, thinnai, and garden

Use broom fibres, a door movement, sandals on dry or damp ground, leaf rustle,
water in a vessel, birds, quiet family steps, and the Family Dog settling or
walking nearby.

The Front Thinnai may hold restrained conversation, a visitor's arrival, or
the quiet of waiting. Do not layer it with busy market sounds.

### Livestock and field work

Use feed, rope, hoof movement at a believable distance, a basket shifting,
field wind, plant movement, and work tools only where established object canon
supports them.

Animal sound must remain gentle and responsive to the scene. Do not amplify
breathing, calls, or movement to make a routine feel threatening.

### Village paths and public locations

Use footfall, bicycle bell where canon permits, distant conversation, a Tea
Stall's restrained activity, schoolyard distance, or a Grocery Store's object
handling when it serves the visit.

Public ambience must match time of day and purpose. It should not make a small
village scene sound like a modern or crowded town.

### Weather

Summer may carry dry wind, lower midday activity, insects or birds where
appropriate, and water-conscious household sounds.

Monsoon may carry tile rain, damp footfalls, intermittent drips, wind in
leaves, protected fire sounds, and softened distance.

Post-monsoon may carry clearer outdoor sound, movement through renewed growth,
and repaired paths underfoot.

Harvest may carry grain, baskets, cooperative work, and field rhythm without
turning labour into a percussive performance.

## Character Applications

### Mother

Mother's sound language often comes from patient household skill: stirring,
pouring, folding, preparing, and placing. Pair these sounds with her attentive
listening and measured speech, not exaggerated domestic noise.

### Father

Father's sound language can include inspection, repair, lifting, rope work,
and deliberate footsteps. The rhythm should suggest steadiness, not
machismo or crisis.

### Elder Brother

Use movement, school items, outdoor steps, and practical assistance to show
Elder Brother's energy and care. A quick sound may become quieter when he
notices Younger Daughter needs him.

### Younger Daughter

Use small, curious contact with safe, established objects, gentle footsteps,
quiet questions, and careful participation. Do not make every movement cute or
overly amplified.

### Family Dog

Use the Family Dog's natural presence sparingly: a soft step, collar or coat
movement only if canon supports it, a settling breath, or a gentle response to
family activity. Never give the dog human-like comic sound cues.

## Story Applications

### A task needs patience

Let the work rhythm lead. For a weak cooking fire, begin with rain and a
muted crackle. Let damp wood make the action less certain. When the family
finds a practical solution, allow the fire and vessel sounds to become steady.
The change should be audible before anyone explains success.

### A relationship needs recognition

Reduce the sound bed slightly when a character notices another's need. Hear
the bag shift, footsteps stop, or a small object returned. Use the resumed
shared pace as the resolution.

### A location needs transition

Carry a bridge sound from the end of one space into the next only when the
transition is physically plausible. Rain heard from a kitchen can continue
into a courtyard shot; a school bell should not suddenly appear inside the
family compound without a route and distance cue.

## Pacing and Silence

Slow down for skilled work, weather, and emotional recognition as described in
`07_Pacing_System.md`.

Leave a little room before and after a meaningful sound. Cutting on every
contact makes the scene feel edited for effect instead of observed.

Use near-silence after:

* a character understands another person's worry;
* a repair is tested and holds;
* food is ready after shared effort;
* rain eases and the family can resume;
* an ending image needs the viewer to settle with it.

Near-silence still contains a truthful room tone or environmental bed.

## Decision Framework: Add, Reduce, or Remove?

### Add a close sound when

* the action is visible and meaningful;
* it clarifies material, effort, or completion;
* it reinforces the scene's primary emotion;
* it belongs to the established place and period.

### Reduce a sound when

* dialogue communicates a needed relationship turn;
* the foreground sound is masking a reaction;
* multiple details compete for attention;
* the moment needs calm rather than stimulation.

### Remove a sound when

* it has no visible source;
* it makes ordinary work feel alarming or comic;
* it implies an undocumented modern object or setting;
* it repeats without revealing progress;
* it conflicts with the season, surface, or location.

## Google Flow Considerations

Google Flow visuals do not guarantee usable, consistent sound. Build the sound
plan from the locked visual action rather than assuming generated imagery has
created its own audio truth.

For each visual shot, document:

* the visible source of the primary sound;
* material and condition: wet tile, dry coir, metal vessel, mud, leaf, water;
* camera distance and implied listener distance;
* season, weather, time, and surrounding sound bed;
* character action start and finish;
* dialogue windows and intended quiet pauses.

If a generated shot changes the object, hand action, ground condition, or
weather, revise the sound plan or regenerate the image. Do not keep a sound
that belongs to an earlier version of the visual.

### Sound Continuity Handoff

Between shots, record:

1. foreground action still active, stopped, or completed;
2. weather intensity and direction;
3. door, fire, vessel, rope, and water states;
4. character distance and movement direction;
5. ambient bed entering and leaving;
6. dialogue or silence planned for the cut.

## Common Mistakes

* Using generic birds, rain, or village ambience in every scene.
* Making every hand contact unnaturally loud.
* Layering many pleasant sounds with no hierarchy.
* Continuing a sound after its visible action has ended.
* Adding modern vehicle, device, or urban traffic sound.
* Turning animal sounds into a joke or threat.
* Covering a key expression or line with a loud foreground effect.
* Using thunder, sharp impacts, or sudden volume changes to manufacture stakes.
* Treating silence as an empty track instead of a quiet, located moment.

## Continuity Rules

* Rain sound must match the visible rain and whether a character is indoors or
  outdoors.
* Footsteps must match ground: dry earth, damp courtyard, interior surface, or
  path.
* Vessel, fire, and food sounds must follow the task's visible progress.
* A person or animal cannot sound close if the frame and established geography
  place them far away, unless the perspective is clearly motivated.
* Keep time-of-day ambience and seasonal activity consistent through the scene.

## Canon Authority

When an attractive sound conflicts with the early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu
setting, documented objects, location activity, weather, or character action,
discard it. Sound must describe this village, not a generic idea of one.

## Guiding Principle

Let truthful sound make a visible act of care feel close enough to notice.

# Location Usage

## Purpose

This manual makes every Village Stories location an active part of an episode.

A location is not a backdrop selected for beauty alone.

It supplies a practical task, a social rhythm, sensory evidence, and a limit on
what can reasonably happen next.

Use only locations established in `02_World/Locations`.

This document assigns story use; individual location documents remain the
authority for layout, objects, routes, atmosphere, and local continuity.

---

## Canon Authority

Village Stories is set in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

The established family is Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and
the Family Dog.

Use the documented village locations only.

Do not invent a Rice Mill, a shop, a road, a house, or a landmark to solve an
episode problem.

Do not rename, combine, relocate, or expand an established location without
updating the relevant World canon first.

When this manual conflicts with a location-specific file, the location-specific
file wins.

---

## Guiding Principle

Choose the place where the character would genuinely go to do the next needed
thing.

The most attractive shot is never a sufficient reason to move the story.

---

## Location Philosophy

Ordinary places become meaningful through repeated family use.

The kitchen holds preparation, care, and unspoken conversation.

The courtyard lets work, play, animals, and household life overlap naturally.

The thinnai makes arrivals, departures, waiting, and neighbourly contact
visible.

The field path creates a believable transition between home and work.

Returning to a location should create recognition, not visual repetition.

Change the task, time, weather, relationship, or emotional point of view while
respecting the same physical canon.

---

## Core Principles

1. Place follows purpose.
2. Travel follows geography.
3. Activity follows the time of day.
4. Season changes use, sound, light, and available work.
5. A location should contribute a story pressure or a story resource.
6. Characters use places according to their existing roles and abilities.
7. Repeated places need a fresh emotional or practical angle.
8. Establish a place before relying on its objects or hazards.
9. Show routes and transitions when they matter to understanding.
10. Leave a place in a continuity-consistent state.

---

## Location Tiers

### High-Use Family Spaces

Use these as the series' dependable emotional base:

* Family House
* Kitchen
* Courtyard
* Front Thinnai
* Backyard
* Vegetable Garden
* Livestock Shelter
* Bedroom
* Puja Corner

These locations work well for openings, returns, small discoveries, preparation,
and quiet endings.

Do not treat all household spaces as interchangeable.

Cooking belongs in the Kitchen.

Animal-care preparation and animal response should connect logically with the
Livestock Shelter.

A private rest, waking, or bedtime concern belongs in the Bedroom when the
documented staging supports it.

### Regular Village Spaces

Use these when the story naturally extends beyond the compound:

* Village Neighbourhood
* Village Square
* Tea Stall
* Small Grocery Store
* Banyan Tree and Vinayagar Shrine
* Paddy fields and their established paths
* Primary School
* River Ghat
* Stone Bridge

These locations give an episode community, errand, work, school, or crossing
logic.

They should not be used just to make a domestic story feel larger.

### Skill and Service Spaces

Use the documented specialist locations only when their trade serves the
episode:

* Potter Clay Yard
* Blacksmith Forge
* Carpenter Workshop
* Barber Under the Big Tree
* Village Utilities

The craftsperson's skill must be relevant to the need.

Do not send characters on a decorative tour of village trades.

### Event Space

The Festival Ground is for documented seasonal or community occasions.

It is not a generic open field, playground, market, or gathering shortcut.

## Functional Location Matrix

| Story need | Preferred established setting | Natural story value |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prepare food or solve a household task | Kitchen | process, care, smell, patience |
| Notice family life in motion | Courtyard | shared action, animal presence, play |
| Wait, receive, or say goodbye | Front Thinnai | thresholds and quiet social contact |
| Tend plants or find a small natural change | Backyard or Vegetable Garden | observation and responsibility |
| Care for animals | Livestock Shelter | routine, gentleness, consequence |
| Begin or return from work | Family House, field path, or paddy field | departure and effort |
| Buy one needed item | Small Grocery Store | limited errand and choice |
| Meet community naturally | Tea Stall or Village Square | conversation without spectacle |
| Learn formally | Primary School | effort, listening, friendship |
| Fetch, wash, or observe water responsibly | River Ghat | rhythm, care, restraint |
| Cross between established areas | Stone Bridge | transition and weather evidence |
| Seek a quiet respectful pause | Banyan Tree and Vinayagar Shrine | reflection and community routine |
| Repair or make a needed object | Relevant documented artisan location | skill, material respect |

The matrix suggests a first choice; it does not override World canon.

## Decision Framework

Before assigning a location, answer these questions in order:

1. What does the character need to do now?
2. Which established location normally supports that action?
3. Why must it happen at this time of day?
4. What route connects the prior scene to this place?
5. What seasonal condition affects the journey or task?
6. Who would plausibly be present?
7. What visible detail can carry the emotion?
8. What must be carried, left behind, or remembered on departure?

If any answer requires invented geography, revise the beat.

### Quick Decision Test

Keep a location when it provides at least two of these:

* a necessary activity;
* a believable encounter;
* a useful obstacle;
* a sensory change;
* an emotional contrast;
* a continuity payoff.

Move or remove it when it provides only scenery.

## Time-of-Day Logic

Morning supports waking routines, sweeping, cooking preparation, animal care,
school departure, and early field work.

Midday supports shade, short errands, school rhythm, quieter indoor work, and
restraint in outdoor exertion.

Late afternoon supports returns, garden tending, animal care, neighbourly
contact, and small repairs.

Evening supports cooking completion, family regrouping, the thinnai, the puja
corner, and emotionally quiet resolution.

Night scenes should be used sparingly and only with the established lighting and
domestic safety logic.

Never place a task at a convenient hour if it contradicts local routine.

---

## Route and Transition Rules

Show the beginning and end of a route whenever the trip changes the story.

For a short familiar movement, one bridging action can be enough: a child
leaving the courtyard, feet on the path, then arrival at the needed location.

For a first visit, a weather-affected trip, a carried object, or a delayed
arrival, give the route more screen attention.

Do not cut from the Kitchen directly to the River Ghat as if the spaces touch.

Do not have a character appear at the school, artisan area, or Festival Ground
without an intelligible reason to leave home.

The Family Dog may accompany an outdoor route only when this matches the
location, safety, and activity.

## Character Applications

### Mother

Mother naturally anchors Kitchen, Courtyard, Backyard, Vegetable Garden, and
household-threshold scenes.

Her movement should arise from care, planning, observation, or practical work.

### Father

Father naturally connects home, field paths, paddy fields, repairs, and
purposeful village errands.

Use quiet competence rather than making every outdoor location his domain.

### Elder Brother

Elder Brother fits school routes, Primary School, outdoor tasks, field paths,
and useful sibling assistance.

His presence should not turn him into an adult substitute.

### Younger Daughter

Younger Daughter brings curiosity to the Kitchen, Courtyard, Garden, school
contexts, and safe nearby routes.

Her discoveries must remain plausible for her documented age and supervision.

### Family Dog

The Family Dog belongs naturally in the compound, Courtyard, Front Thinnai,
paths, and beside routine family activity.

Do not use the dog to create dangerous wandering, chase scenes, or convenient
access to distant places.

## Positive Examples

**Kitchen to Courtyard:** Younger Daughter notices that a prepared leaf parcel
is missing from the tray. The answer is not a mystery plot: the Family Dog has
nudged it while following a smell. Mother calmly redirects the child, and the
family prepares another parcel.

**Field path to home:** Father returns later than expected because monsoon mud
has slowed his walk. Elder Brother sees the mud on his feet, brings water, and
understands the delay without a lecture.

**Small Grocery Store:** Mother sends Elder Brother for one documented household
need. He must remember the amount and protect the purchase from light rain on
the established route home.

**Primary School:** Younger Daughter struggles to keep a paper exercise dry on
a rainy departure. The school scene is brief; the story value is the family's
careful preparation and her small confidence on arrival.

---

## Negative Examples

**Wrong:** A problem at home is solved by suddenly visiting an undocumented Rice
Mill.

**Why it fails:** It invents canon and uses a place as a plot device.

**Wrong:** The family appears at the Festival Ground because an episode needs a
wide visual.

**Why it fails:** The ground needs a supported community or seasonal event.

**Wrong:** The children cross a long route alone at night to create suspense.

**Why it fails:** It breaks tone, safety, and practical family logic.

**Wrong:** The Tea Stall becomes a daily gathering place for every character.

**Why it fails:** It weakens home-centred storytelling and makes community
contact feel mechanical.

## Google Flow

Every location prompt must preserve the documented place across shots.

Include:

* exact established location name;
* time of day and season;
* character names and their immediate action;
* relevant fixed spatial features;
* weather and ground condition;
* objects being carried or already placed;
* camera purpose, not merely camera position;
* the emotional beat.

Example prompt logic:

`Early monsoon afternoon, established Family Courtyard, Mother gathers damp
clothes from the line while Younger Daughter holds a small basket; wet ground,
family house and livestock shelter positioned as documented, Family Dog waiting
under cover, quiet practical urgency.`

Do not ask Google Flow to invent an unrecorded doorway, neighbouring building,
vehicle, business, or landscape feature.

Keep the direction of travel consistent across consecutive shots.

---

## Continuity Checklist

Before locking a location beat, verify:

* The place is established in World canon.
* The action belongs there.
* Arrival follows a believable route.
* Time, weather, and season agree.
* Carried objects appear on arrival and disappear only when set down or used.
* Animal position and supervision remain plausible.
* The right family members are present.
* The scene leaves the location ready for the next beat.

---

## Common Mistakes

Using the Courtyard as a substitute for every household room.

Using the Village Square whenever two people need to speak.

Moving characters between locations with no travel logic.

Treating the River Ghat as a leisure setting divorced from practical care.

Sending a character to an artisan without a material need.

Ignoring seasonal changes to paths, shade, water, and work.

Crowding locations with unnamed people simply to create energy.

Repeating the same establishing shot without a new story purpose.

## Final Rule

Every location needs a reason, time of day, route, activity, and continuity
state.

If a place does not deepen the practical action or emotional meaning, stay
where the story already is.

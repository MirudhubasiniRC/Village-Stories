# Character Usage

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories selects, combines, and writes its established characters for episodes.

It prevents two common failures: using the same family member mechanically in every story, and adding people merely to make a scene look busy. Each character must have a truthful reason to be present, a suitable action, and behaviour consistent with their permanent canon.

Use this manual after choosing the episode’s activity, location, season, and gentle conflict. Use it before assigning scenes, dialogue, or Google Flow prompts.

---

## Philosophy

Village Stories is carried by a family whose daily life is shared but not identical.

Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and the Family Dog each notice different things, move through different parts of the home and village, and contribute in different ways. The wider village adds continuity, knowledge, and community when a story naturally reaches beyond the family.

Character use is not a casting puzzle. It is a question of fit:

> Whose existing skill, responsibility, curiosity, relationship, or point of view makes this ordinary moment more meaningful?

If no answer is clear, remove the character rather than inventing a reason.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Specific Canon Comes First

Individual identity, personality, relationships, appearance, clothing, skills, routines, and continuity documents govern every character. This manual guides selection; it cannot override character canon.

### 2. Presence Requires a Reason

A character should be present because they live there, perform the activity, have a relevant need, offer appropriate help, or naturally observe the event.

### 3. Roles Must Match Age and Ability

Children learn through safe, manageable participation. Adults hold adult responsibility. The Family Dog is a cared-for companion, not a worker of impossible intelligence or an action device.

### 4. Relationships Carry Story

Use established relationships to make actions meaningful: Mother and Father cooperate, siblings learn to include one another, adults guide children, and the Dog belongs naturally within family routine.

### 5. No Character Exists to Be Humiliated

Gentle comedy, correction, and small mistakes may reveal personality. They never make a person, child, animal, elder, or neighbour look foolish, unsafe, cruel, or disposable.

### 6. Supporting Characters Stay Distinct

Neighbours, teachers, artisans, and visitors are recurring people with context. Do not use them as interchangeable crowd figures, instant experts, or plot-delivery machines.

## Cast-Selection Framework

### Step 1: Start With the Activity

State the episode’s action without character names.

Examples:

- Preparing a rain-day meal.
- Carrying out a small garden task.
- Getting ready for school.
- Repairing a useful household object.
- Welcoming a neighbour for a practical visit.

### Step 2: Identify the Natural Lead

Choose the person whose ordinary responsibility, skill, or curiosity begins the action.

Mother may lead a kitchen or home-rhythm story. Father may lead a field, animal-care, weather, or repair story. Elder Brother or Younger Daughter may lead when a child’s learning or observation is genuinely central.

### Step 3: Add Only Necessary Partners

Add a second character if they provide help, contrast, instruction, relationship meaning, or a believable point of view.

Do not add both children, both parents, the Dog, and neighbours automatically.

### Step 4: Define Each Person’s Function

For every character, write one brief function:

```text
Mother — notices the timing problem and adjusts the meal.
Father — brings the stored dry fuel.
Elder Brother — safely carries a prepared item under instruction.
Younger Daughter — observes the rain and helps with one small safe task.
Family Dog — settles under shelter once the household is calm.
```

If two people have the same function, combine or remove one.

### Step 5: Check Relationship Logic

Ask whether the interaction belongs to their documented relationship. A parent may guide; a sibling may include or tease gently; a neighbour may share practical knowledge; a teacher belongs in a school context.

### Step 6: Check Absence Logic

An absent recurring character need not be explained unless their absence contradicts the location, time, routine, or an established plan. Do not force the full family into every episode.

## Family Usage

### Mother

Mother is a dependable centre of household rhythm, care, practical knowledge, and attentive observation.

Use Mother naturally for:

- Cooking and food preparation.
- Household organisation.
- Hospitality and visitor care.
- Garden, courtyard, washing, storage, and home-rhythm tasks when supported by her specific canon.
- Gentle guidance, listening, and emotional repair.
- Noticing fatigue, forgotten details, or a child’s small uncertainty.

Mother can lead an episode through action rather than explanation. Her competence should be visible in choices, timing, hands, and attention.

Do not use Mother as:

- An endlessly burdened figure who must be rescued.
- A source of scolding, guilt, or domestic conflict.
- A background worker while others receive all story attention.
- An unrealistic expert outside documented knowledge.

Good application: Mother sees that rain will affect a planned meal and quietly reorganises the preparation with help from the family.

Poor application: Mother becomes angry at everyone for a minor delay and the episode makes her distress the entertainment.

### Father

Father is a steady centre of outdoor work, practical judgement, care for land and animals, repair, and patient guidance.

Use Father naturally for:

- Field, garden, livestock, and compound work.
- Observing weather and seasonal conditions.
- Practical repairs within his established skills.
- Safe outdoor teaching for Elder Brother and Younger Daughter.
- Calm decisions that require patience or experienced observation.
- Quiet companionship with the Family Dog.

Father’s leadership is cooperative, not dominating. Meaningful household decisions remain shared with Mother.

Do not use Father as:

- A loud authority figure.
- A glamorous hero or fearless rescuer.
- An emotionally distant source of tension.
- A comic fool who cannot manage ordinary work.
- A person who performs undocumented modern or specialised tasks.

Good application: Father notices a loose garden support, explains the safe repair, and lets Elder Brother assist with an age-appropriate part.

Poor application: Father takes a needless risk so the family can worry about him.

### Elder Brother

Elder Brother supports stories of school, outdoor learning, growing responsibility, sibling connection, and safe practical participation.

Use Elder Brother naturally for:

- Preparing for or travelling on an established school route.
- Learning from Father through supervised outdoor tasks.
- Helping with manageable household or garden work.
- Remembering, organising, carrying, sorting, or observing at an age-appropriate level.
- Gentle sibling moments with Younger Daughter.
- Quiet confidence after effort or correction.

He may be eager, occasionally distracted, or still learning. Those qualities create believable small conflicts only when adults respond with calm guidance.

Do not use Elder Brother as:

- A substitute adult or provider.
- A reckless child needing rescue.
- A bully or humiliating comedian toward Younger Daughter.
- A child publicly shamed for mistakes.
- A character whose maturity jumps suddenly to serve the plot.

Good application: Elder Brother realises a school item is missing, retraces his earlier practice calmly, and accepts Younger Daughter’s useful memory.

Poor application: He lies, runs away, or is harshly blamed to create drama.

### Younger Daughter

Younger Daughter supports stories of curiosity, sensory discovery, home learning, tenderness, imagination grounded in observation, and safe participation.

Use Younger Daughter naturally for:

- Noticing small changes in plants, weather, animals, food, or household objects.
- Asking simple, sincere questions.
- Helping with small supervised tasks.
- Learning through Mother, Father, Elder Brother, or familiar village adults.
- Offering a child’s perspective on ordinary care.
- Gentle companionship with the Family Dog.

Her curiosity should invite explanation or shared observation, not put her in unsafe situations.

Do not use Younger Daughter as:

- A child carrying adult worry or marital tension.
- A helpless victim, lost child, or danger device.
- A source of prolonged tears, fear, or humiliation.
- A miniature adult with implausible responsibility.
- A magical interpreter of animals or nature.

Good application: Younger Daughter notices the Dog returning to an empty water bowl and asks why; Mother helps her understand and refill it safely.

Poor application: She is left alone with a dangerous task to create urgency.

### Family Dog

The Family Dog is a healthy adult male German Shepherd and a familiar family companion in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu. He belongs in the compound, known paths, outdoor rest, and family transitions when his presence is logical.

Use the Dog naturally for:

- Greeting or waiting near family members.
- Resting near the thinnai, courtyard, or compound work.
- Accompanying safe, familiar movement.
- Showing ordinary needs such as water, food, shade, or calm attention.
- Offering gentle visual rhythm and companionship.
- Helping a character notice a familiar arrival or routine change without replacing human communication.

The Dog’s scale, breed, coat, alert ears, soft family expression, and calm presence must remain consistent with his specific canon.

Do not use the Dog as:

- A guard-dog threat, attack device, or police-style animal.
- A wolf-like fantasy creature or a different breed.
- A lost, injured, starving, or endangered emotional hook.
- A comic chaos machine.
- A character who understands complex verbal plots or solves human problems.

Good application: The Dog waits at the gate while Father completes a nearby expected task, then relaxes when Father returns along the known path.

Poor application: The Dog runs into danger, attacks a stranger, or discovers a mystery.

## Family Combinations

### Mother and Father

Use them for shared household decisions, coordinated work, quiet married-life warmth, weather adaptation, care of children and animals, and mutual respect.

Their relationship is stable. A difference in task preference can be handled with a glance, a short practical exchange, or cooperation; never construct marital drama from it.

### Mother and Elder Brother

Use for school preparation, food knowledge, household skills, gentle correction, and responsibility through routine.

Mother guides without belittling. Elder Brother can help and learn without being made responsible for the whole household.

### Mother and Younger Daughter

Use for home learning, careful observation, food preparation within safe limits, care, questions, and emotional reassurance.

Their scenes should allow Younger Daughter to participate, not merely watch.

### Father and Elder Brother

Use for supervised outdoor learning, repair, garden or field observation, animals, and growing competence.

Father teaches by demonstration and short practical explanation; Elder Brother learns by trying safely.

### Father and Younger Daughter

Use for short safe outdoor tasks, simple explanation, shared curiosity, gentle protection, and delight in her observations.

Do not burden her with physical work beyond her age or place her close to unguarded tools or animals.

### The Siblings

Use for shared discovery, small cooperation, a gentle disagreement resolved through listening, playful support, or one remembering what the other has missed.

Elder Brother’s protectiveness must remain warm rather than controlling. Younger Daughter’s curiosity must be respected rather than mocked.

### Family With the Dog

Use the Dog as part of the lived environment: nearby during work, resting during meals, following a safe route, or receiving routine care.

The Dog should not steal focus from a human story unless animal care itself is the episode’s stated activity.

## Wider Village Usage

### Neighbours

Neighbours deepen stories of mutual help, familiar geography, shared work, hospitality, and seasonal community life.

Use a specific neighbour when:

- Their home, skill, or relationship is relevant.
- Shared labour is natural for the season.
- A brief visit has a practical purpose.
- The family’s interaction can reveal established community trust.

Do not use neighbours as gossip sources, antagonists, vague crowds, or convenient witnesses to every event.

### School Teacher and Headmaster

The Teacher and Headmaster belong primarily to documented school contexts.

Use them for:

- Classroom learning.
- A school task.
- A calm school-day interaction.
- Age-appropriate encouragement or guidance.

Do not move them into household or farm stories unless a documented, credible reason supports it. They are educators, not generic authority figures or disciplinarians.

### Tradespeople and Artisans

Use a craftsperson when a documented skill, workshop, or repair genuinely serves the episode.

The story should respect the craft: establish why the family comes, what needs attention, and what the artisan does. Do not let a trade character perform a magical instant repair or appear only to deliver exposition.

### Tea Stall Owner and Grocery Store Context

Use recurring people connected to the Tea Stall or Small Grocery Store when an errand, exchange, waiting moment, or community observation belongs at that location.

The location must have a route, a reason, and a time of day. Do not use the shop or tea stall as a modern social-media-style gathering space or a source of gossip drama.

### Postman

The postman is an occasional functional visitor, not a default recurring lead.

Use him for a realistic delivery, a brief familiar greeting, or a story about timing and correspondence when period-accurate and canon-supported. Do not turn letters into alarming secrets or melodramatic news.

### Village Elders and Other Community Members

Use elders for respectful local knowledge, memory, guidance, or a believable community presence. Use other villagers only when their documentation and the scene’s purpose support them.

No community member should be reduced to stereotype, comic background, or hardship spectacle.

## Point-of-View Decisions

Choose whose attention guides the episode.

### Adult-Led Point of View

Choose Mother or Father when the core is skilled work, responsibility, planning, hospitality, land, animal care, or a practical household decision.

Children can observe and participate, but the adult’s action should remain the structural centre.

### Child-Led Point of View

Choose Elder Brother or Younger Daughter when the core is a safe question, school preparation, small responsibility, curiosity, or learning.

An adult must remain emotionally available and physically nearby when safety or reassurance is relevant.

### Shared Family Point of View

Use this for cooking, rain adaptation, festival preparation, a visitor, or a task that naturally requires several roles.

Keep one emotional centre even when several people contribute.

### Community Point of View

Use only when a specific community task, location, or recurring person is genuinely central. Return the emotional landing to a recognisable relationship rather than a faceless crowd.

## Character-Conflict Assignment

Match conflicts to the people who can resolve them naturally.

### Kitchen Timing or Ingredient Issue

Mother is usually the suitable lead. Father or the children may provide help only through a logical, safe task.

### Weather, Land, or Garden Adjustment

Father or Mother leads according to the activity. Children may observe or help within safe limits.

### School Item or Small Child Worry

Elder Brother or Younger Daughter may lead. Mother, Father, or the Teacher can support, but the Teacher belongs only in a proper school context.

### Animal Routine Need

An adult or an observing child may lead the noticing. A family member completes or supervises the care, while the Dog remains calm.

### Household Repair

Father or a documented artisan leads. A child assists only with an age-appropriate, safe part.

### Neighbourly Shared Work

The relevant adult or neighbour leads, with family members contributing only when their roles and abilities fit.

Treat these assignments as a decision aid, not a replacement for specific canon.

## Positive and Negative Examples

### Positive: The Missing Seed Packet

**Lead:** Younger Daughter, because she remembers helping with the seeds.

**Support:** Mother, because the task belongs to home and garden care; Elder Brother, because he recalls a likely storage place.

**Action:** The child’s question begins a calm search. Mother does not accuse. Elder Brother contributes a useful memory. The packet is found, and the family plants only what the season and canon allow.

**Why it works:** Each person has a distinct, believable function and the child is supported.

### Negative: Everyone Solves It

Mother, Father, both children, the Dog, a neighbour, the Teacher, and the Postman all appear to search for one small packet.

**Why it fails:** The cast is inflated, roles overlap, and familiar characters become decorative.

### Positive: A Loose Bicycle Chain

**Lead:** Elder Brother on a documented school-route context.

**Support:** Father, if present before departure, teaches a simple safe response or helps arrange an appropriate next step.

**Action:** The concern is brief; Elder Brother listens and participates without attempting an unsafe repair alone.

**Why it works:** It respects age, skill, safety, and Father’s practical guidance.

### Negative: Child Hero Rescue

Elder Brother repairs every part alone in traffic, races to school, and receives public praise.

**Why it fails:** It adds danger, breaks the setting’s calm scale, and makes a child perform adult competence.

### Positive: Neighbourly Rain Help

**Lead:** Father, who sees a neighbour needs help moving a light, appropriate item before rain.

**Support:** Mother approves the household timing; Elder Brother carries a small safe object; Younger Daughter stays with Mother and the Dog in a secure area.

**Why it works:** Community cooperation is specific, children are protected, and roles remain distinct.

## Seasonal Variations

### Summer

Use adults for water, shade, work timing, and animal care. Children may notice heat, carry small safe items, or learn why tasks begin early. The Dog needs shade and water, never heat-risk drama.

### Monsoon

Use family coordination around shelter, damp work, safe routes, indoor tasks, and rain observation. Keep children and Dog supervised; no storm peril or uncontrolled outdoor play.

### Post-Monsoon

Use renewed outdoor learning, repair, garden growth, cleared paths, and neighbourly activity. This season supports child curiosity and Father’s practical teaching.

### Harvest

Use cooperative roles: adults lead labour, children assist within limits, neighbours contribute through documented relationships, and the Dog remains safely adjacent rather than underfoot.

### Festivals

Use preparation, household collaboration, respectful community interaction, and child excitement moderated by adult guidance. Do not treat festival scenes as an excuse for crowded, interchangeable cast shots.

## Google Flow Considerations

Google Flow prompts must preserve identity and relationship continuity shot by shot.

- Name the character’s concrete action and relation: “Mother guides Younger Daughter’s hands while sorting beans,” not “family enjoys togetherness.”
- Preserve age, height, clothing state, physical appearance, and emotional expression across the scene.
- Keep the adult male German Shepherd’s breed, black-and-tan appearance, size, and calm family behaviour consistent.
- Do not place a character in a location, task, or relationship role that contradicts their canon.
- Keep children near appropriate adult supervision whenever tools, animals, roads, water, weather, or outdoor work are involved.
- Maintain who holds each object and where each person stands between shots.
- Use facial expressions sparingly and naturally: listening, small smile, concentration, mild concern, relief.
- Avoid generated villains, threatening strangers, crowd hostility, dramatic confrontations, aggressive animals, or modern devices.

## Common Mistakes

- Using the full family in every scene regardless of story need.
- Making Mother and Father interchangeable instead of using their documented strengths.
- Making Elder Brother an adult helper or Younger Daughter a helpless emotional device.
- Treating the Dog as a guard, danger signal, comedy prop, or substitute narrator.
- Adding a teacher, neighbour, artisan, or postman only to explain the plot.
- Making a side character abruptly cruel, foolish, or unusually important.
- Forgetting that characters need realistic routes, time, and reasons to arrive.
- Writing dialogue that replaces established body language, work, and care.
- Forcing a character to behave against canon to create a conflict.

---

## Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

- Mother and Father as a respectful, cooperative emotional centre.
- Elder Brother as a protected child learning through suitable responsibility.
- Younger Daughter as a protected child whose curiosity is heard and guided.
- The Family Dog as a healthy adult male German Shepherd, calm and cared for.
- Consistent relationships, skills, daily roles, appearance, clothing, and behavioural traits.
- Period-accurate village life, documented locations, plausible travel, and seasonal logic.
- Respectful, distinct treatment of neighbours and village professionals.

Never:

- Use villains, violence, cruelty, humiliation, threatening suspense, or family rupture.
- Make children unsafe, abandoned, mocked, or responsible for adult emotional problems.
- Make parents cold, domineering, careless, or inconsistent merely for plot.
- Turn the Dog into an attack animal, action hero, or endangered hook.
- Add undocumented characters, abilities, jobs, relationships, locations, or modern technology for convenience.

## Canon Authority

This manual is the permanent authority for casting and using characters within Village Stories episodes.

It does not supersede individual character documentation, which remains the final authority on a person or animal’s identity and behaviour. World, culture, location, object, and Episode System documents retain their own specific authority.

---

## Guiding Principle

Use a character because the moment belongs to them.

When each person is present for a truthful reason and responds in their own established way, even a small village task becomes a story only this family and community could tell.

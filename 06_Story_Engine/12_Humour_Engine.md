# Humour Engine

## Purpose

This manual protects the warm, quiet humour of Village Stories.

Humour should make family life feel observed and alive.

It can release small tension, reveal affection, or turn an ordinary inconvenience
into a shared memory.

It must never replace the episode's emotional truth, practical logic, or gentle
family dignity.

---

## Canon Authority

Village Stories is a gentle family series set in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

The permanent family is Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and the
Family Dog.

Humour must use only established characters, animals, objects, routines,
locations, and cultural practices.

Do not create an undocumented Rice Mill, business, person, animal, vehicle, or
location merely to stage a joke.

Do not contradict a character document for a laugh.

World, Character, Culture, Object, and location files remain the source of
truth for what can appear and how it behaves.

---

## Guiding Principle

The joke should leave every character as respected as before it began.

---

## Humour Philosophy

The best Village Stories humour is usually a small recognition:

The Family Dog has noticed food before anyone else.

Elder Brother has approached a task too confidently.

Younger Daughter has interpreted an adult instruction with sincere literalness.

Mother or Father has seen the whole situation and responds with one quiet look.

The laugh comes from affection, timing, and truthful behaviour.

It does not come from loudness, insult, fear, clumsiness, or a character's lack
of intelligence.

Humour is a seasoning.

The practical action and emotional movement remain the meal.

---

## Core Principles

1. Build humour from an existing task.
2. Let each person behave in character.
3. Keep the inconvenience small and safe.
4. Prefer observation to explanation.
5. Use a brief beat, not a long comic routine.
6. Restore the task and dignity promptly.
7. Let adults react with warmth, not mockery.
8. Treat animals as living beings, never props.
9. Keep ritual, labour, poverty, age, and emotion out of ridicule.
10. End the joke before it consumes the story.

---

## The Gentle Humour Loop

Use this sequence:

1. Establish an ordinary intention.
2. Introduce one believable interruption or mismatch.
3. Allow a character to notice it.
4. Give a brief warm reaction.
5. Restore the practical action.
6. Let the story continue with no lingering embarrassment.

Example:

Mother sets out a prepared item in the Kitchen.

The Family Dog sits beside it with unusually serious attention.

Younger Daughter assumes the dog is guarding it; Father quietly notices the
smell drawing the dog's attention.

The item is moved safely, the dog is redirected, and everyone continues.

The dog is not scolded, and no one is made foolish.

---

## Decision Framework

Before keeping a humorous beat, ask:

1. What ordinary action is already happening?
2. Which character's established trait creates the contrast?
3. Is the interruption physically and emotionally safe?
4. Could the audience understand it without explanatory dialogue?
5. Does the moment reveal care, affection, or personality?
6. Can the task resume within one or two beats?
7. Would the character feel respected if present after the laugh?
8. Does the joke fit the location, season, and period?

If the answer depends on ridicule, danger, an invented prop, or a personality
change, reject the beat.

## Approved Humour Sources

### Family Dog Curiosity

The Family Dog may wait expectantly, follow a familiar smell, choose a shade
spot already wanted by someone else, or quietly appear beside a family member at
the exact wrong-but-harmless moment.

Use patient attention and gentle interruption.

Do not use chasing, theft, fear, aggression, illness, or dangerous wandering.

### Domestic Timing

A household action can create a small mismatch:

* a swept area gains one new set of paw prints;
* a carefully folded cloth attracts a sleepy dog seeking shade;
* a child prepares for a task before the needed item is ready;
* two helpers arrive with the same useful object;
* an item is protected so thoroughly that it is briefly hard to retrieve.

The outcome must remain plausible and easy to resolve.

### Sibling Contrast

Elder Brother and Younger Daughter can see a task differently.

He may focus on speed while she notices a detail.

She may take a phrase literally while he expects the usual routine.

The contrast should end in cooperation, not one sibling proving superior.

### Quiet Adult Recognition

Mother or Father can create humour with a pause, softened expression, exchanged
look, or small practical solution.

Their reaction tells the audience the moment is safe.

Do not give parents sarcastic insults, public scolding, or exaggerated anger.

### Object and Routine Mismatch

An established object may be used in an unexpected but sensible temporary way.

The discovery should arise from the task, not from cartoon logic.

Example: a familiar cover is repurposed briefly to protect a needed item from
light rain, and Younger Daughter is pleased by the practical idea.

Check object canon and safety before using any object this way.

### Harmless Animal Routine

Documented calves, chickens, or goats may create a minor practical delay only
when the relevant World canon supports their presence and care.

The family responds patiently.

Never make an animal frightened, chased, mishandled, or the target of laughter.

## Location Applications

### Kitchen

Use humour from timing, scent, careful preparation, small misunderstandings, and
quiet reactions.

Keep fire, hot vessels, tools, and food handling safe and respected.

### Courtyard

Use humour from shared movement, sweeping, drying, sorting, a family member
crossing another's plan, or the Family Dog's familiar presence.

Do not turn the Courtyard into a slapstick stage.

### Front Thinnai

Use humour from waiting, a respectful arrival, a child listening too carefully,
or a dog choosing the most occupied resting spot.

The thinnai also needs quiet social dignity.

### Backyard, Garden, and Livestock Shelter

Use gentle observation of plants, animal routine, and practical work.

Do not make an animal or child fall, get trapped, or cause a destructive mess.

### Village and Specialist Locations

At the Tea Stall, Small Grocery Store, Primary School, artisan locations, River
Ghat, or Village Square, humour must be especially restrained and tied to a
genuine errand or activity.

Do not make a local worker, teacher, neighbour, or customer a comic fool.

### Festival and Sacred Contexts

At the Festival Ground or Banyan Tree and Vinayagar Shrine, use warmth around
family preparation or quiet human timing only when Culture canon supports the
event.

Never mock a ritual, faith practice, offering, elder, or community gathering.

## Character Applications

### Mother

Mother's humour is often a composed observation, a small practical answer, or
the moment she sees what children have not yet seen.

She is never the target of domestic incompetence jokes.

### Father

Father's humour can arise from dry patience, a restrained look, or a humble
admission that a small plan needs adjustment.

He is not a loud comic authority or a figure to be embarrassed for sport.

### Elder Brother

Elder Brother can be mildly overconfident, eager to finish quickly, or caught by
Younger Daughter's accurate observation.

Let him recover with grace and useful action.

### Younger Daughter

Younger Daughter can bring sincere literalness, delighted observation, or a
small surprising insight.

Never frame her innocence as stupidity.

### Family Dog

The Family Dog can be a silent comic partner through timing, alert posture,
patient waiting, or a perfectly placed presence.

The dog must remain physically safe, calm, and true to documented behaviour.

## Positive Examples

### The Swept Courtyard

Elder Brother finishes sweeping and proudly calls Younger Daughter to see the
clear Courtyard. The Family Dog walks across one edge to settle in the cool
shade. Younger Daughter suppresses a laugh; Mother gives Elder Brother a cloth
without comment. He smiles and fixes the small section.

The joke is brief, safe, and leaves Elder Brother competent.

### Two Covers

Rain threatens a prepared school item. Younger Daughter brings one familiar
cover, and Elder Brother brings another at the same moment. They pause, each
convinced their solution is necessary. Father suggests using one for the item
and one for the bag. Their competing care becomes cooperation.

### The Garden Discovery

Younger Daughter carefully points out a new leaf in the Vegetable Garden.
Elder Brother leans in to inspect it and discovers the Family Dog has already
chosen that patch's shade. He steps back, and the dog looks up calmly. The
family adjusts their observation spot.

---

## Negative Examples

**Wrong:** A child drops food or breaks an important object so everyone can
laugh.

**Why it fails:** Waste and embarrassment are not warm humour.

**Wrong:** The Family Dog steals food and is chased around the village.

**Why it fails:** It risks animal safety, breaks location logic, and creates
slapstick.

**Wrong:** Father publicly scolds Elder Brother for misunderstanding a task.

**Why it fails:** Public humiliation is not gentle family comedy.

**Wrong:** A teacher, craftsperson, neighbour, or devotee is portrayed as
ignorant for an easy joke.

**Why it fails:** It disrespects the community and weakens the village world.

**Wrong:** A sacred or festival practice becomes a punchline.

**Why it fails:** Tradition must be treated with care.

---

## Timing and Pacing

Place humour after the viewer understands the task.

A joke before context looks random.

Use one clear setup, one recognition, and one return to action.

Let silence, a glance, or a small pause do work.

Avoid stacking jokes in a short sequence.

After a tender, worried, or reflective beat, use humour only if it gently
releases pressure without cancelling the emotion.

Do not use humour at the exact point a character needs sincere comfort.

---

## Dialogue Rules

Use short, ordinary dialogue.

Good: “He chose the cleanest place.”

Good: “We both came prepared.”

Good: “Leave him his shade.”

Avoid punchlines, sarcasm, catchphrases, mocking imitations, and explanations
of why something is funny.

The reaction is often enough.

---

## Google Flow

Prompts must preserve natural, restrained behaviour.

Include:

* exact established location;
* time, season, and practical task;
* fixed canonical objects and spatial features;
* one small visual mismatch;
* character reactions that are warm and subtle;
* safe animal and child positioning;
* the action that restores the rhythm.

Example prompt logic:

`Late afternoon in the established family Courtyard, Elder Brother has just
finished sweeping a small clear area; the Family Dog quietly settles at its
shaded edge, leaving a few fresh paw prints; Younger Daughter notices with a
gentle smile, Mother offers Elder Brother the cloth, warm natural family humour,
no exaggerated gestures.`

Do not prompt for cartoon expressions, broad pratfalls, chaotic chasing,
frightened animals, invented props, or crowds reacting to a joke.

---

## Continuity Checklist

Before finalising a humorous beat, verify:

* The location and objects are established.
* The task was already needed.
* Every character reacts in character.
* No person or animal is unsafe, demeaned, or mocked.
* The cause and resolution are visually clear.
* The joke suits the season and time of day.
* The practical task resumes.
* The following beat remembers any moved object or changed location state.

---

## Common Mistakes

Adding humour because a scene feels slow rather than because a truthful moment
exists.

Confusing loudness with warmth.

Repeating the same Family Dog interruption in every episode.

Making Elder Brother the default target of jokes.

Using Younger Daughter's curiosity as foolishness.

Allowing a joke to interrupt a sincere emotional resolution.

Creating unsafe animal or child behaviour.

Importing modern comic language, props, or pacing.

Mocking a village worker, neighbour, tradition, or belief.

## Final Rule

After the laugh, restore dignity, practical action, and story rhythm quickly.

If the humour asks the viewer to laugh at someone rather than with the family,
it does not belong in Village Stories.

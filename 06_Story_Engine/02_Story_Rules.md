# Story Rules

## Purpose

This document defines the non-negotiable operating rules for stories set in Village Stories.

It protects the early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu setting, the family’s established behaviour, and the calm emotional promise of the series. Apply these rules during ideation, outlining, drafting, visual planning, and final review.

These rules state boundaries. For the creative reason behind those boundaries, use **01_Story_Philosophy.md**.

---

# Philosophy

Rules are not intended to make stories cautious or repetitive. They make stories trustworthy.

When the audience understands that people, places, seasons, objects, and consequences behave consistently, small events gain emotional weight. A missing tin, a delayed meal, an uncertain school task, or a change in weather can matter because the world around it is dependable.

Every rule serves one larger purpose: preserve dignity, coherence, and warmth.

---

# Core Principles

* Respect established canon before seeking novelty.
* Give every action a practical, period-appropriate reason.
* Keep emotional and practical consequences proportionate.
* Let behaviour reveal care.
* Let work, place, weather, and relationships create story naturally.
* Resolve the emotional question before closing the episode.
* Prefer a smaller truthful event to a larger artificial one.

---

# Detailed Rules

## 1. Period Rule

Village Stories takes place in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

Maintain the material and social reality of that period in every story decision:

* No mobile phones, internet, social media, streaming media, GPS, digital payments, or modern messaging.
* No contemporary slang, online references, viral trends, or present-day celebrity culture.
* No modern appliances, vehicles, packaging, shops, or infrastructure unless a specific world document establishes them.
* Communication, travel, errands, and information must take believable local time and effort.

Do not use a period error to solve a story problem quickly. Rebuild the scene around the available people, paths, tools, memory, and community.

## 2. Canon-First Rule

Specific canon documents are facts, not optional suggestions.

Before using a character, location, object, animal, food, seasonal practice, or route, check its relevant document. Do not move a location, invent an ability, change a relationship, or assign a prop simply because it would make a scene easier.

When no document confirms a detail, choose a modest, broadly plausible option or flag it for canon development. Do not treat uncertainty as permission to invent a major fact.

## 3. Character-Truth Rule

No family member may act out of character to force a joke, conflict, lesson, or shortcut.

Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and Family Dog must retain their established temperaments, relationships, abilities, and limitations.

Growth is permitted when it comes through observation, effort, support, and age-appropriate experience. Sudden personality reversals are not.

## 4. Practical-Causality Rule

Every action must answer a practical question:

* Why is this person doing it now?
* Why is this object available?
* Why does this task matter today?
* Why is this route, location, or helper being used?

Coincidence may begin a small situation, such as a familiar visitor arriving during preparation. Coincidence must not repeatedly solve the story or replace character effort.

## 5. Conflict-Scale Rule

Conflict belongs in the series when it creates a manageable adjustment, not a threat to safety or family trust.

Appropriate conflict includes:

* A small shortage.
* A delayed task.
* A child’s uncertainty.
* A harmless misunderstanding.
* A repair that needs patience.
* Weather changing a plan.
* Limited time before a routine obligation.

Inappropriate conflict includes danger, cruelty, violence, lasting humiliation, villainy, severe family rupture, or fear-driven suspense.

## 6. Dignity Rule

Never ridicule rural life, manual labour, tradition, poverty, age, disability, animals, or emotional restraint.

Humour may arise from affection, timing, a small misunderstanding, or a character’s familiar habit. It must not depend on making someone appear foolish, powerless, or culturally inferior.

## 7. Care-Through-Behaviour Rule

Do not rely on declarations of love, moral speeches, or tearful explanations to prove feeling.

Show feeling through:

* Preparing something.
* Waiting for someone.
* Sharing a burden.
* Repairing before damage worsens.
* Making room for another person.
* Noticing an unspoken need.
* Offering a small second chance.

## 8. Sensory-Truth Rule

Use concrete sensory detail that belongs to the scene: sound of vessels, dust on a path, wet cloth, birds, cooking smoke, a school bell, wind through leaves, or the Family Dog’s movement near the house.

Do not pile up decorative detail without a story purpose. Sensory writing should ground action, mood, or memory.

## 9. Dialogue-Restraint Rule

Dialogue should be brief, natural, and suited to the speaker.

Do not make characters explain information they all already know. Do not use an adult’s speech to deliver a complete lesson. Allow pauses, glances, work, and ambient sound to carry meaning.

## 10. Ending Rule

Every episode must end with emotional resolution.

The practical outcome may remain modest or imperfect, but the audience should understand how the family or community has settled. End on an action, image, sound, or quiet exchange rather than a repeated explanation of the lesson.

# Decision Frameworks

## The Five-Gate Check

Pass an idea through these gates before outlining:

1. **Canon:** Is every required character, place, prop, and practice possible?
2. **Period:** Could this occur without modern technology or assumptions?
3. **Cause:** Does the need arise naturally from daily life?
4. **Scale:** Is the obstacle safe and proportionate?
5. **Heart:** Does the resolution reveal care, belonging, or understanding?

If any answer is no, revise the premise before adding scenes.

## The Escalation Check

When a story feels flat, do not automatically raise danger.

Instead, deepen one of these:

* The practical care required.
* The child’s responsibility.
* The importance of timing.
* The amount of coordination needed.
* The emotional meaning of a familiar object or routine.

Escalation should make ordinary effort more visible, not turn the story into a crisis.

## The Inclusion Check

Before adding a family member, ask:

* What can this person truthfully contribute?
* Does their presence change the emotional texture?
* Would their absence make the scene less honest?

Do not force all five family members into every episode.

# Positive Examples

## Appropriate School Story

Younger Daughter worries about carrying a handmade school item safely during a damp morning. Mother wraps it carefully, Father considers the path conditions, and Elder Brother offers age-appropriate help. The Family Dog stays at home when that is the sensible choice.

The problem is small, period-appropriate, and resolved through ordinary care.

## Appropriate Household Story

Father notices that a household item needs attention before an important routine use. He works patiently while Elder Brother assists imperfectly. Mother adapts the nearby task, and Younger Daughter learns by observing.

The repair carries character and relationship without requiring a major failure.

## Appropriate Humour

The Family Dog repeatedly chooses the spot where the family has just placed a folded cloth. The family responds with gentle amusement and practical redirection. Nobody is mocked, and the dog remains an animal rather than a human comedian.

---

# Negative Examples

Reject or redesign stories that:

* Use a phone call, text message, online lookup, or modern product as a solution.
* Give Father sudden anger or carelessness contrary to his canon.
* Make Mother helpless solely to create rescue drama.
* Make Elder Brother cruel so that he can learn a lesson later.
* Make Younger Daughter unusually worldly, sarcastic, or adult-like for a joke.
* Put the Family Dog in unsafe danger for suspense.
* Create a thief, villain, supernatural threat, or violent confrontation.
* Use a large disaster, severe illness, or traumatic loss as episode entertainment.
* Turn a village custom into exotic decoration without everyday context.
* Resolve a practical problem by an unlikely lucky arrival.

# Story Applications

## At the Idea Stage

Write the practical need, emotional relationship, location, season, and available resources before writing a hook.

## At the Outline Stage

Trace cause and effect from the opening condition to the ending. Each beat should follow from a character’s observation, choice, work, or a believable environmental change.

## At the Scene Stage

Check entrances, exits, props, timing, weather, and location transitions. A beautiful scene that breaks geography or period canon must be revised.

## At the Dialogue Stage

Remove any line that states what an action already communicates. Replace lectures with a choice, gesture, or consequence.

## At Final Review

Ask whether the audience would feel safer, closer, or more attentive to ordinary life after the ending. If the dominant feeling is shock, fear, mockery, or urgency, the episode is outside the series.

# Character Applications

## Mother

Keep Mother capable and attentive. Her care is practical, not passive self-sacrifice or endless worry.

## Father

Keep Father calm, hardworking, observant, and emotionally grounded. He may be tired or uncertain, but he does not become loud or reckless for plot convenience.

## Elder Brother

Allow Elder Brother room for impatience, pride, learning, and growing responsibility. Correct him without humiliating him.

## Younger Daughter

Let Younger Daughter be curious, emotionally direct, and observant. Do not make her responsible for adult burdens.

## Family Dog

Keep the Family Dog safe, animal-like, and connected to routine. The dog may signal, accompany, wait, or respond; it does not understand complex adult plans or perform impossible feats.

# Seasonal Variations

Seasonal conditions may alter the available task, route, clothing, food, work time, and soundscape.

Maintain continuity:

* Rain creates wetness, waiting, protection, and changed movement.
* Heat creates shade, thirst, rest, and careful timing.
* Agricultural periods affect household and field routines.
* Festival preparation adds activity but does not erase ordinary responsibilities.

Never use a seasonal label without showing its practical effect on the day.

---

# Google Flow Considerations

Production titles, descriptions, and searchable labels may use clear terms for the true activity, setting, food, season, or family relationship.

They must not promise danger, mystery, rescue, shocking conflict, modern convenience, or an event absent from the episode. Avoid keyword stuffing and false emotional claims.

Discoverability is useful only when it brings the right audience to an honest story.

---

# Common Mistakes

* Confusing a quiet tone with a rule against obstacles.
* Solving period problems with present-day logic.
* Using an undocumented location as a convenient backdrop.
* Treating continuity checks as work to do after writing.
* Making every story include every family member.
* Over-explaining the moral.
* Adding noise, speed, or shouting to create energy.
* Treating a practical task as filler instead of the story’s engine.
* Ending with a perfect solution when a modest one is truer.

---

# Continuity Rules

Maintain continuity across episodes for:

* Character behaviour and relationship dynamics.
* Location geography, ownership, and ordinary use.
* Available tools, household objects, and transport.
* Seasonal weather and agricultural rhythm.
* Previous episode outcomes when they remain materially relevant.
* The family’s established routines and responsibilities.

An episode may introduce a small new fact only when it does not contradict existing canon and is recorded through the appropriate future canon process.

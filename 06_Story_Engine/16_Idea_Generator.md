# Idea Generator

## Purpose

This manual generates canon-safe episode seeds for Village Stories.

It converts established season, location, family relationship, skill, object,
and small practical need into a premise that can be developed through the
Episode System. It does not authorize new canon.

## Philosophy

The best Village Stories idea is not the most unusual event. It is a familiar
moment made meaningful because Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter,
and the Family Dog experience it with attention and care.

An idea becomes an episode only when it has:

* a place that makes the action necessary;
* a season that changes the condition of the action;
* a character who has a credible reason to care;
* a gentle obstacle;
* practical action that changes the emotional situation;
* a quiet result.

## Core Formula

```text
Season and time
+ Established location and route
+ Character need, skill, or relationship
+ Established object or ordinary task
+ Gentle practical conflict
+ Primary emotion
+ Plausible shared action
= Episode seed
```

The formula is a starting frame, not a checklist of interchangeable nouns.
Every selected element must affect the others.

## Idea Generation Rules

1. Start from documented canon, never from a desired spectacle.
2. Use one main practical need per episode.
3. Select characters because their relationship or skill matters.
4. Let season alter the task, not just the background image.
5. Use only established locations, objects, routes, and village people.
6. Keep the obstacle small enough to resolve through care, skill, patience, or
   cooperation.
7. Let the final emotion arise from the solution instead of naming a lesson.
8. Reject any seed that requires violence, fear, humiliation, modernity,
   villainy, or unexplained magic.

## Inputs: Season and Time

### Summer

Choose needs involving water, shade, early work, cooling food, tired animals,
or waiting for rain.

Useful question: What must the family do differently because the heat has
changed the day?

### Monsoon

Choose needs involving shelter, wet clothes, protected cooking, drainage, mud,
rain play, safer movement, or renewed fields.

Useful question: What ordinary plan has become more careful because everything
is wet?

### Post-Monsoon

Choose needs involving green growth, repaired paths, clearer air, outdoor
work, or grateful observation.

Useful question: What can resume now that the rain has changed the village?

### Harvest

Choose needs involving cooperation, farm work, stored grain, meals, animal
care, or modest practical celebration.

Useful question: Whose small contribution makes shared work go more smoothly?

### Festivals

Use only when existing Culture and Festival Ground canon supports the specific
activity.

Useful question: What preparation or family responsibility is more meaningful
than the public spectacle?

## Inputs: Established Locations

Choose the location because it creates a useful action.

### Family Kitchen

Best for preparation, cooking, feeding, warmth, waiting, and adapting a
household task.

### Courtyard

Best for shared work, arrivals, departures, washing, weather awareness, and
movement between home areas.

### Front Thinnai

Best for waiting, receiving a visitor, quiet conversation, rest, and observing
the village without leaving home.

### Backyard and garden

Best for tending, learning, seasonal observation, small discoveries, and
careful play.

### Livestock Shelter

Best for feeding, cleaning, checking, repair, responsibility, and animal care.

### Nearby field paths and paddy fields

Best for motivated travel, field work, seasonal change, and sibling
cooperation. Establish the route from home or another known location.

### Village locations

Tea Stall, Grocery Store, Village Square, Primary School, playground, River
Ghat, Vinayagar Shrine, artisan work areas, neighbour homes, and Festival
Ground are available only when their established purpose supplies the action.

## Inputs: Family Story Engines

### Mother: care through skill

Generate from cooking, preparation, noticing, household organisation, patient
adaptation, and dignified care.

Prompt questions:

* What small condition has interrupted a useful routine?
* What does Mother notice before anyone else does?
* How can another family member contribute without taking over her role?

### Father: steadiness through practical action

Generate from repair, planning, farm or household responsibility, animal care,
and quiet support.

Prompt questions:

* What needs checking before it becomes a larger inconvenience?
* How does Father's method give another character confidence?
* What object or route can show his care without a speech?

### Elder Brother: responsibility in motion

Generate from school routes, outdoor tasks, useful assistance, learning, and
protective sibling attention.

Prompt questions:

* What does he notice while moving through a familiar routine?
* How can he solve a small problem with effort rather than authority?
* How does he make space for Younger Daughter's participation?

### Younger Daughter: curiosity becoming care

Generate from observation, questions, learning, gentle attempts, garden or
home discovery, and small responsibilities.

Prompt questions:

* What detail does she notice that adults have not yet seen?
* What can she try safely with guidance?
* How does her curiosity reveal care rather than create chaos?

### Family Dog: belonging in daily rhythm

Generate from plausible following, waiting, resting, gentle attention, or
natural participation around the compound, path, or family task.

Prompt questions:

* Would the Family Dog naturally be present here?
* Does the dog's behaviour make the family space feel lived in?
* Does the dog support the moment without solving the human problem?

## Inputs: Ordinary Tasks

Use documented objects and their proper settings.

* Preparing, cooking, serving, or protecting food.
* Carrying, filling, placing, cleaning, or covering vessels.
* Tying, checking, repairing, or storing an established tool or rope.
* Packing, carrying, returning, or checking school belongings.
* Feeding, tending, sheltering, or observing animals.
* Sweeping, drying, sorting, folding, or preparing a home area.
* Walking a known route for school, a household purpose, field work, or a
  documented visit.
* Tending plants, noticing weather, or responding to a seasonal condition.

Do not use a task merely because it produces a pretty image. The task must
matter to a person in the episode.

## Inputs: Gentle Conflict Types

Choose a complication that slows the task without threatening safety or dignity.

### Material condition

Something is damp, dusty, loose, empty, not yet ready, difficult to carry, or
needs cleaning.

### Timing condition

The family must leave, prepare, finish, or return before a plausible routine
changes.

### Weather condition

Rain, heat, wind, mud, shade, or changing light alters a practical plan.

### Attention condition

An important small item is overlooked, a routine detail is missed, or a person
quietly needs help.

### Skill condition

A task needs patient practice, a second pair of hands, or an established
person's guidance.

### Relationship condition

Two family members have different but compatible priorities and must notice
each other. This is not an argument-heavy conflict.

## Inputs: Primary Emotions

Choose one main emotion and one supporting emotion.

* Patience + relief
* Curiosity + care
* Sibling warmth + responsibility
* Quiet pride + gratitude
* Concern + reassurance
* Belonging + calm
* Hope + shared effort
* Playfulness + tenderness

If the desired emotion needs a shocking event to work, choose a smaller,
truer emotional pair.

## Decision Framework: Does the Seed Have Story?

Answer in order.

1. Can the viewer see the need in the opening image?
2. Does the selected location make the task natural?
3. Does the season change a real condition of the task?
4. Does each character have a documented reason to act this way?
5. Is the obstacle modest but meaningful to the people involved?
6. Is the response practical before it is emotional?
7. Does the solution reveal relationship, skill, or care?
8. Can the ending be shown without a spoken moral?

If any answer is no, rebuild the seed rather than adding plot.

## Decision Framework: Keep, Combine, or Reject

### Keep

Keep a seed when its elements naturally depend on each other.

Example: Monsoon + Family Kitchen + Mother needs to finish a meal + damp
firewood + patience = the family adapts a real household task together.

### Combine

Combine only adjacent, related actions.

Example: a school-route item return can combine with an established Grocery
Store visit only if the route, timing, and purpose make both actions natural.
It cannot become a random tour of village locations.

### Reject

Reject a seed when:

* it requires an undocumented location, object, event, or visitor;
* the season has no practical effect;
* characters could be replaced by strangers with no loss of meaning;
* the problem becomes dangerous, humiliating, or melodramatic;
* the ending depends on coincidence, a lecture, or a new rule of the world.

## Example Combinations

### Monsoon kitchen patience

Monsoon morning + Family Kitchen + Mother needs to finish a meal + damp
firewood + patience and shared care = Mother adjusts the cooking work while
Father and the children contribute in small, plausible ways; steady steam
marks the quiet result.

### School-route sibling warmth

School morning + village path + Elder Brother notices Younger Daughter's
forgotten school item + a short return delays their usual pace + sibling warmth
and responsibility = the siblings solve a small practical problem and resume
their route together.

### Livestock shelter quiet pride

Harvest season + Livestock Shelter + Father checks a rope during animal care +
a hungry calf interrupts the repair rhythm + quiet pride and cooperation =
Father completes the check with a useful contribution from the family.

### Thinnai hope

Ordinary day + Front Thinnai + an established postman brings a letter + the
family waits to understand its news + hope and togetherness = the family
receives and shares the moment without making the letter's contents an
unearned dramatic twist.

### Summer garden care

Summer early morning + Backyard and garden + Younger Daughter notices a thirsty
plant area + water must be used carefully + curiosity and care = guided family
attention turns her observation into a small responsibility.

### Post-monsoon path repair

Post-monsoon afternoon + nearby field path + Father needs to inspect a repaired
route + soft ground changes how the family walks + reassurance and gratitude =
the family moves carefully, observes the improvement, and returns before dusk.

### Courtyard shared preparation

Festival preparation supported by canon + Courtyard + Mother and Elder Brother
need to organise an established household task + a sudden weather change
requires moving items under cover + cooperation and calm = the family protects
the work without turning the event into spectacle.

## Seasonal Variation Method

To create multiple episodes from one task, change the condition, not the core
identity of the village.

For example, a family water task may become:

* summer: conserving effort and seeking shade;
* monsoon: keeping water and footing manageable;
* post-monsoon: using a repaired path or renewed garden;
* harvest: fitting the task around cooperative work.

Verify each variation against established object and location canon. Do not
invent a new activity simply to complete the pattern.

## Relationship Variation Method

Keep the practical need stable and change who notices it first.

* Mother notices a household condition; Father provides practical support.
* Father notices a repair need; Younger Daughter observes and learns.
* Elder Brother notices a sibling problem; Younger Daughter participates in
  the solution.
* The whole family notices the Family Dog's natural response to a routine,
  then continues the human task.

This creates variety while preserving the family's roles.

## Google Flow Considerations

An idea is not ready for Google Flow until it can be expressed as consistent
visible beats.

For every seed, identify:

1. opening location, time, weather, and visual clue;
2. every character's starting position and reason to be present;
3. established object state before the problem;
4. action that makes the conflict visible;
5. route and transition between locations;
6. practical solution and altered object state;
7. final image, light, sound, and emotional residue.

Do not generate images to discover the story. Develop the canon-safe beat
first, then prompt each beat with locked character, location, object, season,
and continuity details.

## Continuity Handoff

When an accepted seed moves into the Episode System, hand off:

* one-sentence premise;
* season, time, weather, and selected location;
* route and travel logic;
* character roles and relationship focus;
* established objects and their starting conditions;
* gentle conflict and practical resolution;
* primary emotion and quiet ending image;
* canon files that must be checked during drafting and generation.

If a later beat sheet changes any of these, re-evaluate the seed rather than
assuming the original approval still applies.

## Common Mistakes

* Beginning with “something exciting happens” instead of an ordinary need.
* Selecting a location for scenery rather than activity.
* Adding every family member without a reason.
* Treating the Family Dog as a plot device.
* Naming a season while leaving task, surface, and behaviour unchanged.
* Making a small issue feel dramatic through fear or shame.
* Generating a list of disconnected images instead of a practical sequence.
* Using an attractive Google Flow frame to justify broken canon.
* Repeating the same cooking, rain, or school premise without changing the
  relationship, condition, or emotional discovery.

## Canon Authority

The Idea Generator may recombine only what canon already establishes. Specific
world, character, object, culture, location, and continuity documents always
overrule a generated seed. Reject or rebuild any seed that conflicts with them.

## Guiding Principle

Generate stories by looking more closely at what the family already does, not
by making the village behave like somewhere else.

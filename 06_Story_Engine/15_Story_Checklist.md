# Story Checklist

## Purpose

This is the mandatory production gate for every Village Stories episode.

Use it when selecting an idea, finishing an episode brief, approving a beat
sheet, preparing Google Flow shots, and reviewing the assembled episode. It
prevents an appealing moment from entering production with broken canon,
unclear emotion, or unearned resolution.

## Philosophy

A checklist does not make a story mechanical. It protects the quiet details
that make a simple story believable.

An episode succeeds when the family behaves like itself, the village behaves
like a place with seasons and routes, practical work has consequence, and the
viewer can feel the resolution without being instructed what to feel.

## How to Use This Manual

Mark each item:

* **Pass** — present and consistent.
* **Revise** — can be corrected without changing the premise.
* **Stop** — premise or production plan conflicts with canon or series rules.
* **Verify** — needs confirmation from the named source document before work
  continues.

Do not convert a Stop into a Pass by adding explanation to the episode. Change
the premise, action, object, location, or season instead.

## Gate 1: Episode Promise

Before drafting, confirm:

- [ ] The episode asks what ordinary moment becomes meaningful for these
  particular people.
- [ ] The emotional outcome is warmth, calm, curiosity, gratitude, hope, or a
  similarly grounded feeling.
- [ ] The practical action is clear enough to carry the episode.
- [ ] The conflict is gentle, human, and proportionate to the promise.
- [ ] The resolution comes from attention, skill, cooperation, patience, or a
  plausible change in circumstance.
- [ ] The premise does not need villainy, danger, humiliation, spectacle, or
  forced suffering to become interesting.

## Gate 2: Canon and Period

- [ ] The setting remains early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.
- [ ] Every featured family member is Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger
  Daughter, or Family Dog, used according to documented character canon.
- [ ] Any wider village character has a documented identity and reason to be
  present.
- [ ] Objects, food, clothing, transport, tools, and language behaviour are
  period-correct.
- [ ] No modern internet, device, transport, consumer culture, or social-media
  behaviour has entered the scene.
- [ ] No undocumented house, business, route, tool, animal, or festival
  activity has been invented for convenience.
- [ ] The story contains no miracle or magic that changes the world rules.

**Stop if:** an episode works only by adding an unestablished element. Search
the existing canon for a valid equivalent or choose another premise.

## Gate 3: Character Truth

### Mother

- [ ] Mother's action reflects her documented care, skill, boundaries, and
  relationships.
- [ ] She is not made passive, helpless, or unrealistically expressive only to
  create plot.

### Father

- [ ] Father's work, decisions, and emotional responses remain consistent with
  his documented identity.
- [ ] He is not turned into a heroic rescuer or a detached authority figure for
  dramatic effect.

### Elder Brother

- [ ] Elder Brother's school, outdoor, practical, or sibling role fits the
  episode.
- [ ] His protective behaviour is helpful, not controlling or cartoonishly
  perfect.

### Younger Daughter

- [ ] Younger Daughter has agency through curiosity, observation, learning, or
  care.
- [ ] She is not only a device for adult lessons or repeated reaction shots.

### Family Dog

- [ ] The Family Dog appears only where its movement and behaviour are
  plausible.
- [ ] The dog supports home, path, or family presence without becoming a comic
  prop or magical problem solver.

### Relationships

- [ ] The episode's most important emotional turn comes from a known
  relationship.
- [ ] Help is offered in a way that respects the other character's dignity.
- [ ] Dialogue and behaviour do not contradict documented family dynamics.

## Gate 4: Place, Route, and Time

- [ ] The selected location is established and has a story-specific purpose.
- [ ] The location suits the action, cast, and time of day.
- [ ] Each location change has a plausible route and enough elapsed time.
- [ ] Characters do not teleport between the Family Kitchen, Courtyard, Front
  Thinnai, garden, Livestock Shelter, path, field, school, or village location.
- [ ] The episode does not use a location solely because it looks attractive.
- [ ] Public locations such as the Tea Stall, Grocery Store, Primary School,
  River Ghat, Vinayagar Shrine, artisan areas, and Festival Ground have a
  documented reason for the visit.
- [ ] The ending location gives the final emotion room to settle.

## Gate 5: Season and Weather

- [ ] The season is named in the brief.
- [ ] The time of day, light, weather, clothing, surfaces, and activity agree.
- [ ] Summer changes water, shade, timing, food, animal care, and work pace.
- [ ] Monsoon changes footing, firewood, rain cover, drainage, travel, and
  outdoor work.
- [ ] Post-monsoon changes growth, paths, outdoor work, and air.
- [ ] Harvest changes work, cooperation, stored grain, and meals.
- [ ] Festival material is used only when established culture canon supports it.
- [ ] Weather creates a practical condition, not an interchangeable colour
  treatment.

## Gate 6: Need, Conflict, and Action

- [ ] The opening shows a sensory, spatial, or practical clue before the
  problem is explained.
- [ ] The need is understandable in one sentence.
- [ ] The conflict complicates work; it does not become a crisis.
- [ ] The characters attempt a practical response before the solution.
- [ ] The obstacle has a visible cause.
- [ ] The solution uses established skills, objects, locations, or cooperation.
- [ ] The solution does not arrive through coincidence or an out-of-character
  rescue.
- [ ] The finished action changes the emotional state of the scene.

## Gate 7: Structure and Pacing

- [ ] The episode follows the core rhythm: sensory opening, need, preparation,
  patient work, obstacle or discovery, shared solution, family feeling, and
  quiet ending.
- [ ] The opening grounds place, weather, and mood before the story turn.
- [ ] Practical work is allowed enough time to be legible and satisfying.
- [ ] Repetitive travel and preparation are compressed only after geography is
  established.
- [ ] The emotional recognition has a pause before the ending.
- [ ] The ending is a lived result: a finished task, shared food, a look, a
  settled animal, easing rain, or another canon-safe image.

## Gate 8: Visual Storytelling

- [ ] Every scene has a primary visual purpose.
- [ ] Need, effort, and emotional turn are shown before dialogue explains them.
- [ ] Each close-up has a meaningful object, hand action, or facial change.
- [ ] Each wide shot establishes geography, weather, or activity.
- [ ] Object state is visible before and after it changes.
- [ ] Seasonal details affect surfaces, light, movement, and task.
- [ ] The Family Dog's placement remains plausible from shot to shot.
- [ ] No shot is included only as generic scenic beauty.

## Gate 9: ASMR and Sound

- [ ] Each close sound has a visible, purposeful source.
- [ ] The primary sound supports the action and emotion.
- [ ] Ambient sound matches the location, time, weather, and season.
- [ ] Dialogue remains intelligible where it carries necessary information.
- [ ] Silence or low room tone supports recognition, concentration, or the
  ending.
- [ ] No sound implies modernity, an undocumented object, urban scale, threat,
  or comic exaggeration.
- [ ] Sound state follows visual state: wetness, fire, vessel, footsteps,
  animal distance, and weather all agree.

## Gate 10: Google Flow Production

- [ ] Each prompt names the exact established location and relevant area.
- [ ] Each prompt carries time, season, weather, light, and ground condition.
- [ ] Each character prompt preserves documented appearance, clothing, age,
  posture, and relationship.
- [ ] Each featured object is the established object in the correct visible
  condition.
- [ ] Camera distance and action are specified for a story reason.
- [ ] Screen direction, character position, carries, and exits are logged.
- [ ] Generated frames do not alter house geometry, costume, object design,
  animal markings, or environmental facts.
- [ ] The next prompt accounts for what changed in the previous frame.
- [ ] Sound planning is revised if a final generated shot changes the visible
  source action.

## Gate 11: Continuity Review

Review the whole episode without stopping after all individual scenes pass.

- [ ] Light moves plausibly from morning, afternoon, or dusk.
- [ ] Rain, mud, drying, smoke, food, and repair progress evolve consistently.
- [ ] A carried item stays with its carrier until shown otherwise.
- [ ] Clothing condition reflects the weather and work already performed.
- [ ] Routes and travel duration remain believable.
- [ ] Character emotional state develops rather than resets between scenes.
- [ ] The location and object state at the ending pays off what the opening
  established.
- [ ] The episode does not contradict the prior episode's documented handoff.

## Gate 12: Emotional Resolution

- [ ] The ending resolves the episode's specific need, not merely the plot
  mechanics.
- [ ] The family or community connection is visible in action, look, space, or
  shared routine.
- [ ] The ending does not force a spoken lesson.
- [ ] The episode leaves dignity intact for every character.
- [ ] The last image is quiet, earned, and memorable.

## Decision Framework

### If a check fails

1. Identify whether the failure is canon, logic, character, production, or
   emotional clarity.
2. Consult the controlling document.
3. Revise the earliest story beat that caused the conflict.
4. Re-run all later gates affected by that revision.
5. Do not patch over a foundational failure with additional dialogue or a
   decorative shot.

### Minimum approval standard

An episode is ready to produce only when every relevant item passes or has a
documented canon-safe reason for non-applicability. A visually attractive
episode with a Stop item is not ready.

## Common Mistakes

* Checking period detail but not character motivation.
* Treating weather as background after naming a season.
* Calling an obstacle “gentle” while it creates fear, shame, or danger.
* Letting a solution appear before the work has been understood.
* Forgetting route logic between attractive locations.
* Approving individual Google Flow shots without reviewing the sequence.
* Adding sound after picture without checking visible sources.
* Ending immediately after the solution instead of allowing emotional residue.

## Canon Authority

If this checklist conflicts with a more specific character, location, object,
season, culture, or episode continuity document, the specific canon controls.
Mark the checklist item Verify, correct the plan, and preserve the canon.

## Guiding Principle

Do not ask whether an episode is merely complete. Ask whether every visible and
audible choice lets this particular family earn a small, truthful feeling.

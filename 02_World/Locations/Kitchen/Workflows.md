# Kitchen Workflows

## Purpose

This document defines the permanent cooking workflows followed within the Family Kitchen.

It establishes the natural sequence of activities, movement between work areas, interaction with traditional tools, and the rhythm of everyday cooking.

Rather than describing individual recipes, this document defines how cooking is performed regardless of the dish being prepared.

---

# Workflow Philosophy

Traditional cooking follows a deliberate rhythm.

Every action prepares for the next.

Nothing is rushed.

Nothing is random.

The Kitchen has developed over many years so that movement is efficient, comfortable, and natural.

Every meal should reflect patience rather than speed.

---

# Universal Cooking Sequence

Every meal follows the same overall process.

```text
Ingredients

↓

Washing

↓

Sorting

↓

Preparation

↓

Grinding / Pounding

↓

Cooking

↓

Seasoning

↓

Serving

↓

Cleaning

↓

Storage
```

No cooking sequence should skip these stages unless appropriate for a very simple recipe.

---

# Ingredient Arrival

Ingredients may arrive from:

* Vegetable Garden
* Water Well
* Rear Veranda
* Village Market
* Neighbours
* Paddy Fields
* Coconut Grove

Fresh ingredients always enter the Kitchen through the Rear Veranda.

---

# Washing Workflow

Location:

Preparation Area

Typical activities:

* Washing vegetables.
* Cleaning greens.
* Washing meat.
* Rinsing rice.
* Washing lentils.
* Filling cooking pots with fresh Well water.

Only clean ingredients move to the next stage.

---

# Sorting Workflow

Ingredients are sorted before preparation.

Examples:

* Removing damaged leaves.
* Separating ripe vegetables.
* Cleaning grains.
* Removing stones from rice.
* Picking curry leaves.
* Selecting spices.

Sorting is calm and unhurried.

---

# Preparation Workflow

Location:

Preparation Area beneath the Garden Window.

Typical activities:

* Peeling onions.
* Chopping vegetables.
* Cleaning fish.
* Cutting meat.
* Breaking coconuts.
* Preparing banana leaves.

Natural daylight always assists this stage.

---

# Grinding Workflow

Location:

Traditional Grinding Area.

Tools:

* Ammikkal.
* Aatukkal.
* Ural & Ulakkai.

Used for:

* Masala paste.
* Coconut paste.
* Chutneys.
* Batter.
* Fresh spice mixtures.

Grinding should feel rhythmic and unhurried.

---

# Cooking Workflow

Location:

Raised Clay Stove.

Typical sequence:

Prepare fire

↓

Heat cooking vessel

↓

Tempering

↓

Add aromatics

↓

Add main ingredients

↓

Cook slowly

↓

Adjust seasoning

↓

Final simmer

↓

Remove from stove

The fire should be adjusted according to the cooking stage.

---

# Serving Workflow

After cooking:

* Food transferred carefully.
* Serving vessels selected.
* Banana leaves prepared when appropriate.
* Dishes carried through the Courtyard.
* Family meal begins.

The Kitchen naturally connects to family gathering spaces.

---

# Cleaning Workflow

Immediately after cooking:

* Remaining food stored.
* Cooking vessels washed.
* Stove cleaned.
* Ash removed if necessary.
* Preparation area wiped.
* Tools dried.
* Items returned to permanent storage.

The Kitchen should always return to an organized state.

---

# Morning Workflow

Typical sequence:

* Open Kitchen.
* Collect fresh water.
* Prepare firewood.
* Light stove.
* Make tea or coffee.
* Prepare breakfast.
* Pack food if required.
* Clean Kitchen.

Morning feels fresh, calm, and purposeful.

---

# Afternoon Workflow

Typical activities:

* Preparing lunch.
* Grinding fresh masala.
* Washing vegetables from the garden.
* Drying ingredients.
* Cleaning utensils.
* Quiet conversation while cooking.

Afternoon cooking is usually slower than morning.

---

# Evening Workflow

Typical sequence:

* Relight stove.
* Prepare dinner.
* Reheat food if needed.
* Boil milk.
* Make herbal drinks.
* Extinguish fire naturally.

Evening cooking feels warm and relaxed.

---

# Festival Workflow

Festival cooking expands the normal workflow.

Additional activities include:

* Larger quantities.
* Multiple burners in use.
* Extra family members helping.
* More traditional sweets.
* Banana leaf preparation.
* Decorative serving vessels.
* Continuous cooking throughout the day.

Despite the increased activity, the Kitchen should remain organized.

---

# Children's Participation

Children may naturally assist by:

* Washing curry leaves.
* Carrying vegetables.
* Bringing water.
* Handing utensils.
* Watching recipes.
* Grinding simple ingredients under supervision.
* Learning family traditions.

Children should never handle dangerous cooking tasks without adult guidance.

---

# Father's Participation

Father occasionally assists by:

* Bringing firewood.
* Harvesting vegetables.
* Carrying heavy water pots.
* Cleaning coconuts.
* Preparing ingredients from the farm.

His movements complement the cooking process without replacing Mother's primary role.

---

# Seasonal Variations

## Summer

* Earlier cooking.
* Increased water use.
* More fresh vegetables.
* Dry ingredients preserved.

---

## Monsoon

* More steaming dishes.
* Herbal preparations.
* Rain visible through the Garden Window.
* Slower fire management.

---

## Harvest Season

* Fresh grains.
* Larger meals.
* Guests visiting.
* Community cooking.

---

# Environmental Integration

Throughout every workflow, the Kitchen remains connected to nature.

Background moments may include:

* Rain beginning outside.
* Birds landing near the Garden Window.
* Butterflies passing.
* Chickens crossing the yard.
* The Family Dog waiting patiently.
* Breeze moving the window shutters.

These moments should enrich the scene without interrupting the cooking process.

---

# Camera Flow

A typical cooking sequence naturally progresses through:

* Ingredients arriving.
* Preparation.
* Grinding.
* Fire being lit.
* Cooking.
* Steam rising.
* Serving.
* Family meal.

Camera movement should follow the workflow rather than jumping between unrelated activities.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Logical cooking order.
* Traditional methods.
* Efficient movement.
* Clean workspace.
* Natural pacing.
* Respect for established work zones.

Never:

* Skip preparation stages without reason.
* Teleport ingredients or utensils.
* Leave tools scattered.
* Ignore cleaning after cooking.

# Guiding Principle

Cooking in the Family Kitchen is never just the act of preparing food.

It is a quiet ritual that connects the family to their home, their land, and one another.

Every meal should feel handcrafted, patient, and deeply rooted in the traditions of village life.

# Kitchen Camera Language

## Purpose

This document defines the permanent cinematic language used to present the Family Kitchen.

Rather than describing the Kitchen itself, this document establishes how it should be viewed through the camera.

Every image prompt, storyboard, animation, and cinematic sequence should follow these principles to maintain a consistent visual identity throughout the Village Stories series.

---

# Philosophy

The camera should behave like a quiet observer.

It does not rush.

It does not seek dramatic action.

Instead, it patiently watches everyday life unfold.

The audience should feel as though they are sitting quietly inside the Kitchen, experiencing traditional cooking alongside the family.

---

# Visual Identity

Every Kitchen scene should communicate:

* Warmth
* Simplicity
* Patience
* Authenticity
* Comfort
* Natural beauty

The camera should emphasize the rhythm of cooking rather than spectacle.

---

# Signature Compositions

The following compositions form the visual identity of the Kitchen.

## Garden Window Composition

The Large Garden Window should frequently appear within the frame.

Possible background elements:

* Vegetable Garden
* Water Well
* Family Dog
* Chickens
* Butterflies
* Rain
* Banana plants
* Coconut trees
* Children playing
* Father returning home

The outdoor world should quietly support the scene.

---

## Clay Stove Composition

Frame:

* Cook
* Raised Clay Stove
* Fire
* Cooking vessel
* Steam

These elements should appear together whenever possible.

---

## Preparation Composition

Show:

* Preparation table
* Traditional tools
* Hands working
* Garden Window
* Fresh ingredients

Natural daylight should dominate these scenes.

---

## Grinding Composition

Emphasize:

* Ammikkal
* Aatukkal
* Rhythmic hand movement
* Fresh ingredients
* Stone textures

The action should feel slow and deliberate.

---

## Serving Composition

Frame:

* Freshly prepared food
* Traditional serving vessels
* Banana leaves when appropriate
* Journey toward the Courtyard

The audience should anticipate the family meal.

---

# Establishing Shots

Before close-up cooking begins, briefly establish:

* Entire Kitchen
* Garden Window
* Clay Stove
* Natural lighting
* Weather

This reinforces location continuity.

---

# Camera Height

Preferred camera height:

* Standing eye level.
* Slightly below eye level.
* Table height during food preparation.

Avoid:

* Extreme overhead views.
* Unrealistically low angles.
* Action-camera perspectives.

The audience should feel physically present in the room.

---

# Camera Distance

Use a balance of:

Wide shots

For:

* Room layout.
* Character movement.
* Environmental storytelling.

Medium shots

For:

* Cooking process.
* Conversations.
* Traditional tools.

Close-ups

For:

* Hands.
* Ingredients.
* Steam.
* Fire.
* Food texture.
* Water droplets.

Extreme close-ups should be used sparingly for emphasis.

---

# Camera Movement

Movement should remain gentle.

Preferred:

* Slow push-in.
* Slow pull-back.
* Gentle pan.
* Subtle tracking.
* Static observation.

Avoid:

* Fast zooms.
* Handheld shake.
* Rapid movement.
* Dramatic rotations.

The Kitchen should feel calm.

---

# Focus

Focus naturally shifts between:

* Cook.
* Food.
* Traditional tools.
* Fire.
* Steam.
* Garden Window.

Background elements should enrich the scene without distracting from the primary activity.

---

# Lighting Priority

Always prioritize:

1. Natural daylight.
2. Firelight.
3. Oil lamp.
4. Moonlight.

Artificial lighting should never dominate.

---

# Weather Integration

Whenever appropriate, include visible weather.

Examples:

Summer

* Bright sunlight.
* Warm colours.

Monsoon

* Rain outside.
* Wet leaves.
* Mist.
* Steam.

Post-Monsoon

* Fresh greenery.
* Clear sky.
* Pleasant daylight.

Weather should become part of the visual storytelling.

---

# Environmental Framing

Allow environmental elements to enter the frame naturally.

Examples:

* Curtain moving.
* Butterfly passing.
* Chicken crossing.
* Dog waiting.
* Steam drifting.
* Leaves moving.
* Rain falling.

These moments should feel spontaneous.

---

# Character Blocking

Characters should move naturally between established work zones.

Typical movement:

Rear Veranda

↓

Preparation Area

↓

Traditional Tools

↓

Raised Clay Stove

↓

Serving

↓

Courtyard

Avoid unnecessary walking.

Every movement should have purpose.

---

# ASMR Composition

Frequently emphasize:

* Steam.
* Bubbling curry.
* Firewood.
* Grinding stone.
* Water pouring.
* Fresh vegetables.
* Wooden ladles.
* Brass reflections.

These details strengthen the series' ASMR identity.

---

# Visual Continuity

Maintain recurring visual anchors:

* Large Garden Window.
* Raised Clay Stove.
* Garden view.
* Traditional tools.
* Brass vessels.
* Clay pots.
* Red oxide floor.
* White walls.

These recurring elements help viewers instantly recognize the Kitchen.

---

# Emotional Framing

Camera choices should reinforce emotion.

Comfort

* Warm fire.
* Gentle steam.
* Soft light.

Illness

* Quiet room.
* Herbal cooking.
* Calm movement.

Festival

* Wider framing.
* More activity.
* Rich colours.

Rain

* Window.
* Steam.
* Fire.
* Cool outdoor contrast.

Emotion should emerge naturally through composition.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Calm pacing.
* Natural framing.
* Visible connection to nature.
* Traditional compositions.
* Consistent camera height.
* Logical movement.

Never:

* Use exaggerated cinematic effects.
* Create unnecessary visual clutter.
* Ignore the established Kitchen layout.
* Block important architectural features.

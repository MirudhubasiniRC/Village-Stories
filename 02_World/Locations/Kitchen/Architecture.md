# Kitchen Architecture

## Purpose

This document defines the permanent physical architecture of the Family Kitchen.

It specifies the room's construction, dimensions, orientation, windows, doors, ventilation, circulation, and permanent work zones.

It does **not** define cooking equipment, tools, storage systems, or workflows.

Those topics are documented in their respective Kitchen files.

---

# Identity

The Kitchen is a traditional 1990s South Indian village cooking space built entirely around natural ventilation, daylight, and efficient movement.

Its architecture reflects generations of practical use rather than decorative design.

Every structural element exists to improve comfort, airflow, safety, or cooking efficiency.

The room should always feel:

* Spacious
* Airy
* Naturally illuminated
* Practical
* Warm
* Connected to the outdoors

---

# Architectural Style

Maintain throughout the series:

* White lime-plastered walls.
* Thick fired-brick masonry.
* Red oxide flooring.
* High sloping Mangalore tiled roof.
* Exposed wooden rafters.
* Heavy timber roof beams.
* Deep roof overhangs.
* Natural cross ventilation.
* Traditional handcrafted construction.

The architecture should always reflect a rural South Indian farmhouse from the 1990s.

---

# Orientation

The Kitchen permanently follows the world's fixed compass orientation.

East

* Courtyard entrance.
* Soft morning light.

West

* Rear Veranda entrance.
* Direct access to backyard activities.

North

* Cooking wall.
* Ventilation window.

South

* Large Garden Window.
* Preparation workspace.
* Natural daylight.

This orientation must never change.

---

# Approximate Dimensions

The Kitchen should comfortably accommodate two or three people cooking together.

Approximate size:

* Width: 15–16 feet
* Length: 17–18 feet
* Ceiling height: 11–12 feet

These dimensions maintain visual consistency rather than architectural precision.

---

# Ceiling

Maintain:

* Open ceiling.
* Visible timber rafters.
* Sloping roof.
* No false ceiling.
* No ceiling fan.
* No decorative panels.

The open ceiling helps the room feel spacious and improves ventilation.

---

# Flooring

The floor permanently consists of:

* Red oxide finish.
* Smooth hand-polished surface.
* Slight natural wear.
* Easy-to-clean finish.

The floor should always appear clean but naturally lived-in.

Never introduce:

* Ceramic tiles.
* Marble.
* Granite flooring.
* Vinyl.
* Concrete polish.

---

# Walls

Maintain:

* Thick masonry construction.
* White lime plaster.
* Soft matte finish.
* Slight natural texture.
* Smoke-darkened upper sections near the cooking wall.

Walls should never appear freshly painted unless required by a specific story.

---

# Doors

## East Door

Connects directly to the Courtyard.

Purpose:

* Family circulation.
* Serving meals.
* Natural ventilation.

---

## West Door

Connects directly to the Rear Veranda.

Purpose:

* Bringing vegetables inside.
* Carrying water.
* Backyard access.
* Additional airflow.

Both doors remain open during most daytime scenes.

---

# Windows

## Large Garden Window

Permanent position:

South wall.

Purpose:

* Natural lighting.
* Cross ventilation.
* Visual connection to nature.
* Signature composition for the series.

Maintain:

* Wide horizontal proportions.
* Thick wooden frame.
* Double wooden shutters.
* No glass.
* No metal grills.
* Low sill height.

The shutters are usually fully open during the day.

---

## Cook-Side Garden Window

Permanent position:

On the **north cooking wall, immediately beside the Raised Clay Stove**.

Purpose:

* Direct garden light beside the cook.
* Strong airflow and smoke release.
* Signature stove-and-open-window composition.
* Visual connection to dense Backyard greenery while cooking.

Maintain:

* **Large rectangular open wooden window** — not a tiny ventilation slot.
* Sill begins near stove/slab height and opening reaches close to the beam line.
* Thick dark-wood frame.
* Double wooden shutters opened fully outward during daytime.
* No glass.
* No metal grills.
* Dense banana, palm, curry-leaf, and garden greens filling the exterior view.

This window is permanently **next to the clay stove** and is a principal visual
identity of the Kitchen. In stove compositions, both stove and open green window
should normally be readable.

---

# Natural Ventilation

Airflow permanently follows:

Garden Window

↓

Across the Kitchen

↓

Cooking Area

↓

Cook-Side Garden Window beside stove

↓

Roof Ventilation

Fresh air should always circulate naturally.

The Kitchen should never appear stuffy.

---

# Roof Ventilation

Small openings beneath the roof tiles provide continuous airflow.

Purpose:

* Release warm air.
* Reduce smoke accumulation.
* Keep the Kitchen comfortable.

These openings are part of the traditional roof construction and should remain subtle.

---

# Spatial Organization

The Kitchen is divided into permanent architectural zones.

* Cooking Wall (North)
* Preparation Wall (South)
* East Circulation Zone
* West Circulation Zone
* Central Working Area

These zones remain fixed regardless of the cooking activity.

---

# Circulation

The room is designed to support uninterrupted movement.

Typical path:

Courtyard

↓

Preparation Area

↓

Cooking Area

↓

Rear Veranda

The central floor area should always remain mostly open.

No permanent furniture should obstruct movement.

---

# Relationship With Nature

The Kitchen should never feel isolated.

The architecture intentionally connects indoor cooking with outdoor life.

From inside the Kitchen, it should be possible to naturally experience:

* Changing sunlight.
* Rain.
* Wind.
* Birdsong.
* Butterflies.
* Family Dog.
* Chickens.
* Vegetable Garden.
* Water Well.
* Seasonal changes.

Nature becomes part of the room itself.

---

# Environmental Behaviour

Morning

* Warm eastern light.
* Fresh airflow.
* Active birdlife.

Afternoon

* Bright southern light.
* Soft shadows.
* Gentle breeze.

Evening

* Golden light entering through the Garden Window.
* Cooler airflow.

Monsoon

* Rain visible outside.
* Moist breeze.
* Soft diffused light.

The architecture should respond naturally to changing weather.

---

# Camera Anchors

The room permanently supports these architectural compositions:

* Looking toward the Garden Window.
* Looking from the Courtyard into the Kitchen.
* Looking from the Rear Veranda inward.
* Wide view showing the complete workspace.
* Low-angle views emphasizing the sloping roof.
* Framing characters through open doors.
* Window-framed outdoor scenery.

These architectural viewpoints should remain consistent across episodes.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Fixed orientation.
* Two permanent doors.
* Large Garden Window.
* North Ventilation Window.
* Red oxide floor.
* White lime walls.
* Timber roof.
* High ceiling.
* Natural ventilation.
* Open central floor.

Never:

* Add modern windows.
* Install glass panes.
* Add ceiling fans.
* Change door positions.
* Change room proportions.
* Rotate the room.
* Introduce modern finishes.

# Guiding Principle

The Kitchen's architecture should quietly support the family's way of life.

It provides light without lamps, fresh air without machines, and constant visual connection to the surrounding land, making every meal feel inseparable from the home, the garden, and the village.

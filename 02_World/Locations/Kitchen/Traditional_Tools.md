# Traditional Cooking Tools

## Purpose

This document defines the permanent traditional cooking tools used within the Family Kitchen.

It establishes where each tool belongs, how it is used during meal preparation, and how it contributes to the Kitchen's overall workflow.

This document does **not** define the physical specifications of each tool. Those are maintained separately in the **Objects** folder.

Physical object authority:

```text
04_Objects/Kitchen_Tools/
```

---

# Philosophy

The Family Kitchen depends on handcrafted traditional tools rather than modern appliances.

Every tool has evolved over generations because it performs its task efficiently and reliably.

Each tool has:

* A permanent storage location.
* A dedicated purpose.
* A natural place within the cooking workflow.
* Traditional methods of use.

Nothing should feel improvised.

---

# Permanent Tool Set

The Kitchen permanently contains:

* Ammikkal (Flat Grinding Stone)
* Aatukkal (Round Grinding Stone)
* Aruvamanai (Traditional Vegetable Cutter)
* Ural & Ulakkai (Mortar & Pestle)
* Coconut Scraper
* Muram (Winnowing Tray)
* Bamboo Baskets
* Wooden Ladles
* Iron Tongs
* Stone Rolling Pin
* Traditional Measuring Cups
* Clay and Brass Serving Ladles

These tools remain part of the Kitchen throughout the series.

---

# Ammikkal

## Purpose

Grinding:

* Coconut
* Ginger
* Garlic
* Green chilli
* Curry masala
* Chutneys

## Permanent Location

South preparation wall beneath the Large Garden Window.

Natural daylight illuminates the grinding surface.

While grinding, the cook remains visually connected to the Vegetable Garden outside.

---

## Typical Workflow

Ingredients

↓

Preparation Area

↓

Ammikkal

↓

Clay Stove

The Ammikkal is one of the most frequently used tools.

---

# Aatukkal

## Purpose

Grinding:

* Idli batter
* Dosa batter
* Spice pastes
* Wet mixtures
* Festival preparations

---

## Permanent Location

Beside the Ammikkal.

The two grinding stones should always appear together.

---

## Usage

The Aatukkal is primarily used for larger quantities requiring extended grinding.

Its use should communicate patience and traditional craftsmanship.

---

# Aruvamanai

## Purpose

Traditional floor vegetable cutting.

Used for:

* Drumsticks
* Pumpkin
* Greens
* Raw banana
* Coconut pieces
* Meat preparation when appropriate

---

## Permanent Location

Stored beneath the preparation table.

Brought out only when needed.

Returned immediately after cleaning.

---

# Ural & Ulakkai

## Purpose

Pounding:

* Pepper
* Garlic
* Dry spices
* Rice flour
* Medicinal herbs

---

## Permanent Location

Near the Preparation Area but away from the main walking path.

Its weight means it remains mostly stationary.

---

# Coconut Scraper

## Purpose

Fresh coconut preparation.

Used for:

* Coconut chutney
* Coconut milk
* Vegetable dishes
* Chicken curry
* Sweets

---

## Permanent Location

Stored on a dedicated hook beneath the preparation counter.

Used beside the Garden Window where natural light improves visibility.

---

# Muram (Winnowing Tray)

## Purpose

Cleaning and sorting:

* Rice
* Lentils
* Grains
* Chillies
* Seeds

---

## Permanent Location

Hung vertically on the wall when not in use.

Several sizes may exist.

---

# Bamboo Baskets

## Purpose

Temporary storage.

Used for:

* Vegetables
* Fruits
* Firewood
* Fresh herbs
* Banana leaves

---

## Permanent Location

Stacked neatly beneath the preparation table.

---

# Wooden Ladles

## Purpose

Cooking.

Used for:

* Stirring curries
* Mixing rice
* Tempering
* Serving

---

## Permanent Location

Stored upright in a clay holder beside the stove.

---

# Iron Tongs

## Purpose

Handling:

* Hot lids
* Burning firewood
* Charcoal
* Clay pots

---

## Permanent Location

Hung beside the Raised Clay Stove.

---

# Stone Rolling Pin

## Purpose

Traditional preparation of selected dishes requiring flattening or rolling.

Used occasionally rather than daily.

---

## Permanent Location

Stored on the lower preparation shelf.

---

# Measuring Cups

Traditional measurements use:

* Brass cups.
* Wooden scoops.
* Coconut shell measures.

Modern measuring spoons should never appear.

---

# Tool Workflow

Every meal naturally follows:

Ingredients

↓

Preparation Table

↓

Traditional Tool

↓

Raised Clay Stove

↓

Serving

↓

Cleaning

↓

Return to Permanent Storage

No tool should remain randomly placed after cooking.

---

# Daily Maintenance

After each cooking session:

* Wash tools.
* Dry thoroughly.
* Return to permanent location.
* Stone tools wiped clean.
* Wooden tools air dried.
* Brass tools polished regularly.

Every tool should appear well cared for.

---

# Relationship With Nature

Many traditional tools are intentionally positioned near the Large Garden Window.

Natural daylight improves precision while preparing food.

The cook may naturally observe:

* Rain
* Butterflies
* Chickens
* The Family Dog
* Vegetable Garden
* Father returning from work
* Children playing

The outdoors quietly accompanies traditional cooking.

# Audio Identity

Characteristic sounds include:

* Stone grinding.
* Rhythmic pounding.
* Vegetable chopping.
* Coconut scraping.
* Wooden ladle stirring.
* Bamboo basket movement.
* Brass utensils gently touching.

These sounds form an important part of the Kitchen's ASMR atmosphere.

---

# Camera Anchors

Useful recurring compositions include:

* Hands grinding on the Ammikkal.
* Batter rotating inside the Aatukkal.
* Coconut being scraped.
* Vegetables cut with the Aruvamanai.
* Pepper being pounded.
* Muram gently tossing rice.
* Garden visible through the window behind the tools.

These activities should frequently appear as quiet transitional moments between cooking stages.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Permanent tool locations.
* Traditional materials.
* Natural workflow.
* Clean appearance.
* Hand-operated methods.

Never:

* Replace traditional tools with electric appliances.
* Store tools randomly.
* Introduce plastic kitchen gadgets.
* Change established working positions.

# Guiding Principle

Traditional tools are extensions of the cook's hands.

Their quiet rhythm—grinding stone against stone, coconut scraping, grains being cleaned, spices being pounded—should make every meal feel handcrafted, patient, and deeply rooted in South Indian village life.

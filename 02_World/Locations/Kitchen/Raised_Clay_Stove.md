# Raised Clay Stove (Viragu Aduppu)

## Purpose

The Raised Clay Stove is the primary cooking system of the Family Kitchen.

It serves as the permanent heat source for preparing every daily meal, festive feast, herbal remedy, and comforting dish featured throughout the Village Stories series.

The stove is the emotional centre of the Kitchen and one of the defining visual symbols of the family's home.

---

# Identity

The stove is a permanently built, wood-fired clay cooking platform.

It was handcrafted by local masons many years before the events of the series and has been carefully maintained by the family ever since.

Although decades old, it remains strong, reliable, and well cared for.

Its appearance should reflect years of respectful use rather than neglect.

---

# Permanent Location

The stove is permanently built against the **north wall** of the Kitchen.

It must never be moved.

A **large open wooden Cook-Side Garden Window** is permanently positioned
**immediately beside the stove on the same north cooking wall**. The stove and
window form one recognizable composition:

```text
NORTH COOKING WALL

┌───────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────┐
│ RAISED CLAY STOVE     │ LARGE OPEN WOOD GARDEN WINDOW   │
│ waist height          │ shutters open · dense greens    │
│ three clay openings   │ sill near stove/slab height     │
└───────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────┘
```

Never place a blank wall, tiny vent, or closed modern window beside the stove.

Its position supports:

* Efficient cooking.
* Safe movement.
* Natural smoke ventilation.
* Easy access to nearby cookware.
* Direct garden light and a lush green view beside the cook.

---

# Construction

Maintain:

* Fired brick internal structure.
* Thick clay outer finish.
* Smooth rounded edges.
* Heat-resistant plaster coating.
* Slight smoke staining around burner openings.
* Handcrafted appearance.
* Raised working height.
* Stable construction.

No concrete or modern materials should ever be visible.

# Working Height

The stove is built at approximately waist height.

Purpose:

* Comfortable cooking posture.
* Reduced physical strain.
* Easier stirring of heavy clay pots.
* Safe handling of boiling liquids.

The cook should never need to squat while using the stove.

---

# Three Burner System

The stove permanently contains three burners.

---

## Burner One

### Large Burner

Purpose:

* Rice.
* Large curries.
* Festival cooking.
* Boiling water.
* Feeding many guests.

Produces the strongest heat.

---

## Burner Two

### Medium Burner

Purpose:

* Chicken curry.
* Fish curry.
* Sambar.
* Vegetable dishes.
* Everyday family meals.

This is the most frequently used burner.

---

## Burner Three

### Small Burner

Purpose:

* Tea.
* Coffee.
* Milk.
* Rasam.
* Tempering spices.
* Reheating food.

Provides gentle, controlled heat.

---

# Firebox

The stove contains one shared front-loading firebox.

Maintain:

* Wide opening.
* Clay-lined interior.
* Brick reinforcement.
* Easy firewood access.
* Easy ash removal.

The firebox should always remain visible during cooking.

---

# Firewood Feeding

Cooking always follows this sequence.

Firewood Rack

↓

Daily Firewood Basket

↓

Firebox

↓

Burning Fire

↓

Ash Collection

Firewood should always be inserted from the front.

Never show wood entering from above or behind.

---

# Fire Behaviour

Maintain:

* Steady orange flames.
* Glowing embers.
* Gentle flickering.
* Natural crackling.
* Occasional sparks contained within the firebox.
* Heat shimmer above active burners.

Fire intensity changes according to the cooking process.

It should never appear exaggerated.

---

# Heat Zones

The burners naturally provide different cooking temperatures.

Large Burner

Highest heat.

Medium Burner

Balanced everyday cooking.

Small Burner

Slow simmering.

Cooking should take advantage of these differences whenever appropriate.

---

# Smoke Behaviour

Smoke rises naturally from:

* Firebox.
* Burner openings.
* Cooking vessels.

It exits through:

* Roof ventilation.
* North ventilation window.
* Garden window when airflow changes.

Smoke should remain soft, light, and realistic.

The Kitchen should never become filled with dense smoke.

---

# Steam Behaviour

Steam rises from cooking vessels depending on the dish.

Maintain:

* Gentle movement.
* Soft diffusion.
* Natural lighting interaction.
* Visible against darker backgrounds.
* More prominent during cool or rainy weather.

Steam is an important visual element of the series.

---

# Ash Collection

Ash gradually accumulates beneath the firebox.

Maintain:

* Fine grey ash.
* Small charcoal pieces.
* Daily removal.
* Clean surrounding area.

Collected ash may later be reused in the Vegetable Garden.

---

# Cooking Position

The primary cook stands facing the stove.

Natural movement includes:

* Stirring.
* Lifting lids.
* Feeding firewood.
* Adjusting pots.
* Tasting food.
* Moving between preparation area and stove.

These movements should always appear relaxed and practiced.

---

# Typical Cookware

Frequently used:

* Clay pots.
* Brass pots.
* Bronze vessels.
* Iron kadai.
* Tempering pan.
* Dosa tawa.
* Wooden ladles.

Each vessel should suit the recipe being prepared.

---

# Daily Maintenance

Morning

* Remove old ash.
* Clean burner openings.
* Prepare fresh firewood.

After Each Meal

* Wipe cooking surface.
* Return cookware.
* Sweep away loose ash.

Night

* Fire dies naturally.
* Stove left clean.
* Ready for the next morning.

The stove should always appear cared for.

---

# Environmental Behaviour

During rain:

* Steam becomes more visible.
* Warm stove contrasts with cool weather.
* Firelight reflects softly on clay pots.

During summer:

* Heat shimmer increases.
* Fire burns more efficiently.
* Less visible steam.

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* North wall location.
* Three burners.
* Single front firebox.
* Clay construction.
* Wood-fired operation.
* Raised working height.
* Clean appearance.

Never:

* Replace with a gas stove.
* Add modern burners.
* Move the stove.
* Change burner positions.
* Use unrealistic flames.
* Fill the Kitchen with excessive smoke.

# Tea Stall

## Purpose

The Tea Stall is the village's everyday refreshment and conversation landmark within the Village Stories universe.

It is where farmers pause before fields, neighbours exchange news, travellers rest briefly, and quiet mornings begin with steaming tea or filter coffee.

Every return to this location should immediately feel familiar, warm, and rooted in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.

---

# Philosophy

The Tea Stall is not a modern cafe.

It is a humble roadside stall that grew from habit, hospitality, and daily need.

People do not rush here.

They sit, sip, talk, watch the road, and continue their day.

The atmosphere should always feel lived-in, sociable, and gently busy without becoming loud or commercial.

---

# Location

The Tea Stall is permanently located in the **North-East** of the village, near the approach toward the Village Entrance and Bus Stop corridor.

It connects naturally to:

* Main Village Path
* Village Entrance area
* Bus Stop
* Routes toward Village Square
* Routes used by farmers heading to fields later in the morning

Father and other farmers may stop here on certain mornings.

Elder Brother may pass it on school-related walks with adults or older village boys when story-appropriate.

It should never be relocated.

---

# Identity

The Tea Stall belongs to early-1990s village life.

Maintain:

* Simple roadside structure
* Practical shade
* Traditional tea-making
* Wooden or simple built-in seating
* Familiar regular customers
* No franchise branding
* No glass-front cafe design
* No modern espresso machines

It should feel like a place the audience could smell before they see.

# Structure

Maintain:

* Simple timber or brick-and-lime support
* Corrugated sheet, country tile, or thatch-supported shade roof appropriate to humble village stalls
* Open front
* Compact counter height for serving
* Packed earth or worn cement-patch floor under the stall only if already traditional and modest
* Weathered wood benches
* Low wooden or metal stools
* Small storage shelf or crate stack behind the counter

Avoid:

* Modern cafe counters
* Neon signboards
* Plastic canopy branding
* Stainless steel fast-food aesthetic
* Glazed display refrigerators as a dominant feature

A small painted wooden board with simple Tamil lettering for the stall name is acceptable if kept humble and hand-painted.

---

# Tea Making Area

The heart of the stall.

Maintain:

* Traditional kettle or metal tea vessel
* Small stove setup suitable for 1990s roadside tea (kerosene or wood/charcoal practical village method)
* Steel or glass tumblers
* Milk vessel
* Sugar tin
* Tea leaf tin
* Strainer
* Cloth for wiping
* Water storage vessel

Filter coffee may also be prepared for customers who prefer it, remaining traditional rather than cafe-style.

Steam, clinking glasses, and the rhythm of pouring should become signature atmosphere.

---

# Typical Items Sold

Common early-1990s offerings:

* Hot tea
* Filter coffee
* Simple biscuits
* Banana or seasonal fruit when available
* Occasional bajji / vadai / bonda during busy mornings or evenings
* Plain bun or local bakery bread pieces when stocked
* Soft peppermints or simple sweets in glass jars

Never introduce:

* Packaged multinational soft-drink walls as a visual identity
* Modern packaged gourmet snacks as default
* Branded cafe menus

---

# Seating Culture

Customers typically:

* Sit on benches facing the path
* Stand briefly at the counter
* Rest one foot on a stool while talking
* Share newspapers when available
* Watch buses, bicycles, and bullock carts pass

Conversation is as important as the drink.

---

# Daily Rhythm

## Early Morning

* Fire or stove lighting
* First kettle
* Farmers and early walkers
* Soft mist or cool air
* Quiet talk about fields and weather

## Mid-Morning

* Steady customers
* School-path passersby
* Brief rest stops
* Biscuit and tea combinations

## Afternoon

* Slower pace
* Shade seekers
* Occasional travellers
* Owner resting between customers

## Evening

* Returning workers
* Stronger social energy
* Hot tea after labour
* News of the day
* Soft lamp or stall light before closing

## Night

* Closed or nearly closed
* Shutters / cloth cover
* Quiet path nearby

---

# People Who Belong Here

Recurring presence may include:

* Tea Stall Owner
* Farmers
* Father on selected mornings
* Elders
* Postman briefly
* Bus travellers near the Bus Stop
* Neighbour men exchanging news
* Occasional women buying tea to carry home in a vessel when story-appropriate

Elder Brother and Younger Daughter do not make this a primary hangout.

Children may pass by or receive a rare biscuit treat with an adult.

---

# Atmosphere

The Tea Stall should always feel:

* Warm
* Sociable
* Humble
* Fragrant with tea and milk
* Slightly smoky at times
* Familiar
* Peaceful even when busy

It is a pause in the village day.

---

# Sounds

Typical sounds include:

* Kettle simmering
* Tea pouring into glasses
* Glass tumblers clinking
* Soft spoon stirring
* Bench wood creaking
* Bicycle bells on the path
* Low male conversation
* Occasional laughter
* Distant bus horn near Bus Stop
* Birds in nearby trees
* Morning rooster far away

The stall should never sound like a loud city canteen.

---

# Lighting

Morning

* Soft cool light
* Steam visible in cooler air
* Gentle shade under the roof

Midday

* Deep stall shade
* Bright path beyond
* High contrast between interior shade and sunny road

Evening

* Warm golden side light
* Soft stall lamp or lantern practical to early 1990s
* Long shadows on packed earth

Night closing

* Dim residual light
* Quiet path

---

# Seasonal Behaviour

## Summer

* Extra demand for tea and shade
* Heavier midday lull
* Dust on path edge
* Customers linger under roof

## Monsoon

* Roof becomes critical shelter
* Wet earth smell
* Customers crowd under cover briefly
* Steam more visible
* Careful walking on muddy path edge

## Post-Monsoon / Harvest

* Busier farmer talk
* More evening visitors after field work
* Conversations about yield and weather

# Front Thinnai

## Purpose

The Front Thinnai is the public face of the Family House.

It is neither completely inside nor completely outside.

It forms the transition between the village and the family's private life.

Almost every visitor first enters this space before stepping into the house.

Throughout the series, it should become one of the most recognizable and emotionally familiar locations.

# 90s Lived-In Lock (approved 2026-07-18)

The Front Thinnai is the first authenticity test viewers apply. If it looks like
a hotel porch or clean villa verandah, comments will call the world “modern /
fake” even when characters are correct.

**Always:**

- Packed-earth floor with natural wear — bare feet, occasional leaves, grain
  scraps; chickens may peck.
- Dark wooden pillars with visible age, not lacquered furniture-showroom finish
  (**2–3 pillars** on a **small** house front).
- Slightly weathered cream / white lime plaster behind the porch.
- Lived-in props: **kayiru kattil**, matkas, broom, woven mat / striped cushions,
  umbrella when story needs it, small clutter.
- Roof eaves that feel thick and protective; runoff curtains in rain.
- Open visual connection to garden and village — low wooden gate only.
- Overall: **small simple thatch homestead**, earthen walls — not cream tiled
  cottage or big estate (CANON-2026-007 / 008).

**Never:**

- Tile plaza / ceramic thinnai floor.
- Deep hotel corridor verandah.
- Long mansion colonnade or oversized façade.
- Mangalore tile roof on this house (neighbours OK).
- Bright cream villa walls.
- Empty perfect porch with zero household objects.
- Steel railing, glass, aluminium, LED.
- Compound wall closing the front.
- Resort / picket-cottage landscaping instead of working homestead yard.

# Identity

The Front Thinnai is more than a veranda.

It is the family's outdoor living room.

People sit here to welcome guests, drink evening tea, shell peas, repair tools, tell stories, watch children play, and simply observe village life.

Unlike the Courtyard, which belongs mainly to the family, the Front Thinnai belongs to both the family and the village.

It should always feel welcoming.

---

# Location

The Front Thinnai sits immediately west of the Front Garden.

It is permanently attached to the front of the Family House beneath the main roof.

It faces east toward the Front Garden and the Main Village Path, receiving the first sunlight of the day.

```text
                     EAST · Front Garden
          (mango · banana · palms · flowers · low open gate)
                       │
        ┌────────────────────────────────────┐  ← WIDE shallow roof
        │  Pillar    Pillar    Pillar          │     (short depth ~1–1.5 m)
        │         packed earth floor           │
        └────────────────────────────────────┘
              │   door   │ win │ win │
                     │
                 Courtyard · WEST
```

**Proportion lock:** Thinnai is **WIDE across the façade** and **SHALLOW front-to-back**
— like a broad entry porch, **not** a long deep corridor. Match
`Family_House_Establishing_Canon_Design_Reference.png`.

The Front Thinnai is open on the eastern side and protected by the extended roof.

It remains visually connected to both the garden and the village.

# Architecture

Maintain — match architecture plantings and pillars; **ground = packed earth**
(see Canon Visual Reference note if PNG still shows tiles):

* Raised platform approximately 2½ feet above the Front Garden earth level.
* Granite or simple plastered entrance steps leading up when visible from garden approach.
* **Packed earth / natural worn land** as the primary floor under the open thinnai —
  scuffed village earth where bare feet, the Family Dog, and **free-range chickens**
  peck and roam. Occasional dry leaves, grain scraps, and worn paths are normal.
* **Not** terracotta floor-tile grids, ceramic tile plazas, or polished paving.
* **Gabled / sloping thatch eaves** with thick exposed beams/poles for shade and
  rain protection — roof spans **wide** across façade; platform stays **shallow**.
* **Dark wooden support pillars** (2–3) across the **width** of this small porch —
  not a deep row pushing far into the garden; not a long mansion colonnade.
* Warm **earthen ochre mud-plaster** walls — weathered / lived-in, never bright
  cream villa paint.
* Centered **carved two-panel traditional wooden entrance door** on the rear
  wall — may stand slightly ajar, revealing warm blurred interior family presence.
* Open eastern side visually connected to garden and village.

The proportions should feel **simple and compact** — a small village porch, not
a generous estate verandah.

# Permanent Furniture

These items permanently belong here:

* **One kayiru kattil** (rope-woven charpai / *kayathu kattil*) along the porch
  — sit, nap, evening rest; viral-village rest language (CANON-2026-005).
* Long wooden bench.
* One traditional wooden reclining chair.
* **Soft striped floor cushions** in red, blue, and yellow tones for resting.
* One woven grass mat (rolled when unused).
* Large clay drinking-water pot on a wooden stand.
* Brass tumbler placed nearby.
* Coconut broom leaning against a pillar.
* Pair of wooden footwear stands near the entrance.
* Small umbrella stand made from bamboo.

Furniture should remain simple and practical. The kayiru kattil should be
readable in most thinnai establishing shots unless a story beat clears the porch.

---

# Permanent Planting And Decorative Elements

Maintain — match approved reference:

* **Bougainvillea vine** with **magenta flowers** climbing the left wall.
* **Clay pots** with lush green **ferns** at porch edges.
* **Clay pots** with bright orange **marigolds**.
* Fresh kolam at the entrance whenever appropriate.
* Brass hanging oil lamp.
* Small framed family calendar appropriate to the 1990s.
* Wooden ceiling rafters visible beneath the roof.
* Traditional woven bamboo basket occasionally hanging from a beam.

Decoration should always remain understated and lived-in.

# Relationship With The Village

The Front Thinnai naturally connects the family with village life.

Typical interactions include:

* Neighbours stopping for a short conversation.
* Postman delivering letters.
* Vegetable vendor calling from the road.
* Flower seller visiting.
* Children inviting friends to play.
* Travellers asking for directions.
* Guests waiting before entering.

The family should never appear isolated from the village.

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* East-facing orientation.
* Raised platform.
* Granite or simple plastered entrance steps when visible from approach.
* **Packed earth / natural worn land** as the primary thinnai floor (chickens
  may peck and roam).
* Dark wooden support pillars under gabled roof.
* Carved two-panel wooden entrance door.
* Bougainvillea, marigolds, and fern pots as established planting.
* Clay water pot.
* Long wooden bench or approved striped cushions.
* Relationship with the Front Garden.
* Prefer regenerated location still when available; until then **do not** copy
  terracotta floor tiles from
  `11_Assets/Location_References/Front_Thinnai_Canon_Design_Reference.png`.

Never:

* Enclose the thinnai with walls.
* Add glass windows.
* Introduce plastic furniture.
* Replace the wooden pillars.
* Remove the open connection to the village.
* Change its position relative to the Front Garden or Courtyard.
* Cover the thinnai ground in terracotta floor-tile grids, ceramic, marble, or
  polished patio paving (packed earth is required for village authenticity).

# Guiding Principle

The Front Thinnai is where the family's private life gently meets the village.

Some of the series' quietest and most meaningful moments should happen here—not through dramatic events, but through ordinary conversations, shared laughter, passing neighbours, and the comforting rhythm of everyday life.

# Front Thinnai

## Purpose

The Front Thinnai is the public face of the Family House.

It is neither completely inside nor completely outside.

It forms the transition between the village and the family's private life.

Almost every visitor first enters this space before stepping into the house.

Throughout the series, it should become one of the most recognizable and emotionally familiar locations.

# Identity

The Front Thinnai is more than a veranda.

It is the family's outdoor living room.

People sit here to welcome guests, drink evening tea, shell peas, repair tools, tell stories, watch children play, and simply observe village life.

Unlike the Courtyard, which belongs mainly to the family, the Front Thinnai belongs to both the family and the village.

It should always feel welcoming.

---

# Location

The Front Thinnai sits immediately west of the Front Garden.

It is permanently attached to the front of the Family House beneath the main roof.

It faces east toward the Front Garden and the Main Village Path, receiving the first sunlight of the day.

# Architecture

Maintain — match `Front_Thinnai_Canon_Design_Reference.png`:

* Raised platform approximately 2½ feet above ground.
* Granite entrance steps leading up when visible from garden approach.
* **Terracotta-toned floor tiles** on the thinnai platform.
* **Gabled wooden roof** with thick exposed beams and deep overhang for shade and
  rain protection.
* **Dark wooden support pillars** framing the open porch.
* White lime-plastered walls.
* Centered **carved two-panel traditional wooden entrance door** on the rear
  wall — may stand slightly ajar, revealing warm blurred interior family presence.
* Open eastern side visually connected to garden and village.

The proportions should feel generous but modest.

# Permanent Furniture

These items permanently belong here:

* Long wooden bench.
* One traditional wooden reclining chair.
* **Soft striped floor cushions** in red, blue, and yellow tones for resting.
* One woven grass mat (rolled when unused).
* Large clay drinking-water pot on a wooden stand.
* Brass tumbler placed nearby.
* Coconut broom leaning against a pillar.
* Pair of wooden footwear stands near the entrance.
* Small umbrella stand made from bamboo.

Furniture should remain simple and practical.

---

# Permanent Planting And Decorative Elements

Maintain — match approved reference:

* **Bougainvillea vine** with **magenta flowers** climbing the left wall.
* **Clay pots** with lush green **ferns** at porch edges.
* **Clay pots** with bright orange **marigolds**.
* Fresh kolam at the entrance whenever appropriate.
* Brass hanging oil lamp.
* Small framed family calendar appropriate to the 1990s.
* Wooden ceiling rafters visible beneath the roof.
* Traditional woven bamboo basket occasionally hanging from a beam.

Decoration should always remain understated and lived-in.

# Daily Activities

Morning

* Sweeping.
* Drawing kolam.
* Receiving milk.
* Morning greetings with neighbours.
* Children putting on school bags.
* Father preparing to leave for work.

Afternoon

* **Family Dog resting on terracotta tiles** in dappled afternoon shade, ears
  alert toward family voices inside.
* Dog sleeping in the shade.
* Mother cleaning vegetables.
* Elderly neighbours stopping briefly.
* Children removing muddy footwear before entering.

Evening

* Entire family sitting together.
* Drinking tea.
* Shelling groundnuts.
* Peeling vegetables.
* Neighbours chatting.
* Watching children play in the Front Garden.
* Enjoying cool evening air.

Night

* Quiet conversation.
* Oil lamp glowing softly.
* Occasional visitor.
* Listening to insects and distant temple bell.

# Relationship With The Village

The Front Thinnai naturally connects the family with village life.

Typical interactions include:

* Neighbours stopping for a short conversation.
* Postman delivering letters.
* Vegetable vendor calling from the road.
* Flower seller visiting.
* Children inviting friends to play.
* Travellers asking for directions.
* Guests waiting before entering.

The family should never appear isolated from the village.

# Seasonal Behaviour

## Summer

* Strong morning sunlight.
* Cool shade throughout the afternoon.
* Clay water pot used frequently.
* Family spends more time outdoors after sunset.

---

## Monsoon

* Rain dripping from roof edges.
* Damp breeze.
* Family watching rainfall from the safety of the thinnai.
* Wet footwear left on the steps.
* Occasional puddles near the garden.

---

## Post-Monsoon / Harvest

* Pleasant cool evenings.
* Fresh flowers decorating the entrance.
* Harvest baskets temporarily resting near the steps.
* Increased neighbour visits.

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* East-facing orientation.
* Raised platform.
* Granite entrance steps when visible from approach.
* **Terracotta-toned floor tiles.**
* Dark wooden support pillars under gabled roof.
* Carved two-panel wooden entrance door.
* Bougainvillea, marigolds, and fern pots as established planting.
* Clay water pot.
* Long wooden bench or approved striped cushions.
* Relationship with the Front Garden.
* Match visual identity in
  `11_Assets/Location_References/Front_Thinnai_Canon_Design_Reference.png`.

Never:

* Enclose the thinnai with walls.
* Add glass windows.
* Introduce plastic furniture.
* Replace the wooden pillars.
* Remove the open connection to the village.
* Change its position relative to the Front Garden or Courtyard.
* Swap terracotta tiles for modern ceramic or marble flooring.

# Guiding Principle

The Front Thinnai is where the family's private life gently meets the village.

Some of the series' quietest and most meaningful moments should happen here—not through dramatic events, but through ordinary conversations, shared laughter, passing neighbours, and the comforting rhythm of everyday life.

# Festival Ground

## Purpose

The Festival Ground is the village's permanent open celebration field within the Village Stories universe.

It is where community festivals, harvest gatherings, temporary fair stalls, processions' resting points, and large joyful assemblies naturally take place.

Every return to this location should feel instantly familiar as an early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu open ground—earthy, communal, decorated only for the occasion, and empty again when celebration ends.

---

# Philosophy

The Festival Ground is not a permanent amusement park.

It is ordinary open land that becomes extraordinary through people, tradition, music, lamps, and shared food.

Most days it rests quietly.

On festival days it fills with colour, voices, and movement—then returns to stillness.

This rhythm of fullness and quiet is part of its identity.

---

# Location

The Festival Ground is permanently located in the **South-West** of the village.

It connects naturally to:

* Paths from Village Centre / Village Square
* Western neighbourhood and trades approach routes
* Broader open land toward future temple-fair expansion areas without requiring those areas yet
* Routes used by processions leaving the Banyan Tree / shrine direction during major celebrations

Families, including Father, Mother, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and occasionally the Family Dog at the edges, may attend events here.

The ground must never be relocated.

---

# Identity

Maintain early-1990s authenticity:

* Broad open packed-earth field
* Native shade trees at the edges
* No permanent carnival rides
* No concrete stadium structure
* No modern LED stage identity
* Temporary bamboo / wooden stalls only during festivals
* Hand-decorated cloth banners and simple festooning
* Oil lamps and clay lamp rows
* Latinam lanterns (லத்தீன் விளக்கு)
* Color lights (கலர் லைட்ஸ்) — temporary coloured bulb strings
* Festival music with traditional instruments and modest period loudspeakers
* Never nightclub / modern DJ concert sound culture

The ground should feel like land the village has always used for gathering.

# Permanent Features

Always present even on ordinary days:

* Wide open earth ground
* Irregular natural edges
* Mature shade trees along sides
* Worn footpaths crossing the field
* Occasional stone or log resting spots under trees
* Clear approach path from the village side

These permanent features allow the location to remain recognizable between festivals.

---

# Temporary Festival Features

Appear only during celebrations, then disappear:

* Bamboo pole frames
* Cloth roof shades for stalls
* Sweet and snack counters
* Flower sellers
* Small toy / balloon / simple game stalls appropriate to 1990s village fairs
* Temporary stage or raised plank platform for announcements, music, or drama
* Decorative banana stems, mango leaves, and toranam at entrances
* Kolam near ceremonial spots
* Clay lamp rows for night festivals
* Latinam lanterns hung along bamboo frames, stall fronts, and entrance paths
* Color lights strung across poles, pandal edges, and stall lines
* Temporary music point near the performance / announcement space
* Sugarcane bundles during harvest festivals when relevant

After the festival, the ground returns to open quiet land.

Temporary structures must never become permanent architecture.

---

# Festival Lighting Canon

Festival lighting is temporary and early-1990s authentic.

It appears for celebrations and is removed afterward.

## Clay Lamps and Oil Lamps

Maintain:

* Rows of clay lamps (அகல் விளக்கு) along ceremonial edges
* Warm oil-lamp glow during night festivals
* Soft flickering practical flame light
* Especially strong during Deepavali-linked and lamp-centered celebrations

## Latinam Lanterns (லத்தீன் விளக்கு)

Latinam lanterns are a recurring festival-ground decoration.

Maintain:

* Traditional hanging lanterns / latinam lights along entrance paths
* Bamboo or wire hanging points
* Warm glowing lantern bodies at dusk and night
* Gentle sway in evening breeze
* Humble village decorative quality, not luxury chandelier styling

Latinam lanterns help viewers immediately recognize that the ground is in festival mode.

## Color Lights (கலர் லைட்ஸ்)

Color lights are permanent festival-night visual canon for the Festival Ground when celebrations continue into evening.

Maintain:

* Strings of coloured electric bulbs
* Common village festival colours: red, green, blue, yellow, and clear/white mixed simply
* Wired along bamboo poles, stall roofs, pandal edges, and open-field frames
* Soft practical brightness — festive, not blinding
* Occasional gentle flicker consistent with 1990s temporary wiring
* Visible from approach paths as a distant celebration signal

Avoid:

* Modern programmable LED walls
* Laser shows
* Neon club lighting
* RGB strip aesthetics from later decades
* Permanent year-round installation

Color lights and latinam lanterns may appear together.

Clay lamps may also appear in the same night scene.

All three layers should feel handmade, temporary, and local.

## Lighting Layering at Night

Canonical night festival light mix:

```text
Distant color light strings
        +
Latinam lanterns along paths / stalls
        +
Clay / oil lamp rows near ceremonial spots
        +
Warm food-stall flame / stove glow
```

This layered glow is one of the Festival Ground's strongest visual signatures.

# Festival Music Canon

Music is part of festival atmosphere on the ground.

## Live Traditional Music

When celebration includes performance or procession energy, maintain:

* Hand drums
* Folk rhythm patterns
* Nadaswaram / traditional wind music when story-appropriate
* Tavil or similar percussion support when suitable
* Ankle bells during dance or drama
* Clapping and chorus from villagers

Traditional music should feel communal rather than professionally staged for cameras.

## Period Loudspeaker Music

Modest early-1990s loudspeakers may play during peak festival hours.

Maintain:

* Simple horn / box loudspeakers mounted on bamboo or temporary poles
* Slightly raw amplified sound quality of the period
* Temple festival songs, folk songs, or familiar film-era celebration music appropriate to rural Tamil Nadu
* Announcements between songs
* Volume festive but not city-concert extreme

Avoid:

* Modern DJ drops
* Bass-heavy EDM
* Crystal-clear digital concert systems
* Nonstop blasting with no village breathing room

## Music Placement

Typical source points:

* Near temporary stage / announcement platform
* Near entrance during peak arrival
* Soft spill across stall rows and family mat areas

Music should rise and fall with the festival rhythm, quieter during sacred pauses if the story requires reverence.

---

# Ground Surface

Maintain:

* Packed earth
* Fine dust in summer
* Soft mud patches after rain
* Sparse grass at edges
* Footprint layers during events
* No paving, tiles, or asphalt

The earth itself should show decades of celebration wear.

---

# Daily Non-Festival Life

Most ordinary days:

* Quiet open field
* Children occasionally running across
* Goats or distant grazing at far edges only when story-safe and supervised
* Birds in edge trees
* Wind moving dry grass
* Villagers using it as a shortcut path

The Festival Ground should not feel abandoned—just resting.

---

# Festival Day Rhythm

## Morning Preparation

* Sweeping and clearing
* Banana stem / leaf decoration
* Stall frames rising
* First vendors arriving
* Children watching setup with excitement

## Peak Celebration

* Crowds in traditional clothes
* Festival music in the air
* Color lights and decorations visible as evening approaches
* Food aromas
* Family groups sitting on mats
* Elders watching from shade
* Procession arrivals and departures

## Evening / Night Festivals

* Latinam lanterns glowing along paths and stall fronts
* Color light strings across bamboo frames
* Clay / oil lamp rows near ceremonial areas
* Stronger golden and amber glow mixed with coloured bulbs
* Soft smoke from food stalls
* Music carrying across the open field
* Silhouettes of families walking home

## After Festival

* Dismantling stalls
* Litter carefully cleared by community habit
* Ground returning to open quiet
* Lingering footprints and trampled grass

During festivals:

* Whole families
* Neighbours
* Elders
* Children including Elder Brother and Younger Daughter
* Vendors
* Temple-linked organizers
* Teachers and village helpers
* Occasional visitors from nearby villages

During ordinary days:

The Family Dog may wait at edges with the family, never disrupting crowds.

# Atmosphere

## Festival Days

* Joyful
* Crowded but local
* Colourful through cloth, flowers, and lamps
* Fragrant with food and incense
* Noisy in a warm village way

## Ordinary Days

* Open
* Quiet
* Windy
* Earthy
* Peaceful

Both states are canonical.

# Sounds

Festival days:

* Crowds talking
* Children's excitement
* Live traditional drums and folk music
* Nadaswaram or similar traditional tones when present
* Period loudspeaker songs and announcements
* Stall calls
* Laughter
* Ankle bells or simple performance sounds
* Generator / modest power hum only if temporary lighting and speakers require it and remain background
* Firecracker sounds only when festival canon and story specifically allow, kept rare and careful

Music should be recognizable as village festival sound — warm, slightly rough, joyful, and local.

Ordinary days:

* Wind
* Birds
* Distant village life
* Soft footsteps on earth
* No festival loudspeakers
* No color-light electrical presence

---

# Lighting

Day festivals:

* Bright open sun
* Cloth shade contrast at stalls
* Colourful clothing in hard light
* Color light strings may be visible unlit or faintly testing before evening

Evening festivals:

* Color lights glowing across the ground
* Latinam lanterns along entrances and stalls
* Warm clay / oil lamp rows
* Long shadows
* Soft smoke haze
* Path lanterns and light spill leading home

Ordinary days:

* Broad sky
* Strong midday glare
* Soft tree-edge shade
* No festival lighting left behind

# Seasonal Behaviour

## Summer

* Dusty field
* Deep edge-tree shade valued
* Hot midday pauses under trees

## Monsoon

* Soft or muddy ground
* Fewer full-ground events unless weather allows
* Fresh green edges after rains
* Careful walking

## Post-Monsoon / Harvest

* Ideal celebration season
* Strong Pongal and harvest-linked use
* Firmer ground after drying
* High emotional importance

Useful compositions include:

* Crowd mid-shots keeping faces local and period-true

The ground must remain geographically and visually consistent whether full or empty.

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* South-West permanent location
* Open packed-earth field identity
* Edge shade trees
* Temporary-only festival structures
* Latinam lanterns during festival nights
* Color lights during festival nights
* Festival music from traditional sources and modest period loudspeakers
* Early-1990s village celebration culture
* Return to quiet after events

Never:

* Install permanent carnival infrastructure
* Leave color lights or latinam lanterns mounted year-round
* Add modern LED concert staging as default
* Use EDM / nightclub music culture
* Relocate the ground
* Keep stalls standing forever between festivals
* Turn it into a paved civic plaza

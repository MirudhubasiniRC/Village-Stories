# Family House

## Purpose

The Family House is the primary setting of the Village Stories universe.

It is where the family's daily life unfolds, where traditions are passed from one generation to the next, and where many of the series' most memorable moments take place.

Although the village is rich with many recurring locations, the Family House remains the emotional centre of the entire series.

Every room, pathway, and outdoor space should feel familiar to viewers over time.

---

# Identity

The house is a modest, well-maintained South Indian farming home built decades before the events of the series.

It has grown naturally with the family's needs while preserving its traditional architecture.

Nothing feels luxurious.

Nothing feels neglected.

Everything reflects years of careful use, quiet maintenance, and deep family history.

The home should always feel warm, welcoming, practical, and authentic.

---

# Architectural Style

Maintain throughout the series:

* Traditional 1990s South Indian village architecture.
* Whitewashed lime-plastered walls.
* Terracotta Mangalore tiled roof.
* Exposed wooden rafters beneath the roof.
* Thick load-bearing masonry walls.
* Red oxide flooring throughout most interiors.
* Solid teak and country-wood doors.
* Large wooden windows for natural ventilation.
* Wide roof overhangs protecting walls from rain.
* Natural ventilation rather than mechanical cooling.
* Hand-built construction using local materials.

The house should never appear modernized.

---

# Property Orientation

The house permanently follows the world's compass system.

Front of House:

Rear of House:

West

Rear garden leading toward the coconut grove and agricultural land.

This orientation must never change.

# Construction Materials

Only traditional materials should appear.

Maintain:

* Lime plaster.
* Fired clay bricks.
* Granite foundation stones.
* Terracotta roof tiles.
* Teak wood.
* Coconut timber where appropriate.
* Bamboo.
* Clay.
* Brass.
* Copper.
* Cotton.
* Coir rope.
* Stone.

Never introduce modern building materials without an intentional story reason.

---

# Roof

Maintain:

* Traditional sloping Mangalore tile roof.
* Deep overhangs.
* Timber roof structure.
* Natural ventilation beneath tiles.
* Rainwater dripping from roof edges during monsoon.
* Small birds occasionally nesting beneath eaves.

The roof should be instantly recognizable.

---

# Doors

The house uses traditional wooden doors.

Maintain:

* Solid teak construction.
* Brass hinges.
* Wooden locking bar.
* Hand-carved details.
* Natural weathering.

Doors should remain open during most daytime scenes unless privacy or weather requires otherwise.

---

# Windows

Large wooden windows are an important feature of the house.

Maintain:

* Wooden shutters.
* Cross ventilation.
* Soft natural light.
* Views toward gardens.
* Cotton curtains where appropriate.

Windows connect the family with nature.

# Daily Rhythm

Morning

* Front Garden receives first sunlight.
* Family begins the day.
* Breakfast preparation.
* Prayer.
* Children leave for school.
* Father leaves for work.

Afternoon

* Courtyard remains active.
* Kitchen busiest.
* Animals resting.
* Garden work.

Evening

* Family gathers on the Front Thinnai.
* Lamps are lit.
* Dinner preparation.
* Quiet conversations.

Night

* Calm.
* Soft oil-lamp lighting.
* Cricket sounds.
* Gentle breeze.

The house should visibly change with the time of day.

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* East-facing entrance.
* Traditional architecture.
* Permanent room arrangement.
* Property boundaries.
* Construction materials.
* Roof design.
* Walking flow.
* Natural ventilation.
* Traditional appearance.

* Rotate the house.
* Mirror the layout.
* Add modern rooms.
* Change room locations.
* Replace traditional materials.
* Alter the property's orientation.

This document is the master architectural reference for the Family House.

# Guiding Principle

The Family House is more than a building.

It is a place where memories accumulate.

Every doorway, every window, every worn floor tile, and every shaded corner should quietly remind viewers that generations have lived here before, and that every new story becomes part of the home's continuing history.

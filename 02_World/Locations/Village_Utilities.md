# Village Utilities

## Purpose

This document defines the permanent public infrastructure shared by everyone in the Village Stories universe.

Unlike individual homes or private properties, these utilities belong to the entire village and support everyday life.

They provide consistency across stories and ensure that common public features remain permanent throughout the series.

---

# Identity

The village is simple but self-sufficient.

Its public infrastructure has developed gradually over generations using traditional materials and local craftsmanship.

Everything should feel handmade, practical, well-maintained, and appropriate for a 1990s South Indian farming village.

Modern utilities should never appear unless a story is intentionally set during a period of change.

---

# Public Infrastructure

The village permanently includes:

* Main Village Path
* Footpaths
* Stone drainage channels
* Stone culverts
* Public seating
* Directional signboard at the Village Entrance
* Traditional oil lamp posts
* Community notice board
* Public resting places
* Waste compost pits
* Bullock cart resting area
* Bicycle parking spaces near gathering areas

These remain permanent parts of the village.

---

# Village Entrance Sign

Located beside the Village Entrance.

Maintain:

* Wooden construction.
* Hand-painted Tamil village name.
* Weathered appearance.
* Supported by wooden posts.
* Surrounded by native plants.

The sign should feel handcrafted rather than official.

---

# Main Village Path

The primary road connecting the village.

Maintain:

* Packed earth surface.
* Naturally worn walking tracks.
* Wide enough for:

  * Bullock carts
  * Bicycles
  * Livestock
  * Pedestrians

During:

Summer

* Dry
* Dusty

Monsoon

* Damp
* Firm
* Occasional puddles near edges

---

# Secondary Footpaths

Smaller paths connect:

* Individual houses.
* River Ghat.
* Coconut Grove.
* Fields.
* Pond.
* Irrigation Canal.

Maintain:

* Narrow earthen paths.
* Grass growing along edges.
* No concrete paving.

---

# Stone Drainage Channels

Rainwater is managed through open stone drains.

Maintain:

* Hand-built stone construction.
* Shallow depth.
* Natural water flow.
* Grass along edges.
* Clear during most of the year.

The drains should never dominate a scene but should exist wherever appropriate.

---

# Stone Culverts

Small stone culverts allow water to pass beneath walking paths.

Maintain:

* Granite slabs.
* Traditional masonry.
* Moss after rain.
* Naturally aged appearance.

---

# Community Notice Board

Located near the Village Square.

Maintain:

* Wooden frame.
* Small tiled roof.
* Handwritten notices.
* Festival announcements.
* Village meeting information.
* Agricultural updates.

No printed posters or modern advertisements.

---

# Traditional Oil Lamp Posts

Located at important public spaces.

Examples:

* Village Square.
* Banyan Tree.
* River Ghat entrance.

Maintain:

* Cast iron or stone base.
* Brass oil lamp.
* Lit during evenings and festivals.
* No electric bulbs.

The village generally becomes dark after sunset except for these traditional light sources.

---

# Public Seating

Permanent seating exists at:

* Banyan Tree.
* Village Square.
* River Ghat.
* Along selected walking paths.

Maintain:

* Granite benches.
* Stone platforms.
* Large tree shade.

These encourage spontaneous conversations.

---

# Bullock Cart Resting Area

Located near the Village Entrance.

Maintain:

* Open earth.
* Shade tree.
* Water trough.
* Wooden hitching posts.

Travellers naturally pause here before entering the village.

---

# Bicycle Parking

Villagers often leave bicycles near:

* Village Square.
* Banyan Tree.
* River Ghat.
* School (future).

There are no formal bicycle racks.

Bicycles simply lean against trees, walls, or verandas.

---

# Waste Management

Organic waste is handled traditionally.

Maintain:

* Compost pits behind homes.
* Cow dung collection.
* Leaf piles.
* Ash disposal from kitchens.

No plastic bins or municipal collection vehicles.

---

# Public Trees

Important roadside trees include:

* Neem.
* Tamarind.
* Pungam.
* Banyan.
* Mango.

Trees provide:

* Shade.
* Bird habitat.
* Gathering places.
* Seasonal flowers.
* Natural beauty.

They should never be removed without a major story event.

---

# Seasonal Behaviour

## Summer

* Dry roads.
* Dust.
* Deep shade beneath trees.
* Low roadside grass.

---

## Monsoon

* Wet pathways.
* Water flowing through stone drains.
* Fresh green vegetation.
* Moss on stone surfaces.

---

## Post-Monsoon / Harvest

* Clean paths.
* Healthy trees.
* Bright vegetation.
* Pleasant walking conditions.

---

# Audio Identity

Common sounds include:

* Footsteps.
* Bicycle bells.
* Bullock cart wheels.
* Birds.
* Wind through trees.
* Village conversations.
* Distant temple bell.
* Dogs barking occasionally.
* Roosters crowing.

These sounds create the everyday atmosphere of the village.

---

# Story Opportunities

Village utilities naturally support:

* The postman delivering letters.
* Travelling vendors stopping to rest.
* Village announcements.
* Children cycling.
* Bullock carts arriving with harvest.
* Festival decorations.
* Neighbours meeting unexpectedly.
* Helping travellers.

These elements should quietly support stories without becoming the main focus.

---

# Continuity Rules

Always maintain:

* Traditional construction.
* Natural materials.
* Permanent locations.
* Functional simplicity.
* Historical authenticity.

Never:

* Add electric street lights.
* Introduce concrete sidewalks.
* Install traffic signs.
* Add modern waste bins.
* Replace traditional materials with modern infrastructure.

---

# Canon Authority

This document defines the permanent shared infrastructure of the Village Stories universe.

Every story, image, animation, and prompt should treat these utilities as stable parts of the village unless a permanent canonical change is intentionally introduced.

---

# Guiding Principle

The village utilities are not merely functional—they quietly shape everyday life.

They should feel so natural that viewers rarely notice them individually, yet together they create the unmistakable atmosphere of a real South Indian village where generations have lived, worked, and cared for one another.

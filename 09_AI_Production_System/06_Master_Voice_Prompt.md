# Master Voice Prompt

## Purpose And Scope

This is the permanent production manual and copy-ready master prompt for
Village Stories narration, scripted dialogue, AI voice generation, recording,
review, revision, and handoff.

Use it after the approved script, picture plan, and sound plan identify what
spoken audio is actually needed. Use it before music, final mix, captions,
YouTube upload, and release approval.

Its purpose is to make the spoken track feel like an attentive companion to a
real, lived-in moment in the Village Stories world.

It does not write a premise, settle a story dispute, invent dialogue, invent
Tamil words, infer pronunciation from spelling, replace cultural review, create
visual prompts, or repair a defective image or Flow clip with explanation.

The script establishes what may be said.

The scene establishes what can be seen and heard.

This manual establishes how approved speech is prepared and delivered.

## Operating Principle

> Let the narration notice, connect, and then step aside.

The voice is not a trailer announcer, a tourism guide, a teacher performing a
lecture, a marketing voice, or an all-knowing storyteller.

It is warm because it pays attention.

It is culturally grounded because it follows approved names, terms, context,
and pronunciation evidence.

It is emotionally clear because it respects action, silence, and the viewer.

It is not made more Tamil or South Indian by adding invented language,
unverified accents, decorative transliteration, or generic regional stereotypes.

When language evidence is absent, say less, retain the approved wording, or
hold the line for a qualified language or cultural reviewer.

affected line `HOLD FOR CANON, RESEARCH, OR LANGUAGE REVIEW`.

Do not perform it as if the uncertainty were harmless.

## Permanent Series Voice Locks

- The world is early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu; exact year may flex but the period
  may not.
- Spoken references must fit documented objects, work, travel, family roles,
  infrastructure, season, time, and local social context.
- Never casually introduce current slang, internet culture, social media,
  contemporary brands, digital technology, modern consumer language, or a
  present-day point of view.
- Preserve the original premium animated Village Stories tone: observant,
  humane, restrained, precise, and warm.
- Do not imitate a named narrator, actor, public figure, studio, franchise, or
  other external property.
- Do not use accent performance as a shortcut for cultural specificity.
- Do not treat Tamil, South Indian, village, family, or rural life as costume,
  comedy, exotic atmosphere, or a single homogeneous identity.
- Narration must serve the image and natural sound rather than compete with
  them.
- Ordinary work, faith, food, weather, animals, children, age, and practical
  difficulty receive dignity, never spectacle or pity.
- No voice treatment may manufacture panic, threat, humiliation, violence,
  false urgency, child endangerment, animal distress, or melodrama.

## Narration Role

Narration earns its place by doing at least one defined job.

Approved narration jobs are:

- Orient the viewer when visual context alone is insufficient.
- Connect two images, actions, or time steps cleanly.
- Reveal a small meaning not visible in the action.
- Preserve a useful story fact without forcing exposition.
- Make room for a quiet reflection at a natural resting point.
- Carry a gentle transition across an edit.
- Clarify an educational detail already supported by research and picture.
- Close a thought without turning the ending into a moral lecture.

Narration is not approved merely because there is open time on the timeline.

Delete or hold a line that only:

- names an action the image already communicates;
- repeats a character’s visible emotion;
- explains a continuity error;
- advertises the channel inside the story;
- adds poetry unrelated to the practical moment;
- tells the audience what to feel;
- substitutes generic nostalgia for observed detail;
- fills a featured ASMR or silence window;
- changes a character’s knowledge, intention, or relationship;
- gives a broad cultural claim without source support.

## Voice Identity

The default narration profile is:

- warm, but never syrupy;
- clear, but never commercial;
- patient, but not automatically slow;
- intimate, but not whispery or intrusive;
- observant, rather than omniscient;
- emotionally responsive, rather than emotionally demonstrative;
- articulate, rather than overly polished;
- grounded, rather than theatrical;
- welcoming, rather than promotional;
- natural enough to leave room for breath and thought.

The listener should feel that the narrator has noticed the moment carefully.

The listener should not feel pushed toward a predetermined reaction.

### Delivery Temperature Labels

Use one primary temperature per unit.

`OBSERVANT` means neutral warmth and clear attention.

`TENDER` means care is present, but the voice does not soften into sentiment.

`QUIET_DELIGHT` means a small inward smile, not a cheerful performance.

`THOUGHTFUL` means a little more space between ideas, not a solemn tone.

`PRACTICAL` means direct, calm, and unembellished.

`QUIET_CONCERN` means contained attention to a proportionate difficulty.

`PLAYFUL` means light and brief, never broad comedy or teasing humiliation.

`RESTING` means the final phrase settles and leaves air after it.

Avoid combining incompatible temperatures in one short unit.

If the scene changes temperature, split the line or use silence between units.

## Language And Pronunciation Care

The production may use English, Tamil, another approved language, or a
documented mixture only when the episode packet specifies that choice.

This manual does not presume a default spoken language from the setting.

The language field in the approved script and release packet controls.

### Evidence-First Pronunciation Rule

For every culturally specific name, place, kinship term, food, tool, crop,
ritual reference, song title, or Tamil phrase:

1. Use the exact approved spelling from the source packet.
2. Locate a documented pronunciation source.
3. Record the source type and reviewer where required.
4. Write a performer-friendly cue only if it is verified.
5. Test it in the selected voice, not only in isolation.
6. Review it in its sentence and scene context.
7. Preserve the approved decision in the pronunciation ledger.

Do not invent phonetic spelling because an English spelling appears obvious.

Do not force English phonetics into a Tamil word without verification.

Do not guess gender, honorific, kinship relation, religious use, dialect, or
regional origin from a name.

Do not add Tamil words as atmosphere when the script, culture review, and viewer
context do not support them.

Do not translate a known local term into a generic word solely to make the
voice generator’s output easier.

### Approved Pronunciation Sources

Use, in this order where available:

1. An explicit pronunciation note in the approved canon or script packet.
2. A recording supplied or approved by a qualified speaker or language reviewer.
3. Approved research that includes a reliable spoken source.
4. A review decision documented by the designated language or cultural owner.

Machine pronunciation, general web spelling, text-to-speech output, and the
operator’s familiarity are not approval sources by themselves.

## Script Markup Standard

Mark the approved wording before the session or generation request.

Use lightweight markings that guide a truthful read.

`/` marks a small natural breath or micro-pause.

`//` marks a meaningful held pause.

`[SILENCE: duration / reason]` marks intentional non-speech.

`[EMPHASIS: word / reason]` marks one justified point of attention.

`[PRON: ledger ID]` points to an approved pronunciation entry.

`[ASMR: source / protected range]` marks a sound that speech must not cover.

`[CUT: permitted]` marks a line that may be shortened by the editor.

`[NO CUT]` marks a phrase whose meaning breaks if trimmed.

Do not mark every phrase for emphasis.

Do not use punctuation alone to force emotional acting.

Do not add delivery marks that imply a fact or feeling not in the scene.

### Marked Unit Example

```text
Unit: E012_S03_N03
Purpose: connect the child's observation to the mother’s practical choice.
Temperature: QUIET_DELIGHT
Pace: NATURAL_MEDIUM

"She waited for the rain to soften / before carrying the baskets out."

[ASMR: rain on tiled roof, preserve before first word]
[EMPHASIS: soften / the change matters more than the weather]
[NO CUT: before carrying the baskets out]
[PRON: NONE]
```

This example models pacing only.

It does not authorize the fact for a different episode.

## Pacing And Timing

Pace follows comprehension, shot length, available sound, and emotional space.

It does not follow a fixed “AI narration speed.”

### Pace Classes

`SPACIOUS` is used for reflection, held images, and endings with room to hear.

`NATURAL_SLOW` is used when an action needs observation or a term needs clarity.

`NATURAL_MEDIUM` is the default for clear, practical narration.

`BRISK_CLEAR` is used sparingly for a Short hook or a sequence with compact,
simple facts.

`QUICK` requires explicit episode approval and must never make words unclear.

Words-per-minute is a review indicator, not a creative instruction.

If a unit must be unnaturally fast to fit, shorten the copy or redesign the
edit.

### Timing Rules

- Enter after the viewer has enough visual information to orient.
- Leave before a featured action, look, or sound needs to breathe.
- Do not land the final word on an important cut unless the cut is designed to
  carry it.
- Preserve head and tail handles around every recorded or generated unit.
- Keep breaths when they support human rhythm and remove them only when they
  distract.
- Do not compress natural pauses merely to increase narration density.
- Let a line complete before a major new visual fact when possible.
- Place transitions on a stable visual or sound bridge.
- For a long sentence, test whether two units would be more intelligible.
- If a word must be rushed to reach the cut, the edit or script is wrong.

## Emotion Direction

Direction must name a scene fact, relationship, or observable action.

Do not direct with abstract intensity alone.

Instead of “make it emotional,” use:

```text
Mother has already noticed the rain changing.
The line shares her practical patience.
Keep the feeling small; leave the roof-rain audible after the first phrase.
```

Instead of “sound happy,” use:

```text
This is a private discovery, not an announcement.
Let a slight smile arrive near the last word, then return to stillness.
```

Instead of “sound sad,” use:

```text
The task has become harder, but no one is defeated.
Keep the concern contained and make the next practical step sound possible.
```

### Emotion Boundaries

- Never narrate tears, fear, pride, shame, anger, or love unless the approved
  script and scene support the claim.
- Do not inflate a minor inconvenience into a crisis.
- Do not use a low, breathy, or trembling voice to manufacture seriousness.
- Do not infantilize children or make elders sound fragile by default.
- Do not make the narrator sound morally superior to the characters.
- Do not reward hardship with decorative suffering language.
- Let humour come from recognition and timing, never mockery.
- Let tenderness come from attention and pause, never sentimental vocabulary.

## Human Recording Direction

Use a short calibration passage before a full session.

The calibration should contain:

- one neutral observation;
- one practical transition;
- one gentle warm line;
- one quiet ending;
- any recurring name or culturally specific term with a ledger entry.

Approve the vocal temperature before recording the episode.

Record a faithful primary take first.

Then record only purposeful alternates.

Useful alternate labels include:

`A — approved wording, natural pacing`

`B — shorter pause after visual beat`

`C — quieter ending for music entry`

`D — pronunciation repair only`

Do not request vague alternate takes such as “more feeling” without a scene
reason.

Keep clean pre-roll and post-roll.

Retain the unprocessed original.

## Synthetic Voice Direction

Synthetic voice is subject to the same canon, rights, pronunciation, delivery,
and review gates as a human performance.

Use only an approved, rights-cleared voice identity.

Never clone, simulate, imitate, or prompt toward a real person’s recognisable
voice without explicit permission and lawful authorization.

Do not request “sound like” a living or deceased person, actor, narrator,
creator, or voice artist.

Generate short scene-specific units rather than one uncontrolled episode-long
read.

Use the smallest necessary generation unit:

- one short narration thought;
- one dialogue turn;
- one pronunciation repair;
- one alternate ending cadence;
- one pickup after picture revision.

Inspect joins for changed pitch, loudness, breath, speed, microphone character,
or emotional temperature.

If the model cannot speak an approved term correctly, do not silently replace
the term or accept a near approximation.

Escalate to an approved different voice, human pickup, revised wording approved
by the script owner, or a documented language-review solution.

## Shorts Voice Design

Shorts require clarity early, not constant speech.

The first spoken line, if one is used, must earn attention through a truthful
scene-specific observation, question, or practical change.

Do not use a generic hook such as “You won’t believe,” “watch till the end,” or
an exaggerated emotional promise.

Keep the first unit easy to understand on a phone speaker.

Do not cover the opening image before the viewer has a chance to orient.

Prefer one concise idea per narration unit.

Use direct syntactic structure where it improves caption readability.

Protect natural sound even in fast formats.

Let the final line settle before the loop point, end card, or next action.

Do not turn the ending into an aggressive subscription request.

### Shorts Checklist

```text
[ ] Opening spoken unit has a truthful scene-specific purpose.
[ ] First phrase is intelligible without high playback volume.
[ ] Copy does not over-explain the opening visual.
[ ] Each unit has one idea.
[ ] Captions can follow the spoken wording exactly.
[ ] Protected ASMR or silence remains audible.
[ ] No word is rushed to satisfy a cut.
[ ] Ending preserves emotional rest.
[ ] Any call to action is optional, truthful, and outside story integrity.
```

## Long-Form Voice Design

Long-form episodes can carry more narration only when it creates understanding,
connection, or rhythm.

Vary spoken density across the episode.

Use narration clusters around orientation, a practical decision, a transition,
or a quiet reflection.

Use silence and environmental sound after information-heavy passages.

Avoid an uninterrupted “documentary voice” layer.

Return to established terms consistently after pronunciation approval.

Do not change the narrator’s tense, distance, or cultural vocabulary midway
without a documented story reason.

Let repeated work rhythms be heard rather than described every time.

### Long-Form Density Check

For each scene ask:

```text
Does the viewer need spoken orientation here?
  Yes → state the smallest useful fact.
  No  → preserve picture, sound, dialogue, or silence.

Does the line create a new thought?
  Yes → retain if timing and canon are clean.
  No  → cut or merge it.

Does the sound plan have a featured moment?
  Yes → make space before, during, or after it.
  No  → check that narration still leaves visual breathing room.
```

## Google Flow And Picture Integration

Lock voice only against approved picture or a clearly labeled provisional
animatic.

Do not time final narration from a text description of a Flow clip.

Review:

- the actual visible action;
- any visible mouth movement;
- start and end handles;
- camera movement;
- new natural-sound opportunities;
- object, weather, and continuity facts;
- whether the action already communicates the intended line.

If Flow creates an unwanted action, incorrect prop state, unclear facial
behaviour, or continuity error, return the shot to animation.

Do not explain an error into plausibility with narration.

For voice-over on a silent visible-mouth shot, ensure the image does not imply
that the character is speaking different words.

For dialogue synchronization, provide the approved exact dialogue and sync
requirements to the owning animation process; this manual does not authorize
lip-motion invention.

## Audio Quality Standard

Capture or render the cleanest practical source before creative processing.

Retain a raw or source-original version.

Maintain a dry dialogue or narration stem for mix flexibility.

Technical goals are intelligibility and consistency, not sterile perfection.

Reject or repair:

- clipped peaks;
- persistent hiss, hum, or room noise;
- metallic denoising;
- warbling or watery synthetic artifacts;
- abrupt breath removal;
- inconsistent microphone tone;
- sentence-join pitch shifts;
- audible synthetic consonant glitches;
- swallowed words;
- accidental doubled words;
- harsh sibilance that distracts;
- loudness changes that force listener adjustment;
- reverb or music baked into a required clean stem.

Do not over-compress intimate speech until every phrase has the same weight.

Do not process away breath, warmth, or natural micro-dynamics.

## Review And Approval

Review every unit in context, not only through headphones in isolation.

### Meaning Review

Confirm:

- exact words match the approved script;
- all named facts are source-backed;
- no implied fact contradicts picture or canon;
- tense, point of view, and relationship language are consistent;
- the line has a defined narration job;
- the line does not explain what the viewer already sees.

### Cultural And Language Review

Confirm:

- language choice matches the approved episode decision;
- every ledger term has a valid source;
- pronunciation was tested in the actual voice;
- no invented Tamil or decorative code-switching was added;
- no unverified accent or stereotype shapes the delivery;
- captions preserve approved wording and terms.

### Performance Review

Confirm:

- warmth is present without sweetness or salesmanship;
- pace fits the picture;
- pauses support, rather than delay, the cut;
- emotion is proportionate;
- emphasis falls on a meaningful word;
- the ending leaves the intended amount of air;
- the performer or model does not drift into a different character.

### Technical Review

Confirm:

- spoken words remain intelligible on a representative playback system;
- no glitches, clipping, unwanted processing, or inconsistent joins remain;
- clean stems and handles are present;
- filenames, revisions, and take IDs are traceable;
- timing aligns with approved picture;
- caption transcript matches the approved voice track.

### Approval States

`DRAFT` means a unit exists but has not passed context review.

`TECHNICAL_CHECK` means source quality is under review.

`CONTENT_CHECK` means wording, canon, and language care are under review.

`MIX_READY` means approved voice units may enter sound post.

`LOCKED` means picture, voice, captions, and edit timing agree.

`REVISE` means a named defect must be corrected.

`HOLD` means an upstream fact, language decision, rights record, or picture
issue blocks the unit.

## Revision Workflow

Preserve the approved prior take before changing anything.

Classify the defect before revision.

`SCRIPT_DEFECT` means wording, meaning, factual support, or narration purpose is
wrong.

`PRONUNCIATION_DEFECT` means the approved term was spoken incorrectly or lacks
adequate source evidence.

`PERFORMANCE_DEFECT` means temperature, pace, emphasis, pause, or phrasing is
wrong while the words remain correct.

`TIMING_DEFECT` means the line conflicts with picture, sound, or edit.

`TECHNICAL_DEFECT` means the audio source or processing is unusable.

`PICTURE_DEFECT` means the visual action or continuity conflicts with the
spoken text.

Change the smallest responsible component.

Do not alter wording to repair a picture defect without script approval.

Do not add processing to conceal a wrong performance.

Do not regenerate the entire episode for a single word repair.

## YouTube, Captions, And Accessibility

The voice track, captions, title, description, chapter wording, and public
claims must not contradict each other.

Create captions from the locked approved track, then review them against the
audio and source script.

Do not let automatic captions silently replace a verified local term with a
different word.

Correct timing around pauses, dialogue turns, and line breaks for readability.

Provide language metadata according to the approved release packet.

If the episode uses more than one approved language, make the caption and
subtitle plan explicit before release.

Do not add a voice-over call to action that damages the story ending.

If a channel prompt is used, keep it truthful, optional, brief, and outside the
emotional resting image unless the approved episode format requires otherwise.

Before publishing, listen to the actual processed YouTube version where
possible.

Check for loudness change, caption mismatch, clipping, timing shift, and loss
of quiet detail after platform processing.

## Final Voice Gate

Approve only when all statements below are true.

```text
[ ] The spoken text is the approved script revision.
[ ] Every named fact is canon-safe and scene-supported.
[ ] The line has a specific narration purpose.
[ ] The delivery is warm, calm, and restrained.
[ ] No language, accent, pronunciation, or cultural detail was invented.
[ ] All recurring terms have approved pronunciation treatment.
[ ] Picture, Flow action, dialogue visibility, and voice agree.
[ ] Featured ASMR, music, and silence have protected space.
[ ] Shorts or long-form timing fits the approved format.
[ ] Audio is clean, intelligible, and delivered as usable stems.
[ ] Captions match the approved final track.
[ ] Rights, source, revision, and approvals are traceable.
[ ] The handoff package is complete.
```

If any box is false, the voice unit is not ready for final mix or release.

## Guiding Reminder

The right Village Stories voice does not decorate the scene from above.

It shares a careful moment, speaks only when it can add something true, and
leaves enough quiet for the village to be heard.

# Camera Direction Bible

## Permanent Role

This is the permanent studio cinematography manual for Village Stories.

It is the sole operational authority for **camera direction** in:

- still-image prompts;
- Google Flow animation prompts;
- storyboards;
- shot lists;
- production plans;
- generation review; and
- editorial handoff notes.

Camera direction means the camera's chosen point of view at the start of a shot:

- its distance from the subject;
- its height;
- its angle;
- its side of the action;
- its lens-feel and spatial relationship;
- the visible field behind and around the subject; and
- the composition inside the frame.

Camera direction does **not** mean camera movement.

Do not use this document to authorize a pan, tilt, push-in, pull-back, orbit,
crane, tracking move, handheld drift, rack focus, zoom, or transition.

Those are motion decisions and belong exclusively to
`20_Camera_Movement_Bible.md`.

A static camera direction may be used in a moving clip.

The movement document decides whether the directed camera may move and how.

If the documents appear to conflict, preserve the approved camera direction,
then resolve movement through Document 20.

Never invent a different framing merely because a movement is difficult.

## Guiding Principle

> Place the viewer where a calm, respectful human observer would naturally
> stand to understand the work, the relationship, and the village.

Village Stories does not use the camera to make ordinary life look dangerous,
grandiose, comic, invasive, or artificially dramatic.

The camera observes before it announces.

The camera clarifies before it decorates.

The camera earns intimacy through story context.

The camera lets rural Tamil life, practical work, and small human responses
remain dignified.

## Authority And Boundaries

This document inherits all higher visual constraints.

It never creates canon, changes story facts, chooses a new location layout,
changes character design, or overrules approved light and continuity.

Use the highest applicable authority.

```text
Approved current production decision
↓
01_Canon/ — timeline, visual locks, continuity locks
↓
02_World/ · 03_Characters/ · 04_Objects/ · 05_Research/
↓
06_Story_Engine/ — story truth and emotional boundary
↓
Approved episode brief, script, storyboard, scene ledger
↓
07_Episode_System/ — format, pacing, opening and ending rules
↓
08_Production/ — execution and delivery requirements
↓
19_Camera_Direction_Bible.md — framing and viewpoint authority
↓
20_Camera_Movement_Bible.md — movement authority
↓
Tool settings and generated asset
```

An attractive candidate does not become a camera precedent merely because it
looks cinematic.

A tool default does not decide camera direction.

A reference image controls only the role explicitly assigned to it.

An `OPEN` camera fact is a production hold when it affects continuity.

## Permanent Visual Constraints

Early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu is fixed.

Every camera view must preserve period-correct people, objects, buildings,
roads, utilities, clothing, vegetation, and daily practice.

The visual language is original Village Stories anime for the entire frame.

Use painterly 2D anime-inspired illustration matching
`11_Assets/Character_References/Father_Canon_Design_Reference.png`; thin clean
line art; soft cel-style painted shading; natural South Indian people; warm
cinematic lighting; and detailed culturally accurate environments in the same
anime finish.

Do not name, imitate, or invoke a studio, artist, franchise, or film.

The camera is a calm human observer.

Use natural perspective.

Keep faces, bodies, tools, and architecture proportionate.

Avoid fisheye distortion.

Avoid action-camera distortion.

Avoid surveillance distance or surveillance height.

Avoid drone spectacle.

Avoid aggressive hero angles.

Avoid Dutch angles except the rare, specifically approved use defined below.

Avoid gratuitous shallow focus that erases useful location information.

Preserve the documented world orientation.

Morning light comes from the east.

Evening light comes from the west.

The house, rear yard, and kitchen are west.

Fields and pond are east.

The banyan tree and river ghat are north.

The canal is south.

Frame geography so that visible sun direction, shadow direction, entrances,
paths, and background landmarks remain possible.

Ordinary life remains dignified.

Never use a low angle, close framing, a tilt, or an obstructed view to imply
threat, humiliation, panic, danger, pursuit, violence, child endangerment,
animal distress, or melodrama that is not in the approved story.

## Direction Before Movement

Write camera direction before writing any movement instruction.

Correct:

```text
Medium Full, eye level, three-quarter front view of Mother at the grinding
stone; camera on the kitchen-door side; her hands, stone, and morning window
light visible; shallow room depth retained behind her.
```

Movement is a separate later decision:

```text
Movement: no camera movement.
```

Incorrect:

```text
Camera slowly pushes in on Mother grinding spices.
```

The incorrect instruction contains movement but leaves distance, height, side,
composition, background, and screen direction unresolved.

When a board needs a moving shot, first specify its start direction.

Then consult Document 20 for the allowed movement.


Use `NONE` only when a field is genuinely irrelevant.

Use `OPEN — owner and resolution` when a decision is pending.

Do not bury direction in poetic prose.

The production team must be able to draw the camera position from the record.

## Shared Composition Rules

Give every shot one primary subject.

Give every shot one primary visual question.

Do not make an establishing frame also carry a subtle facial reaction.

Do not make a close-up also carry essential geography.

Leave intentional room in the direction of attention, travel, or hand action.

Do not place a looking character against a frame edge with no look room.

Do not cut off hands when the hands are the story.

Do not crop feet when grounded stance, walking, or a full outfit matters.

Do not crop the working object when it explains the action.

Keep horizon lines stable unless the approved shot type says otherwise.

Avoid accidental tangencies between heads and background poles, branches,
doorframes, or cookware.

Use foreground only when it clarifies depth, place, privacy, or point of view.

Foreground must not hide the action.

Keep important faces away from harsh backlit darkness unless the approved light
supports a readable silhouette.

Frame negative space as an emotional or editorial resource, not emptiness.

## Screen Direction And The 180-Degree Rule

Screen direction is continuity, not decoration.

Establish the action axis before coverage.

For a two-person conversation, the axis is the line joining the people.

For cooking work, the axis may run from worker to stove, stone, cutting board,
or serving area.

For travel, the axis follows the established route.

For a handoff, the axis runs between giver and receiver.

Keep subsequent cameras on one side of the axis unless the board explicitly
establishes a reset.

If Father faces screen right in a medium shot while speaking to Mother, Mother
normally faces screen left in her matching coverage.

If a person walks screen left to screen right, preserve that direction across
the adjacent shots unless an approved geographic reset intervenes.

A new wide establishing view may reset orientation when the route and positions
are clearly visible.

An intentional cross-axis shot requires a reason, an approval, and a bridge
shot that lets the viewer relearn geography.

Never cross the axis merely to make a reverse angle look different.

Maintain hand continuity.

If a knife is in the right hand in one cut, do not reverse the image so it
appears in the left hand without an established physical change.

Maintain object-side continuity.

If the pot is screen right of the stove in the established kitchen geography,
do not swap it in the reverse coverage.

## Lighting Direction In Camera Notes

Camera direction must name the motivated light relationship when it matters.

Use clear language:

```text
morning east-window light entering from screen left
```

```text
late-afternoon west light behind the subjects, faces softly filled from the
courtyard
```

```text
overcast monsoon daylight, even soft light, no invented hard sun
```

Do not use:

```text
beautiful cinematic lighting
```

Do not place a camera only for a decorative flare.

Do not reverse a location solely to make a face brighter when that reversal
breaks established sun direction or screen geography.

Faces must be readable, but readability should come from plausible bounce,
shade, window light, or reflected courtyard light.

## Direction-Only Prompt Format

Camera direction is a concise module.

It must be usable in image prompts, boards, and Flow prompt briefs without
accidentally instructing movement.

Use this format:

```text
CAMERA DIRECTION —
[shot type], [height], [angle], [camera side / view];
[subject framing and readable action];
[composition and foreground];
[background visibility and geography];
[light relationship]; [screen-direction / continuity lock].
```

Example:

```text
CAMERA DIRECTION — Medium Full, eye level, three-quarter front view from the
kitchen-door side; Mother framed from head to below knee as she rolls dough,
hands, board, and vessel fully visible; balanced frame with open work space
beside her, rear kitchen wall and east-window glow readable; soft morning light
from screen left; she faces screen right toward the stove, preserve established
pot and doorway positions.
```

Do not add movement verbs to this module.

Forbidden movement verbs include pan, tilt, push, pull, track, follow, orbit,
circle, crane, rise, descend, zoom, drift, sway, shake, and rack focus.

For Google Flow, write direction first and movement separately:

```text
Camera direction: Close-Up, eye level, three-quarter view of Father's hands
sorting seeds in a shallow brass bowl; bowl rim and textured seeds dominate the
lower frame, his dhoti edge and sunlit yard softly visible behind; right hand
enters from screen right, preserve hand continuity.

Movement: [resolved only under 20_Camera_Movement_Bible.md].
```

## Shot Type Catalogue

The following types are approved vocabulary.

Choose the simplest type that answers the shot's story question.

### Extreme Wide

**Definition:** The human subject is small or absent within a large expanse of
land, sky, settlement, water, or built environment.

**Purpose:** Establish scale, route, weather, season, isolation, belonging, or
the relationship between people and their village.

**Emotion:** Spaciousness, patience, quiet anticipation, renewal, distance, or
shared place.

**Use when:** Opening a location, showing a long walk, revealing monsoon
weather, locating field work, or giving an ending room to breathe.

**Do not use when:** A face, hand action, object state, or conversation line
must be understood.

**Character framing:** Full body is tiny; do not rely on identity or expression.

**Composition:** Use a clear path, roofline, field edge, waterline, or tree
mass to lead the eye; place people where they are discoverable, not lost.

**Background visibility:** Maximum; the environment is the subject.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong as a stable atmosphere or simple distant
activity shot; use only tiny approved human and weather actions.

**Common mistakes:** Drone-like height, unreadably small action, random new
buildings, empty generic landscape, overdramatic sky, or landscape without a
story reason.

**Positive example:** Extreme Wide, eye level from the village path: Father and
the dog are small figures walking screen left to right beside eastern paddy
fields; pond, low morning mist, and distant tiled roofs establish the route.

**Negative example:** Epic aerial over the village with tiny Father running
through stormy fields.

**Select when:** Place, journey, weather, or time is the message.

**Do not select when:** The viewer must read a human decision.

### Wide

**Definition:** A full environment view in which principal people are visible
and their actions can be broadly understood.

**Purpose:** Establish geography while preserving usable human activity.

**Emotion:** Openness, routine, social life, practical calm.

**Use when:** Beginning a scene, covering shared work, showing a courtyard,
tea stall, pond edge, or family arrangement.

**Do not use when:** The scene turns on a minute reaction or detailed craft.

**Character framing:** Full bodies with meaningful room around them.

**Composition:** Arrange people, work area, exits, and foreground depth so the
viewer understands who is where.

**Background visibility:** High; major landmarks and working surfaces remain
readable.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for restrained walking, washing, cooking,
or ambient village life; avoid many simultaneous independent actions.

**Common mistakes:** Overcrowding, subject too small, missing action axis,
generic backdrop, or cutting essential feet and tools.

**Positive example:** Wide, eye level from the courtyard edge: Mother at the
wash stone faces screen right, Father repairs a basket farther right, and the
house doorway sits behind them, making the shared morning geography clear.

**Negative example:** Wide low-angle view that turns a simple courtyard task
into an action-film tableau.

**Select when:** Relationships between people, work, and place matter.

**Do not select when:** One face or hand must carry the beat.

### Full

**Definition:** One person is framed approximately head to toe, with modest
context around the body.

**Purpose:** Show posture, clothing, body-language, and a complete practical
action.

**Emotion:** Grounded presence, readiness, modest confidence, everyday rhythm.

**Use when:** A character stands, walks, carries, sweeps, dresses, greets, or
first enters a smaller space.

**Do not use when:** The important information is a tiny hand action or subtle
eye change.

**Character framing:** Complete body; retain feet and ground contact.

**Composition:** Give travel and gaze room; include the relevant door, path,
work surface, or companion without shrinking the subject.

**Background visibility:** Moderate; enough to establish the immediate zone.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for one simple full-body action; protect
limbs from occlusion and avoid crowded crossing traffic.

**Common mistakes:** Cropped feet, exaggerated stride, mannequin pose, floating
body, or background that hides the silhouette.

**Positive example:** Full, eye level, three-quarter side view: Father carries
an empty woven basket from the rear yard toward screen right, both bare feet
and the kitchen threshold visible.

**Negative example:** Full-body heroic low angle of Father holding a farm tool
against a blazing sky.

**Select when:** Stance or full action tells the story.

**Do not select when:** The object alone or face alone is the point.

### Medium Full

**Definition:** A person is framed from approximately mid-thigh or knee upward,
with hands and the working area usually available.

**Purpose:** Balance body language, face, and practical work.

**Emotion:** Participation, warmth, useful concentration, conversational ease.

**Use when:** Cooking, washing, shelling, serving, carrying, or standing
conversation requires both expression and hands.

**Do not use when:** Feet establish movement or the action is too fine to read.

**Character framing:** Mid-thigh or knee to head; preserve both hands for work.

**Composition:** Keep tools in the lower frame and face in the upper third;
use a three-quarter angle for depth without hiding the task.

**Background visibility:** Moderate; immediate location cues remain visible.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Excellent for a single principal action and
small response.

**Common mistakes:** Cropping wrists, hiding the utensil, blocking face with
hands, or framing so close that the work surface vanishes.

**Positive example:** Medium Full, eye level, three-quarter front: Mother
grinds chutney at the stone, framed from below knee up; her hands, stone, and
window are visible while her calm concentration remains readable.

**Negative example:** Medium Full from directly behind Mother where neither her
face nor the grinding action can be understood.

**Select when:** Work and human presence share equal importance.

**Do not select when:** A scene needs wider geography or a closer reaction.

### Medium

**Definition:** A person is framed approximately from waist upward.

**Purpose:** Carry dialogue, gesture, simple interaction, and clear expression.

**Emotion:** Familiarity, calm attention, gentle social connection.

**Use when:** Conversation, instruction, greeting, listening, simple serving,
or a readable pause.

**Do not use when:** Full-body movement, foot placement, or fine object detail
is essential.

**Character framing:** Waist to head; keep active hands if they carry meaning.

**Composition:** Use a stable eye-level reverse pattern; allow look room toward
the other person or task.

**Background visibility:** Selective; retain a doorway, stove, courtyard, or
tree line as location proof.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Excellent for subtle head turns, hand gestures,
and quiet dialogue; avoid elaborate gesturing.

**Common mistakes:** Floating torso, chopped elbows, reverse-angle axis break,
or empty background with no place identity.

**Positive example:** Medium, eye level, over the near shoulder of Mother:
Father faces screen left while explaining how to sort the seeds; the bowl and
courtyard edge remain visible.

**Negative example:** Centered Medium with both speakers facing camera as if
posing for a poster.

**Select when:** The viewer should understand what a person says or hears.

**Do not select when:** The beat is entirely in the eyes or in the object.

### Medium Close-Up

**Definition:** A person is framed from upper chest or shoulders upward.

**Purpose:** Make a quiet emotional thought legible without invading privacy.

**Emotion:** Tenderness, contemplation, recognition, restrained concern, pride.

**Use when:** Listening, receiving news, a small realization, soft humor, or a
transition into emotional intimacy.

**Do not use when:** Hands, clothing condition, or location mechanics are
needed to understand the scene.

**Character framing:** Chest or shoulders to head; preserve headroom and look
room.

**Composition:** Prefer three-quarter view for natural facial modeling; use
background shapes that support rather than compete with the face.

**Background visibility:** Low to moderate; enough texture and light to retain
place and time.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for one subtle change in gaze, breath,
smile, or head position; identity references are especially important.

**Common mistakes:** Oversized eyes, melodramatic tears, beauty-filter skin,
hard backlight, or a sudden unexplained emotion.

**Positive example:** Medium Close-Up, eye level, three-quarter view: Mother
looks screen right from the kitchen doorway as she hears the child's small
success, warm courtyard bounce keeping her expression gentle and readable.

**Negative example:** Tearful, high-contrast Medium Close-Up implying tragedy
during an ordinary meal.

**Select when:** Emotion is present but remains socially contained.

**Do not select when:** An extreme emotional emphasis would distort the beat.

### Close-Up

**Definition:** The frame is primarily face, or one object/hand area with
little surrounding context.

**Purpose:** Direct attention to a decisive small expression, a spoken thought,
or a meaningful object interaction.

**Emotion:** Intimacy, care, realization, sensory attention, quiet importance.

**Use when:** A character reacts after established context, a hand gives food,
or a small object changes story state.

**Do not use when:** The audience has not yet been oriented, or the moment is
routine with no specific detail worth isolating.

**Character framing:** Face from upper chest to head, or a self-contained hand
and object composition.

**Composition:** Maintain eye line; use side light or soft bounce to preserve
natural facial planes; never crop so tightly that identity becomes unstable.

**Background visibility:** Low; use recognizable colour, texture, or bokeh-like
painted forms only as continuity support.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Good with approved identity references and one
small action; risky for anatomy and facial drift, so review strictly.

**Common mistakes:** Glamour pose, exaggerated eye size, lip-sync assumptions,
unmotivated tears, missing hand anatomy, or background identity drift.

**Positive example:** Close-Up, eye level: Father's weathered right hand places
the final measured seeds into a small cloth packet; the brass bowl edge and
earth-toned yard blur behind it.

**Negative example:** Extreme beauty Close-Up of Mother with decorative wind in
her hair while she is indoors grinding.

**Select when:** One detail changes the viewer's understanding.

**Do not select when:** The shot only repeats information already clear.

### Extreme Close-Up

**Definition:** A very small facial area, hand area, or object surface fills
most of the frame.

**Purpose:** Isolate a precise sensory or emotional detail.

**Emotion:** Delicacy, concentration, tactile attention, fragile recognition.

**Use when:** A fingertip tests rice texture, a bindi is adjusted, steam beads
on a lid, or eyes register a contained realization.

**Do not use when:** Identity, location, action sequence, or emotional context
is not already clear.

**Character framing:** Partial face, hand, or object only.

**Composition:** Keep one readable plane; use texture and light as information,
not ornament.

**Background visibility:** Minimal; continuity comes from known colour,
material, and adjacent coverage.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Use sparingly; high risk for anatomy, object
shape, and unwanted morphing. Prefer a still if the change is not simple.

**Common mistakes:** Enlarged pores or hyperrealism, distorted fingers, abstract
unreadable texture, horror-like eye framing, or false intensity.

**Positive example:** Extreme Close-Up, side-lit: Mother's fingertips separate
a few soaked lentils on the stone, water sheen and grain texture clear.

**Negative example:** Extreme Close-Up of an eye with a dramatic reflected
storm during a peaceful kitchen scene.

**Select when:** Tactile detail is the story beat.

**Do not select when:** The detail can be read in a normal close-up.

### Over-the-Shoulder

**Definition:** The camera looks past the near person's shoulder, head edge, or
torso toward the primary subject.

**Purpose:** Preserve relationship, listening, and spatial connection in
dialogue or instruction.

**Emotion:** Inclusion, attention, trust, gentle distance, shared perspective.

**Use when:** Covering conversation, teaching, serving, watching work, or
showing a response to another person.

**Do not use when:** The foreground person would hide the key face, object, or
action, or when the axis is not established.

**Character framing:** Near shoulder soft but recognisable; far subject usually
Medium or Medium Close-Up.

**Composition:** Keep the near shoulder on the correct screen edge and leave
the far subject look room toward it.

**Background visibility:** Moderate; shared space should remain identifiable.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Good for minor far-subject movement; keep the
foreground stable to prevent unwanted head motion or occlusion.

**Common mistakes:** Random shoulder ownership, shoulder blocking eyes, crossing
the axis, foreground too sharp, or fake depth that separates the people.

**Positive example:** OTS from behind Father's left shoulder: Mother, facing
screen left, explains the spice mixture beside the stove; her hands and bowl
remain visible under soft window light.

**Negative example:** OTS from an unidentified stranger's shoulder in a family
scene with no established observer.

**Select when:** Relationship matters as much as dialogue.

**Do not select when:** A clean object demonstration is needed.

### POV

**Definition:** The frame represents what an established character can directly
see from their physical position.

**Purpose:** Let the viewer share a simple discovery, choice, task, or gaze.

**Emotion:** Curiosity, care, anticipation, sensory presence.

**Use when:** Looking into a seed basket, choosing a mango, seeing a prepared
meal arrive, or noticing rain at the doorway.

**Do not use when:** It becomes a disembodied game-camera view, violates the
character's actual height, or removes needed social context.

**Character framing:** Usually no face; optional natural hand edge only when it
belongs to the action.

**Composition:** Match the person's standing, seated, or child height; retain
natural peripheral location clues.

**Background visibility:** What the character genuinely sees; avoid impossible
wide field of view.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Good for a simple hand-to-object action; risky
if it requires complex hands, sudden turns, or walking.

**Common mistakes:** Fisheye, floating body, wrong height, visible unrelated
hands, or action-game urgency.

**Positive example:** POV from Mother's seated height toward the grinding stone:
her hands enter naturally at the lower edge as she checks the smoothness of the
paste; the kitchen floor and window light give orientation.

**Negative example:** Fast first-person rush through village lanes toward a
problem.

**Select when:** The act of seeing is the beat.

**Do not select when:** The audience needs to watch the person's expression.

### Eye Level

**Definition:** The camera lens is approximately level with the primary
subject's eyes or natural working height.

**Purpose:** Provide neutral, respectful, observational coverage.

**Emotion:** Equality, familiarity, calm truthfulness.

**Use when:** Default for people, conversations, practical work, and most
object interactions.

**Do not use when:** A higher or lower view is necessary to clarify a surface,
route, group arrangement, or approved story relationship.

**Character framing:** Any size; adjust camera height to child, seated, or
standing person rather than forcing adult height.

**Composition:** Keep verticals natural and horizon stable.

**Background visibility:** Determined by shot size, typically natural and
unforced.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Best default; stable, legible, and low risk.

**Common mistakes:** Calling every neutral shot eye level while camera is
actually above a seated worker, or using it as an excuse for flat composition.

**Positive example:** Eye level with Father seated at the repair stool, Medium
Full framing, hands and basket visible.

**Negative example:** Camera at standing adult height looking down on a seated
child while labelled eye level.

**Select when:** No perspective emphasis is required.

**Do not select when:** The task itself cannot be seen from this height.

### High Angle

**Definition:** The camera looks moderately downward at the subject from above
their eye line, while remaining plausible for a human observer.

**Purpose:** Clarify a work surface, group arrangement, a child's task, or
quiet vulnerability without degradation.

**Emotion:** Gentle observation, care, order, smallness within a safe space.

**Use when:** Showing food preparation on a low surface, a seated child
learning, objects arranged on a mat, or a person resting.

**Do not use when:** It makes a person look helpless, humiliated, diminished,
or watched.

**Character framing:** Usually Medium Full to Close-Up; protect face and hands.

**Composition:** Let the work surface lead into the face; keep perspective
natural.

**Background visibility:** Moderate; floor, mat, or immediate environment often
helps orientation.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Good for stable tabletop and seated actions.

**Common mistakes:** Security-camera view, overly steep pitch, tiny head, or
unwanted dominance over child or elder.

**Positive example:** High Angle from just above Mother's shoulder, showing her
hands arranging banana leaves and the serving bowls on the floor mat.

**Negative example:** Severe high angle making a child alone in the courtyard
appear abandoned.

**Select when:** The surface and arrangement need more clarity than the face.

**Do not select when:** The emotional relationship should remain equal.

### Low Angle

**Definition:** The camera looks moderately upward from below the subject's eye
line while retaining a natural human observer position.

**Purpose:** Reveal a task against a roofline or tree, show a child looking up,
or add quiet grounded stature when context justifies it.

**Emotion:** Warm admiration, discovery, rooted presence.

**Use when:** A child looks up at a hanging lantern, a person reaches a shelf,
or a banyan canopy is viewed from the ground.

**Do not use when:** It creates heroism, threat, sexualisation, intimidation, or
action-film grandeur.

**Character framing:** Full, Medium Full, or environmental; avoid facial
distortion.

**Composition:** Keep verticals controlled; include grounding foreground such as
earth, threshold, or basket edge.

**Background visibility:** Moderate to high, often roof, canopy, or sky.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Moderate; keep body and object geometry simple.

**Common mistakes:** Extreme chin view, towering adult, sky-only background, or
unmotivated dominance.

**Positive example:** Low Angle from a child's natural height: Father reaches
up to take down a drying bundle beneath the eave, with the tiled roof explaining
the view.

**Negative example:** Low angle of Father holding a sickle like an action hero.

**Select when:** Natural height difference or overhead task motivates it.

**Do not select when:** Eye level would express the scene more truthfully.

### Top Down

**Definition:** The camera looks nearly straight down onto a small work surface,
mat, arrangement, or contained action from a plausible close overhead position.

**Purpose:** Explain process, ingredient order, object placement, or a shared
meal arrangement.

**Emotion:** Care, craft, order, sensory satisfaction.

**Use when:** Cutting ingredients, sorting grains, laying banana leaves,
assembling a meal, or arranging flowers.

**Do not use when:** Face or body language is the primary meaning, or when the
surface geometry cannot be kept stable.

**Character framing:** Hands and forearms may enter; faces usually absent.

**Composition:** Keep edges parallel where appropriate; place ingredients with
clear working order and realistic mess level.

**Background visibility:** Low; mat, board, counter, or floor surface is the
background.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for one repeated hand action; review
finger count, tool orientation, and ingredient continuity.

**Common mistakes:** Impossible floating utensils, mirrored hands, pristine
generic food, wrong knife hand, or visual clutter.

**Positive example:** Top Down over a floor mat: Mother's right hand slices
okra on a wooden board, sorted pieces in a steel bowl at upper right, knife and
hand positions consistent with the prior shot.

**Negative example:** Perfectly symmetrical food flat-lay with no evidence of
human preparation.

**Select when:** Process order is more important than the cook's face.

**Do not select when:** The shot must carry dialogue or reaction.

### Bird's Eye

**Definition:** A high, broad overhead view of an entire usable area, route, or
group arrangement.

**Purpose:** Establish spatial layout, communal pattern, path relation, or the
shape of a place.

**Emotion:** Calm overview, rhythm, connectedness, seasonal scale.

**Use when:** Establishing a field pattern, festival preparation layout, a
market arrangement, or the relationship of courtyard zones.

**Do not use when:** It requires unavailable aerial logic, feels like a drone,
or substitutes spectacle for story.

**Character framing:** People are small but legible as positions and actions,
not faces.

**Composition:** Use paths, field divisions, rooftops, water edges, and groups
as graphic but truthful patterns.

**Background visibility:** Maximum within the selected area.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Use sparingly; stable overview with limited
motion is safest. A generated aerial view must remain period-correct.

**Common mistakes:** Modern drone look, impossible height, tiny unreadable
people, newly invented roads, or map-like sterility.

**Positive example:** Bird's Eye, restrained high viewpoint over the known rear
yard: washing area, kitchen entrance, drying line, and vegetable patch show
the family's morning workflow without inventing new structures.

**Negative example:** Sweeping drone panorama above the entire village for a
minor dialogue scene.

**Select when:** Layout itself tells the story.

**Do not select when:** A wide or high angle can provide the needed geography.

### Dutch Angle (Rare)

**Definition:** The camera horizon is intentionally tilted.

**Purpose:** This is not a normal Village Stories tool.

**Emotion:** At most, a brief, specifically approved subjective imbalance.

**Use when:** Only when an approved story event genuinely requires a contained
moment of disorientation and higher canon permits it.

**Do not use when:** Ordinary cooking, conversation, weather, comedy, a child,
an animal, routine work, or any scene can be served by normal composition.

**Character framing:** Any, but the tilt must not distort anatomy or dignity.

**Composition:** Tilt minimally and preserve readable spatial facts.

**Background visibility:** Enough to make the tilt intentional rather than an
accidental crooked frame.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Poor; it can cause warped architecture, unstable
horizons, and false drama.

**Common mistakes:** Using it to make an image “cinematic,” tilting every
emotional scene, or turning calm life into suspense.

**Positive example:** Not selected. Replace with eye level unless a written,
approved exception identifies the exact subjective reason.

**Negative example:** Dutch-angle shot of Mother noticing rain on the roof.

**Select when:** Written director and canon approval are attached.

**Do not select when:** Almost always.

### Macro Detail

**Definition:** A close study of material, texture, small object part, food,
water, cloth, plant, or tool surface.

**Purpose:** Deliver tactile information and ASMR-compatible visual attention.

**Emotion:** Sensory calm, craft, care, abundance, seasonal presence.

**Use when:** Showing grinding paste, rice grains, woven fiber, rain on a leaf,
steam on a vessel, or hands completing a precise craft step.

**Do not use when:** It has no narrative or sensory function, or the material
would become unrecognisable without context.

**Character framing:** Usually hands or no character; never use macro solely as
facial glamour.

**Composition:** Isolate one texture and one action; retain a recognisable edge
of tool or vessel when needed.

**Background visibility:** Very low.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Moderate; simple material motion is safer than
complex transformations.

**Common mistakes:** Hyperreal grossness, incorrect food texture, unexplained
abstract surface, extra fingers, or artificial liquid motion.

**Positive example:** Macro Detail of coconut chutney being pressed smooth by
the grinding stone, tiny flecks and wet stone texture visible.

**Negative example:** Macro of sweating skin to manufacture intensity during
routine cooking.

**Select when:** Texture adds story, rhythm, or sensory meaning.

**Do not select when:** The viewer needs to know who, where, or why.

### Reaction

**Definition:** A camera direction selected to record a specific person's
visible response after an established stimulus.

**Purpose:** Let the emotional consequence land without explanatory dialogue.

**Emotion:** Depends on story, always proportionate and grounded.

**Use when:** A child succeeds, food is appreciated, an elder remembers,
rain begins, or a small problem resolves.

**Do not use when:** No stimulus has been established, the reaction repeats
dialogue, or the response would be exaggerated.

**Character framing:** Usually Medium Close-Up or Close-Up; choose Medium when
gesture or relation to another person matters.

**Composition:** Keep the stimulus direction in the character's eye line and
preserve the conversation axis.

**Background visibility:** Low to moderate; include enough context to identify
the moment.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for one subtle change only: glance,
softening expression, small smile, or lowered eyes.

**Common mistakes:** Reaction before cause, theatrical shock, prolonged crying,
or cutting away before the response is readable.

**Positive example:** Reaction Medium Close-Up, eye level: Father looks screen
left toward the child’s finished drawing, then allows a small proud smile; the
veranda wall retains the scene location.

**Negative example:** Sudden wide-eyed gasp after Mother places a normal meal
on the mat.

**Select when:** A person's change in understanding is the beat.

**Do not select when:** The action itself says enough.

### Environmental Establishing

**Definition:** A location-led frame that identifies a specific place, its
time, condition, and usable geography before or between human beats.

**Purpose:** Orient the audience and make the village a persistent lived-in
world rather than a generic backdrop.

**Emotion:** Arrival, familiarity, seasonal memory, transition, calm.

**Use when:** Opening a new scene, returning after time passage, marking
weather, locating a village institution, or giving an edit breathing room.

**Do not use when:** The audience already knows the place and a human beat is
more urgent.

**Character framing:** Optional; people may be absent or incidental.

**Composition:** Feature canonical landmark, architecture, path, water, field,
or working evidence; include a clear entry or visual route where useful.

**Background visibility:** Maximum; environment is primary.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Excellent for subtle weather, leaves, smoke,
water, or approved distant routine activity.

**Common mistakes:** Generic “Indian village,” modern intrusions, impossible
landmark placement, postcard prettiness without lived detail, or wrong season.

**Positive example:** Environmental Establishing, wide eye-level view of the
tea stall from the north lane: tiled awning, known bench, damp monsoon road,
and restrained morning activity establish the location before dialogue.

**Negative example:** Stylised sunset village skyline with no canonical
landmark or usable scene entrance.

**Select when:** The audience needs place, time, or transition information.

**Do not select when:** It delays a clear existing scene without adding meaning.

### Object Detail

**Definition:** A framed view of one approved object, its relevant state, and
its relationship to the current action.

**Purpose:** Track story-critical object continuity, demonstrate use, or make a
small practical change readable.

**Emotion:** Care, anticipation, utility, memory, tactile satisfaction.

**Use when:** Showing a key, seed packet, brass vessel, repaired basket,
ingredient bowl, folded cloth, or serving portion.

**Do not use when:** The object has no current story role or could be understood
within a broader shot.

**Character framing:** Optional hand, torso edge, or no person.

**Composition:** Show enough object silhouette, material, scale, and surrounding
surface to identify it and its state.

**Background visibility:** Low to moderate; preserve its known location.

**Google Flow compatibility:** Strong for one simple interaction; maintain exact
object features and position.

**Common mistakes:** Swapping material, adding modern branding, wrong scale,
changing fill level between shots, or treating a prop as decorative stock.

**Positive example:** Object Detail, high three-quarter view: the repaired
woven basket rests beside Father’s stool with the newly tied reed end visible,
earth floor and his bare foot confirming scale and location.

**Negative example:** Glossy isolated product shot of a brass bowl on a black
studio background.

**Select when:** The object changes state or carries the next story beat.

**Do not select when:** It merely interrupts useful human coverage.

## Shot Selection Guide

### Cooking Preparation

Start with an Environmental Establishing or Wide only if the kitchen or meal
phase is newly entered.

Use Medium Full as the default cooking shot.

It keeps face, hands, utensils, and immediate kitchen geography readable.

Use High Angle when the low work surface needs clarification.

Use Top Down only for a short ingredient arrangement or sequence step.

Use Object Detail for the vessel, ingredient, or fuel only when its state
matters.

Use Macro Detail for texture only after the task is established.

Use a Reaction only after a meaningful smell, taste, help, or result.

Do not make every cut a macro food montage.

### Cutting

Establish the cutter, board, hand dominance, and ingredient state in Medium
Full or High Angle.

Use Top Down for a clearly ordered cutting action.

Use Object Detail or Macro Detail for one decisive texture or completed pile.

Keep blade direction, hand position, board, bowl, and ingredient amount
continuous.

Do not use a face Close-Up while the cutting action itself remains unclear.

Do not use movement language to compensate for unreadable hand geometry.

### Grinding

Use Medium Full, eye level or a gentle High Angle, as the master direction.

The viewer must see worker, stone, hands, paste, and plausible kitchen context.

Use Close-Up or Macro Detail only for texture after the grinding process is
understood.

Keep the grinding stone orientation, hand rhythm, vessel location, and paste
progression consistent.

Do not frame the stone so tightly that it becomes an abstract object.

### Serving

Begin with Medium Full or Wide to establish recipients and serving layout.

Use High Angle or Top Down for banana leaf or mat arrangement.

Use Object Detail for a serving spoon, portion, or vessel only when it marks a
relationship or completion.

Use Reaction coverage for a quiet appreciation after serving, not before.

Preserve who receives what, hand direction, seating position, and vessel state.

Do not shoot serving as luxury-food advertising.

### Village Introduction

Begin with Environmental Establishing or Extreme Wide when geography, weather,
or season carries the opening.

Use Wide when identifiable people and routine activity should anchor the place.

Select a canonical landmark and a usable route into the next scene.

Use eye level or a restrained natural-height view.

Bird's Eye requires a documented production reason and must never become a
default drone opener.

Avoid a random montage of picturesque details that does not locate the story.

### Conversation

Establish both people and the action axis in a Wide or two-person Medium.

Cover principal speech with eye-level Medium or Medium Close-Up.

Use matching OTS directions when the relationship benefits from visible
listening.

Cut to Reaction only when the response changes the beat.

Return to a two-shot or wider geography after emotional tightening when useful.

Keep look directions complementary.

Keep background objects and light direction consistent across reverses.

Do not cross the axis for visual variety.

### Emotional Ending

Choose the smallest honest frame that lets the final feeling remain.

Use Medium Close-Up for a contained response.

Use Full or Wide when the ending is about companionship, departure, or place.

Use Environmental Establishing or Extreme Wide when quiet continuity of the
village is the final idea.

Allow negative space toward the character's gaze or onward path.

Do not use forced tears, extreme facial framing, low-angle heroism, sunset
spectacle, Dutch angles, or unexplained symbolic objects.

### Farming And Garden Work

Use Wide to establish field row, garden patch, task, and directional route.

Use Full for carrying, bending, watering, or moving between rows.

Use Medium Full for sowing, sorting, tying, or speaking while working.

Use Object Detail for seed, tool condition, or plant result only when needed.

Respect sun direction, soil condition, season, and the documented field side.

### Washing And Water Work

Use Wide or Medium Full to establish water source, safe working position, and
the relevant vessel.

Use High Angle for hands and garment washing only when it does not make the
person feel observed or diminished.

Use Macro Detail for water on cloth, soap, or vessel only as a sensory accent.

Keep water level, wetness, vessel count, and clothing condition continuous.

### Repair And Craft

Use Medium Full as the default to retain maker, tool, object, and concentration.

Use Object Detail for the repair point.

Use Macro Detail sparingly for fiber, knot, wood grain, or stitch detail.

Use Reaction only for the maker's quiet satisfaction after the repair is clear.

Do not obscure the actual repair with decorative foreground.

### Child Learning Or Play

Put the camera at the child's natural eye or working height.

Use Full or Medium Full for play that depends on body and ground.

Use Medium for a parent or elder's nearby guidance.

Avoid high angles that isolate or belittle the child.

Avoid low angles that make the child theatrically heroic.

Never use camera urgency to invent risk.

### Animal Presence

Frame the family dog as a gentle household companion in the shared activity.

Use Wide or Medium Full when the dog adds domestic geography.

Use Object Detail only for an approved interaction such as a bowl, leash-free
resting place, or paw near a familiar person.

Never use tight aggressive framing, chase perspective, threat angles, or
dramatic POV.

### Rain, Wind, And Seasonal Change

Use Environmental Establishing to make weather legible at a place.

Use Wide for people adapting naturally to the condition.

Use Object Detail or Macro Detail for drops on leaves, wet threshold, rippling
water, or covered vessel only when the sensory detail serves the transition.

Do not turn weather into disaster imagery unless canon explicitly requires it.

## Character Direction Guidance

Frame Father with grounded, natural proportion.

Show his lean athletic build, broad shoulders, strong forearms, neatly trimmed
black mustache, and recurring hair only when the selected size can genuinely
support them.

Do not use low angles to make him imposing.

When Father works, retain hands, relevant object, and credible body mechanics.

Frame Mother with the same stable recurring identity.

Preserve her bindi, left-nostril gold nose pin, thin glass bangles, small gold
jhumkas, and one simple thali chain when their visibility is appropriate to the
frame.

Do not force jewellery into every close shot.

Do not omit or multiply documented features when a clear face or costume view
requires them.

Use respectful practical composition for all characters.

No body part should be isolated without a story, craft, or sensory reason.

Do not turn domestic work into glamour imagery.

## Location And Seasonal Direction Guidance

Use a recognisable canonical location marker in the first useful wide or
establishing direction for a scene.

Do not rebuild a location from whichever attractive background the tool offers.

Choose camera side with the documented house, yard, field, pond, banyan, ghat,
and canal orientations in mind.

Winter-like coolness, summer dust, monsoon wetness, harvest colour, and time of
day must come from approved episode facts.

Weather changes what can be seen, not the underlying location map.

Wet ground, clouds, shadows, smoke, laundry, roof use, crop state, and water
level must agree with the scene ledger.

## Shorts Guidance

Shorts need immediate visual comprehension.

Place the primary readable subject inside the central crop-safe area.

Use Wide or Environmental Establishing only when it communicates location in
one beat.

Prefer Medium Full, Medium, Object Detail, or Top Down for practical actions.

Protect headroom and hands against vertical crops.

Do not use an Extreme Wide whose human story disappears on a phone screen.

Keep direction changes economical.

A short may move from place to task to reaction, but each frame must answer one
clear question.

## Long-Form Guidance

Long-form episodes may spend more time establishing relation, place, and pace.

Use a wider master before fragmented coverage when geography changes.

Vary shot size according to new information, not visual restlessness.

Let a Wide hold a routine action when the environment carries comfort or rhythm.

Reserve close framing for actual emotional or tactile turns.

Return to place between intimate beats when it restores temporal breathing room.

## Storyboard Workflow

1. Read approved scene purpose, action, location, time, and emotional boundary.

2. Identify what the viewer must know first: place, person, work, object,
relationship, or reaction.

3. Establish the action axis and screen direction.

4. Choose the minimum shot type that makes that information readable.

5. Set height and angle based on the actual physical situation.

6. Name camera side, foreground, background, and lighting relationship.

7. Check the preceding and following shot for duplicated information, axis
breaks, and object or hand reversals.

8. Write the direction-only prompt module.

9. Add movement only through Document 20.

10. Record the approved direction in the shot ledger before generation.

## Image Prompt Workflow

Load only canon facts that constrain the selected frame.

Attach recurring character references when face identity is important.

Attach location references when geography, layout, or landmark accuracy matters.

Write the direction module before action and style reinforcement.

Generate candidates that differ only in the unresolved camera variable.

Do not revise character identity, weather, camera side, and shot size all at
once.

Approve a direction only after it is readable beside adjacent planned shots.

## Google Flow Direction Workflow

Use an approved still or two approved stills whose camera direction is already
known.

State the still's camera direction explicitly in the Flow brief.

Preserve its shot size, camera side, angle, screen direction, and lighting
relationship unless an approved movement instruction changes framing under
Document 20.

Do not ask Flow to discover coverage.

Do not ask Flow to turn a close-up into an establishing shot.

Do not ask Flow to circle an action merely to reveal missing geography.

If the source still lacks the required camera information, return to image
generation or storyboard planning.

## Direction QA

Reject or revise a shot if any answer is no.

```text
Does the selected framing answer the shot's actual story question?
Is the camera distance named?
Is the camera height named or unambiguously implied?
Is the camera angle named?
Is the camera side or screen direction clear?
Does the frame preserve the established action axis?
Are face, hands, feet, and object visibility appropriate to the action?
Is the required background visible but not competing?
Does the location remain canonical and period-correct?
Does light direction agree with time, weather, and geography?
Does the framing preserve dignity and avoid false drama?
Is any intimate framing earned by the preceding story beat?
Is the camera direction free of movement instructions?
Has movement, if any, been deferred to Document 20?
Does the frame survive its intended Shorts or long-form crop?
Does the direction support the preceding and following cut?
```

## Common Direction Failures

**“Cinematic” without a shot type:** Replace with a precise distance, height,
angle, side, and background instruction.

**A beautiful but generic village:** Add the canonical location, orientation,
landmark, season, and route.

**Hands disappear during work:** Use Medium Full, High Angle, Top Down, or
Object Detail according to the task.

**A conversation feels reversed:** Restore the established axis and matching
look directions.

**A face is emotionally unreadable:** Move from Wide or Medium Full to Medium
or Medium Close-Up only if the story now needs the response.

**A close-up feels intrusive:** Return to Medium Close-Up, include contextual
background, or use a wider reaction.

**The action looks dramatic:** Replace hero low angle, Dutch angle, harsh
contrast, or invasive close framing with eye-level observation.

**The Flow clip changes its camera:** Regenerate from a direction-locked still
and use Document 20 for any approved motion.

**The place has changed between cuts:** Re-establish with a canonical wide,
then correct source assets rather than hiding the error with close-ups.

## Final Operating Rule

Choose the camera position that lets the audience understand the truthful,
approved moment with the least visual force.

When in doubt, use an eye-level Medium Full or Medium from the established side
of the action, retain the useful environment, preserve hands and objects, and
let the people and work speak for themselves.

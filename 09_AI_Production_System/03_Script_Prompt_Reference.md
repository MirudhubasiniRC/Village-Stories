# Detailed Script Prompt Reference

## Purpose

This is the permanent production manual and copy-ready master prompt for turning
an approved Village Stories episode premise into a production-ready script.

It converts a settled story decision into observable scenes, economical dialogue,
narration, sound opportunities, continuity records, and downstream visual
requirements. It does not develop a new premise, create canon, or replace a
storyboard, image prompt, Google Flow motion prompt, edit, or release plan.

A production-ready script is a reliable instruction set for making the approved
episode. It must let a director, storyboard artist, sound lead, editor, and AI
production operator understand what happens, why it happens, what must remain
true, and what the viewer should be able to see and hear.

---

## Script Philosophy

Village Stories finds feeling in truthful daily life: care, work, waiting,
weather, attention, learning, small repairs, food, animal companionship, and
family rhythm.

The script does not inflate a small premise into spectacle. It makes the
practical cause clear, follows a proportionate response, and gives the final
feeling enough visible space to settle.

Write action before explanation.

Write evidence before interpretation.

Write a known village, not a generic nostalgic setting.

---

## Guiding Principle

> Script the smallest truthful sequence in which practical care visibly changes
> the day, while preserving the known world around it.

The script succeeds when a viewer can understand the condition, action,
adjustment, and emotional rest even with sparse dialogue and muted sound.

---

## Scope And Use

Use this manual only after:

* a premise has been approved for development;
* the intended format is known or has an approved range;
* the applicable canon sources are available;
* open factual questions have named owners; and
* the premise has a practical centre, emotional centre, and honest ending.

Use it before:

* storyboarding;
* image-prompt assembly;
* Google Flow task creation;
* voice recording;
* final sound design;
* thumbnail selection; and
* title, description, and publishing approval.

Do not use it to:

* solve a missing canon fact with plausible prose;
* change an approved premise silently;
* invent a recurring person, relationship, location, route, object, skill,
  cultural practice, past event, or future event;
* make an incomplete connected episode feel complete only through narration;
* write a full visual-generation prompt or animation instruction;
* conceal a production problem with vague “cinematic” language; or
* add drama, risk, mystery, or cliffhanger material for platform retention.

```text

↓
09_AI_Production_System/ — reusable prompt architecture and AI handoff control
↓

```

When instructions conflict:

1. Name the disputed fact or decision.
2. Locate the highest applicable authority.
3. Apply that source without averaging contradictory instructions.
4. Record the conflict in the script validation result.
5. Return the affected material to its owning stage for clarification or revision.

A generated idea, prior draft, reference image, or visually attractive AI output
never becomes evidence that an unsupported fact is allowed.

If a required fact is `OPEN`, do not hide it in lyrical language. Mark the
affected item `HOLD FOR CANON OR RESEARCH`.

## Permanent Series Locks

Every script generated with this manual preserves the following locks.

### Time And Place Lock

* The series is set in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu.
* The exact year may be flexible; the historical period is not.
* Work, travel, communication, household routines, and problem-solving require
  believable local time, effort, and available materials.
* Every episode names a season, time of day, weather condition, and visible
  practical consequence where relevant.
* No mobile phones, internet, digital payments, social media, GPS, current
  slang, contemporary appliances, LED lighting, current vehicles, modern
  packaging, or unsupported modern infrastructure.

### Family Lock

The established core family is:

* Mother;
* Father;
* Elder Brother;
* Younger Daughter; and
* the German Shepherd Family Dog.

Do not use all five merely to make the episode feel like an ensemble piece.
Each present family member requires a source-backed reason to be in the place,
at that moment, contributing through an age-appropriate and documented role.

The Family Dog is a gentle, loyal household companion. It may wait, follow,
rest, observe, respond softly to a familiar sound, play naturally, or receive
ordinary care. It must never become a police, military, attack, rescue, chase,
missing-pet, injury, danger, or threat device.

### Character Truth Lock

* Mother is capable, attentive, practical, and humane.
* Father is calm, hardworking, observant, and emotionally grounded.
* Elder Brother may be eager, distracted, proud, or learning; correction stays
  dignified and age-appropriate.
* Younger Daughter may be curious, direct, observant, and safely included; she
  does not carry adult burdens.
* Individual character documentation controls appearance, clothing, habits,
  language, relationships, ability, and continuity whenever it is more specific.
* No character changes personality, knowledge, competence, or relationship
  role merely to make a plot shortcut work.

### Style And Tone Lock

Village Stories is an original anime illustration world for the entire frame,
matching `11_Assets/Character_References/Father_Canon_Design_Reference.png`:

* painterly 2D anime-inspired characters with natural South Indian features;
* thin clean line art and soft cel-style painted shading;
* warm cinematic lighting and handcrafted animation aesthetic;
* culturally specific, lived-in early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu; and
* warm, observant slice-of-life feeling with restrained emotion.

The script must provide concrete images that support this identity. It does not
authorize generic “cinematic” spectacle, named-studio imitation, hyper-realism,
plastic CGI, chibi comedy, action-camera language, or decorative poverty.

### Gentle Story Lock

Use one ordinary practical action and one proportionate source of pressure.

Movement may arise from weather, timing, routine, a small object condition,
care, learning, memory, a harmless misunderstanding, or community rhythm.

Do not use villains, violence, crime, panic, humiliation, severe illness,
family rupture, dangerous rescue, traumatic loss, child endangerment, animal
distress, or false emergency.

The practical result may be imperfect. The episode's immediate emotional
question must settle before the ending.

## Variables That Cannot Override Canon

All variables below are selection constraints only.

They may choose among supplied, approved options. They may not alter, extend,
reinterpret, or replace canon, the approved premise, or a higher authority.

Variables may not:

* add a modern solution to a period problem;
* make a character act against their packet;
* make the Dog carry stakes or solve the story;
* change a documented location's layout or purpose;
* substitute an unapproved prop, material, route, or practice;
* turn a calm premise into a danger hook;
* make an optional continuation replace current emotional closure;
* convert a Short into a long-form episode through filler; or
* convert a long-form episode into a rushed montage to meet a target runtime.

If a variable conflicts with the immutable locks or source-backed facts, the
variable loses. State the conflict and return `REVISE` or `HOLD`.

## Script Assembly Workflow

### Step 1 — Confirm The Approved Story Contract

State the approved premise in one sentence:

```text
When [practical condition] affects [character's task or need],
[documented people] respond through [canon-safe action],
arriving at [grounded emotional rest].
```

Do not begin scenes until the script can state:

* the ordinary condition;
* the practical need;
* the one primary gentle pressure;
* the response that earns the result;
* the visible ending; and
* the format-specific viewer promise.

Return the premise for revision if the script would need a new fact, stronger
conflict, rescue, coincidence, or unexplained emotional speech to function.

### Step 2 — Establish The Continuity Baseline

Record the opening state before drafting:

* current location and documented sub-area;
* time, season, weather, light direction, and surface condition;
* present characters, clothing, posture, emotion, and relative positions;
* object owner, amount, condition, orientation, and placement;
* ongoing task state;
* current sound environment; and
* facts inherited from previous material.

Every later scene must visibly preserve or deliberately change this baseline.

### Step 3 — Build The Episode Spine

Use the functional chain:

```text
Active opening condition
→ orientation and intention
→ preparation or meaningful process
→ gentle turn or recognition
→ practical response
→ visible payoff
→ emotional rest
→ optional truthful continuation seed
```

This is a function chain, not a mandatory scene count. Combine functions only
when the viewer can still see cause and effect.

### Step 4 — Assign Scene Purpose

Give every scene one primary function:

* hook;
* orientation;
* intention;
* preparation;
* process;
* turn;
* response;
* payoff;
* rest; or
* optional continuation seed.

If a scene has no primary function, remove it, merge it, or return to the
episode spine.

### Step 5 — Write Scene Cards Before Prose

For each scene, define observable facts before writing action lines or dialogue.

```text
Scene ID and intended duration:
Primary purpose:
Location and orientation:
Time / season / weather / light:
Characters present and why:
Start state:
One primary action:
Visible change:
Emotional movement:
Featured object facts:
Sound decision:
Camera intention:
End state:
Transition and next-scene handoff:
Flow and reference requirements:
```

The scene card controls the script. A beautiful paragraph cannot replace a
missing start state, action, result, or handoff.

### Step 6 — Write Observable Scene Action

Write what a viewer can see, hear, or infer from grounded behaviour.

Prefer:

> Mother tests the damp wood beside the weak fire. It catches briefly, then
> settles into a thin line of smoke.

Avoid:

> Mother feels the whole morning becoming difficult.

The first example gives direction, performance, sound, prop state, and a
visible cause. The second can be supported only after the physical evidence is
on screen.

### Step 7 — Add Dialogue And Narration Last

Dialogue and narration are support layers. They must not repair missing visual
logic or announce emotions that action can already express.

Use a line only when it:

* clarifies a practical choice;
* responds specifically to another person;
* reveals a relationship beat action cannot fully carry;
* provides a necessary, verified cultural or procedural fact;
* improves orientation without slowing the episode; or
* creates a natural pause, acknowledgement, or invitation.

### Step 8 — Add Sound, Silence, And ASMR Intent

Every scene needs one deliberate sound decision:

* dominant natural sound;
* natural sound with restrained music support;
* music-forward transition with retained environmental evidence; or
* intentional quiet.

Name the plausible sound source: rain on a roof, water in a vessel, cloth,
footsteps, birds, insects, wind, fire, ladle against brass, grinding, a gate,
or the Dog's ordinary movement.

Do not script a sound effect merely to manufacture suspense or sentiment.

### Step 9 — Add Camera And Visual Handoff Facts

The script is not a rigid shot list. It must still state enough visual intent
for production to board and generate the scene faithfully.

For every scene, include:

* the focal action or held image;
* useful scale or perspective when essential;
* camera side and screen direction when continuity-critical;
* caption-safe space when planned;
* visible weather, light, and environmental behaviour;
* object state at entry and exit; and
* the final factual state needed by the next scene.

### Step 10 — Test Google Flow Feasibility

For each candidate shot opportunity, ask:

* Is there one stable location?
* Is there one dominant visible action?
* Can a person, prop, weather state, and camera intention remain controlled?
* Does the action need a start or end keyframe?
* Is the motion `M1`, `M2`, or `M3`, rather than overloaded `M4`?
* Would a still, insert, cut, or sound bridge communicate this more reliably?

Split a scene into smaller shots when a single visual request contains multiple
people acting, several object handoffs, a location transition, major weather
change, dialogue performance, or more than one camera behavior.

### Step 11 — Check Format And Platform Integrity

Apply the Short or long-form rules before locking the script. Do not leave
format adaptation to editing.

### Step 12 — Validate, Lock, And Hand Off

Run the quality gates below. An approved script produces:

* a source manifest;
* a beat and scene map;
* scene cards and continuity ledger entries;
* dialogue and narration copy with purpose;
* sound and ASMR opportunities;
* visual, camera, and Flow handoff facts;
* production risks and targeted exclusions; and
* an approval or revision status.

## Scene Writing Rules

### One Visible Change Per Scene

Each scene must change the visible task state, viewer understanding, emotional
temperature, or required orientation.

Do not retain a scene only because its image is attractive.

### Cause Before Consequence

Show the object before its absence matters.

Show the weather before it alters the task.

Show the condition before the response.

Show the work before the result.

Show the result before asking the final image to carry emotion.

### Cast Discipline

Use the smallest truthful cast.

Every present person needs one documented action, reaction, or meaningful
presence. Omit a family member who has no honest role.

Children may observe, ask, learn, attempt safe help, and take part in a repair.
Adults hold adult responsibility. No child is humiliated, frightened, isolated,
or tasked with solving an adult problem.

### Location And Route Discipline

Use each location for its established purpose. State why the characters are
there and how they plausibly arrived.

When a location change matters, write departure evidence, what is carried or
left behind, route logic or elapsed time, arrival condition, and changed
environmental facts.

For a Short, imply routine travel through clear departure and arrival evidence
when the route itself is not the story.

### Object Discipline

Story objects are evidence, not generic texture. Track:

* material and period fit;
* owner or current user;
* count or amount;
* starting place and condition;
* hand or storage position when relevant;
* action performed;
* ending place and condition; and
* next-scene requirement.

### Environmental Discipline

Season must affect the script's physical logic when visible.

Track sun direction, cloud cover, rainfall, ground wetness, wind, vegetation,
smoke, steam, dust, heat, clothing condition, and elapsed time.

Do not use changing weather or light solely to make an image more dramatic.

## Narration Rules

Narration is optional and must earn its place.

Use narration for:

* concise orientation that cannot be shown quickly enough;
* a verified cultural, seasonal, or procedural context;
* a gentle bridge across an approved time or location change;
* a restrained reflection after the evidence is already visible; or
* a simple closing thought that does not over-explain the ending.

Do not use narration to:

* describe actions already visible;
* tell viewers what a character feels before performance shows it;
* replace a missing cause, route, object state, or outcome;
* add facts absent from canon;
* make an ordinary situation sound dangerous or profound beyond evidence;
* turn the episode into a lesson; or
* promise a later resolution that the current episode withholds.

Prefer short, factual, image-supportive narration. Leave silence where action,
ambience, or an expression has already done the work.

## Dialogue Limits And Rules

Dialogue is economical by design.

### Dialogue Limits

For a Short:

* use no dialogue unless it adds clarity or relationship meaning unavailable in
  action;
* normally retain only one to four short spoken exchanges or their equivalent;
* do not let speech delay the visible hook, response, payoff, or final rest.

For long form:

* use dialogue in short, practical clusters separated by action, ambience, and
  observation;
* give each exchange a specific decision, response, correction, or relationship
  purpose;
* do not sustain conversation through a sequence that should be shown.

These are restraint limits, not quotas. A silent episode section may be more
truthful than a line added to meet an assumed dialogue requirement.

### Dialogue Standards

Each retained line must:

* sound like the documented speaker;
* respond to the immediate task, person, or visible condition;
* preserve dignity;
* be age-appropriate;
* remain compatible with the period and language guidance;
* leave space for natural sound; and
* advance a practical or emotional decision.

Remove a line that merely names what the camera already proves.

### Dialogue Prohibitions

Do not write:

* moral speeches;
* exposition monologues;
* modern slang or technical vocabulary;
* insults, threats, humiliation, shouting, or melodramatic declarations;
* dialogue that treats the Dog as a hero, weapon, or danger signal;
* dialogue that makes a child responsible for adult safety or emotion; or
* dialogue that establishes unverified canon.

## Visual, ASMR, Camera, And Google Flow Handoff

The script must leave downstream production a factual handoff, not a vague
creative wish.

### Visual Handoff Requirements

For each scene, state:

* location and documented zone;
* time, season, weather, light source, and visible environmental condition;
* characters present, wardrobe state, posture, and emotional temperature;
* featured props with owner, material, count, position, and condition;
* primary action and required visible result;
* key orientation, process, reaction, detail, or payoff image;
* opening and closing factual state; and
* continuity risks.

### ASMR And Sound Handoff Requirements

For each relevant beat, state:

* the sound source;
* whether it is foreground, ambience, transition, or intentional quiet;
* what physical action proves the sound;
* whether dialogue must leave a clear listening window; and
* whether music must remain absent, restrained, or supportive.

Generated sound must never be the sole source of a required fact. Critical
sound, dialogue, narration, and music decisions remain controllable
post-production work.

### Camera Handoff Requirements

State camera information only when it serves story or continuity:

* required frame scale or subject relationship;
* camera side, height, or screen direction;
* one allowed behavior: static, gentle push-in, slow pull-back, slow pan, or
  gentle tracking;
* protected central action for 9:16 delivery; and
* caption-safe area when planned.

The camera is an observer. Avoid orbiting, whip pans, fast zooms, shaky action
language, drone-like movement, and camera motion used only as spectacle.

### Google Flow Handoff Requirements

Every proposed Flow shot must be expressible as:

```text
From this approved start state, [subject] performs [one action]
while the camera [one behavior], ending when [observable end state].
```

If that sentence has multiple actions, multiple camera moves, or more than one
meaningful “while,” split the shot before Google Flow receives it.

State:

* preferred asset type: still, one-image Flow, two-image Flow, insert, hold, or
  editorial transition;
* reference assets required and their roles;
* motion class: `M1`, `M2`, `M3`, or `M4 — REDESIGN`;
* beginning and end state;
* targeted drift risks; and
* whether the final frame must match the next shot exactly.

Google Flow may animate a bounded approved action. It may not decide story,
dialogue, emotional purpose, canonical fact, or historical detail.

## Short-Form Script Rules

A Short must be immediate without being rushed.

It needs:

* a truthful first image or sound-backed condition;
* one readable practical question or action line;
* a visible adjustment, discovery, or response;
* a modest emotional payoff; and
* a final image, action, or sound state that feels complete on its own.

Write for 9:16 from the first scene:

* keep the key face, hands, and object in the central vertical field;
* make essential action understandable without audio;
* avoid tiny background events as the only evidence;
* protect room for captions without covering the action; and
* plan a clean final cut, hold, loop, or resting frame.

Do not use rapid cuts, danger bait, a false question, an unfinished ending, or
“Part 2” language to manufacture retention.

A Short may connect to a wider thread only after its own condition and emotion
have settled.

---

## Long-Form Script Rules

Long form earns duration through meaningful observation, not additional
incidents.

Use five to ten minutes only when the story needs:

* several meaningful stages of a practical process;
* a genuine change in weather, time, or light;
* a relationship movement that requires preparation, recognition, and rest;
* documented travel or place rhythm that affects the main task; or
* one light supporting thread that deepens the same day and main action.

Long form retains one primary physical and emotional thread.

Additional scenes must add new process, knowledge, relationship meaning,
location understanding, or sensory value. If “more of the same happens,” merge
or remove the material.

Use 16:9 width to establish geography and relationships, while keeping the
story subject readable at mobile scale.

---

## Serialized But Emotionally Complete Episodes

Village Stories may continue a real process, memory, repair, season, or family
feeling across releases. It must not split the only resolution merely to force a
next view.

For every connected episode, script:

* the prior confirmed state;
* the present episode's self-contained condition;
* the immediate action and visible result;
* the present emotional resolution;
* the exact remaining fact, if any; and
* an optional, truthful continuation seed.

An allowed continuation seed:

* shows a real next practical step after payoff;
* names no unsupported future outcome;
* does not create new danger or urgency;
* remains understandable without another episode; and
* does not cancel the current final rest.

Reject a continuation if the current script ends before the viewer sees the
promised response, result, or emotional settling.

## Season, Character, Location, And Object Continuity

### Season Ledger

Record:

* named season;
* time and elapsed time;
* weather state;
* light direction;
* ground, roof, water, vegetation, smoke, or heat condition; and
* what may change by the next scene.

### Character Ledger

Record:

* who is present and why;
* current clothing, hair, footwear, wetness, dirt, and carried items;
* position, gaze, screen direction, and body state;
* practical task and emotional temperature; and
* required state at scene exit.

### Location Ledger

Record:

* documented location and sub-area;
* entry and exit logic;
* fixed landmarks, furniture, path, doorway, or work surface that matters;
* camera geography; and
* condition changes caused by the action or weather.

### Object Ledger

Record:

* object name and source;
* owner or current user;
* material, amount, orientation, condition, and location;
* action performed;
* ending state; and
* next required appearance.

No scene boundary may silently alter a documented state. A cut, dissolve, music
swell, crop, or blur cannot conceal an impossible change.

## Quality Gates

### Gate A — Input And Authority Readiness

Pass only when:

* the premise is approved and identifiable;
* applied sources are available;
* format, runtime, and delivery ratio are known or explicitly open;
* required continuity has been supplied;
* open facts have owners; and
* variables cannot contradict canon.

### Gate B — Story Fidelity

Pass only when:

* the script preserves the approved ordinary condition, pressure, response,
  emotional movement, and ending;
* there is one primary practical thread;
* the response is earned through attention, skill, patience, cooperation, or
  plausible circumstances;
* no new conflict, spectacle, rescue, or coincidence has been introduced; and
* the final feeling rests without a lecture.

### Gate C — Canon, Period, And Dignity

Pass only when:

* every person, place, object, task, route, and factual claim is documented;
* character use matches individual packets;
* the period remains early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu;
* no modern object, language, convenience, or background assumption appears;
* rural life, work, tradition, and material wear are treated with dignity; and
* children and the Dog are safe, natural, and never used for artificial stakes.

### Gate D — Scene And Continuity Clarity

Pass only when:

* each scene has a primary purpose, start state, action, visible change, and
  end state;
* cause precedes consequence;
* location changes have believable route or time logic;
* object, wardrobe, weather, light, screen direction, and emotion remain
  traceable; and
* the final scene provides a real resting point.

### Gate E — Dialogue, Narration, And Sound

Pass only when:

* dialogue is necessary, brief, dignified, and character-appropriate;
* narration does not duplicate visible evidence;
* natural sound has a plausible source;
* silence has purpose; and
* music supports rather than declares the feeling.

### Gate F — Visual And Flow Readiness

Pass only when:

* each scene has usable visual evidence and production handoff facts;
* potentially complex actions are divided before prompting;
* each Flow candidate can be bounded to one subject action, one camera behavior,
  and one end state;
* reference requirements are known; and
* no generated asset is expected to decide missing story or canon information.

### Gate G — Format And YouTube Integrity

Pass only when:

* the opening shows the truthful point of interest promptly;
* the action is readable at intended delivery size;
* a Short has an emotionally complete mini-arc;
* long form earns each additional scene;
* title and thumbnail can later describe an actual episode moment; and
* any serial connection is optional, factual, and not retention bait.

---

## Rejection Rules

Reject the script immediately when it:

* contradicts specific canon or a supplied state;
* depends on an unsupported location, object, route, practice, skill, or past
  event;
* introduces modern technology, language, material culture, or convenience;
* turns weather, work, a child, or the Dog into danger spectacle;
* makes Mother helpless, Father reckless, a child adult-like, or the Dog heroic
  or threatening;
* humiliates a person, animal, worker, neighbour, or family member;
* adds more than one unrelated primary conflict;
* replaces cause and effect with montage, narration, music, or coincidence;
* gives dialogue the job of explaining a scene that has no visual proof;
* includes a scene with no changed state or viewer value;
* ends on only a tease, cliffhanger, title card, or moral statement;
* asks one Google Flow task to perform a full scene; or
* makes a public promise the finished episode could not honestly fulfill.

Use `HOLD FOR CANON OR RESEARCH` rather than rejection when the story could
work after an owner confirms a necessary fact.

---

## Revision Rules

Revise the smallest broken layer. Do not randomly rewrite a valid script.

| Failure | Required revision |
| --- | --- |
| Premise cannot support the scene | Return to premise review; do not add invented plot |
| Canon fact missing | Hold and request source-backed clarification |
| Character acts out of role | Revise the action, not the character |
| Period drift | Replace with a documented period-appropriate mechanism |
| Scene repeats information | Cut, merge, or give it a distinct visible change |
| Cause is unclear | Show the starting condition before the response |
| Conflict is too large | Reduce it to a task-related adjustment |
| Emotion is vague | Add two visible behaviour or result cues |
| Emotion is melodramatic | Remove speech, tears, or music dependence; restore practical care |
| Dialogue explains action | Replace the line with an observable beat or pause |
| Short feels rushed | Remove decorative cuts; preserve decisive process and rest |
| Long form feels thin | Reduce to Short or add only documented meaningful process |
| Continuity is unclear | Complete the affected ledger and rewrite entry/exit states |
| Flow handoff is overloaded | Split the action, use an insert, still, cut, or return to board |
| Packaging implication is inflated | Reframe around the actual task, object, season, or relationship |

For every revision, record:

```text
Episode ID:
Script revision:
Failure classification:
Earliest affected scene:
Single corrective change:
Canon sources rechecked:
Downstream gates to rerun:
Validation result:
```

Do not stack unexplained revisions. A corrected script must show what changed
and why the change addresses the actual failure.

## Positive Examples

### Positive — Short: Protecting School Paper

**Approved condition:** Early monsoon rain reaches Younger Daughter's school
paper in the documented courtyard.

**Script movement:** The first drops darken the packed earth. Younger Daughter
looks at the paper beside her bag. Mother wraps it in a dry cloth while Elder
Brother holds the bag open. The paper is safe inside; the child steps toward
the thinnai as rain continues on the roof.

**Why it works:** The weather changes a real task. The cast is small and
purposeful. The visual hook is truthful. The final state resolves the immediate
need and lands on reassurance rather than a school-danger cliffhanger.

### Positive — Long Form: Rain-Adjusted Meal

**Approved condition:** Monsoon dampness weakens Mother's cooking fire.

**Script movement:** Mother tests damp wood; the fire smokes. Father retrieves
documented dry pieces from the approved storage area. Elder Brother moves one
vessel carefully under direction. Younger Daughter keeps a cloth from the wet
threshold while supervised. The fire steadies, food finishes, and the family
settles into the meal while rain remains audible outside.

**Why it works:** The duration comes from meaningful stages of one household
process. Every action is visible and bounded. Rain, sound, light, objects, and
the final meal produce a clear production handoff.

### Positive — Connected Shorts: Seedling Care

**Current script:** Younger Daughter notices a drooping seedling in summer.
Mother explains through a small action when and how to water it. The soil
darkens; the vessel is returned; the child looks at the now-settled plant.

**Possible later episode:** On a later documented day, the family notices new
growth during their normal garden routine.

**Why it works:** The first Short resolves the current care need. The later
observation is an optional factual continuation, not the withheld answer.

## Non-Examples

### Reject — “Will She Reach School?”

The script opens with Younger Daughter crying in hard rain, thunder crashes,
and the episode ends before her papers are protected.

**Reject because:** It exploits child distress, turns weather into false danger,
and withholds the only resolution for retention.

### Reject — Hero Dog Rescue

The German Shepherd races through a storm to find a missing child.

**Reject because:** It turns the Dog into a rescue and danger device, violates
the series scale, and cannot be corrected through a better prompt.

### Reject — Narration Repairs Missing Logic

“Everyone knew the old basket was important,” says narration, but no action,
history, or object state makes the basket relevant.

**Reject because:** Narration is being used to invent meaning. Either document
the object and show its practical role or remove it.

### Reject — Full Scene In One Flow Handoff

“Mother cooks, Father arrives, both children run in, the dog barks, rain begins,
and the camera circles the house.”

**Reject because:** It contains several actions, people, state changes, and
camera decisions. Script separate action, arrival, weather, reaction, and
transition units.

## Edge Cases

### An Attractive Image Has No Scene Function

Use it later as a possible atmosphere, transition, or thumbnail candidate only
if it meets those specific needs. Do not force it into the script.

### The Practical Result Is Imperfect

An imperfect result is valid when the family reaches useful progress, a real
plan, shared understanding, or emotional rest. Do not force perfect success or
artificial sadness.

### A Child Makes A Mistake

Keep it small, observable, repairable, and age-appropriate. An adult listens,
explains, and includes the child in correction. Do not use fear, isolation, or
public shame.

### A Location Transition Is Necessary

State the documented reason, route, time, carried objects, and arrival state.
If travel dominates a Short without being the story, redesign around one place.

### An Educational Opportunity Appears

Show the skill, seasonal reasoning, object handling, or routine through action.
Do not convert the scene into a quiz, lecture, moral, or unsupported historical
claim.

### A Trend Requests More Drama

Treat metrics as non-authoritative feedback. Improve truthful opening clarity,
visual focus, pacing, or packaging only after reviewing the completed episode.
Do not add shock, speed, fear, or false urgency.

### Generated Audio Adds Dialogue Or Facts

Mute, remove, or replace it in controlled post-production. Generated sound
cannot establish essential dialogue, plot, names, cultural facts, or canon.

### A Flow Control Changes Or Disappears

Retain the same production discipline: approved inputs, bounded action,
restrained camera, explicit end state, independent review, and durable records.
Do not depend on an outdated interface assumption.

## YouTube Considerations

YouTube requirements clarify how an honest episode is presented; they do not
change what the episode is.

Every script must identify:

* a truthful first point of interest;
* a visible action, condition, object, or relationship a future title can name;
* an accurate thumbnail-worthy image;
* whether the central action reads at phone size;
* an ending image or sound state that feels complete; and
* any real relationship to a playlist or connected episode.

The opening must show a real condition promptly: a task underway, first rain
touching a task, a needed object, a child noticing something, or a practical
action beginning.

Do not script frightened faces, growls, thunder crashes, rapid cutting, broken
objects, secrets, rescues, or “will they survive?” framing as bait for ordinary
weather and work.

Generate no readable text, logos, labels, subtitles, watermarks, or packaging
copy inside the world. Add approved text in post-production.

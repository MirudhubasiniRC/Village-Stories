# Master Script Prompt

## Purpose

This is the directly usable operational manual and copy-ready prompt for
turning an approved Village Stories premise into a production-ready script.

Use it after premise approval and before storyboarding, image-prompt assembly,
Google Flow work, voice, sound, edit, packaging, or release.

It turns settled story decisions into observable scenes, economical dialogue,
sound opportunities, continuity records, and visual-production requirements.

It does not create canon, develop a premise, replace a storyboard, write image
or Flow prompts, make public metadata, or conceal a production problem with
vague “cinematic” language.

For detailed source-loading, scene craft, format, continuity, Flow, revision,
examples, edge cases, YouTube, and related-document rules, use
[`03_Script_Prompt_Reference.md`](03_Script_Prompt_Reference.md).

---

## Authority Summary

This manual inherits authority; it does not create it.

```text
01_Canon and timeline
> 02_World / 03_Characters / 04_Objects / 05_Research
> 06_Story_Engine
> approved premise and confirmed continuity facts
> 07_Episode_System
> 08_Production
> 09_AI_Production_System
> this manual and generated script
```

When sources conflict:

1. Name the disputed fact.
2. Apply the highest applicable authority.
3. Do not average contradictory instructions.
4. Record the conflict in validation.
5. Return the affected decision to its owning stage.

Generated prose, a prior draft, attractive output, metrics, or a reference
image never authorizes an unsupported fact.

If a required fact is `OPEN`, mark its affected material
`HOLD FOR CANON OR RESEARCH`.

## Permanent Locks

### Time And Place

- The series is early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu; the exact year may flex, the
  historical period may not.
- Work, travel, communication, household routines, and problem-solving must
  take believable local time, effort, and available materials.
- State season, time of day, weather, and any relevant visible consequence.
- Never introduce mobile phones, internet, digital payments, social media,
  GPS, current slang, contemporary appliances, LED lighting, current vehicles,
  modern packaging, or unsupported modern infrastructure.

### Family And Character

- The established family is Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter,
  and the German Shepherd Family Dog.
- Use only people with a documented reason to be present and an
  age-appropriate role.
- Mother is capable, attentive, practical, and humane.
- Father is calm, hardworking, observant, and emotionally grounded.
- Elder Brother may be eager, distracted, proud, or learning; correction is
  dignified and age-appropriate.
- Younger Daughter may be curious, direct, observant, and safely included; she
  does not carry adult burdens.
- Individual character packets override this summary where more specific.
- Do not change personality, knowledge, competence, appearance, clothing,
  relationship, or ability to make plot work.
- The Dog is a gentle household companion. It may wait, follow, rest, observe,
  respond softly, play naturally, or receive ordinary care.
- The Dog is never police, military, attack, rescue, chase, missing-pet,
  injury, danger, threat, or stakes device.

### Style, Tone, And Story

- Preserve original Village Stories anime style for the entire frame, matching
  `11_Assets/Character_References/Father_Canon_Design_Reference.png`: painterly
  2D anime-inspired illustration; thin clean line art; soft cel-style painted
  shading; warm cinematic lighting; handcrafted animation aesthetic.
- Show culturally specific, lived-in village life with warm, observant,
  restrained slice-of-life feeling.
- Do not use named-studio imitation, hyper-realism, plastic CGI, chibi comedy,
  action-camera language, decorative poverty, or generic nostalgia.
- Build one ordinary practical action around one proportionate gentle pressure.
- Let practical care, attention, work, learning, weather, routine, memory,
  harmless misunderstanding, or community rhythm create movement.
- Never use villains, violence, crime, panic, humiliation, severe illness,
  family rupture, dangerous rescue, traumatic loss, child endangerment, animal
  distress, or false emergency.
- The result may be imperfect, but the immediate emotional question settles.

## Minimum Canon Packet

Supply a compact packet of episode-constraining facts, not copied manuals.

Always include:

- applicable `01_Canon/` style, timeline, and continuity locks;
- period research and documented exclusions;
- the approved premise and its validation result;
- applicable `06_Story_Engine/` story philosophy and rules;
- relevant `07_Episode_System/` format, opening, scene, pacing, ending, sound,
  visual-rhythm, and review rules;
- `08_Production/04_Script_Workflow.md`; and
- `09_AI_Production_System/README.md` and `01_Master_Workflow.md`.

Add selected character, location, object, research, and prior-state sources
when they constrain the episode.

Record every applied fact:

```text
Source path:
Source heading:
Applied fact:
Script consequence:
Continuity risk if ignored:
```

See the reference's “Canon Source Loading” section for the full matrix.

## Variables

Variables select only among supplied approved options. They never alter,
extend, reinterpret, or replace canon, the approved premise, or a higher
authority.

They may not:

- add a modern solution;
- make a character act against their packet;
- make the Dog carry stakes or solve the story;
- change a location’s documented layout or purpose;
- substitute an unapproved prop, material, route, or practice;
- turn a calm premise into danger;
- use continuation instead of current closure; or
- use filler to reshape a Short or long-form episode.

If a variable conflicts with canon, canon wins. State the conflict and return
`REVISE` or `HOLD`.

## Assembly Workflow

### 1. Confirm The Contract

State the approved story as:

```text
When [practical condition] affects [task or need],
[documented people] respond through [canon-safe action],
arriving at [grounded emotional rest].
```

Confirm the condition, need, one gentle pressure, response, visible ending, and
format-specific viewer promise. Return to premise review if any needs a new
fact, stronger conflict, rescue, coincidence, or unexplained emotional speech.

### 2. Establish Baseline

Before prose, record location and sub-area; time, season, weather, light, and
surface condition; cast, clothing, posture, emotion, and positions; object
state; task state; sound environment; and inherited facts.

Every scene must preserve or deliberately change this baseline.

### 3. Build The Functional Spine

```text
active opening
> orientation and intention
> preparation or process
> gentle turn or recognition
> practical response
> visible payoff
> emotional rest
> optional truthful continuation seed
```

Combine functions only when cause and effect remain visible.

### 4. Assign Scene Purpose

Each scene has one primary purpose: hook, orientation, intention, preparation,
process, turn, response, payoff, rest, or optional continuation seed.

Remove, merge, or redesign a scene with no primary function.

### 5. Write Scene Cards Then Script

Define observable facts before action, dialogue, or narration:

```text
Scene ID and intended duration:
Primary purpose:
Location and orientation:
Time / season / weather / light:
Characters present and why:
Start state:
One primary action:
Visible change:
Emotional movement:
Featured object facts:
Sound decision:
Camera intention:
End state:
Transition and next-scene handoff:
Flow and reference requirements:
```

Write what a viewer can see, hear, or infer from grounded behaviour. Show
action and evidence before interpretation.

### 6. Add Support Layers

Add dialogue and narration only after visual logic works. Add one deliberate
sound decision per scene. State only camera facts that serve story or
continuity. Test each Flow candidate as one action, one camera behaviour, and
one observable end state.

### 7. Validate And Hand Off

Run the gates below. Lock only with a source manifest, beat map, complete scene
cards, continuity entries, dialogue/narration purpose, sound opportunities,
visual/Flow facts, risks, exclusions, and status.

## Quality Gates

### A. Input And Authority

Pass when the premise is approved and identifiable, sources are available,
format/runtime/ratio are known or explicitly open, continuity is supplied,
open facts have owners, and variables cannot contradict canon.

### B. Story Fidelity

Pass when the approved condition, pressure, response, emotion, and ending stay
intact; one practical thread remains; the response is earned; no spectacle,
rescue, or coincidence appears; and the ending rests without a lecture.

### C. Canon, Period, And Dignity

Pass when people, places, objects, tasks, routes, and claims are documented;
individual packets are followed; period drift is absent; village life is
dignified; and children and the Dog remain natural and safe.

### D. Scenes And Continuity

Pass when every scene has purpose, start state, action, visible change, and end
state; cause precedes consequence; transitions are plausible; and objects,
wardrobe, weather, light, direction, and emotion are traceable.

### E. Dialogue, Narration, And Sound

Pass when speech is necessary, brief, dignified, and in character; narration
does not repeat evidence; sound has a plausible source; silence has purpose;
and music supports rather than declares emotion.

### F. Visual And Flow Readiness

Pass when every scene has usable visual evidence and handoff facts; complexity
is divided before prompting; Flow actions are bounded; references are known;
and generated assets are not deciding story or canon.

### G. Format And YouTube Integrity

Pass when the opening truthfully shows interest promptly, action reads at
delivery size, Shorts complete their mini-arc, long form earns duration,
packaging can name a real moment, and serial links are optional and factual.

## Revision And Rejection

Reject immediately for a direct canon conflict; unsupported required fact;
modern drift; danger spectacle; role reversal; humiliation; unrelated conflict;
missing cause and effect; a scene with no changed state; a tease-only ending;
an overloaded Flow request; or a public promise the episode cannot fulfill.

Use `HOLD FOR CANON OR RESEARCH` when confirmation of a named missing fact could
make the story valid.

Revise the smallest broken layer:

- unsupported premise: return to premise review;
- missing fact: request a source-backed clarification;
- character mismatch: revise the action, not the character;
- period drift: use a documented mechanism;
- repeated scene: cut, merge, or create a distinct visible change;
- unclear cause: show the starting condition;
- oversized conflict: reduce to a task adjustment;
- vague emotion: add visible behaviour or result cues;
- melodrama: remove speech/music dependence and restore practical care;
- explanatory dialogue: replace with action or pause;
- rushed Short: remove decorative cuts, retain process and rest;
- thin long form: reduce to a Short or add documented meaningful process;
- continuity ambiguity: complete ledgers and rewrite entry/exit states;
- overloaded Flow: split action or use an insert, still, cut, or board;
- inflated packaging: reframe around the actual task, object, season, or bond.

For each revision, record:

```text
Episode ID:
Script revision:
Failure classification:
Earliest affected scene:
Single corrective change:
Canon sources rechecked:
Downstream gates to rerun:
Validation result:
```

See the companion reference for detailed rejection tests, edge cases, and
positive/non-example explanations.

## Production Handoff

Deliver the locked script with:

- source manifest and validation result;
- beat map and complete scene cards;
- continuity baseline and per-scene ledger changes;
- dialogue/narration copy and purpose;
- sound, ASMR, music, and silence decisions;
- required character, location, and object references;
- still, Flow, two-image, insert, hold, and transition decisions;
- bounded Flow inputs and drift risks;
- caption-safe and crop needs; and
- a status of `APPROVE`, `REVISE`, or `HOLD FOR CANON OR RESEARCH`.

Google Flow receives bounded approved action only. It does not decide story,
dialogue, emotional purpose, historical detail, or canon.

## Concise Examples

### Valid Short — Protecting School Paper

Early monsoon drops reach Younger Daughter’s school paper in the documented
courtyard. Mother wraps it in dry cloth while Elder Brother holds the bag open.
The paper is safe beneath the thinnai as rain continues. The weather changes a
real task; the response and reassurance are visible.

### Valid Long Form — Rain-Adjusted Meal

Monsoon dampness weakens Mother’s cooking fire. Father brings documented dry
wood from approved storage, Elder Brother moves one vessel under direction, and
Younger Daughter helps with a cloth while supervised. The fire steadies and the
family settles to a meal with rain outside. Meaningful process earns duration.

### Reject — “Will She Reach School?”

A frightened child in hard rain and an ending before her papers are protected
uses distress and withheld resolution as retention bait. Return to premise
review; do not repair it with stronger prose.

### Reject — Hero Dog Rescue

A Dog finding a missing child during a storm violates the Dog, scale, and danger
locks. Reject; it is not a prompt-quality problem.

## Final Check

Before lock, ask:

> Can every important action, sound, object state, location transition, and
> feeling happen here because this established village makes it true?

If no, revise the earliest unsupported decision. Do not ask prose, camera,
music, or AI generation to make it true after the fact.

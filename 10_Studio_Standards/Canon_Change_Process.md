# Canon Change Process

## 1. Purpose

This manual governs every intentional change to studio canon.

It is a permanent operating standard, not a planning template.

Its purpose is to preserve audience trust and production continuity.

It also allows necessary corrections without rewriting history carelessly.

Canon is a shared production asset.

No individual contributor owns canon by default.

Canon changes are exceptions to preservation, not routine maintenance.

When uncertain, preserve the established version.

When a change is required, make it explicit, reviewable, reversible where possible, and traceable.

This process applies before prompts, images, video, edits, narration, metadata, thumbnails, and publication are changed.

It applies to both visible story facts and hidden production facts that protect continuity.

It applies whether the work is created by a human, an AI system, or a hybrid workflow.

## 2. Guiding Principle

Preserve what the audience and production have already learned.

Correct errors honestly.

Clarify ambiguity without inventing a contradiction.

Extend the world without weakening its foundations.

Retcon only when the benefit clearly exceeds the cost of changing established truth.

Never use convenience as the sole reason to alter canon.

Never silently replace a completed canon record.

Never treat an AI variation as evidence that the canon has changed.

The latest generated asset is not automatically the authoritative asset.

The most visually attractive output is not automatically the canonical output.

Continuity is a quality requirement, not an optional aesthetic preference.

## 3. Scope

This process covers narrative canon.

Narrative canon includes facts, events, relationships, history, rules, and outcomes.

This process covers visual canon.

Visual canon includes stable appearance, material, shape, scale, wardrobe, color, and environment features.

This process covers sonic canon.

Sonic canon includes established voices, recurring sound identity, language choices, and signature ambience.

This process covers technical production canon.

Technical production canon includes recurring camera rules, prompt modules, generation constraints, and approved lock systems.

This process covers publication canon.

Publication canon includes released videos, public descriptions, community posts, thumbnails when story-significant, and approved corrections.

This process covers internal continuity records.

Internal continuity records include bibles, manifests, asset registries, prompt records, and approved decisions.

This process does not force every experimental draft to become canon.

This process does not prevent non-canonical tests, provided they are labeled and isolated.

## 4. Definitions

Canon is the authoritative set of facts and approved representations for the studio universe.

Completed canon is canon that has passed its stated completion or approval status.

Released canon is canon communicated to an external audience in a published work.

Production continuity is the consistency needed to create compatible future work.

A canon change is an approved alteration to an existing canonical fact, representation, rule, or governed production constraint.

A proposal is a request for a canon change before approval.

A change record is the permanent evidence of the decision and its implementation.

A lock is a reusable constraint that keeps a recurring entity or visual system consistent.

A bible is an authoritative reference that collects stable rules for a subject area.

A prompt module is an approved reusable prompt component.

A generated asset is output from a generation system and is not canonical until selected and recorded.

A release is a published audience-facing item.

A migration is the controlled update of dependent records, prompts, assets, and workflow instructions after approval.

A rollback is the documented restoration of the previous approved state.

## 5. Canonical Sources

The highest authority is an approved released work, unless a formally approved correction supersedes it.

The next authority is the current approved master canon record.

Approved character, location, object, style, and camera bibles are authoritative within their stated scope.

Approved lock-system records are authoritative for recurring production use.

Approved change records explain how and when an authority changed.

Approved prompt modules are authoritative only for their designated production purpose.

Asset manifests identify authoritative visual or audio exemplars.

Drafts, brainstorming notes, unapproved prompts, and test generations are non-canonical.

Informal chat decisions are non-canonical until recorded in an approved change record.

Memory is not a canonical source.

Search results are not a canonical source unless they point to an approved record.

When sources conflict, stop production and resolve the conflict through this process.

## 6. Preservation Rules

Completed canon remains valid until explicitly superseded.

Released work remains part of the publication history.

Do not overwrite released video to hide a canon conflict unless an emergency correction requires it.

Do not delete original canon evidence after approving a change.

Do not rewrite dates, authorship, or previous decisions in change records.

Do not alter a lock just because a generation tool produces it inconsistently.

Do not convert a workaround into canon without review.

Do not let a temporary production exception silently become permanent.

Do not replace approved reference assets in place without preserving their identifiers and history.

Do not change a recurring character’s core identity through isolated prompt edits.

Do not change a recurring location’s spatial logic through a single scene shortcut.

Do not change an object’s recognized silhouette merely to make a shot easier.

Do not alter camera bible defaults only because a new tool favors another framing.

## 7. Change Categories

Every proposal must use exactly one primary category.

It may list secondary effects, but its governing category remains singular.

The categories below have different evidence and approval thresholds.

### 7.1 Documentation Error Correction

A documentation error correction fixes a record that incorrectly describes already-established canon.

It does not change the underlying intended or released truth.

Examples include a mistyped name, wrong asset path, incorrect date, or omitted approved source citation.

Evidence must show the existing canonical truth unambiguously.

The correction must cite at least one higher-authority source.

The correction must preserve an audit note stating what was wrong.

The correction may be approved by the Canon Steward and the responsible domain owner.

If the error appears in released work, use the release correction procedure as well.

### 7.2 Clarification

A clarification makes an already-supported fact more precise without contradicting established material.

It resolves ambiguity that has blocked writing, prompting, editing, or review.

Clarification must not introduce a new plot event.

Clarification must not invalidate a reasonable audience reading of released material.

Evidence must show that the clarification follows from existing sources.

The proposal must state the ambiguity and why the chosen interpretation is best supported.

Clarifications need Canon Steward approval and affected domain-owner review.

### 7.3 Additive Extension

An additive extension adds new information that is compatible with existing canon.

It may introduce new places, history, minor characters, rules, or capabilities.

It must not silently redefine established limits.

It must establish why the addition was previously unknown or irrelevant when needed.

Evidence must include compatibility analysis against the affected canon.

Extensions affecting future releases require Narrative Lead and Canon Steward approval.

Extensions that alter recurring visual systems also require the relevant Visual Lead approval.

### 7.4 Retcon

A retcon changes, replaces, nullifies, or materially reinterprets established canon.

It includes changing a fact that an audience could reasonably rely on.

It includes retroactively changing an event outcome, relationship, origin, rule, or identity.

It has the highest ordinary approval threshold.

Retcons require a written necessity case, alternatives analysis, and audience-impact analysis.

Retcons require unanimous approval by the Canon Authority group.

If unanimity is unavailable, the retcon is rejected or deferred.

Retcons must specify how prior released work remains understandable.

### 7.5 Visual Redesign

A visual redesign changes the stable appearance of a canonical person, place, object, costume, or style system.

It includes material changes to silhouette, proportions, palette, age cues, landmarks, props, or signature motifs.

It does not include a normal change caused by time, weather, lighting, camera angle, or wardrobe context.

Visual redesigns require side-by-side comparison and recognition testing.

They require approval from the Canon Steward, Visual Lead, and affected Narrative Lead.

Recurring entities need updated locks, reference assets, and prompts before production resumes.

### 7.6 Temporal Progression

Temporal progression changes a canonical subject because time has passed within the story world.

It may cover aging, recovery, weathering, construction, seasonal change, status change, or relationship development.

It must state the in-world time interval and causal link.

It must preserve recognizability unless an approved redesign is also necessary.

Temporal progression needs timeline validation and affected-domain approval.

It must never be used to disguise an unreviewed redesign.

### 7.7 Production-Only Change

A production-only change modifies workflow, tools, prompt structure, file handling, or technical execution without changing canon.

Examples include changing model settings, aspect-ratio workflow, storage path, render software, or internal shot labels.

It must be explicitly marked production-only.

It must demonstrate that audience-facing and continuity-critical output remains compatible.

Production-only changes require the responsible Production Lead and continuity review.

If they change a lock, bible, visible result, or reusable interpretation, reclassify the request.

## 8. Authority

The Canon Authority group protects the studio’s authoritative continuity.

The Canon Steward maintains this manual, the change register, and source precedence.

The Narrative Lead evaluates story logic, character motivation, and temporal coherence.

The Visual Lead evaluates visual recognition, design language, and reference integrity.

The Production Lead evaluates tooling, prompts, assets, schedules, and implementation feasibility.

The Release Lead evaluates public-facing corrections, publication timing, and audience communication.

The QA Lead verifies that approved changes were implemented without collateral drift.

The Archive Custodian preserves source versions, evidence, assets, and prior states.

One person may hold multiple roles in a small studio.

Role consolidation does not remove approval requirements.

A proposer may not be the sole approver of their own retcon.

Conflicted approvers must disclose the conflict before review.

No contributor may bypass a gate because a deadline is close.

## 9. Decision Framework

Assess each proposal in order.

First ask whether a change is actually necessary.

Second ask whether the issue is a documentation error rather than a canon change.

Third ask whether clarification solves the issue without adding or contradicting canon.

Fourth ask whether an additive extension solves the need without revising established truth.

Fifth ask whether a production-only adjustment solves the problem without changing canon.

Only then consider retcon or visual redesign.

Prefer the narrowest change that fully resolves the problem.

Prefer future-facing story development over backward revision.

Prefer an in-world explanation over an unexplained external correction.

Prefer documented uncertainty over a false certainty.

Reject a request if its claimed benefit is only novelty, speed, tool convenience, or personal preference.

Defer a request if evidence is incomplete or consequences are unknown.

## 10. Proposal Requirements

Every proposal must receive a unique change identifier.

Use the format `CANON-YYYY-NNN`.

The identifier must remain stable through approval, implementation, and rollback.

The proposal title must describe the intended outcome, not merely the problem.

The proposer must state the primary change category.

The proposal must name all affected canonical sources.

The proposal must state the current canonical condition.

The proposal must state the proposed canonical condition.

The proposal must explain the reason for the change.

The proposal must identify the initiating issue, release, scene, asset, or production blocker.

The proposal must identify all affected recurring entities.

The proposal must identify all affected locks, bibles, prompt modules, and assets.

The proposal must state whether released material is affected.

The proposal must state whether audience communication is required.

The proposal must identify a rollback point.

The proposal must include a recommended decision: approve, reject, defer, or request evidence.

## 11. Evidence Requirements

Evidence must be traceable to a stable source.

Evidence must distinguish fact from interpretation.

Evidence must identify source title, location, version, and access date when relevant.

Evidence may include released scenes, scripts, approved bibles, change records, asset manifests, and production logs.

Evidence may include creator intent only when recorded contemporaneously or approved by the Canon Authority.

Evidence may include audience feedback as a signal, not as automatic authority.

Evidence must not rely solely on a generated output.

Evidence must not rely solely on an undocumented recollection.

Evidence for a correction must prove the record is wrong.

Evidence for a clarification must show the conclusion is already supported.

Evidence for an extension must show compatibility.

Evidence for a retcon must show necessity and insufficient alternatives.

Evidence for a visual redesign must show why current visual canon cannot meet the need.

Evidence for temporal progression must show in-world cause and elapsed time.

Evidence for a production-only change must show no visible or semantic canon drift.

## 12. Impact Analysis

Impact analysis is mandatory before approval.

The proposer must assess narrative impact.

The proposer must assess visual impact.

The proposer must assess temporal impact.

The proposer must assess technical prompt impact.

The proposer must assess asset impact.

The proposer must assess camera impact.

The proposer must assess release impact.

The proposer must assess audience-trust impact.

The proposer must assess training and handoff impact.

The proposer must assess rollback complexity.

The analysis must list all dependent documents.

The analysis must list all dependent prompt modules.

The analysis must list all dependent generated assets that could become misleading.

The analysis must list all upcoming productions that must use the new state.

The analysis must distinguish mandatory migration from optional refresh work.

The analysis must name an owner for each required migration item.

## 13. Risk Ratings

Use low, medium, high, or critical risk.

Low risk affects a local internal record with no released dependency.

Medium risk affects a controlled production dependency or an unreleased recurring element.

High risk affects a recurring lock, multiple episodes, or an audience-recognized fact.

Critical risk affects released central canon, a flagship character, a foundational location, or a core world rule.

Risk rating must consider irreversibility.

Risk rating must consider how easily viewers can notice a contradiction.

Risk rating must consider whether old and new assets may circulate together.

Risk rating must consider whether AI tools could amplify inconsistent versions.

High and critical proposals require an independent QA reviewer.

Critical proposals require a release communication plan before approval.

## 14. Approval Gates

Gate 0 is intake.

At intake, the Canon Steward confirms category, completeness, and ownership.

Incomplete proposals are returned without substantive review.

Gate 1 is evidence review.

At evidence review, reviewers validate source authority and factual claims.

Gate 2 is impact review.

At impact review, reviewers validate dependencies, risks, migration, and rollback.

Gate 3 is decision review.

At decision review, required approvers approve, reject, or defer the proposal.

Gate 4 is implementation readiness.

At readiness, owners confirm reference updates, prompt changes, and handoff plans.

Gate 5 is post-implementation validation.

At validation, QA confirms the approved scope was implemented and no unintended drift occurred.

Gate 6 is closure.

At closure, the change record is finalized and linked to all authoritative sources.

No later gate may be assumed complete because an earlier gate was completed.

## 15. Approval Matrix

Documentation corrections require Canon Steward and affected domain owner approval.

Clarifications require Canon Steward and affected domain owner approval.

Low-risk additive extensions require Canon Steward and Narrative Lead approval.

High-risk additive extensions require Canon Steward, Narrative Lead, and affected Visual or Production Lead approval.

Visual redesigns require Canon Steward, Visual Lead, Narrative Lead, and QA Lead sign-off.

Temporal progression requires Canon Steward, Narrative Lead, and affected Visual Lead approval.

Production-only changes require Production Lead and continuity reviewer approval.

Retcons require unanimous Canon Authority approval.

Critical release corrections require Canon Steward, Release Lead, and the responsible domain owner approval.

An approver may approve only their reviewed scope.

Conditional approval is not final approval until all conditions are satisfied and recorded.

Silence is not approval.

## 16. Rejection and Deferral

Reject a proposal that contradicts stronger evidence without necessity.

Reject a proposal that lacks an implementable migration.

Reject a proposal that treats unreleased preference as a reason to revise released truth.

Reject a proposal that would create more confusion than it resolves.

Defer a proposal when a future release may solve the issue naturally.

Defer a proposal when source conflict is unresolved.

Defer a proposal when the proposed scope is broader than the demonstrated need.

Every rejection must state the reason and the source basis.

Every deferral must state the missing condition for reconsideration.

Rejected proposals remain in the register for future context.

## 17. Change Record

Each approved or rejected proposal requires a permanent change record.

The record must retain the unique change identifier.

The record must include the proposal title and category.

The record must include the submission date and decision date.

The record must identify proposer, reviewers, approvers, and implementation owners.

The record must summarize current and proposed canon.

The record must link evidence sources.

The record must include the impact analysis.

The record must include risk rating and rationale.

The record must include the approval decision and any conditions.

The record must include the effective date.

The record must include release implications.

The record must include migration tasks and completion evidence.

The record must include validation results.

The record must include rollback instructions and rollback status.

The record must never erase the prior state.

The record must link superseded records rather than duplicating their history.

## 18. Versioning Rules

Canon records must use explicit versions.

Use semantic intent even if full semantic versioning is not used.

Increment a patch-level version for documentation corrections.

Increment a minor-level version for clarifications and additive extensions.

Increment a major-level version for approved retcons or foundational redesigns.

Record the effective version in the change record.

Tag or otherwise preserve the pre-change source state.

Do not relabel a prior release as though it always contained the new canon.

Use version history to explain change, not to conceal it.

Version control preserves files; it does not itself grant canon approval.

A commit may implement an approved change but is not the approval record.

Commit messages should include the change identifier where applicable.

The change record should link relevant commits, asset versions, and release identifiers.

## 19. Prompt Governance

Prompts are production instructions and may encode canon.

Any prompt that defines a recurring character, location, object, style, or camera behavior is continuity-critical.

Continuity-critical prompts require controlled versions.

Do not edit an approved master prompt in place without a change record or production-only review.

Preserve the prior prompt version when a replacement is approved.

Identify whether a prompt change changes visible output, semantic identity, or only phrasing.

If the visible output could change canon, route through the appropriate canon category.

Prompt improvements that preserve all locked attributes may be production-only.

Prompt changes that alter identity descriptors are never assumed production-only.

Prompt changes that alter a location’s defining layout require location-lock review.

Prompt changes that alter object material, scale, or silhouette require object-lock review.

Prompt changes that alter lens, height, movement, or composition defaults require camera-bible review.

Attach before-and-after prompt excerpts to the change record when a lock is affected.

## 20. Generated Asset Governance

Generated assets must be traceable to their prompt and reference context.

An asset becomes canonical only after selection, approval, and registration.

Do not treat a near-match as a replacement for a registered canonical asset.

Mark exploratory generations as non-canonical.

Keep approved reference assets separate from candidate assets.

Record model, date, prompt version, seed or equivalent reproducibility data when available.

Record image-to-video references and transformations where they affect continuity.

When a redesign is approved, retain the original asset set as superseded evidence.

Do not delete older canonical references solely to reduce storage clutter.

Use clear status labels such as candidate, approved, superseded, retired, and non-canonical.

Do not reuse retired assets in new work unless the change record permits historical use.

## 21. Character Locks

Recurring characters require stable identity locks.

Character locks include name, age range, build, face cues, hair, skin tone, clothing logic, accessories, manner, and prohibitions.

A character lock must distinguish invariant traits from scene-variable traits.

Do not change invariant traits without a visual redesign, temporal progression, or retcon decision.

Wardrobe changes may be ordinary scene variation when they comply with established wardrobe logic.

Damage, aging, healing, and status changes require temporal explanation where material.

Do not introduce a new facial scar, body proportion, or signature accessory by accident.

Update all character prompts and reference sheets after an approved character change.

Run recognition QA across stills, motion, close shots, and wide shots.

Preserve prior locks for flashbacks and historical scenes when needed.

## 22. Location Locks

Recurring locations require stable spatial and visual locks.

Location locks include geography, orientation, landmarks, material, scale, climate, color logic, and entry paths.

Location locks should identify immutable anchors and allowed seasonal variation.

Do not move a well, house, temple, road, field boundary, or landmark without review when it affects continuity.

Do not change daylight direction or terrain relationship in a way that breaks established geography.

Temporary set dressing is not a location redesign if documented as scene-specific.

Construction, damage, and seasonal change require temporal progression records when recurring.

Update establishing-shot references after an approved location change.

Validate continuity from prior location shots and future planned scenes.

## 23. Object Locks

Recurring objects require stable recognition locks.

Object locks include purpose, owner, material, scale, color, wear state, markings, and silhouette.

Narratively important objects require an asset history.

Do not replace a signature object with a similar but materially different prop.

Do not alter markings or inscriptions without narrative and visual review.

Normal wear may be permitted if it follows an approved progression.

Broken, repaired, inherited, or upgraded states require a recorded transition.

Update object reference images and prompt modules after approval.

Verify object continuity at close-up scale as well as scene scale.

## 24. Camera Bible Protection

The camera bible protects how the world is consistently observed.

Camera defaults may encode genre, intimacy, status, geography, and emotional grammar.

Do not change recurring lens logic, camera height, movement language, framing rules, or edit rhythm casually.

A one-off shot may depart from defaults if the shot rationale is documented.

A new recurring camera convention requires camera-bible review.

Tool limitations do not automatically justify changing the camera bible.

If a tool cannot reproduce a required camera behavior, seek a production workaround before altering canon-adjacent guidance.

Validate redesigned camera defaults against released visual identity.

Record whether a camera change is production-only or audience-perceptible.

## 25. Style Lock Protection

Style locks protect recurring art direction and visual coherence.

They include palette hierarchy, rendering density, contrast behavior, texture, cultural specificity, and realism level.

Do not shift the overall visual language because a tool update makes another style easier.

Scene mood variation is allowed when it remains inside the approved style range.

Major changes in realism, color pipeline, texture grammar, or character rendering need visual redesign review.

Test style changes alongside existing canonical frames.

Record acceptable variation ranges to avoid false redesign alarms.

## 26. Migration Planning

No approved change is ready until migration is planned.

Migration begins with a dependency inventory.

Inventory master canon records first.

Inventory character, location, object, style, and camera locks next.

Inventory prompt modules next.

Inventory registered assets next.

Inventory active scripts, storyboards, and shot lists next.

Inventory unreleased edits and scheduled releases next.

Inventory public-facing documentation last.

For each dependency, choose update, annotate, preserve, retire, or no action.

Assign one owner and one due date to each required action.

Do not migrate by bulk replacement without reviewing context.

## 27. Handoff Requirements

Every approved change must have a concise handoff note.

The handoff note must name the change identifier and effective version.

The handoff note must describe what changed in plain language.

The handoff note must describe what did not change.

The handoff note must list required new references and prompts.

The handoff note must list assets that are no longer valid for new production.

The handoff note must state whether historical work remains valid.

The handoff note must state any temporary implementation precautions.

The handoff note must identify who can answer continuity questions.

Handoffs are required before dependent work begins.

## 28. Implementation Controls

Implement only the approved scope.

Do not combine unrelated cleanup with a canon migration.

Do not make discretionary wording changes to authoritative records during implementation.

Use the change identifier in implementation task names where practical.

Preserve before-state snapshots of affected master records.

Update source links rather than copying uncertain facts.

Flag unresolvable conflicts immediately.

Stop implementation if the approved solution creates a new contradiction.

Return to review if scope must expand.

## 29. Validation

Validation checks both correctness and containment.

Correctness asks whether the approved new state is present.

Containment asks whether unapproved things stayed unchanged.

Validate all updated authoritative records.

Validate all updated locks.

Validate all affected prompt modules.

Validate all newly approved reference assets.

Validate at least one representative generation when prompts or assets change.

Validate story chronology when temporal facts change.

Validate shot continuity when visual or camera facts change.

Validate release notes when public communication is required.

QA must compare against both the approved change record and pre-change sources.

QA must record pass, fail, or pass-with-follow-up.

Pass-with-follow-up may not conceal a release-blocking discrepancy.

## 30. Release Implications

Released works are durable audience evidence.

Do not quietly revise a released title, description, thumbnail, or video to create a false historical record.

For a harmless typo, correct the public surface and note the correction internally.

For a material factual correction, publish an appropriate correction in the relevant public channel.

For a future-facing clarification, communicate it through new work when feasible.

For a retcon, use explicit language appropriate to audience expectations and story importance.

Assess whether old releases need annotations, pinned comments, updated descriptions, end cards, or follow-up episodes.

Avoid overexplaining a minor internal clarification to viewers.

Do not promise a release change until the migration and QA gates are complete.

Preserve screenshots, descriptions, and release metadata when a public correction is material.

## 31. YouTube Considerations

YouTube releases may be copied, clipped, downloaded, quoted, and recommended long after publication.

Assume older versions remain discoverable.

Do not rely on an edit alone to resolve audience confusion about a major canon change.

Consider a pinned comment for a limited correction that viewers need immediately.

Consider a description update when searchers need durable context.

Consider a community post when the correction affects an active audience conversation.

Consider a follow-up video when the change is central to story understanding.

Avoid changing thumbnails solely to conceal a continuity issue.

If a thumbnail depicts a canonical entity, assess it during visual redesign impact analysis.

Keep public wording accurate without exposing unnecessary internal production detail.

Archive publication URLs, dates, and correction text in the change record.

## 32. Google Flow Considerations

Google Flow output must be treated as candidate production material until approved.

Flow may introduce visual drift across generations, shots, or iterations.

Do not accept drift merely because it looks cinematic.

Use approved locks and references in every recurring-entity workflow.

Record the Flow project, prompt version, input references, and relevant settings for approved assets.

Use named, versioned prompt modules rather than ad hoc remembered phrases.

When Flow exposes a new capability, test it in a non-canonical sandbox first.

Do not alter visual canon to match a temporary model behavior.

If Flow cannot maintain a lock, use reference conditioning, narrower shots, post-production, or another approved workflow.

Escalate persistent capability conflicts as production-only proposals before considering a canon change.

Review generated motion for identity drift, object substitution, spatial contradictions, and camera-rule violations.

## 33. Emergency Corrections

An emergency correction addresses a serious error that risks harm, legal exposure, severe misinformation, safety issues, or major audience deception.

Emergency status does not eliminate documentation requirements.

The Canon Steward and Release Lead may authorize immediate containment.

Containment may include pausing publication, correcting metadata, unlisting a release, or halting dependent production.

Emergency containment must preserve the original evidence before alteration.

A formal change record must be opened within one business day.

Emergency decisions must be reviewed by the normal authority group after containment.

If the action changed canon, complete the ordinary approval and migration process retroactively.

Do not label embarrassment, schedule pressure, or aesthetic dislike as an emergency.

## 34. Rollback

Every high-risk or critical change must have a rollback plan before approval.

Rollback must identify the precise prior source versions to restore.

Rollback must identify assets, prompts, releases, and handoffs that need reversal or annotation.

Rollback must state whether any generated work must be quarantined.

Rollback must have an owner and decision threshold.

Rollback is required when validation fails, new contradictions appear, or adverse release impact exceeds the approved risk.

Do not delete the failed change record during rollback.

Mark the change as rolled back and explain why.

Create a new change record if a revised solution is proposed.

Do not reuse the original approval as blanket approval for a replacement approach.

## 35. Examples

Correcting a location bible from “north gate” to “east gate” is a documentation correction when released scenes and maps already show the east gate.

Stating that a recurring farmer’s blue shawl is worn only during winter is a clarification when earlier scenes support that seasonal pattern.

Introducing the farmer’s previously unmentioned cousin is an additive extension when it does not alter existing relationships.

Changing the farmer’s established sibling relationship to a cousin relationship is a retcon.

Adding gray hair after a documented ten-year story jump is temporal progression.

Replacing a recognizable clay water pot with a steel vessel across future episodes is a visual redesign if the object is recurring and story-significant.

Changing a prompt’s ordering while preserving every locked descriptor is a production-only change.

Changing “elderly woman with silver braid” to “young woman with black bob” is not production-only.

Updating a model version while output tests preserve all locks is production-only.

Changing standard wide establishing shots to extreme close-ups across the series requires camera-bible review.

## 36. Non-Examples

A spelling correction in an internal file is not a retcon.

A new camera angle for one scene is not automatically a camera-bible change.

A rainy-day color grade is not automatically a style redesign.

A different seed producing a slightly different face is not a character evolution.

A prop missing from one generation is not evidence that the canonical object disappeared.

A fan interpretation is not canon unless formally adopted.

A production delay is not a reason to redefine a character’s appearance.

A tool’s default output is not an approved visual standard.

A scene draft is not canon merely because it was rendered.

A deleted local file is not proof that a canonical fact no longer exists.

## 37. Common Mistakes

Mistake: editing a bible first and asking for approval later.

Why it fails: it creates false certainty and contaminates review evidence.

Mistake: calling a retcon a clarification.

Why it fails: it avoids necessary scrutiny and damages the record.

Mistake: using an attractive generated image as the decision rationale.

Why it fails: appearance does not establish compatibility or necessity.

Mistake: updating only the master prompt.

Why it fails: dependent locks, assets, and productions may continue old canon.

Mistake: overwriting prior references.

Why it fails: rollback and historical interpretation become impossible.

Mistake: treating a released video as editable internal draft.

Why it fails: the audience has already received the earlier state.

Mistake: changing a lock to solve one broken generation.

Why it fails: the tool, not canon, may be the problem.

Mistake: omitting what did not change.

Why it fails: teams infer unnecessary scope and create drift.

Mistake: approving a change without a handoff.

Why it fails: future production unknowingly mixes versions.

## 38. QA Checklists

### 38.1 Record QA

Confirm the change identifier is unique.

Confirm the category matches the actual effect.

Confirm all sources are linked and authoritative.

Confirm approval names and dates are recorded.

Confirm the prior state remains accessible.

Confirm scope and non-scope are explicit.

### 38.2 Lock QA

Confirm affected locks were updated deliberately.

Confirm unaffected locks did not drift.

Confirm references match lock text.

Confirm prompt modules carry the correct version.

Confirm historical locks remain available where needed.

### 38.3 Asset QA

Confirm selected assets are registered.

Confirm candidate assets are not mislabeled as approved.

Confirm superseded assets remain traceable.

Confirm recurring entity recognition is preserved.

Confirm object and location anchors remain consistent.

### 38.4 Release QA

Confirm public correction wording is accurate.

Confirm release links and dates are archived.

Confirm communications match the approved scope.

Confirm no misleading old metadata remains where correction is required.

Confirm rollout timing does not expose mixed canon unnecessarily.

## 39. Roles in Practice

The proposer prepares evidence and does not self-certify disputed facts.

The Canon Steward classifies the request and protects process integrity.

The Narrative Lead asks whether the story remains coherent.

The Visual Lead asks whether viewers still recognize the world correctly.

The Production Lead asks whether implementation preserves repeatability.

The Release Lead asks whether audience communication is proportional and truthful.

The QA Lead asks whether the approved change was implemented exactly.

The Archive Custodian asks whether future reviewers can reconstruct the decision.

Every role may request a narrower scope or additional evidence.

Every role must state objections before final approval whenever possible.

## 40. Operating Cadence

Review low-risk proposals in the next available continuity review.

Review high-risk and critical proposals in a dedicated decision meeting.

Do not batch unrelated retcons merely for efficiency.

Maintain a visible register of open, approved, rejected, deferred, implemented, and rolled-back changes.

Review deferred proposals at planned intervals or when new evidence appears.

Audit active locks and prompt versions before starting a major production cycle.

Audit release-facing canon after major seasonal, timeline, or visual changes.

Use post-release reviews to identify process gaps, not to silently revise canon.

## 41. Final Standard

No canon change is complete when it is merely approved.

It is complete only when records, locks, prompts, assets, productions, and communications match the approved decision.

No canon change is invisible to the archive.

No production shortcut outranks continuity without an explicit approved exception.

No generated output outranks established canon.

No release history is erased to make a later decision look inevitable.

Preserve the past.

Explain the present.

Build the future on a traceable foundation.

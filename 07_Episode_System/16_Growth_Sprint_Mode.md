# Growth Sprint Mode

## Purpose

Growth Sprint is the default high-velocity format for future Village Stories
Shorts when the goal is rapid audience acquisition and YouTube monetization.

It deliberately uses modern feed clarity:

- immediate action, weather, emotion, or sensory promise on frame one;
- prompt, meaningful visual progression;
- dense subject-first ASMR;
- captions and pattern interrupts;
- short scenes and hard editorial exits;
- loopable endings.

It does **not** mean mass-produced, interchangeable AI content. YouTube's
monetization policy excludes generic, repetitive, or templated channels that
appear produced only for views. Every release therefore needs a distinct
character action, setting, sound sequence, and payoff.

This mode applies to **future releases only**. Finished episodes remain untouched
unless the producer explicitly requests a re-edit.

---

## Monetization Reality

Current official YPP routes:

| Access | Subscribers | Shorts route | Long-form route |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Expanded YPP (eligible regions; fan funding/shopping) | 500 + 3 public uploads / 90 days | 3 million valid public Shorts views / 90 days | 3,000 valid public watch hours / 12 months |
| Full ad revenue | 1,000 | 10 million valid public Shorts views / 90 days | 4,000 valid public watch hours / 12 months |

Shorts-feed watch time does not count toward the 4,000-hour route.

No production system can guarantee quick monetization. Growth Sprint maximizes
test volume and retention learning while protecting originality.

---

## Default Release Strategy

For growth periods:

1. Publish **4–7 original Shorts per week**.
2. Publish **one strong long-form episode every 1–2 weeks**.
3. Cut 2–4 standalone Shorts from each long-form concept, but give each a
   complete mini-story and unique opening.
4. Review each upload at 48–72 hours.
5. Repeat structures that win; never duplicate the same audiovisual template.

## Mandatory Viral-Benchmark Refinement

Whenever the producer supplies a story — even one sentence — run this gate
**before** scripting, storyboarding, still generation, or animation prompting:

1. Write the supplied story in one sentence without changing its intent.
2. Compare its structure with current, publicly verified winners in the same
   village-life / cooking / family-story niche and with Village Stories'
   internal analytics.
3. Score the concept for:
   - first-frame clarity;
   - emotional stake or relationship reason;
   - specific cultural/food promise;
   - tactile ASMR opportunities;
   - meaningful visual progression;
   - obstacle, anticipation, or discovery;
   - satisfying emotional and sensory payoff;
   - reason to subscribe for the recurring world/characters.
4. State what is missing.
5. Produce a refined Village Stories spine, three honest hook options, primary
   sound anchors, and a recommended runtime cohort.
6. Confirm that the refinement preserves canon and does not copy another
   creator's expression.

Do not use a benchmark as a rigid formula. Public view counts reveal patterns,
but only the creator's private retention data can prove the exact cause of a
video's performance.

### Verified benchmark lesson — 2026-07-17 snapshot

Public review of `@Backto90s-09` and `@villagecookingtales2123` showed that
breakout Shorts in this niche were often **118–178 seconds**, with complete
mini-stories built from rain, relationships, farming, regional food, tactile
cooking, and a warm payoff. The lesson is:

> **Fast meaningful progression, not compulsory short runtime or chaotic cuts.**

These channels and figures are dated research snapshots, not permanent targets.
Refresh benchmarks when their relevance changes.

### Village Stories self-audit — 2026-07-17

Public review of `@RadioPettiASMR` found **49 subscribers, 10 uploads, and eight
Shorts** approximately nine days after the first public Short. This is too early
to declare the channel unsuccessful. The observed story pattern was:

- strongest Short: father cooks chicken curry for his sick wife — a specific
  dish attached to a clear relationship need;
- next strongest: Poori and Mutter Paneer — a specific food/process promise;
- weaker concept: siblings, butterfly, dog, and mud-stove toy — several pleasant
  elements without one causal story;
- newest teaser: “Wait Until the Ending” redirected to a full episode instead
  of completing its own promise;
- recurring weakness: empty establishing openings and routine montages delay
  the event promised by the title.

The butterfly concept was recommended by the assistant. It is recorded as a
story-development failure by the production system, not as a weak idea supplied
by the producer.

**Correction for all future unfinished episodes:**

1. Put the title event, need, or sensory promise in the opening.
2. Build one causal chain; remove adjacent routine beats that do not cause the
   next action.
3. Make every Short complete its own promise, even when it promotes long-form.
4. Reject “beautiful situation only” concepts until they gain a need, change,
   decision, and earned payoff.
5. Preserve finished ep_002 unless the producer explicitly requests changes.

## Short Runtime And Cadence

Select runtime **after** the story-refinement gate:

| Cohort | Runtime | Best use |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Tactile micro-story | 20–45 s | One process, reaction, or transformation |
| Complete mini-story | 60–120 s | Setup → action/process → emotional payoff |
| Extended narrative Short | 120–180 s | Relationship stake + parallel work/cooking + resolution |

Do not stretch a thin idea to match a viral runtime. Do not crush a complete
story into 15–35 seconds merely to appear fast.

### Editorial cadence

- Typical action shot: **1.5–4 seconds**.
- Hero ASMR contact: **2–5 seconds** while material, sound, or state changes.
- Emotional face: hold long enough to read the relationship turn; trim only the
  dead tail.
- Establishing wide: keep only when weather, geography, danger, arrival, or
  atmosphere is story information.
- Renew attention whenever information stalls — not on a mechanical timer.

Attention renewals may be:

- wide → macro;
- character → object;
- top-down → side angle;
- action sound → reaction;
- visual state change;
- caption emphasis;
- new tactile sound;
- speed change inside a single real action.

Do not use unrelated random imagery. Every renewal must advance the same
mini-story.

## Hook Rules

Frame one must show one of:

- liquid already pouring;
- hands already mixing, cutting, grinding, pulling, planting, or feeding;
- animal already reacting;
- character already running, discovering, or deciding;
- a clearly changed object state;
- payoff preview for 0.3–0.7 s, then immediate cause.

Never begin with:

- calendar;
- logo;
- empty village wide with no active weather, story, or sensory information;
- slow push-in before action;
- dialogue setup;
- generic birds/breeze;
- unexplained crying used as bait.

### Caption formula

Use one short curiosity line, preferably 3–7 words:

```text
She made tea before sunrise.
Ramu thought he could win.
One ingredient was missing.
Watch the tea change colour.
```

Captions must describe the real video. No fake stakes.

## Controlled Brainrot Editing

Approved in Growth Sprint:

- rapid hard cuts;
- punch-in crops in edit;
- short speed ramps on non-delicate travel;
- rhythmic match cuts;
- macro insert chains;
- large readable captions;
- visual loops;
- strong tactile sound accents;
- immediate payoff preview;
- reaction cutaways;
- brief whooshes only when tied to a real movement.

Avoid even in Growth Sprint:

- random meme clips;
- unrelated game footage;
- copyrighted reaction clips/music without rights;
- false danger;
- repetitive AI slideshow templates;
- identical narration and outcome across uploads;
- speed ramps on hands, faces, liquid, or fragile continuity;
- effects that obscure the original animation.

## Audio: Hyper-Clear ASMR

The primary subject sound is the main retention tool.

### Mix order

1. Dialogue, if essential.
2. Primary tactile action.
3. Character reaction.
4. Supporting ambience.
5. Music.

### Growth Sprint audio rules

- Primary contact begins in the first **0–0.5 s**.
- Every cut carries a matched contact, movement, or sound handoff.
- Primary action is clearly louder than birds, breeze, and room tone.
- Music is optional; tactile sound remains intelligible over it.
- Remove generic ambience when it masks the hero sound.
- Sync every splash, pour, peck, scrape, footstep, crackle, and vessel contact
  frame-accurately.
- Use natural attack/body/decay, but trim dead silence after decay.
- Test on phone speaker and headphones.

Suggested master: around YouTube comfort (~−14 LUFS integrated) with safe peak
headroom. Do not flatten all ASMR dynamics.

## AI Production Rule

Generate each Flow clip as one coherent action with stable identity. Create
brainrot pace **in editorial assembly**, not by asking Flow to perform five
camera moves or scene changes in one generation.

Recommended workflow:

```text
Generate 4–6 s stable action
→ select strongest 0.7–2.5 s
→ layer synced primary ASMR
→ cut to a new approved angle/state
```

This protects faces, hands, props, and geography while achieving feed speed.

## Originality Gate

Before upload, answer:

- Is the story/action different from the previous three Shorts?
- Is there a new visual state or payoff?
- Is the sound sequence unique to this action?
- Are characters/location used meaningfully, not as interchangeable wallpaper?
- Does the Short work without borrowed clips or copyrighted music?

If not, redesign. High volume is useful only while every upload remains
recognizably original.

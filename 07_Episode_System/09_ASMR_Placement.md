# ASMR Placement

## Purpose and Authority

This manual translates `06_Story_Engine/14_ASMR_Storytelling.md` into episode
planning, shot selection, sound edit, and quality-control rules. It governs
where close natural sound belongs in Shorts and long-form episodes.

ASMR is a storytelling layer, not a separate attraction. It makes visible work,
weather, material, and care feel close enough to notice: a vessel settling
after a careful pour, rain on tile while the family protects an item, coir
rope tightening during a repair, or footsteps slowing so a child is not left
behind.

Early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu canon is binding. Established locations, objects,
materials, weather, work practices, character actions, and animal behaviour
define every permitted sound. A generic “village” sound, modern device,
overstated animal cue, or attractive but unsupported texture must be removed.

## Guiding Principle

**Let one truthful sound deepen one visible act of care.**

## Sound Philosophy

Purpose comes before pleasantness. A close sound earns attention when the
audience can identify its source, see or infer the action causing it, and
understand why the moment matters. Sound can reveal effort, sequence, weather,
waiting, relief, or recognition without interrupting the story.

The series never treats rural labour, people, or animals as an exotic texture
collection. Do not amplify every contact, turn cooking into a rhythm show, or
use sharp sounds to manufacture stakes. Natural sound must preserve dignity
and leave dialogue, expression, and emotional information clear.

Silence is also purposeful. Near-silence retains truthful room tone or
environmental bed; it is not an empty technical track.

## Placement Workflow

### 1. Identify the story function

For each proposed beat, write the visible action and its purpose: establish a
place, reveal skill, mark a change in effort, allow reflection, bridge a
transition, or settle an ending. If the only reason is “it sounds nice,” omit
it.

### 2. Choose one foreground source

Pick one primary sound from a documented source: grinding, stirring, pouring,
chopping where safe and visible, a vessel placed down, broom fibres, rain on
tile, leaves, water, fire, coir rope, bicycle bell where canon permits,
footsteps, or gentle animal movement.

Confirm who causes it, what material produces it, where it occurs, how close
the listener is, and what state the object is in.

### 3. Place it in an episode beat

Use a short ASMR pause only where it advances the episode:

* **Opening:** a truthful sensory hook that establishes the task or weather.
* **Preparation:** a material detail that introduces skill or attention.
* **Main activity:** a work rhythm that makes progress, difficulty, or
  cooperation audible.
* **Emotional turn:** a reduced sound bed or small accent that leaves room for
  a look, apology, or recognition.
* **Transition:** a physically plausible environmental carry-over between
  adjacent spaces.
* **Ending:** one settling sound or near-silence after the emotional proof is
  complete.

Do not use every placement in one Short. One or two purposeful close beats are
usually enough.

### 4. Build the hierarchy

Every scene needs a clearly ranked mix:

1. **Primary sound:** the meaningful visible action.
2. **Supporting ambience:** the established place and weather.
3. **Human presence:** cloth, breath, footsteps, short dialogue, or restrained
   laughter.
4. **Optional emotional accent:** one brief sound that marks completion or
   changed understanding.

Never mix all layers at equal prominence. Dialogue and a character's reaction
override texture when they carry the scene's meaning.

### Subject-first ASMR contract (2026-07-17 lock)

Primary-subject audio must be **audibly stronger** than ambience on phone
speakers and headphones. “Soft birds under everything” is a defect when hands,
vessels, water, fire, or food are the hero action.

For every featured ASMR beat, record before mix approval:

| Field | Required answer |
| --- | --- |
| Source | Exact object / body / material visible on screen |
| Contact | Attack → body → decay of the sound |
| Listener distance | CU / MCU / MS / wide (must match framing) |
| Prominence | Primary / supporting / bed only |
| Level intent | Primary must remain clear above ambience |
| Competing layers | List what must be ducked or removed |
| Sync points | Exact visual contacts that must hit |

**Mix rule of thumb:**

- Primary ASMR / action: clearly forward
- Room / birds / breeze: soft bed — never equal to primary
- Music: usually off during featured ASMR; if present, −18 to −24 dB relative to primary
- Phone-speaker test: if the pour/stir/splash cannot be heard, the mix fails

**Prompt rule for Flow audio:** name the primary sound first, with concrete verbs
(pour, splash, creak, peck, stir, scrape). Do not write only “soft morning ambience.”

### 5. Protect the transition and result

Record when the action starts, changes, and stops. The sound must evolve with
the action and must not continue after its source ends. Leave a little room
before and after a meaningful sound; rapid cuts on every contact make the
work feel performed for effect.

## Decision Rules

### Add close sound when

* the source is visible or clearly established;
* it clarifies material, effort, sequence, or completion;
* it fits the location, season, and period;
* it reinforces the present emotional beat;
* it does not obscure speech, a facial reaction, safety information, or
  continuity.

### Reduce sound when

* a parent gives a necessary concise instruction;
* siblings repair a misunderstanding;
* an expression supplies the emotional answer;
* several details compete for attention;
* the moment needs calm rather than stimulation.

### Remove sound when

* the source is absent, transformed, or too far away;
* it repeats without revealing changed action;
* it makes ordinary work sound alarming, comic, luxurious, or spectacular;
* it conflicts with visible rain, ground condition, time of day, or geography;
* it implies a modern object, vehicle, device, or urban setting;
* it turns an animal into a joke, threat, or emotional shortcut.

## Location, Season, and Character Application

### Household spaces

In the established Kitchen and household work areas, use sounds that follow
real process: grinding, careful chopping where appropriate, stirring, pouring,
firewood placed safely, vessel movement, water, steam, and fabric or bangles
moving with work. The sequence must correspond to preparation, heat, waiting,
or completion—not an unrelated sensory montage.

The Courtyard and Front Thinnai can carry broom fibres, a door movement,
footsteps on the correct ground, leaf rustle, water in a vessel, birds, quiet
family steps, and the Family Dog settling. Keep public or market activity at
a believable distance.

### Field, garden, animal, and village locations

Use field wind, plant movement, water, basket shift, rope, distant livestock,
and safe documented tools only when the activity supports them. Animal sound
must remain gentle and proportionate.

On village paths and at public locations, footfall, a permitted bicycle bell,
distant conversation, schoolyard distance, a Tea Stall's restrained activity,
or a Grocery Store's object handling can establish purpose. A small village
must not suddenly sound like a modern, crowded town.

### Seasonal conditions

Summer supports dry wind, quieter midday activity, and careful water sounds.
Monsoon supports rain on tile, damp footfalls, intermittent drips, protected
fire sounds, and softened distance. Post-monsoon can reveal clearer outdoor
sound and movement through renewed growth. Harvest may use grain, baskets, and
cooperative work without making labour percussive spectacle.

Weather intensity, surface wetness, indoor/outdoor position, and time of day
must remain consistent through the sequence.

### Family sound language

Mother's sound language is patient household skill: stirring, pouring, folding,
preparing, and placing. Pair it with attentive listening and measured speech.

Father's sound language includes inspection, repair, lifting, rope work, and
deliberate footsteps. It should suggest steadiness, never machismo or crisis.

Elder Brother may bring energetic movement, school items, outdoor steps, and
practical assistance. Let the pace soften when care for another character
becomes the point.

Younger Daughter's sound language is safe, curious participation: careful
contact with established objects, gentle footsteps, and quiet questions. Do
not amplify every movement to make her cute.

The Family Dog may provide a soft step, coat or collar movement only where
canon supports it, a settling breath, or a gentle response to routine. The dog
never receives human-like comic cues.

## Examples and Non-Examples

### Meaningful work rhythm

Good: Rain muffles the kitchen's fire sound while the family works patiently.
After a practical adjustment, the crackle and vessel sound become steady. The
audience hears progress before anyone says it.

Not good: Loud, evenly amplified cutting, stirring, steam, fire, birds, and
music play together while no character action matters.

### Relationship recognition

Good: Elder Brother's footsteps stop; a school bag shifts; the ambient bed
reduces as he waits for Younger Daughter. Their resumed shared footsteps close
the beat.

Not good: A loud bell, swoosh, or comic animal sound announces that he has
become kind.

### Transition

Good: Rain heard through the Kitchen opening continues naturally as the camera
moves to the Courtyard, with surface and distance changing gradually.

Not good: A school bell appears at full volume inside the family compound
without a route, distance, or visual source.

### Final settling beat

Good: After the family completes a small repair, the object is set down, its
natural contact sounds once, and a quiet room tone allows the look of relief to
land.

Not good: The sound repeats after the object has stopped moving, then music
swells to tell viewers what to feel.

## Google Flow and Sound Handoff

Google Flow visuals do not guarantee usable or consistent sound. Create the
sound plan from locked visual action, never from assumed generated audio.

For every generated shot, record:

* exact visible sound source and material;
* action start, contact points, change, and finish;
* camera and implied listener distance;
* location, time, season, weather, and ground/wetness condition;
* character and animal position and movement direction;
* dialogue window, reaction window, and intended quiet;
* ambient bed entering and leaving the cut.

Prompt for clear, workable visual sources. For example:

`Early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu established family Kitchen, Mother gently stirs
a metal vessel at a traditional cooking area while rain falls softly on the
tile roof outside; Younger Daughter watches from a safe documented distance;
visible wet courtyard, restrained morning activity, medium close shot focused
on the real hand action and vessel, no modern objects or exaggerated motion.`

Reject or regenerate Flow output when the tool, vessel, surface, rain,
wardrobe, hand action, or character distance changes from the locked sequence.
Revise the sound plan if the image changes; never keep sound that belongs to
an earlier visual version.

## Shorts and Long-Form Execution

### Shorts

Open with one truthful sensory detail only when it reveals the episode's
activity, weather, or feeling immediately. Keep ASMR pauses brief enough that
the visual hook and story need remain clear. Use one primary sound family and
one natural ending rest; do not build a sequence from disconnected pleasant
clips.

If a Short continues, resolve today's sound and emotional beat before carrying
a practical environmental sound into the next story. A rain bed may continue
across a truthful time-contiguous sequence; it may not become an ominous
cliffhanger.

### Long form

Long-form episodes may return to a sound family as work progresses—grinding,
rain, a repair rhythm, or shared walking—when the repetition reveals time,
effort, or changed emotion. Vary it with real action and quiet. Never extend
runtime with looping sound, repeated insert shots, or constant ambience.

Use rests between main and supporting threads so the soundscape helps the
viewer understand location and pacing. The final sound must support the
resolved main thread under `07_Endings.md`.

## Continuity and Quality Gate

Approve an ASMR plan only when:

* each foreground sound has a visible, canon-supported source;
* material, distance, action state, and volume are plausible;
* one element clearly leads the mix;
* dialogue, safety cues, and emotional reactions remain intelligible;
* weather, surface, location, time, and animal position match image and
  adjacent shots;
* sound starts, changes, and stops with the visible action;
* repeated sounds reveal progress rather than decorate empty time;
* no modern, urban, comic, threatening, or exoticising texture has entered;
* the ending gives natural sound or near-silence room to settle.

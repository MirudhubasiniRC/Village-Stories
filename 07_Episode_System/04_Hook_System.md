# Hook System

## Authority

This manual governs the viewer-facing hook of every Village Stories episode: the first image, first sound, first action, first line, and first clear reason to continue.

It is authoritative for episode execution. `06_Story_Engine/` remains authoritative for story truth, character truth, conflict boundaries, and emotional doctrine. Where this manual and Story Engine appear to differ, Story Engine decides what may happen; this manual decides how the permitted material is introduced to a viewer.

---

## Guiding Principle

**Earn attention by showing the real beginning of something worth seeing, then repay that attention with the episode viewers were invited into.**

A hook is not a promise of spectacle. It is the opening proof that the episode already has movement, feeling, or useful curiosity. The audience should not need to wait through a title card, a general village montage, or explanatory narration to discover why this particular episode matters.

Truthful retention is the operating standard. A high-performing opening that misrepresents the episode is a production failure, even if its first-minute retention is strong.

---

## 1. Purpose

The hook system has five production purposes:

1. establish immediate attention without manipulation;
2. identify the episode's active subject;
3. orient the viewer before confusion becomes friction;
4. set the correct emotional and sensory expectation; and
5. hand the viewer cleanly into Scene 1.

The hook does not need to explain the whole premise. It must give the audience a stable question, a visible change, or an action whose completion they can naturally want to witness.

For this channel, the strongest hooks are often small:

- a pot that has unexpectedly run dry;
- a school bag left at the gate;
- Father testing a repaired handle;
- Mother pausing over an old recipe note;
- the Family Dog refusing to leave a particular spot;
- rain arriving before harvested grain is covered.

Small stakes are not weak stakes when the image makes the practical or emotional importance legible.

---

## 2. What a Hook Is—and Is Not

### A hook is

- an episode-specific opening beat;
- a concrete invitation to observe an action, need, choice, or discovery;
- a truthful preview of the episode's actual center;
- an editorial agreement between the thumbnail/title expectation and Scene 1;
- a bridge from scrolling attention to calm, sustained viewing.

### A hook is not

- a generic “welcome back” introduction;
- a detached beauty shot used because it looks pleasant;
- a spoiler montage of the ending;
- a threat, accident, or crisis manufactured in edit;
- a loud sound effect used to substitute for story;
- a title card placed before the viewer has been given a reason to stay;
- an unanswered question that the episode has no intention of addressing.

Do not confuse speed with urgency. A quiet close-up can hook better than rapid cutting when it reveals a specific change and lets the viewer understand it immediately.

---

## 3. The Retention Philosophy

YouTube retention is useful feedback, not a license to deceive. The opening must respect two realities at once:

- viewers decide quickly whether an episode is relevant and understandable; and
- Village Stories earns loyalty by delivering gentleness, coherence, and ordinary life without exaggeration.

Therefore, production should optimize for **clarity before intensity**.

An effective hook answers, within the first moments:

| Viewer need | Production answer |
| --- | --- |
| What am I looking at? | A legible object, person, place, or action |
| What changed? | The missing item, arriving weather, waiting dog, unfinished task |
| Why continue? | The change will lead to a real attempt, choice, or family moment |
| What will this feel like? | The correct rhythm, sound world, and emotional temperature |

If analytics show an early drop, first diagnose clarity, relevance, audio comprehension, and title-to-opening alignment. Do not automatically add danger, captions, faster cuts, or a louder music cue.

---

## 4. Hook Anatomy

Every approved hook contains these functional parts, though they may occur in one shot.

### 4.1 The anchor

The anchor is the first understandable image or sound. It should be specific: hands searching an empty spice shelf, rain striking drying clothes, a bicycle wheel stopping at the gate.

Avoid anchors that require prior knowledge, such as an unexplained extreme close-up of a tool part, unless the next shot clarifies it immediately.

### 4.2 The change or need

Show what is different, wanted, unfinished, or emotionally active. The need can be practical or relational, but it must be visible in behavior.

### 4.3 The forward pull

The forward pull tells the viewer that the episode will proceed. It can be a character deciding to act, a question asked naturally, or a physical process beginning.

### 4.4 The orientation

By the transition into Scene 1, viewers must know where they are, who is involved, and what the episode is broadly about.

### 4.5 The handoff

The last hook shot must lead into the first scene rather than restart it. If Mother looks at an empty jar, Scene 1 begins with her deciding what to cook or collect—not a second unrelated village introduction.

---

## 5. Timing Standards

Timing follows function, not a fixed number of seconds. Use the ranges below as review targets.

| Format | First anchor | Episode premise clear | Scene 1 underway |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Short under 60 seconds | 0–1 second | 1–4 seconds | 3–6 seconds |
| Short 60–120 seconds | 0–2 seconds | 2–7 seconds | 5–10 seconds |
| Long form | 0–3 seconds | 4–15 seconds | 10–25 seconds |

Long-form episodes may breathe, but they may not postpone relevance. A beautiful dawn can open a long-form episode only when dawn itself is the meaningful condition: for example, the family must leave before heat, or Father is starting work while the village wakes.

Do not use a fixed “hook shot” duration. Hold a shot long enough for recognition and emotional reading; cut when new information, action, or attention is needed.

---

## 6. Hook Families

Choose a hook family that matches the episode's actual engine.

### 6.1 Need hook

Open on a practical absence or task.

Positive: Mother lifts an empty rice container, looks toward the garden, and begins gathering what is available.

Negative: Mother says, “Something terrible has happened,” before revealing that the rice container is nearly empty.

### 6.2 Process hook

Open after a recognizable process has already begun.

Positive: a repaired bicycle chain clicks once, slips, and Father calmly examines it.

Negative: a montage of the final smooth ride suggests a repair success before the repair story begins.

### 6.3 Relationship hook

Open on a readable interaction with emotional direction.

Positive: Younger Daughter hides a handmade card when Elder Brother enters, then watches him leave for school.

Negative: a crying close-up with no context, edited as if a serious loss has occurred.

### 6.4 Arrival hook

Open on a person, message, object, or weather condition arriving.

Positive: the Family Dog hears the postman's bicycle bell and runs to the gate.

Negative: a door opening cut to dramatic music when the visitor is simply a neighbor bringing seeds.

### 6.5 Discovery hook

Open on the first true moment of noticing.

Positive: Father brushes dust from an old wooden toy and recognizes a carved mark.

Negative: holding the toy up to camera with text promising “A SHOCKING SECRET.”

### 6.6 Sensory-process hook

Open on a sound or texture that is central to the episode.

Positive: rain begins on a tin roof while Mother moves drying herbs inside.

Negative: an attractive fire crackle opening an episode whose subject is a school errand.

## 7. Decision Framework

At scripting, answer these questions in order:

1. What is the episode's actual center of action?
2. What visible moment first proves that center exists?
3. Which character can make that moment emotionally legible?
4. Which location naturally supplies the needed context?
5. What would a new viewer understand without prior episode knowledge?
6. What must not be promised because the episode cannot deliver it?
7. What exact action begins Scene 1?

Select the hook only after the scene sequence is stable. A hook cannot rescue an episode with no clear progression; it can only make the existing progression visible sooner.

Use this selection rule:

- choose the **need hook** for a task-led episode;
- choose the **relationship hook** for a feeling-led episode;
- choose the **arrival hook** when the arrival changes the day;
- choose the **process hook** when making, repairing, cooking, or tending is the reward;
- choose the **discovery hook** when the discovery genuinely changes the character's next action.

If two hook options seem equal, choose the one that requires less explanation and leads most directly into the first scene.

## 8. Workflow

### 8.1 Story and script

The episode writer supplies three candidate opening beats in the episode brief. Each candidate must state:

- the visible anchor;
- the active character;
- the practical or emotional need;
- the expected first sound;
- the Scene 1 handoff; and
- the promise the opening makes to the audience.

The director selects one candidate after confirming it accurately represents the completed script.

### 8.2 Storyboard

The storyboard artist boards the hook as a continuous mini-sequence, not as isolated “attention shots.” Include screen direction, character eyelines, object geography, and the transition to Scene 1.

Show the information hierarchy: first the anchor, then the change, then the response. Do not board three competing focal points in one opening frame.

### 8.3 Editorial plan

The editor receives an opening beat sheet with:

- target format and duration;
- first-frame requirement;
- permitted reveal order;
- title-card placement, if any;
- mandatory sound cue;
- first-scene in point; and
- prohibited misleading inserts.

### 8.4 Review

Review the hook without the title, description, or prior episode context. If a reviewer cannot identify the basic situation quickly, improve staging or shot order.

Then review it with the title and thumbnail. If those materials promise a different event, revise packaging or the opening; never solve the mismatch with deceptive wording.

## 9. Google Flow Handoff

Google Flow generation must serve the planned hook, not invent a more dramatic one.

For each hook shot, the handoff packet includes:

- episode ID and format;
- shot ID, duration range, and camera framing;
- character model/version and approved wardrobe;
- location model/version, time of day, weather, and season;
- exact action start and end state;
- required prop position and continuity notes;
- emotional performance direction;
- sound source or required silence;
- transition-in and transition-out conditions;
- negative constraints, including “no panic,” “no exaggerated reaction,” and “no extra people.”

Example production prompt direction:

> Medium close-up in the family kitchen at early morning. Mother opens the rice container, sees it is nearly empty, pauses thoughtfully, then turns toward the garden basket. Calm practical concern, not alarm. Soft kitchen ambience and distant birds. Keep the container on the left shelf; preserve her approved blue work sari and kitchen layout. End with her hand reaching for the basket.

Do not prompt “make it engaging” or “cinematic surprise.” Specify the real action and its feeling.

### Flow review gates

Reject or regenerate a hook shot when it:

- changes the character's established appearance or costume;
- adds an unplanned disaster, expression, or prop;
- obscures the anchor with decorative camera movement;
- begins after the meaningful action has already happened;
- cannot cut cleanly into Scene 1;
- depicts weather, season, or location inconsistently with the episode brief.

## 10. Shorts Execution

In Shorts, the hook and first story beat overlap. The viewer should encounter the active condition immediately and see a response start before the opening feels complete.

Shorts standards:

- begin on an action or condition, never a channel ident;
- use one primary question only;
- keep setup visual whenever possible;
- reveal the episode subject before a viewer can mistake the clip for unrelated ambience;
- do not consume the ending payoff in the first seconds;
- move to the next beat once the action is understood.

Short positive example: Rain hits the laundry line; Elder Brother looks up, grabs a basket, and runs toward the courtyard. The problem and response are clear in one continuous movement.

Short negative example: Ten seconds of rain montage, title text, rooster sound, then a line that explains the clothes are outside.

A Short may open with a moment later than the chronological beginning only if the cut remains truthful. It cannot imply that the final result is in danger when the episode is actually a calm, routine task.

---

## 11. Long-Form Execution

Long-form openings may establish a wider sensory world, multiple participants, or a more gradual emotional premise. They still need an active center.

Long-form standards:

- place the meaningful condition in the first sequence;
- allow atmosphere to support the premise, not replace it;
- introduce the central character before adding secondary activity;
- use a title card only after the opening has earned attention;
- reserve slower observational shots for an episode where observation itself is the stated reward;
- create a first-scene objective before the opening sequence ends.

Positive example: At dawn, Father arranges tools beside an old bench. He tests one loose leg; it wobbles. The dawn, tool sounds, and quiet house establish atmosphere while the repair episode begins.

Negative example: a 45-second dawn montage followed by Father saying he might repair a bench sometime today.

## 12. Season, Character, and Location Use

Hooks must use the channel's world as story evidence, not decoration.

### Season

Season changes what can be urgent in a gentle way: monsoon rain, harvest timing, summer heat, festival preparation, school reopening, or a garden's first growth. Confirm the condition in the seasonal plan before scripting it.

### Character

Assign the hook to the character most naturally affected by the episode's opening condition. Do not use the Family Dog as a generic attention device if the dog does not matter to the episode.

### Location

Choose a location that makes the task readable. A tea stall works for a neighbor exchange; a kitchen works for an ingredient problem; the school gate works for a school-related concern. Do not relocate an opening merely for visual variety.

---

## 13. Continuity Requirements

The hook is part of the episode timeline and must match every later scene.

Track:

- time of day and weather;
- character clothing, dirt, wetness, and possessions;
- prop condition and placement;
- physical progress of cooking, repair, cleaning, or travel;
- whether a character has already learned information;
- emotional state carried into Scene 1;
- established entrances, exits, and screen direction.

If the hook shows Father finding a letter, later scenes cannot present the letter as newly discovered. If the hook begins during rain, the next scene cannot be dry unless the passage of time is shown or stated.

---

## 14. Quality Standards

Approve a hook only when all statements are true:

- It begins with a readable, episode-specific anchor.
- It truthfully represents the episode's actual subject.
- The central action or question is clear at the format's required speed.
- The audio supports comprehension and does not force false urgency.
- The character behavior is canon-consistent.
- The setting, season, props, and time are continuous.
- The cut into Scene 1 feels inevitable.
- The hook does not need the thumbnail, title, or prior episode to make sense.
- The hook makes no promise the ending cannot satisfy.

### Common failure corrections

| Failure | Likely cause | Correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Viewer cannot tell what matters | Too many scenic shots | Begin on the object or action that changes the day |
| Early exit after click | Packaging mismatch | Align title/thumbnail with the actual opening |
| Hook feels loud but empty | Music or cutting replaces story | Restore a concrete action and reduce editorial emphasis |
| Opening repeats weekly | Habitual sunrise use | Select from the episode's active need or process |
| Scene 1 feels delayed | Hook is separate from story | End the hook on the first-scene action |

---

## 15. Edge Cases

### Quiet episode

If there is no obvious problem, hook the intentional activity: a family member choosing seeds, preparing a gift, or beginning a remembered recipe. Do not manufacture an obstacle.

### Ensemble episode

Anchor with one readable action, then widen to the group. Starting with every character at once creates visual and emotional ambiguity.

### Sequel or continuation

Briefly show the continuing task in a way a new viewer can understand. Do not rely on “last time” exposition unless the information is essential and cannot be shown.

### Educational episode

Open with the practical question or observable method, not a lecture. Show the leaf, tool, grain, or step first; name it after attention has a physical reference.

### No dialogue available

Use action, sound, and staging to make the need clear. Captions may clarify essential information but must not sensationalize it.

---

## 16. Prohibited Practices

Never use:

- false danger, injury, loss, or conflict;
- reaction shots from another moment;
- an ending image presented as if it is about to fail;
- text that promises a revelation absent from the episode;
- a child or animal's distress purely as a scroll-stopping device;
- a misleading title card before the story starts;
- excessive visual noise that conflicts with the channel's calm identity.

When an edit feels more exciting than the actual episode, evaluate it as a warning sign, not an achievement.

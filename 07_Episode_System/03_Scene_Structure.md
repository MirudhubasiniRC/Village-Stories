# Scene Structure

## Purpose

This manual translates an approved episode spine into scenes that can be boarded, generated, edited, and reviewed.

A scene is not simply a change of camera angle.

A scene is a controlled unit of time, place, action, and emotional purpose.

It begins when the viewer needs a new piece of orientation, progress, feeling, or sensory attention.

It ends when that purpose has been delivered and the episode needs to move.

This document governs physical scene construction.

It does not override the Story Engine’s authority over story doctrine, emotional truth, character use, or canon eligibility.

## Guiding Principle

Every scene must earn its screen time by changing the episode’s visible state or the viewer’s understanding of it.

## Authority

This is the primary `07_Episode_System/` authority for scene purpose, scene order, visual proof, transitions, and scene-level continuity.

Use it with the relevant World, Character, Location, Object, Culture, and Story Engine documents.

If a scene requirement conflicts with canon, change the scene.

Never change canon to preserve a convenient shot sequence.

## What Makes a Scene

A valid scene contains:

* A documented location or a continuous area within one documented location.
* A discernible time, weather, and task state.
* One primary action or observation.
* A reason the present character or characters are there.
* A visual change, emotional turn, or necessary setup.
* An end state that can hand off cleanly to the next scene.

A scene may contain several shots.

A shot may contain more than one beat.

Neither fact removes the scene’s responsibility to move the episode.

## The Core Scene Chain

Most Village Stories episodes draw from this chain:

```text
Active opening image
↓
Orientation and present state
↓
Need, intention, or gentle question
↓
Preparation
↓
Meaningful process
↓
Adjustment, discovery, or proportionate obstacle
↓
Response and practical progress
↓
Completion or honest pause
↓
Emotional settling image
↓
Optional truthful continuation seed
```

This is a function chain, not a mandatory ten-scene template.

In a Short, one scene may perform orientation, need, and preparation together.

In long-form, process and response may unfold through several distinct locations or stages.

Do not omit an essential function because a formula says the scene count must remain low.

Do not add a scene simply because the formula lists another function.

## Scene Purpose Categories

Before boarding, assign each scene one primary purpose.

### 1. Hook Scene

The hook scene provides an immediate, truthful reason to watch the next moment.

It starts in action or on a condition that visibly matters.

It must make a promise the episode can keep.

Examples of function include a task already underway, a weather change affecting work, a needed object missing from its usual place, or a family member noticing a meaningful practical detail.

Do not use a generic scenic opening as a hook unless the image itself changes the day’s action.

### 2. Orientation Scene

The orientation scene tells the viewer where the action is occurring and what state it is in.

It may establish the known location, time of day, weather, object state, and active character.

It must be brief in a Short and clear in any format.

If the hook already provides all of this, do not repeat it.

### 3. Intention Scene

The intention scene makes the present task or relational movement legible.

The audience should understand what the character is trying to do, prepare, finish, share, repair, or respond to.

Show intention through selection, movement, gaze, placement, or beginning an action.

Use dialogue only where it genuinely improves clarity.

### 4. Preparation Scene

The preparation scene gathers materials, changes the work area, establishes sequence, or makes a later difficulty understandable.

Preparation is not filler when it changes the viewer’s expectation.

For example, a prepared surface, an object checked before use, or a family member joining a task can give later action meaning.

Do not show every repetitive preparation step.

### 5. Process Scene

The process scene shows the central work in progress.

It should contain visible transformation, decision, effort, or sensory discovery.

Process may be physical, relational, or observational.

The first process scene explains how the activity works.

Later process scenes must add a new stage, condition, or emotional value.

### 6. Turn Scene

The turn scene changes the anticipated path.

The turn may be a small obstacle, a discovery, a weather shift, an object response, a changed understanding, or another character’s helpful action.

The turn must be proportionate to everyday life.

It does not require danger, humiliation, or raised volume.

### 7. Response Scene

The response scene shows what the characters do because of the turn.

This is where practical intelligence, patience, care, cooperation, or learning becomes visible.

Never cut from a difficulty directly to a solved result without the adaptation that gives the episode its meaning.

### 8. Payoff Scene

The payoff scene proves that the opening question, effort, or feeling has reached a satisfying point.

It may show the task’s result, an object’s new state, a changed behaviour, a shared meal, a completed route, or a quiet acknowledgment.

Payoff must be screen evidence, not an assertion in music or narration.

### 9. Rest Scene

The rest scene lets the payoff breathe.

It uses a closing action, location image, natural sound, or family response to settle the episode.

Rest is especially important after a dense Short or a long-form sequence with many task stages.

It should not restart the story.

### 10. Continuation Seed Scene

Use a continuation seed only when a real next step remains.

The seed can be part of the rest scene.

It must never erase the satisfaction of the payoff.

## Scene Value Test

Ask these questions of every scene:

* What is different by the end of this scene?
* What does the viewer now know, feel, expect, or notice?
* Which next scene becomes possible because of this one?
* Could the same information be shown in fewer, clearer images?
* Does the scene use documented people, place, object, season, and behaviour?

If there is no clear answer, cut, merge, or rebuild the scene.

## The State Ledger

Before Google Flow generation, record the scene state.

The ledger must include:

* Scene number and intended duration range.
* Scene purpose category.
* Location and spatial orientation.
* Time of day and weather.
* Present family members.
* Character clothing and physical condition.
* Object start positions.
* Action performed.
* Object end positions.
* Environmental changes.
* Emotional temperature.
* Required opening image for the next scene.
* Required natural sound, music, or silence.

The ledger is not administrative overhead.

It is the bridge between script intention and repeatable visual generation.

## A Scene Card Template

Use this production card for each scene:

```text
Scene:
Episode / format:
Purpose:
Location and orientation:
Time / season / weather:
Characters present:
Start state:
Primary action:
Visible change:
Emotional movement:
End state:
Transition out:
Google Flow prompt facts:
Sound plan:
Continuity checks:
```

Write observable facts in the card.

Avoid abstract directions such as “make it nostalgic” or “make it heartwarming.”

Specify the action, object, light, sound, pause, or response that will create the desired feeling.

## Short-Form Scene Construction

A Short generally contains three to six scene units.

The number depends on whether a scene carries more than one function.

A typical Short may use:

```text
Scene 1: active hook plus orientation
Scene 2: intention and preparation
Scene 3: process and turn
Scene 4: response and payoff
Scene 5: rest image with optional seed
```

This is a starting structure, not a quota.

Keep the first scene immediate.

Give the middle a visible change around the central action.

Reserve the last seconds for completion rather than an extended outro.

In a Short, a new scene must arrive because the task, place, scale, or feeling has changed.

Do not cut rapidly only to imitate fast-paced platform editing.

## Long-Form Scene Construction

Long-form usually contains seven to fourteen meaningful scene units.

It must retain one primary physical thread across the full runtime.

The additional scenes should provide:

* A more complete process.
* A documented movement between relevant locations.
* A changing seasonal or weather condition.
* A secondary relationship beat that affects the main task.
* Time for natural sound and observed work.

Long-form scene order should still be easy to describe in one sentence per scene.

If a sequence cannot be described without “then more of the same happens,” it is probably filler.

## Scene Duration Rules

Duration follows value, not a fixed stopwatch.

Hold longer when viewers need to:

* Read a new location.
* Understand a practical process.
* Notice a meaningful reaction.
* Hear a defining natural sound.
* Feel the completion of a task.

Move sooner when:

* The action has been understood.
* A repeat does not change meaning.
* A transition has already communicated its purpose.
* Music is carrying an image without story evidence.

Use the pacing timeline document for platform timing targets.

Use this manual to decide whether the content deserves that time.

## Internal Shot Rhythm

Within a scene, select shots for a clear reason:

* Establishing view for place and relation.
* Medium action view for body, tool, and task.
* Detail view for material change or sensory emphasis.
* Reaction view for emotional interpretation.
* Result view for proof of progress.

Do not collect every angle as insurance.

Plan the fewest shots that make the scene readable and alive.

In Google Flow, fewer variables per shot produce more reliable continuity.

## Visual Cause and Effect

Each scene should visually answer the previous scene’s question or create the next one.

Show the cause before the consequence.

Show the object before its absence matters.

Show the weather before it alters the plan.

Show a misunderstanding before the repair.

Show a repaired or prepared state before relying on it in a final image.

This rule protects viewers from confusion when dialogue is sparse.

## Transition Rules

Use a cut when the action continues with energy or clarity.

Use a held shot when a response needs time.

Use a sound bridge when the connection is sensory or temporal.

Use an establishing image when location geography needs renewal.

Use a match cut only when the matched visual form also advances meaning.

Do not use decorative transitions that make ordinary work feel artificially grand.

Do not hide a continuity break behind a dissolve, blur, or music swell.

## Location Transitions

Only move between established locations.

Before a location change, confirm:

* Why the family needs to move.
* Who is travelling.
* What they carry or leave behind.
* Which documented path or spatial relationship makes the movement plausible.
* How the destination’s condition differs from the departure location.

For a Short, often imply travel with departure and arrival evidence rather than extended route footage.

For long-form, show travel only where route, weather, relationship, or task meaning changes during it.

## Character Transitions

Characters may enter or leave a scene when the task makes their movement meaningful.

Show a handoff, a look toward the next action, a completed contribution, or another clear reason.

Do not make Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, or the Family Dog appear and disappear only to satisfy an assumed ensemble requirement.

Check individual character documentation before staging ability, temperament, clothing, route, and interaction.

## Seasonal and Environmental Continuity

Every scene belongs to a real environmental state.

Track:

* Sun angle and light direction.
* Cloud cover and rainfall.
* Ground wetness and accumulated water.
* Wind effects.
* Vegetation condition.
* Smoke, steam, dust, and heat.
* Ambient sound.
* Time needed for task progress.

Do not place dry clothing, dry ground, and active heavy rain in adjacent shots without an explanatory transition.

Do not use a changing sky merely because it looks cinematic.

Season must alter the scene’s physical logic when it is visible.

## Object Continuity

Objects carry story evidence.

Track their place, condition, amount, ownership, and handling.

Before a scene ends, record:

* Which object is in which character’s hand.
* Which object has been set down, used, emptied, covered, cleaned, repaired, opened, or moved.
* Which condition must be visible in the next scene.

Generate inserts from the same approved state as wider shots whenever possible.

If Google Flow returns an inconsistent object, regenerate or revise the shot plan.

Do not cut around an impossible state change.

## Google Flow Scene Workflow

1. Approve the scene card before prompting.
2. Generate the clearest wide or medium action shot that establishes the scene state.
3. Generate only the inserts or reactions required to clarify action, texture, or emotion.
4. Check character identity, clothing, hands, object count, location features, period details, weather, and direction of movement.
5. Record the selected clip’s final state in the ledger.
6. Build the next scene from that final state.
7. Re-review adjoining clips after editing, not only as isolated generations.

Prompts should state location, time, characters, physical action, objects, and camera purpose.

Prompts should not rely on a model to infer hidden story context.

## Sound and Silence at Scene Level

Every scene needs a sound decision.

Choose one of:

* Dominant natural sound.
* Natural sound supported by restrained music.
* Music-forward transition with retained environmental evidence.
* Intentional quiet.

Give special attention to sounds that prove the action: grinding, water, rain, footsteps, utensils, fabric, wind, fire, gates, or the Family Dog’s plausible movement.

Do not bury all natural sound under continuous music.

Do not use silence as an empty gap when a location’s sound could orient the viewer.

## Dialogue and Caption Economy

Dialogue should clarify a relationship beat, practical decision, or cultural detail that action cannot carry alone.

Captions should support comprehension, not repeat obvious imagery line by line.

Do not add narration because a scene has weak visual cause.

First rebuild the scene.

Keep captions away from hands, faces, tools, and other essential visual information.

## Scene-Level Positives

A strong scene:

* Has one clear focus.
* Opens on a readable state.
* Shows a meaningful action.
* Ends in a changed state or a deliberate handoff.
* Uses documented world details accurately.
* Lets the character’s behaviour reveal feeling.
* Supplies edit points without losing cause and effect.
* Leaves a useful continuity record.

## Scene-Level Negatives

Rework a scene that:

* Repeats an earlier action without a new result.
* Requires narration to explain why it exists.
* Contains a character, object, or location not in canon.
* Has a dramatic reaction without a visible trigger.
* Changes weather, wardrobe, object state, or time of day without explanation.
* Uses montage to conceal missing process.
* Contains more simultaneous actions than the viewer can follow.
* Ends only because the planned duration has been reached.

## Edge Case: A Scene Is Beautiful but Static

Keep it only if it orients the audience, carries a needed emotional pause, establishes a relevant environmental condition, or prepares a payoff.

Otherwise reduce it to the essential image or remove it.

Beauty supports the episode.

It is not an independent scene purpose.

## Edge Case: The Process Needs Many Repeated Actions

Show the first action to teach the process.

Show the later action that reveals change.

Use a transition, sound bridge, or detail insert to imply the repeated middle.

Keep one clear visual marker of elapsed work.

## Edge Case: A Long-Form Scene Slows the Episode

Determine whether it provides observation or merely duration.

If it provides observation, protect the detail that makes it valuable.

If it merely extends an already-understood task, merge it, shorten it, or replace it with a later meaningful state.

## Edge Case: A Short Feels Rushed

Do not immediately add scenes.

First combine redundant orientation, remove decorative cuts, and preserve one decisive process shot plus one earned rest image.

The audience needs clarity before speed.

## Edge Case: The Final Scene Has No Emotional Weight

Return to the opening contract.

Make the final image show the completed state, response, or shared quiet that directly answers it.

Do not attempt to create weight with only music, slow motion, or a title card.

## Required Scene Workflow

1. Identify the episode’s central visible change.
2. Map the required function chain.
3. Create a numbered scene card for each scene.
4. Assign one primary purpose to every scene.
5. Verify all canon-dependent facts.
6. Create and approve the state ledger.
7. Board the minimum shot set that proves action and emotion.
8. Generate clips using the scene card facts.
9. Assemble scenes in causal order.
10. Review each scene boundary for visual, environmental, object, and emotional continuity.
11. Remove or merge any scene that fails the scene value test.
12. Confirm the ending is a resting point, not merely the last available clip.

## Scene Review Questions

Before lock, ask:

* Can a new viewer identify the main action in each scene?
* Does each scene lead naturally to the next?
* Is the primary type still visible in the construction?
* Are Mother, Father, Elder Brother, Younger Daughter, and Family Dog used only where canon and purpose support them?
* Are all locations established and geographically plausible?
* Does the season remain continuous?
* Are Google Flow artifacts or modern details absent?
* Does sound clarify place and process?
* Does the episode preserve calm without becoming repetitive?

## Final Rule

Scenes should make ordinary life easy to follow and worthwhile to watch.

If a scene cannot show what changed, it has not yet found its purpose.

# Pacing Timeline

## Authority

This manual defines the timing architecture, beat density, hold discipline, and editorial rhythm for Village Stories episodes. It governs how an approved story is distributed through time for YouTube Shorts and long-form releases.

`06_Story_Engine/` determines what a story means and what kinds of conflict or emotion are allowed. This document does not replace that doctrine. It translates an approved story into watchable screen time without confusing retention tactics for story truth.

---

## Guiding Principle

**Move promptly between meaningful beats; remain present inside meaningful moments.**

Village Stories must never feel delayed. From `ep_004` Shorts onward, **Growth
Sprint fast retention is the default** (`16_Growth_Sprint_Mode.md` § Fast
Retention Default): hook immediately, short cuts, Locked Camera preferred, no
leisurely establishing montages.

Long-form may still hold process texture when the board says so — but never
confuse “calm authenticity” with slow empty time. Good pacing is progress +
readable action, not continuous acceleration into noise.

The audience should always sense that the episode is moving somewhere. A held
shot earns its duration only when it reveals process, emotion, place, sound, or
a change the viewer needs — then cut.

---

## 1. Purpose

Pacing serves six production outcomes:

1. keep the episode intelligible from first frame to final image;
2. give each scene a clear reason to exist;
3. protect calm from becoming stagnation;
4. protect movement from becoming noise;
5. allow emotional turns and sensory actions to land; and
6. deliver the duration promised by the chosen format.

Retention data may show where the viewer disengages, but it cannot by itself say why. Treat every dip as a diagnostic question about clarity, relevance, repetition, audio, packaging, or timing—not as proof that the episode needs greater drama.

---

## 2. The Pacing Philosophy

The channel's rhythm is built from contrast:

- a direct opening after a quiet establishing sound;
- a quick decision followed by a patient making process;
- a practical obstacle followed by an unhurried observation;
- a small emotional reaction followed by visible resolution;
- a busy courtyard followed by an empty, peaceful final image.

Pacing fails in two opposite ways:

### Under-paced

The episode repeats information, postpones an action the viewer already expects, lingers after the feeling has been understood, or uses ambience unrelated to the episode's purpose.

### Over-paced

The episode cuts before actions can be read, uses music to force emotion, removes natural sound, compresses cause and effect, or treats every small condition as an emergency.

The correct rhythm is legible. Viewers should understand what changed, why a character responds, what effort occurs, and how the result feels.

---

## 3. Core Units of Time

Use four nested units when planning timing.

### 3.1 Moment

A moment is one readable action or reaction: a lid lifting, a child noticing rain, a dog waiting, a hand tightening a bolt.

### 3.2 Beat

A beat is a small change in situation, understanding, intention, or feeling. It must move the episode forward.

### 3.3 Scene

A scene contains a local objective, progression, and exit condition. A scene should not end merely because its allotted seconds have passed.

### 3.4 Sequence

A sequence groups scenes around one episode phase: opening need, preparation, attempt, turn, resolution, or release.

Edit at the moment level, plan at the beat level, review at the scene level, and protect balance at the sequence level.

---

## 4. Timing Decisions: A Practical Test

For every shot and beat, ask:

1. What new information, feeling, or sensory value is this time giving?
2. Has the viewer had enough time to read it?
3. Has the viewer already understood it?
4. Does the next beat create a natural forward pull?
5. Would removing two seconds harm clarity or feeling?
6. Would adding two seconds deepen the intended experience?

Keep time only when it has a purpose. Cut time only when the purpose is complete.

---

## 5. Beat Density Standards

Beat density is the rate of meaningful change, not the number of cuts.

| Format | Target experience | Beat density |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 15–30 second Short | One action with one turn | High; nearly every shot advances the action |
| 30–60 second Short | One compact beginning-to-end arc | High between beats; selective sensory holds |
| 60–120 second Short | One focused task or feeling-led arc | High; new information every 1–3 seconds |
| 3–6 minute long form | One substantial process or family day segment | Moderate-high; renewed progression every 4–8 seconds |
| 6–12 minute long form | Layered process, ensemble, or reflective episode | Moderate with reorientation every 45–75 seconds |

Do not count cuts as beats. Five cuts of a pot boiling are usually one beat unless each cut shows a meaningful new stage.

### Retention-first cadence (2026-07-17 lock)

**Goal:** viewers feel constant progress. **Method:** compress waiting, walking, repeated setup, and already-understood actions. Keep ASMR and emotion long enough to land.

| Situation | Default |
| --- | --- |
| Recognition complete (viewer already understands the image) | Cut or advance immediately |
| Process ASMR (pour, stir, grind, wash, chew) | Hold only while the sound/action still changes |
| Walk between locations | Compress unless geography or relationship is the story |
| Establishing wide | 1–3 seconds max unless the place itself is the hook |
| Reaction / emotion | Hold until the feeling is readable, then exit |
| Calendar / logo / title | After relevance; Shorts often skip or place at end |

**Information-change test:** every 2–4 seconds of screen time must deliver at least one of:

- new action stage
- new object state
- new character intention/feeling
- new place relation
- new primary sound event

If none occur, the shot is padding. Remove it.

**Forbidden “fast” tactics:**

- brainrot micro-cut spam with no readable cause/effect outside approved
  `16_Growth_Sprint_Mode.md`
- jump cuts that hide missing story
- distress or shock packaging for retention
- cutting ASMR mid-contact before attack/body/decay complete

### Growth Sprint override (future releases)

When explicitly selected in the episode brief, Growth Sprint uses:

- 0.7–2.0 s typical shots;
- pattern interruption every 1–3 s;
- 15–35 s preferred Shorts runtime;
- strongest subject sound in first 0–0.5 s;
- stable Flow generation followed by aggressive editorial trimming.

See `16_Growth_Sprint_Mode.md`. Finished episodes are not retroactively changed.

## 6. Short-Form Timelines

### 6.1 Fifteen to thirty seconds

Use this length for one visible action, discovery, or compact emotional exchange.

| Phase | Typical share | Function |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Hook and condition | 0–3 seconds | Show the active situation immediately |
| Response | 3–8 seconds | Character begins a specific action |
| Attempt/process | 8–20 seconds | Show only the essential steps |
| Turn/result | 20–27 seconds | Reveal the earned change |
| Release | final 2–4 seconds | Let the result register |

Avoid multiple subplots, backstory, or a separate introduction.

### 6.2 Thirty to sixty seconds

Use this length for a simple task with a small complication or a relationship moment with a visible resolution.

| Phase | Typical share | Function |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Hook | 0–4 seconds | Condition and subject become clear |
| Orientation | 4–10 seconds | Who, where, and immediate need |
| Preparation | 10–25 seconds | Necessary action begins |
| Turn | 25–42 seconds | Change, obstacle, discovery, or choice |
| Resolution | 42–55 seconds | The task or feeling settles |
| Release / return seed | final 3–5 seconds | Peaceful finish, only if truthful |

### 6.3 Sixty to 120 seconds

Use this length when the audience benefits from process, a second attempt, or a fuller emotional landing.

| Phase | Typical timing | Function |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Hook | 0–5 seconds | Strong truthful entry |
| Grounding | 5–15 seconds | Context without repeating the hook |
| Preparation | 15–40 seconds | Materials, plan, or relationship approach |
| Main action | 40–75 seconds | The most satisfying work or interaction |
| Turn | 75–95 seconds | A real adjustment, discovery, or feeling shift |
| Resolution | 95–112 seconds | Completed result or emotionally clear outcome |
| Release | final 5–8 seconds | Atmosphere and optional honest future thread |

These are ranges, not quotas. A simple episode may resolve at 50 seconds; do not inflate it to reach two minutes.

## 7. Long-Form Timelines

Long form earns duration through depth, not through elongated Short structure.

### 7.1 Three to six minutes

Recommended for one process with enough physical stages to reward observation.

| Sequence | Typical timing | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Direct opening | 0:00–0:12 | Active condition and episode promise |
| Orientation and plan | 0:12–0:40 | Place, participants, and objective — no calendar-first delay |
| First movement | 0:40–1:40 | Preparation or initial attempt |
| Development | 1:40–3:30 | Process, interaction, and real adjustment |
| Result | 3:30–4:45 | Outcome becomes visible and shared |
| Release | final 15–30 seconds | Quiet reflection, cleanup, or place return — do not pad |

### 7.2 Six to twelve minutes

Recommended only when the episode has multiple genuine stages: a seasonal task, group preparation, an intergenerational memory, or an educational process with a complete practical arc.

Use reorientation approximately every 60–90 seconds. Reorientation can be a new task stage, location move, character perspective, result check, or sensory reset. It is not a demand for a twist.

| Sequence | Typical timing | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Opening and promise | 0:00–0:30 | Start with the day’s active condition |
| Context | 0:30–1:20 | Explain only what action cannot show |
| Phase one | 1:20–3:00 | Begin the process or journey |
| Phase two | 3:00–5:30 | Develop with a new practical demand |
| Midpoint check | 5:30–6:15 | Confirm progress and remaining work |
| Phase three | 6:15–8:45 | Complete or adapt the effort |
| Shared result | 8:45–10:30 | Let people use, see, or feel the outcome |
| Release | final 30–90 seconds | Close in the world of the episode |

If the structure does not contain enough real stages, make a shorter film. Length without progression erodes trust.

## 8. The Scene Rhythm

Each scene should have a distinct internal rhythm:

1. **Entry:** viewers understand where and when they are;
2. **Objective:** someone wants, needs, or begins something;
3. **Action:** behavior or process develops;
4. **Shift:** new information, result, feeling, or adjustment occurs;
5. **Exit:** the next action becomes natural.

A scene does not require spoken exposition. A character taking tools from a shelf, checking a loose chair leg, and moving the chair outside may complete all five functions visually.

Avoid entering too early. Start when the action becomes meaningful, not when a character is walking toward it from an unrelated room.

Avoid leaving too late. Once the scene has created its exit condition, transition before the same point is restated in dialogue or another reaction shot.

---

## 9. Holds and Sensory Time

Sensory time is a storytelling asset when it is connected to the episode.

Appropriate holds include:

- steam rising as a dish reaches its finished stage;
- hands sorting seeds while a character remembers why they matter;
- rain falling while the family waits for it to ease;
- a repaired object working for the first time;
- the Family Dog settling near a returning family member;
- a wide shot that confirms the work changed the space.

Inappropriate holds include:

- a repeated sky shot that provides no temporal or emotional change;
- ambient footage inserted because the scene lacks a next beat;
- a close-up held so long that the action’s outcome is already clear;
- music-only pauses that obscure a missing story decision.

For review, mute the music once. If the hold has no visual or natural-sound purpose without the score, shorten or replace it.

---

## 10. Dialogue and Silence

Dialogue must create understanding, relationship, or decision. Do not use it to narrate what the image already states.

### Use dialogue when

- a character's choice cannot be read visually;
- a family memory changes the meaning of an object;
- instruction needs one concise practical clarification;
- the relationship requires a verbal repair or acknowledgment.

### Use silence when

- an action has clear natural sound;
- a character needs time to notice or feel;
- the location is communicating season, weather, or time;
- the final result should be experienced rather than explained.

Never add dialogue merely because a quiet shot feels risky. A quiet shot becomes safe when it is specific and connected to the episode's movement.

---

## 11. Music and Sound Rhythm

Music supports tempo; it must not manufacture stakes.

Use music to:

- ease an edit between task stages;
- gently carry a making montage whose actions remain legible;
- support a warm outcome after it has been earned;
- provide contrast beneath a moment of reflection.

Do not use music to:

- tell viewers a small problem is dangerous;
- hide confusing geography or missing coverage;
- force a laugh, tear, or reveal;
- fill every gap in natural sound.

Plan intentional sound transitions: rain to indoor roof sound, bicycle bell to gate movement, grinding tool to stillness after repair. These transitions can renew attention without cutting faster.

---

## 12. Continuity and Pace

Continuity errors damage pacing because they force viewers to reorient.

Maintain:

- time of day, season, and weather progression;
- the amount of food cooked, wood cut, soil turned, or repair completed;
- object positions and handoffs;
- character clothing, wetness, dirt, and fatigue;
- travel time between locations;
- knowledge and emotion gained in previous scenes;
- the rhythm of work: tasks should take believable time.

Do not cut from an empty basket to a full basket without showing or clearly compressing the gathering. A montage is valid when it preserves the logic of work; it is not valid when it removes the cause of the payoff.

---

## 13. Season, Character, and Location Rhythm

### Season

Season determines plausible timing. Monsoon preparation may need quicker response; a summer afternoon task may slow under heat; harvest work can provide repeated stages. The seasonal condition must shape the beat plan, not merely the color palette.

### Character

Character pace is behavioral. Father may examine and repair patiently; a child may move with eager bursts; Mother may organize a task through practiced economy. Do not give every character the same timing language.

### Location

Location changes transition cost and sound rhythm. A kitchen allows close process beats. A path to school supports movement and encounter. A tea stall supports social turns. Do not cut between distant locations merely to add variety; show the journey, use a clear time transition, or keep the episode localized.

## 14. Google Flow Handoff

Pacing must be designed before generation. Google Flow cannot reliably infer editorial rhythm from a vague request for a “slow cinematic village scene.”

The shot packet for Flow includes:

- shot ID and sequence/scene/beat number;
- exact action start and finish;
- duration range and intended hold reason;
- framing, camera movement, and screen direction;
- character model, wardrobe, expression, and energy level;
- location, weather, season, time of day, and required props;
- previous-shot continuity state;
- next-shot exit state;
- sound priority: dialogue, natural sound, music, or silence;
- banned additions and continuity risks.

Example:

> Shot 23, 3–4 seconds. Close view of Father’s hands tightening the final bolt on the wooden stool. His sleeves remain rolled and dusty from Shot 22. Hold long enough to hear the last turn of the wrench and the wood settle. No dramatic camera push, no extra tools, no music swell. End with Father placing the stool upright, facing screen right.

Flow review must confirm that a generated clip supports its planned beat. Do not retain a visually attractive clip if it delays the action, changes the task state, or creates a false emotional intensity.

## 15. Editorial Workflow

### 15.1 Before boards

Create a beat map with sequence purpose, scene objective, estimated duration, transition type, and intended hold points.

### 15.2 In storyboard

Mark:

- start and end state for each shot;
- process stages that cannot be omitted;
- reaction shots necessary for emotional comprehension;
- optional atmosphere shots;
- hard cuts, sound bridges, and time compressions.

### 15.3 In assembly

Build a clarity cut before a beauty cut. Confirm the episode works with direct, chronological coverage; then add atmosphere only where it strengthens the existing structure.

### 15.4 In fine edit

Review each transition for one of three functions: progression, contrast, or release. If it performs none, remove it.

### 15.5 In final review

Watch once at normal speed for feeling, once with sound low for visual logic, and once with the opening/title context for retention alignment.

## 16. Positive and Negative Examples

### Example: repairing a stool

**Positive pacing:** Father finds the wobble in the opening, gathers a tool, checks the loose joint, repairs it through two or three legible steps, tests it, and the family uses the stool at dinner. The edit holds on the tightening and final stability, but skips repetitive tool retrieval.

**Negative pacing:** The episode opens on a polished final stool, spends 30 seconds on unrelated village scenery, shows every screw in real time, and ends immediately after the repair without letting the result be used.

### Example: rain and laundry

**Positive pacing:** Rain begins, the children notice, the family gathers clothes, one cloth slips into mud, it is washed and rehung indoors, and the final room feels warm and dry.

**Negative pacing:** Thunder sound repeats, multiple running shots create false panic, then the edit spends equal time on every garment with no change in task state.

### Example: a remembered recipe

**Positive pacing:** Mother finds the note, identifies one missing ingredient, the family gathers it, the cooking process highlights only distinct stages, and a shared taste triggers the memory's quiet resolution.

**Negative pacing:** A long narration explains the recipe before any cooking begins, while the visual process is reduced to a fast montage that makes the final dish feel unearned.

## 17. Edge Cases

### No obstacle

An episode does not need a problem. Pace it through stages of intention, preparation, making, sharing, and release. The forward movement is the process itself.

### Repetitive work

Use a representative first action, a concise transition that proves continued work, and a later action that shows progress or new texture. Do not show every repetition.

### Emotional episode

Allow one extra beat after a meaningful line or recognition. Do not add slow motion, repeated close-ups, or music manipulation to insist that the viewer feel it.

### Educational material

Let each instruction coincide with a visible stage. If an explanation cannot be seen, keep it concise and return to action quickly.

### Multiple locations

Give each move a reason. Use a sound bridge, travel detail, or clear visual reset so the viewer knows time and geography have changed.

---

## 18. Quality Standards

Approve an episode timeline only when:

- every sequence has a defined function;
- every scene changes action, understanding, or feeling;
- the opening arrives before general atmosphere;
- process holds are earned by sensory or emotional value;
- repeated information has been removed;
- dialogue does not duplicate images;
- music and silence are intentional;
- task progress is believable and continuous;
- the final release has enough time to register;
- Short and long-form durations match the actual story scope.

### Retention review prompts

- Is the first drop caused by unclear subject, slow entry, or packaging mismatch?
- Is a middle dip caused by repetition, not insufficient drama?
- Is a late exit natural because the story resolved, or did the ending lose its emotional release?
- Did a high-retention moment accurately represent the channel's values?

No timing change is approved solely because it follows a trend. It must solve an identified viewing problem without breaking the episode's truth.

## Final Production Reminder

The correct pace lets a viewer feel that time was spent well. Arrive at the point early, show the work honestly, hold what matters, and leave only after the result has had room to breathe.

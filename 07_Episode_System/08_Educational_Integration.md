# Educational Integration

## Purpose and Authority

This manual turns the educational principles in
`06_Story_Engine/11_Educational_Engine.md` into shot-ready episode practice.
It governs how writers, storyboard artists, Google Flow operators, editors,
and reviewers place learning inside a Short or long-form episode.

The series teaches through observation, participation, gentle correction, and
visible consequence. It does not teach by pausing the story for a lesson,
quiz, slogan, narrator explanation, or adult speech to the audience.

Early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu canon is binding. Character, Location, Object,
School, World, and seasonal documents determine which skills, materials,
places, and responsibilities are available. When an educational opportunity
conflicts with canon, safety, character age, or the episode's emotional truth,
discard it.

## Guiding Principle

**Start with a real family need; let understanding appear through the response.**

## Philosophy

Learning is not a separate segment added to make an episode useful. It is the
change that occurs when someone cares enough to look again, try carefully,
accept support, and adjust a practical action.

The viewer should feel the meaning before naming it. A covered school item
staying dry can communicate preparation. A child waiting for the right step in
a kitchen task can communicate patience. A sibling returning a tool to its
place after a repair can communicate respect for work. No character needs to
announce the value.

The purpose is not to prove that adults are always right or children are
always wrong. Parents guide without humiliation; children retain agency; Elder
Brother can learn as well as help; and a result may be partial when weather,
time, or skill honestly limits it.

## Episode Workflow

### 1. Find the existing story need

Begin with a task that already belongs in the household, season, relationship,
or location. Examples include preparing food, protecting schoolwork from rain,
checking a familiar animal-care routine, sharing water carefully, returning a
repaired object, or resolving a small sibling disagreement.

Do not begin with “teach measurement” or “teach responsibility.” Convert the
topic into a reason a known character wants to complete something well.

### 2. Select one learnable action

Choose one observable, age-appropriate action within the task: using a
familiar measure, covering an item, waiting for an adult-safe step, noticing
where water goes, sorting what is needed, or taking turns.

One episode normally carries one primary learning action. A long-form episode
may include a supporting learning beat only when it grows from the same main
task. Do not make a checklist of lessons.

### 3. Build the learning loop

Use this sequence:

1. **Need:** the character has a practical reason to act.
2. **Attempt:** the character acts with present understanding.
3. **Notice:** an object, result, weather condition, or trusted person's
   response makes a detail visible.
4. **Adjustment:** the character changes one action.
5. **Result:** the task improves, or a truthful next step becomes clear.

The notice can be visual or sonic. A wet paper edge, uneven measure, waiting
animal, loose knot, changed rain sound, or sibling pausing can do more than a
long explanation.

### 4. Give support without takeover

The guide demonstrates, points, asks a concrete question, protects safety, or
offers a smaller role. The learner must still perform the meaningful
adjustment. Correct the action, never the child's worth.

### 5. Close on proof, not a moral

End the beat with the visible improved result, a calmer relationship, or
honest partial progress. Use `07_Endings.md` to resolve the feeling. Never
overlay “the lesson is...” dialogue or on-screen moral text.

## Decision Rules

Add an educational beat only if all answers are yes:

* Does the task belong naturally to this episode and established location?
* Does the character have a personal reason to care?
* Is the skill small enough to see and understand?
* Is the responsibility safe and suited to the character?
* Can the audience infer the learning from action and result?
* Does the correction preserve dignity?
* Does the beat respect period, season, object, and relationship canon?

Remove or redesign the beat if it needs a lecture, a contrived error, modern
materials, a dangerous task, a forced question-and-answer exchange, or a
result that appears without a changed action.

## Practical Domains

### Kitchen and household care

Use documented household work to reveal attention, sharing, clean handling,
sequence, and safe participation. Mother may demonstrate a familiar measure
or invite a child to complete a safe final step. Children must not handle fire,
sharp tools, or heavy work beyond documented capability just to create a
lesson.

*Good:* Younger Daughter slows down, follows Mother's visible measure, and
later repeats the careful action.

*Not good:* Mother stops cooking to explain every fact about ingredients while
everyone waits.

### Garden, field, and seasonal work

Use only documented activities to show observation, patience, water care,
timing, or cooperation. Season changes the valid action: rain may postpone
work; summer may make water use more consequential; harvest rhythm may require
shared pacing.

*Good:* A child notices water running where it should not and adjusts after
Father's short practical cue.

*Not good:* A child performs adult agricultural labour alone to demonstrate
discipline.

### Animal care

Show routine, gentleness, and observation through familiar animals and
established care spaces. An adult protects the animal and the child; the
animal is never distressed to create instruction.

*Good:* The Family Dog's routine waiting helps Younger Daughter remember a
safe, adult-approved care step.

*Not good:* An animal becomes ill, frightened, or endangered so the family can
deliver a responsibility lesson.

### School and study

School learning should centre preparation, listening, persistence, asking for
help, and confidence after effort. The Primary School and route must remain
period-appropriate and canon-supported.

*Good:* Elder Brother shows how he has protected his own work from rain, and
Younger Daughter makes the adjustment herself before leaving.

*Not good:* A modern classroom, exam-prep montage, invented worksheet text, or
teacher lecture inserted only to teach viewers.

### Repair, craft, and material respect

Use a documented workshop or household repair only when a real need brings the
family there. Learning can occur through watching skilled work, using objects
carefully, returning a tool, or understanding why an item is repaired instead
of discarded.

*Good:* After a necessary repair visit, Elder Brother returns his small item
to its proper place.

*Not good:* A craftsperson gives an unrelated history lecture with no family
need or follow-through.

### Family and community life

Taking turns, listening, apologising, keeping promises, welcoming help, and
sharing a limited resource are valid learning when enacted, not preached.
Conflict must remain gentle and solvable.

*Good:* Siblings resume the delayed task together after one acknowledges the
other's effort.

*Not good:* A child is mocked, punished severely, or publicly embarrassed to
make a moral memorable.

## Character Application

Mother teaches through demonstration, concise practical instruction, and trust
in a safe small responsibility. She is not the series' lecturer.

Father teaches through careful pacing, repair awareness, outdoor judgement,
and questions that help a child notice what is already present. He does not
enter solely to pronounce wisdom.

Elder Brother can show growing competence, impatience corrected with dignity,
and sibling support. He must not become a scolding parent or adult worker.

Younger Daughter can initiate a learning beat through curiosity and meaningful
participation. Her questions must lead to action, not an adult monologue.

The Family Dog may reveal routine, waiting, gentle care, or a change in family
attention. The dog neither understands abstract lessons nor causes contrived
problems.

## Dialogue, Visual, and Sound Rules

Keep dialogue tied to what is visible.

Good: “Keep it covered until we leave.”

Good: “Try the smaller measure first.”

Good: “Watch where the water goes.”

Avoid abstract speeches about success, discipline, duty, or values. Avoid
questions that force a child to recite information for the audience.

Show hands, tools, surfaces, faces noticing, and the changed result. Reserve a
short ASMR detail for meaningful material action—a measured pour, cloth being
folded around an item, rope tightened, or water settling—only when it does not
mask a necessary instruction or reaction. See `09_ASMR_Placement.md`.

## Google Flow Workflow

Break the loop into controllable shots: need, attempt, notice, adjustment,
result. For each shot, lock the exact established location, period-appropriate
object, action start and finish, character positions, safety distance,
weather, time, and emotional restraint.

Example prompt logic:

`Established early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu family Kitchen: Mother calmly points
to the familiar measuring vessel while Younger Daughter levels a small
ingredient herself; Elder Brother watches supportively from a respectful safe
distance; documented traditional tools, clothing, warm morning light, focused
hands and quiet concentration, no posed teaching tableau.`

Reject generated imagery that substitutes modern educational materials,
changes tools or clothing, moves a child into an unsafe relationship with
fire/water/animals, makes an adult gesture like a classroom lecturer, or
creates an exaggerated failure expression. Generated visuals must show the
actual adjustment; do not use a generic smiling result shot as proof.

Before edit, preserve hand position, object state, measure amount, wet/dry
condition, character placement, and time/weather across the loop. Build sound
after the visual action is locked; a sound plan cannot repair a visual
continuity error.

## Shorts and Long-Form Rules

### Shorts

Use one need, one learnable action, and one visible result. Place the learning
inside the main activity, not after it. The final seconds must resolve today's
feeling even if a practical next step invites another Short.

Example connected sequence: Short one shows siblings repairing a
misunderstanding and protecting a school item; Short two follows their
truthful school-route continuation. Short one never cuts before the
reconciliation merely to force the second view.

### Long form

Long form can give the learning loop breathing room: observation, a first
attempt, quiet correction, resumed action, and an earned result. It may carry
one light supporting thread, but it must not convert everyday process into a
lecture or repeat the same instruction.

Use shifts in location, season, or family role only when the task naturally
requires them. Resolve the primary learning movement and use `07_Endings.md`
for the final emotional proof.

## Continuity and Quality Gate

Approve only when:

* the task, setting, object, and process are canon-supported;
* character ability and adult supervision fit established roles;
* the learning loop has a visible cause and result;
* error and correction are manageable, gentle, and non-shaming;
* dialogue serves the action rather than explaining a moral;
* season, weather, light, task progress, clothing, and object state match
  across shots;
* the sound source matches the visible action and preserves dialogue;
* the ending honours the learning without announcing it;
* a returning viewer can recognise any continuing task without a false
  cliffhanger.

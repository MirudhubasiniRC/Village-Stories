# Episode Checklist

## Purpose

This is the operating checklist for turning an approved Village Stories premise
into a release-ready episode. It makes episode-level decisions visible before
they become expensive production, continuity, or YouTube problems.

Use it at four points: premise selection, episode-plan lock, rough-cut review,
and release handoff. It is a decision tool, not a substitute for detailed
character, location, object, culture, Story Engine, or Production manuals.

## Guiding Principle

An episode is ready only when its story promise, visible world, sound, and
viewer-facing presentation all tell the same truth.

## Authority and Use

Episode System organises how an approved story is experienced by a viewer:
hook, scene sequence, pacing, opening, ending, sound placement, and continuity
between episodes. Story doctrine belongs to `06_Story_Engine/`; asset creation,
Google Flow operation, editing, publishing, and archive controls belong to
`08_Production/`.

Use this precedence when a check exposes a conflict:

```text
Specific character, location, object, world, culture, research, and timeline canon
↓
06_Story_Engine/ doctrine and story boundaries
↓
Approved episode brief and documented continuity facts
↓
07_Episode_System/ episode organisation
↓
08_Production/ execution and release procedure
```

Never mark an item passed because a visual is attractive, production is late,
or a platform tactic appears promising. A specific factual source outranks a
convenient episode decision. If the needed fact does not exist, stop and seek
canon clarification rather than inventing it.

## Status Definitions

Mark every applicable item:

* **Pass** — verified against the final intended evidence.
* **Revise** — incomplete, unclear, weak, or dependent on a correction.
* **Block** — contradicts canon, breaks the series promise, risks dignity,
  safety, rights, accessibility, or makes the public promise false.
* **Not applicable** — deliberately not used, with a reason recorded.

A Block stops the affected stage. A Revise item cannot be assumed fixed by a
later team. Name an owner, required correction, and recheck point. Do not
convert a Block to Not applicable to meet a schedule.

## Required Episode Record

Create or update an episode record before the first gate. It must identify:

* episode ID, working title, format, intended runtime, version, and owner;
* one-sentence premise and emotional centre;
* main thread and any approved supporting thread;
* selected characters, locations, season, time of day, and canon sources;
* practical need, gentle obstacle or discovery, response, and final image;
* hook, opening promise, and optional continuation seed;
* continuity start and end state;
* visual, ASMR, music, silence, caption, and accessibility plan;
* Google Flow shot references and selected-asset status when production begins;
* thumbnail, title, metadata, playlist, and related-episode plan; and
* approvals, defects, waivers, and handoff recipients.

The record is the handoff object. A verbal description, an unlabeled draft, or
an assumed “latest version” is not enough.

## Gate 1: Premise and Canon Readiness

Complete before writing scenes, commissioning research, or generating assets.

- [ ] The premise can be stated in one calm sentence with one central practical
      thread.
- [ ] The need comes from an ordinary condition, task, relationship, or
      seasonal reality already supported by canon.
- [ ] The selected characters have a documented reason and ability to be
      involved.
- [ ] The selected location supports the task, access, route, atmosphere, and
      number of people shown.
- [ ] Season, weather, time of day, clothing, tools, animals, food, and travel
      are plausible together.
- [ ] The obstacle is practical, proportionate, and solvable without danger,
      villainy, humiliation, coincidence, or a miracle.
- [ ] The emotional centre is visible through action, not dependent on a speech
      or music cue.
- [ ] The ending can provide rest even if the practical result is modest.
- [ ] Any educational or cultural detail is visible, accurate, and not framed
      as a universal claim about all villages or Tamil households.
- [ ] No new character, object, profession, location, modern convenience, or
      cultural practice has been assumed to make the premise work.

**Decision:** If the episode needs an unsupported fact, return to research or
canon clarification. Do not send an unresolved premise to script or Google
Flow planning.

**Handoff:** approved premise, applicable source documents, open questions
resolved, format rationale, and initial continuity state.

## Gate 2: Episode Structure and Viewer Promise

Complete before script and storyboard lock.

- [ ] The first seconds contain a truthful sensory, visual, or emotional point
      of interest rather than a generic introduction.
- [ ] The hook, title concept, thumbnail concept, and eventual opening promise
      describe the same actual episode.
- [ ] Viewers can quickly understand who is present, where they are, what
      needs attention, and why it matters today.
- [ ] The episode progresses from need to preparation, action, gentle turn,
      response, resolution, and emotional rest.
- [ ] Every scene advances task, place, character, feeling, or payoff; repeated
      information is removed or compressed.
- [ ] Preparation shows practical competence rather than delaying the plot.
- [ ] The main activity is shown in meaningful stages rather than narrated as a
      summary.
- [ ] The response follows from established resources, knowledge, and
      cooperation.
- [ ] Dialogue adds what image, sound, and action cannot; it does not explain
      every feeling or repeat the visible.
- [ ] The ending answers today's emotional question before any future thread is
      planted.
- [ ] A continuation, Part label, tomorrow reference, playlist link, or
      end-screen connection is used only for a real documented relationship.

For long form, also verify that added duration reveals process, place, or
emotional breathing room—not repeated action or unrelated subplots. For Shorts,
verify that the single activity and ending remain understandable to a new
viewer.

**Handoff:** locked beat sheet, scene purpose list, hook wording, final-image
intent, continuation status, and thumbnail-worthy truthful moment.

## Gate 3: Character, Location, Season, and Continuity Plan

Complete before visual generation or filming.

- [ ] Each character's goal, skill, voice, body language, patience, humour,
      and relationship behaviour match their individual documentation.
- [ ] No character is made harsh, helpless, unusually wise, comic, or
      emotionally extreme merely to create energy.
- [ ] Children are protected from public shame, danger, adult burden, and
      implausible competence.
- [ ] The Family Dog remains a gentle household companion and is never used as
      a threatening, rescuing, military, or attack-dog device.
- [ ] Every character has a practical scene reason; no one is present only to
      decorate a frame.
- [ ] Documented location geometry, entry points, paths, work zones, materials,
      background activity, and distance are preserved.
- [ ] Travel has an established reason, route, elapsed time, and arrival image.
- [ ] Season affects task, light, sound, surfaces, clothes, animals, and pace;
      it is not interchangeable scenery.
- [ ] Time of day, weather, wetness, mud, smoke, fire, food, plant, and
      household states progress credibly.
- [ ] Each story prop has an owner, start position, orientation, condition,
      amount or fill level, and end state.
- [ ] Each scene records incoming and outgoing costume, hair, carried item,
      screen direction, emotional state, and task status.
- [ ] Any stated link to a prior or future episode is consistent with release
      order, season, chronology, object state, and relationship progress.

**Decision:** A continuity uncertainty must be answered before the next scene
is planned. Do not ask the editor or AI operator to choose an unrecorded
character, prop, weather, or route state.

**Handoff:** storyboard continuity ledger, character/location/prop references,
season and time map, and documented cross-episode facts.

## Gate 4: Visual and Google Flow Readiness

Complete for every planned generated shot before submission to Google Flow.

- [ ] The shot has an approved ID, storyboard panel, source scene, and revision.
- [ ] The start state matches the current continuity ledger.
- [ ] The intended action is one observable, physically clear change.
- [ ] The end state gives the editor a clean factual handoff.
- [ ] Character, location, prop, wardrobe, and keyframe references are approved
      and have declared roles.
- [ ] Camera size, height, side, movement, screen direction, and focal subject
      follow the storyboard.
- [ ] Format is selected for delivery: central, mobile-readable action and
      caption-safe space for Shorts; readable subject and useful geography for
      long form.
- [ ] The prompt protects identity, current clothing, prop state, architecture,
      lighting, early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu setting, and approved art style.
- [ ] The prompt excludes likely drift: extra limbs, faces, people, animals,
      props, text, logos, modern objects, costume changes, dramatic weather,
      and excessive camera movement.
- [ ] Motion is small enough for reliable execution; multi-person, multi-stage,
      or spectacle-heavy action is divided into smaller shots.
- [ ] Generated audio, if any, is treated as a candidate and cannot establish
      dialogue, canon, plot, or cultural fact without separate approval.

After generation:

- [ ] Anatomy, objects, faces, backgrounds, and motion remain stable.
- [ ] The clip matches its start frame and supports the next shot.
- [ ] No visually impressive drift has been accepted in place of factual
      accuracy.
- [ ] The selected candidate, prompt version, references, settings, reason for
      selection, and revision history are recorded.

**Handoff:** selected and reviewed clip IDs, updated continuity ledger, defect
log, and asset status for the editor.

## Gate 5: Sound, Silence, Education, and Accessibility

Complete at sound plan and sound-lock review.

- [ ] Every foreground sound has a believable visible or established source.
- [ ] Ambience matches location, time, weather, distance, and period.
- [ ] ASMR is placed around a meaningful action, not layered constantly or used
      to perform rural life as spectacle.
- [ ] Music supports an earned turn and never commands an emotion, masks story
      information, or makes a small problem feel dangerous.
- [ ] Silence or low ambience contains a purposeful image, action, reflection,
      or rest.
- [ ] Dialogue is intelligible, belongs to the shown speaker and moment, and
      remains consistent with character voice and relationship.
- [ ] Educational learning is demonstrated through process, care, choice, or
      consequence rather than a lecture, quiz, or forced moral.
- [ ] Captions accurately represent essential speech, names, cultural terms,
      and meaningful non-speech sound.
- [ ] Captions are readable, timed to the final edit, and do not obscure
      essential visual action.
- [ ] No unapproved modern sound, brand, urban traffic, voice, music, or
      electronic cue contaminates the setting.

**Handoff:** sound-lock version, caption file and language status, rights and
credit notes, and any required accessibility correction.

## Gate 6: Episode Experience Review

Complete on the rough cut and again before final approval.

Watch once without stopping as a viewer, then review with the brief, canon
sources, continuity ledger, and sound plan.

- [ ] The opening makes a true promise immediately.
- [ ] The viewer can follow place, task, cause, response, and result without
      explanation outside the episode.
- [ ] The emotional centre remains clear and proportionate.
- [ ] The cut gives important work, choice, sound, and recognition enough time,
      but does not repeat information.
- [ ] Character conduct feels familiar and dignified.
- [ ] Location and season feel lived in and consequential rather than generic.
- [ ] The edit preserves screen direction, routes, object states, costumes,
      weather, time, and emotional progression.
- [ ] The ending is a concrete, emotionally satisfying rest—not a slogan,
      sudden cut, unexplained sadness, false danger, or baited cliffhanger.
- [ ] The episode remains truthful with audio muted and visually understandable
      with captions enabled.
- [ ] The experience is calm without becoming empty, and engaging without
      becoming melodramatic or tool-driven.

Record each finding with timestamp, severity, evidence, owner, correction, and
recheck state. Check adjacent shots and downstream package elements after any
revision.

## Gate 7: YouTube and Release Handoff

Complete only after picture and sound are locked.

- [ ] The thumbnail uses a genuine episode moment, is period-safe, and remains
      readable at mobile size.
- [ ] The title names a visible action, person, object, place, season, or
      feeling without inflation, spoilers, or false urgency.
- [ ] The description, chapters, tags where useful, playlist, cards, end
      screen, and pinned comment describe or connect only real content.
- [ ] Thumbnail, title, description, and first moments make one matching
      promise.
- [ ] Captions, language fields, credits, rights, audience setting, and links
      are complete and verified.
- [ ] Long-form chapters improve navigation without revealing the ending or
      fragmenting the calm flow.
- [ ] Related videos and playlists are genuinely relevant; release order does
      not imply an unsupported chronology.
- [ ] The exact final master, thumbnail, metadata, captions, approval state,
      and release package version are identified.
- [ ] Final platform checks are assigned to the publishing workflow; this
      checklist does not authorize bypassing Quality Control.

**Handoff:** complete release package to `08_Production/14_Quality_Control.md`,
then `08_Production/15_Publishing_Workflow.md`. Return any missing item to its
owner rather than fixing it silently in publishing.

## Fast Decision Tests

### The Promise Test

Ask a reviewer who did not write the episode: “What do you expect from the
thumbnail, title, and first thirty seconds?” The answer must match the final
cut. If it promises danger, a different outcome, a modern setting, or an
unshown lesson, revise the package or opening.

### The Specificity Test

Ask: “Could this exact event happen only to this family, in this documented
place, season, and time?” If any ordinary generic village could substitute,
add supported practical specificity or reconsider the premise.

### The Duration Test

Ask: “What would the viewer lose if this scene were removed?” If the answer is
nothing new, cut or combine it. If a Short becomes unclear after removal, the
scene may be essential; if long form feels thin, add truthful process rather
than another conflict.

### The Continuation Test

Ask: “Has today's task and feeling resolved?” If not, do not add a next-episode
seed. If yes, a seed may show the next honest need without withholding the
current reward.

## Common Failures and Corrections

| Failure | Why it fails | Required correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Beautiful opening with no task | Delays the real episode | Bring the truthful need or action forward. |
| Modern or generic visual detail | Breaks period and place trust | Replace or regenerate from approved references. |
| Every family member solves the issue | Flattens character truth | Assign roles by documented skill and relationship. |
| Repeated task footage | Adds runtime without progress | Retain one revealing stage and compress the rest. |
| Music makes routine feel dangerous | Inflates gentle stakes | Restore proportionate ambience, silence, or softer support. |
| Generated clip changes a prop or person | Breaks continuity | Reject or regenerate; update only through documented approval. |
| “Part 2” without a real continuation | Misleads viewers | Remove the label or develop an actual linked process. |
| Metadata promises a bigger event | Causes expectation mismatch | Rewrite from the locked final cut. |

## Edge Cases

* **An unresolved factual question:** Stop the relevant gate and request
  research or canon clarification. A production choice is not a canon ruling.
* **A post-lock visual change:** Recheck all affected continuity, sound,
  captions, thumbnail, metadata, and adjacent edit points; a replacement asset
  is a new revision.
* **A modest ending:** Pass it when the practical and emotional result are
  visible. Do not add celebration, a moral speech, or a new obstacle to make it
  seem larger.
* **A multi-part episode:** Each part needs its own complete emotional beat.
  Record season, time, object, costume, relationship, and remaining-task state
  for the next part.
* **A low-performing upload:** Use post-release evidence to examine clarity,
  pacing, packaging, and audience fit. Do not retroactively weaken canon or
  redesign the series around a single metric.

## Canon Authority

This checklist is the Episode System's operational quality gate. It can reject
or return an episode plan that does not meet the established requirements, but
it cannot amend canon, overrule Story Engine doctrine, or replace Production
release authorization. When sources conflict or evidence is missing, pause,
record the issue, and escalate it to the authority that owns the fact or
decision.

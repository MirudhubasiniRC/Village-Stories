# Endings

## Purpose and Authority

This manual governs the final story beat, final image, final sound, and any
truthful continuation invitation in every Village Stories episode. It applies
to Shorts, connected Shorts, and long-form episodes.

An ending is the audience's last proof that the family, village, and episode
have been treated with care. It must resolve the feeling created by the
episode's ordinary need, not merely stop the runtime.

The early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu canon is binding. Established Character,
World, Location, Object, season, and relationship documents decide what may
remain in the final image. If a pleasing ending image conflicts with canon,
time of day, weather, an object's state, or a character's established
behaviour, revise or remove it.

## Guiding Principle

**Complete today's feeling; leave tomorrow honestly possible.**

## Ending Philosophy

Village Stories does not promise that every task becomes perfect or every
worry disappears. It promises that the family responds with attention,
patience, and dignity. A satisfying ending may therefore show success, a
useful partial result, shared acceptance, or a clear next practical step.

The audience should leave calmer, warmer, more curious, or quietly hopeful.
They should not be left anxious because the production withheld the answer,
invented danger, or cut away before a character's reaction.

An ending earns emotion through visible change:

* a repaired object returns to careful use;
* a child changes a small action after understanding it;
* siblings resume a shared task after a sincere repair;
* a parent notices another parent's effort without a speech;
* rain eases enough for the household to continue;
* the Family Dog settles once the familiar routine is restored.

Warmth is not compulsory cheerfulness. A modest disappointment may end with a
cleaned work area, an item saved for another day, a hand on a shoulder, or the
family making a realistic plan. Never turn difficulty into a false victory.

## The Ending Workflow

Build the ending before final scripting, then confirm it after edit:

1. **Name the episode's present-tense question.** What needed care today:
   finishing a task, repairing a feeling, waiting for weather, preparing for
   school, or helping an animal?
2. **Name the emotional movement.** Identify the change from the opening
   state to the final state: uncertainty to confidence, irritation to
   cooperation, strain to relief, or hope to patient acceptance.
3. **Choose the proof.** Select one visible action, object state, or
   relationship gesture that proves the change.
4. **Choose the resting image.** Place that proof in an established location
   at a truthful time, with correct weather, props, and character positions.
5. **Plan sound and silence.** Give the image room for its natural sound,
   restrained music, or near-silence. Do not cut away at the instant emotion
   becomes clear.
6. **Test continuation separately.** If the story continues, identify a
   small remaining practical question only after today's emotional beat is
   complete.
7. **Check continuity in the locked edit.** Verify task progress, costume,
   object placement, daylight, rain, food, animal location, and relationship
   state against the prior shot and the next episode where applicable.

If the team cannot describe the final proof in one concrete sentence, the
episode has not yet earned an ending.

## Decision Rules

### Choose a resolved ending when

* the main task has been completed;
* a relationship misunderstanding has received a visible response;
* the episode's emotional question has a truthful answer;
* the episode is a standalone Short or a long-form chapter closing a thread.

Example: Father tests the repaired household item, it holds, and Elder Brother
quietly puts the tool back where it belongs. The repair and the learning are
both visible.

### Choose a partial-but-honest ending when

* weather, time, or a believable limitation prevents completion;
* the family has done the useful action available today;
* the next step is specific and gentle, rather than a threat.

Example: Rain prevents the family from finishing an outdoor task. They protect
the materials, share a look of acceptance, and Father says they will return
when the ground is right. The rain remains rain; it is not scored as disaster.

### Use a continuation invitation only when

* an established activity naturally extends into the next period;
* the current Short remains understandable to a first-time viewer;
* today's feeling is already resolved;
* the next episode can begin from the remaining task without re-explaining a
  manufactured cliffhanger.

Good: Younger Daughter sincerely apologises and Elder Brother accepts by
helping her pack. The next Short may show them taking the protected items to
school.

Wrong: Younger Daughter apologises, but the cut occurs before Elder Brother
responds with an ominous music sting.

### Never use

* unexplained sadness, danger, illness escalation, or separation as retention;
* a sudden cut before a key reaction;
* “wait for part two” in place of a completed beat;
* a reveal that contradicts known character knowledge or location facts;
* a modern visual, dramatic lighting convention, or generic village tableau
  that breaks early-1990s Tamil Nadu canon;
* narration that states a moral the action has already shown.

## Ending Forms

### Return to use

Use when the episode centres on cooking, repair, preparation, or care. Show
the object serving its intended purpose after the work.

*Good:* A covered school item remains dry when it is lifted for departure.
*Not enough:* A close-up of the item with no visible consequence or person
reacting to it.

### Shared completion

Use when cooperation is the episode's real movement. The final act should be
small and mutual: carrying together, serving together, returning a tool, or
sitting down after work.

Do not force a group celebration when one quiet acknowledgement fits better.

### Emotional recognition

Use when a character has noticed another's effort, worry, or need. Let a look,
returned object, altered pace, or short truthful line carry the change.

Good dialogue: “You waited for me.”

Avoid: “Today I learned that family is the most important thing.”

### Seasonal release

Use when the season shapes the story. Rain easing, a shaded pause after heat,
or a field task safely deferred can close an episode only when it follows the
actual seasonal action. The weather must not be decorative wallpaper.

### Quiet household rest

Food shared, a lamp-lit practical task completed, a swept courtyard, parents'
quiet recognition, or the Family Dog sleeping after routine is restored can
end an episode. Use only the established home spaces and documented household
objects. Rest must follow action, not replace it.

## Character, Location, and Season Application

Mother's endings often show care made visible: food correctly shared, an item
protected, a child trusted with a safe final step, or a quiet release after
work. Do not reduce her to an endlessly smiling final image.

Father's endings often show steadiness: checking a repair, making a practical
weather decision, returning equipment, or acknowledging effort without
dominating the moment. He does not deliver a closing sermon.

Elder Brother may complete an ending by choosing patience, accepting help, or
using growing competence responsibly. He remains a young family member, not a
replacement adult.

Younger Daughter may close a beat through genuine curiosity, a safe attempt,
or a small act of repair. Do not make her perform cuteness or receive praise
for learning a lesson she has not visibly acted on.

The Family Dog may settle, wait, accompany a familiar routine, or gently
confirm restored calm. The dog cannot solve the episode, signal danger without
cause, or receive human-like comic reactions.

Every location has an ending logic. A Kitchen ending should remember the food
or work state; a school-route ending needs a plausible departure; a field or
garden ending must respect light, weather, labour, and travel time; a public
location should not become suddenly private or empty without transition.
Consult `09_Location_Usage.md` and `10_Seasonal_Engine.md` before locking the
shot.

## Google Flow and Post-Production Rules

For each generated final shot, specify the established location, time of day,
season, character positions, exact object state, last completed action,
expression, camera distance, and ambient sound source. Generate a reaction or
resting moment, not a posed promotional tableau.

Example prompt logic:

`Early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu family courtyard at rain easing, established
Mother and Younger Daughter place the covered school item safely beneath the
thinnai; Elder Brother relaxes beside them, Family Dog settles on dry ground;
the family home, wet earth, clothing, props, and gentle relief match the
locked previous shot; quiet observational medium-wide frame.`

Reject a Flow output if it changes rain intensity, creates an undocumented
object, alters a character's wardrobe or relationship posture, makes the
family excessively expressive, or supplies a completion that the story has
not shown. Use the locked final visual to build the sound mix; generated audio
or generic music is never evidence of continuity.

Hold the final image long enough for recognition, but never pad it. Let one
natural foreground sound, a truthful ambient bed, or restrained music support
the image according to `10_Music_And_Silence.md` and `09_ASMR_Placement.md`.

## Shorts and Long-Form Execution

### Shorts

The final seconds must contain one completed emotional beat and one readable
image. A connected Short may reveal the next practical stage only after the
completion. Label serial parts clearly, but do not require viewers to have
seen an earlier part to understand today's resolution.

For a 30-60 second Short, avoid adding a new conflict in the ending. Use the
existing final action, reaction, and resting image.

### Long form

End a long-form episode by resolving the main thread and acknowledging any
supporting thread. A supporting thread may remain open only if it has changed
or acquired a clear truthful next step. Do not stack several miniature
cliffhangers.

Use more breathing room than a Short: a final action, a reaction, a location
settling beat, then the final image. More duration is not permission for
repeated sentimental looks or unrelated montage.

## Continuity and Quality Control

Before approval, confirm:

* The ending answers the episode's actual need and emotional question.
* The proof follows from prior action rather than appearing by convenience.
* Characters retain agency, dignity, age-appropriate responsibilities, and
  established relationships.
* Object condition, food, tools, weather, light, clothing, animal position,
  and geography match the preceding sequence.
* Sound, silence, and music leave the emotional information intelligible.
* The final image belongs to the location and early-1990s Tamil Nadu canon.
* Any next-episode invitation is practical, gentle, and understandable without
  withholding today's answer.
* No ending relies on a lecture, false peril, unexplained sadness, or abrupt
  retention cut.

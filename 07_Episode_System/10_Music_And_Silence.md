# Music and Silence

## Purpose

This manual defines the episode-level decision system for music, natural sound, and intentional quiet in Village Stories. It tells the director and editor **why** a scene needs score, ambience, ASMR, reduced sound, or silence, and **where** that choice belongs in the episode.

It does not replace `08_Production/10_Music_And_SFX_Workflow.md`, which governs sound sourcing, foley, mixing, rights, stems, and technical delivery. It also does not replace `06_Story_Engine/14_ASMR_Storytelling.md`, which establishes the story use of sensory detail. This document converts approved story and scene beats into an audio plan before final sound production.

---

## Philosophy

The village should be heard before the production is heard.

Sound establishes that a place is lived in: rain has a surface, a kitchen has a distance and material, a person’s work has weight, and a quiet look has room to arrive. Music is one possible layer, not the default emotional language. Silence is not an empty timeline; it is a deliberate choice to let a visible action, a natural sound, or a feeling remain undisturbed.

The audience must be allowed to recognise a feeling before music gently supports it. No cue may manufacture grief, danger, triumph, nostalgia, or urgency that the story has not earned.

---

## Scope and Authority

Use this authority order when an audio decision is unclear:

1. Canon, World, Character, Object, Research, and location documents define what can truthfully be heard.
2. Series Style Lock audio guidance defines the permanent sonic identity.
3. `06_Story_Engine/` defines story, emotion, season, and ASMR intent.
4. The approved episode brief, scene plan, and continuity ledger define current events.
5. This manual decides the episode-level placement and narrative role of music and silence.
6. `08_Production/10_Music_And_SFX_Workflow.md` and `11_Post_Production.md` execute, mix, and deliver the approved plan.

A beautiful cue, impressive generated sound, or technically clean mix cannot override factual continuity or emotional truth.

---

## Guiding Principle

Let the audience hear what matters, then add only what helps them feel its meaning.

---

## Audio Roles in an Episode

Every scene should identify its primary audio mode before animation or final editing:

| Mode | Use when | Typical result | Do not use it to |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Natural sound and ambience | Place, process, weather, or observation is the point | Present, specific, unforced | Fill every gap with generic birds or wind |
| Featured ASMR | One tactile action deserves focused attention | A short, intimate sensory pause | Turn routine work into glossy spectacle |
| Soft music | A real emotional connection, transition, memory, or earned rest needs gentle support | A modest layer that can enter and leave quietly | Explain the scene’s emotion or repair weak storytelling |
| Reduced sound | Narration, a look, or a delicate action needs clearer space | Attention narrows without becoming artificial | Erase the truthful acoustic world |
| Intentional silence | The audience needs to absorb a change, absence, apology, understanding, or final image | Emotional breathing room | Create false suspense or a dramatic “shock” |

“Silence” may retain a very low, truthful room presence. It does not mean inserting an unrelated drone, pad, or complete digital vacuum.

## Episode Audio Workflow

### 1. Read the Episode’s Audio Need

Before assigning cues, state:

* the episode’s practical activity;
* the primary location, season, weather, and time of day;
* the emotional movement from opening to ending;
* one or two actions worth hearing closely;
* the moment that most needs space rather than explanation.

If these cannot be stated, return to the scene or episode plan. Music cannot create a missing emotional turn.

### 2. Build an Audio Beat Map

For each scene, mark one dominant purpose:

```text
Scene / picture beat / primary sound mode / music decision / protected quiet / continuity handoff
```

Example:

```text
Courtyard rain begins / rain on tile and leaves / no music / Mother’s glance gets two quiet seconds / rain carries into kitchen
```

This map is the handoff to storyboard, Google Flow, sound, and post-production. It is not a substitute for their detailed workflows.

### 3. Plan Sound Before Motion Is Generated

For each Google Flow shot, identify the visible sound source and whether it must remain audible. Ask the operator for a visual action that can be heard truthfully: one vessel set down, rain beginning, a cloth folded, a dog shifting its paws. Do not ask generation to invent essential dialogue, music, or plot facts.

Record whether Flow-generated audio is:

* absent by design;
* reference-only and to be replaced;
* potentially usable but subject to sound review.

Use `08_Production/07_Google_Flow_Workflow.md` for reference locks, clip creation, and technical review. Use `08_Production/10_Music_And_SFX_Workflow.md` to evaluate or replace actual audio.

### 4. Place the Natural Pass

Plan location bed, weather, action sound, and one selected ASMR detail before considering score. The episode should remain understandable and emotionally credible in this pass.

### 5. Test Music Against Silence

For every proposed cue, review three versions of the cut:

1. natural sound only;
2. reduced sound or intentional quiet;
3. music at a restrained level.

Keep the version that most clearly preserves action, character, and feeling. “More emotional” is not by itself a reason to choose music.

### 6. Lock Entrances, Exits, and Bridges

Mark cue entrance, cue exit, sound bridges, narration windows, and protected quiet in the scene ledger. A cue must not abruptly cut across an action or force a transition that picture has not motivated.

### 7. Review in Episode Context

Judge audio across the whole episode, on phone speaker and headphones, after picture rhythm is stable. Final mixing, export, and rights verification belong to Production.

## Music Decision Matrix

| Episode condition | Preferred choice | Why | Reject when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Opening introduces a location, weather, or practical task | Natural sound first; optional modest cue after orientation | Lets the viewer enter the real place | Music arrives as a “trailer” announcement |
| Focused cooking, repair, walking, gardening, or animal care | Natural sound with one ASMR priority | Process and material are story information | Score hides hands, tools, footsteps, or animal behaviour |
| A character notices a meaningful change | Reduce layers; use silence or a very late soft cue | The recognition must land first | A cue tells viewers the discovery is monumental |
| Gentle family cooperation resolves a need | Light music may support the earned release | Supports connection without claiming victory | Cue turns ordinary care into a triumph montage |
| Memory, seasonal transition, or return to a recurring place | A documented recurring motif or natural bridge | Builds recognition if used consistently | Motif assigns a new or contradictory meaning |
| Grief, apology, uncertainty, or reflective ending | Quiet, room tone, or a minimal cue after the beat | Protects dignity and viewer reflection | Swelling score performs emotion for the character |
| Comedic minor mistake | Natural reaction and timing | Kind humour comes from character and action | Stings, boings, or ridicule make a person foolish |
| Transition between locations | Sound bridge or clean acoustic reset | Preserves geography and attention | Decorative whooshes or unrelated music hide travel logic |

## Shorts and Long-Form Use

### YouTube Shorts

Shorts need immediate sensory clarity, not loudness. In the first seconds, use a recognisable image and, where useful, one truthful sound: rain on tile, water in a vessel, a broom on the courtyard, a stove catching, or a hand beginning a craft action.

* Keep the opening sound readable on a phone speaker.
* Choose one featured audio idea; dense layering makes vertical short-form feel rushed.
* Reserve a small quiet pocket for the turn or ending, even in a brief runtime.
* End on completed action, natural decay, a gentle final cue, or a truthful quiet image.
* Never use sudden impacts, suspense pulses, fake alarms, or abrupt volume jumps to force retention.

### Long-Form Episodes

Long-form episodes may establish distinct acoustic spaces and let actions take their natural duration.

* Use sound bridges to connect a route, room, or time change.
* Let recurring location beds vary with season, time, and distance rather than repeating identical loops.
* Use music sparingly enough that major cues retain meaning.
* Give narration, ASMR, scene transitions, and final rest separate space.
* Do not confuse duration with permission for wallpaper score or prolonged ambient filler.

## Character, Location, and Seasonal Applications

### Characters

* **Mother:** kitchen and household scenes can feature vessel, cloth, food, fire, and measured footsteps; music should not turn her routine care into self-sacrifice melodrama.
* **Father:** outdoor work, repair, route, land, and animal-care scenes benefit from material detail and open-air perspective; avoid heroic rhythms or exaggerated tool impacts.
* **Elder Brother:** school and learning beats need clear practical sounds and room for his response; do not use anxious music to make a small error a crisis.
* **Younger Daughter:** curiosity may be carried by a close natural detail, then a pause as she understands; do not make her wonder a magical cue or danger signal.
* **Family Dog:** soft paws, collar or breathing only where canonically appropriate, and calm response to household activity can add life. Never use growls, attack music, or distress sounds to manufacture suspense.

### Locations

Use the documented location file as the sound source authority. A kitchen, courtyard, thinnai, field path, school, tea stall, grocery store, river ghat, and workshop must not share the same generic ambience. Perspective must follow camera distance and whether the viewer is indoors, under cover, or outside.

### Seasons

Season is audible action, not a music genre:

| Condition | Episode audio application |
| --- | --- |
| Summer | restrained insects, dry footsteps, shade, water care, and slower outdoor rhythm |
| Monsoon | rain’s specific surface, protected interior sound, wet movement, restrained roof and leaf detail |
| Post-monsoon | damp ground, renewed outdoor activity, careful routes, clearer open air where appropriate |
| Harvest | material work, sorting, carrying, shared rhythm, and respectful rests rather than celebratory montage |
| Documented festivals | real preparation and location sound; no generic crowd spectacle or “exotic” scoring |

Never let a seasonal cue replace weather continuity. If it is raining in picture, the episode must retain a credible rain-world until a documented change occurs.

## Continuity Rules

Maintain an episode audio ledger alongside the visual continuity ledger. Record:

* location, indoor/outdoor perspective, time, season, and weather;
* base ambience and significant off-screen activity;
* visible action sounds and material identity;
* recurring cue or motif name and approved association;
* narration windows, ASMR priorities, and intentional quiet;
* music entrance, exit, and bridge logic;
* incoming and outgoing sound state for each scene.

Do not carry sunny courtyard birds into an enclosed rainy kitchen, make a brass vessel sound like plastic, change the dog’s implied position without picture support, or restart a cue after every cut as if each shot is a separate advertisement.

## Positive and Negative Applications

### Positive: Rainy Kitchen Adjustment

Rain begins while Mother protects a cooking fire. Rain on the roof, firewood placement, and a pot’s small simmer lead the scene. The mix thins for Mother’s glance toward the damp threshold. A soft cue enters only after the family has practically adjusted the work.

**Why it works:** weather, action, and emotional relief are readable before music supports them.

### Negative: “Sad Rain” Shortcut

The first raindrop triggers a loud melancholy melody while rain, fire, and family action disappear.

**Why it fails:** it labels rain as sadness, erases the episode’s practical reality, and makes a manageable event falsely dramatic.

### Positive: Father’s Repair

The episode holds on rope tension, wood contact, and Father’s quiet demonstration. Elder Brother’s listening and the repaired object’s final stable state receive a brief moment of room tone before a warm closing cue.

**Why it works:** the sound follows skill, learning, and earned resolution.

### Negative: Repair Montage

Fast cuts, pounding percussion, exaggerated clanks, and a victory sting make a small household repair feel like a competition.

**Why it fails:** it breaks camera, character, and emotional scale.

## Edge Cases and Corrections

| Edge case | Required decision |
| --- | --- |
| Picture is emotionally clear but feels “empty” | Start with truthful ambience or quiet; do not add music solely to occupy space |
| Narration competes with a key tactile action | Move, shorten, or remove the line; protect the action before compressing all sound |
| A Google Flow clip contains unwanted dialogue, score, or modern sound | Treat it as a source defect; mute or replace only if picture remains valid, otherwise return it for correction |
| A cue is beautiful but culturally generic or period-inaccurate | Reject it; beauty does not justify a false sound world |
| A recurring motif is requested for a new emotion | Do not reuse it until its association is documented and approved |
| A Short must work muted | Ensure the visual hook and captions carry meaning; do not compensate with an aggressive audio opening |
| A sequence needs more energy | Check story clarity, action progression, or visual rhythm first; do not default to faster music |
| A final image is covered by end-screen needs | Preserve its emotional rest in the edit plan and coordinate delivery treatment with Production |

---

## Quality Standard

An episode-level audio plan is ready for handoff only when:

* every score cue has a stated story function and a planned entrance and exit;
* natural sound remains the primary evidence of place, material, season, and work;
* selected ASMR moments have picture time and uncluttered audio space;
* silence or reduced sound protects genuine recognition, reflection, or resolution;
* audio perspective, weather, objects, character behaviour, and routes match continuity;
* the Short or long-form format has been considered before framing and edit decisions are locked;
* no sound choice introduces danger, stereotype, sentimentality, modernity, or false urgency;
* the handoff identifies unresolved sound risks for `08_Production/`.

# Episode Review System

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories reviews an episode before release,
learns from its YouTube performance after release, and converts evidence into
careful improvements. It ensures that review protects the series promise rather
than rewarding only a polished image, a high click-through rate, or a short-term
retention result.

Episode review asks two linked questions:

1. Does this exact episode truthfully work for a viewer now?
2. What can the team learn for future episodes without weakening canon,
   character truth, or the calm everyday-life promise?

This is the Episode System's viewer-experience review manual. It does not
replace Story Engine doctrine or Production quality-control, publishing,
analytics, version-control, and archive procedures.

## Guiding Principle

Use evidence to improve clarity, rhythm, and care. Never trade the audience's
trust in the village for a temporary metric.

## Authority and Scope

Review in this order:

```text
Specific character, location, object, world, culture, research, and timeline canon
↓
06_Story_Engine/ story construction, emotional truth, pacing, and prohibited patterns
↓
Approved episode brief, storyboard, and continuity record
↓
07_Episode_System/ viewer-facing episode structure and this review system
↓
08_Production/ asset, quality-control, publishing, metadata, version, and archive controls
```

Episode review can identify and block a contradiction, weak promise, unclear
scene, pacing failure, or false continuation. It cannot make a generated clip,
public comment, popular request, or successful metric into canon. A public
preference is evidence about reception, not authority to rewrite the world.

## Review Principles

1. **Review the full experience.** Story, character, place, sound, packaging,
   accessibility, and platform presentation must agree.
2. **Use evidence, not impressions alone.** Cite a timestamp, canon source,
   continuity record, viewer behaviour, or recurring comment pattern.
3. **Separate defects from ideas.** A defect must be corrected or formally
   decided; an improvement idea is not a release blocker.
4. **Review independently.** No person approves their own unresolved mistake.
5. **Judge the actual release.** A local edit, draft title, or remembered
   storyboard cannot substitute for the processed YouTube upload.
6. **Protect proportion.** Calm does not mean unclear, and retention does not
   justify spectacle, false urgency, or emotional manipulation.
7. **Preserve history.** Every decision applies to an identified version; a
   later revision requires re-review of affected dependencies.

## Roles and Professional Handoffs

### Episode Review Lead

Plans the review, assembles the correct evidence, assigns reviewers, records
findings, and recommends pass, revise, block, or learn-only action.

### Story Reviewer

Reviews premise, hook, need, character choices, conflict, emotional centre,
pacing, ending, and continuity seed against Story Engine and Episode System.

### Canon and Continuity Reviewer

Checks character identity and behaviour, location geography, object state,
season, weather, time, routes, chronology, culture, and cross-episode facts.
May block a contradiction.

### Picture and Google Flow Reviewer

Checks selected assets and edit sequence for generated drift, screen direction,
camera restraint, prop handoffs, visual readability, period accuracy, and
format suitability. Google Flow output is always lower authority than approved
input references.

### Sound and Accessibility Reviewer

Checks dialogue, ambience, ASMR, music, silence, captions, language, and
essential sound context.

### Packaging Reviewer

Checks thumbnail, title, description, chapters, cards, end screens, playlists,
and continuation labels against the locked final cut.

### Release and Community Owner

Verifies the final platform release, maintains the public record, and brings
relevant viewer questions, corrections, and recurring feedback into the
post-release review without manufacturing engagement.

### Handoff Standard

Every reviewer receives an identified version and the evidence needed to judge
it: episode ID, format, runtime, current cut or public URL, brief, relevant
canon references, continuity ledger, sound/caption status, package draft, prior
findings, and requested decision. Every returned review names the timestamp or
field, severity, evidence, owner, correction, and recheck requirement.

Never hand off “the latest” without a version or ask a reviewer to approve a
moving target.

## Findings and Decision Rules

Classify findings consistently:

| Level | Meaning | Decision |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Blocker | Contradicts canon; materially misleads; risks dignity, safety, rights, accessibility, or technical delivery | Stop release or pause public exposure. |
| Major | Materially harms comprehension, continuity, pacing, emotional truth, or platform promise | Fix and re-review, or document a formal Quality Control waiver. |
| Minor | Visible but does not materially change truth, usability, accessibility, or trust | Fix when practical; log if deferred. |
| Observation | Useful future improvement with no current release defect | Record in the learning log. |

The default status is **not approved** until every applicable pre-release gate
passes. A waiver is not silence: it must name the exact version, issue, viewer
impact, reason it cannot be corrected, approvers, and future-use limitation.

## Review Package

Prepare one package for the episode review cycle:

* episode ID, format, runtime, revision, owner, and review date;
* one-sentence premise, main thread, emotional centre, and final image;
* source canon and Story Engine references;
* beat sheet, script or narration, locked storyboard, and shot list;
* character, location, season, prop, time, and sound continuity ledger;
* Google Flow selected-asset record, prompts, references, revisions, and
  candidate decisions;
* rough cut or final master, captions, music/rights status, and sound plan;
* thumbnail, title, description, chapters, playlist, cards, end screens, and
  continuation plan;
* earlier review findings, corrections, approvals, and waivers; and
* post-release URL, platform checks, and analytics observations when available.

An incomplete package may support exploratory feedback, but not a release
decision.

## Pre-Release Review Workflow

### 1. Verify the review baseline

Confirm that the cut, selected assets, captions, thumbnail, metadata, and
continuity record refer to the same revision. If a visual, line, sound cue, or
title has changed after a lock, identify which review gates it invalidates
before proceeding.

### 2. Watch once as the intended viewer

Watch without pausing. Record:

* the promise made in the opening;
* when the practical need becomes clear;
* where attention naturally moves;
* any point of confusion, redundancy, emotional overstatement, or false
  expectation; and
* whether the ending feels complete before navigation or promotion appears.

This pass detects viewer experience. It does not replace evidence checking.

### 3. Review against source evidence

Watch again with the package. Check each relevant factual and structural
requirement. Pause, compare, and record precise timestamps or platform fields.

### 4. Check the cut as a sequence

Inspect every change and its adjacent shots. A corrected prop in one frame may
invalidate a later handoff; a changed line may require caption, metadata, title,
or emotional-pacing review.

### 5. Decide and hand off

Issue one of four decisions:

* **Approved for next gate** — all applicable items pass.
* **Revise and return** — findings identify a repairable owner and recheck.
* **Blocked pending authority** — a canon, rights, safety, or factual question
  needs the authority that owns it.
* **Rejected or redesign required** — the episode's premise or structure cannot
  be repaired locally without breaking its promise.

Send only the exact revision, findings log, required correction, and affected
downstream owners to the next stage.

## Pre-Release Quality Gates

### Gate A: Story and Episode Experience

Check:

- [ ] The hook is immediate, visible, and truthful.
- [ ] The episode promptly orients the viewer to people, place, task, and
      immediate purpose.
- [ ] One central practical and emotional thread carries the episode.
- [ ] Every scene advances process, place, character, feeling, or payoff.
- [ ] Preparation and work are visible enough to make the resolution earned.
- [ ] The obstacle or discovery is gentle, proportionate, and follows from the
      established day.
- [ ] The response uses plausible knowledge, resources, and cooperation.
- [ ] Pacing holds meaningful care and compresses repetition.
- [ ] Dialogue, narration, sound, and image do not explain the same point
      repeatedly.
- [ ] The ending resolves today's feeling and rests on a concrete image, sound,
      or action.
- [ ] Any next-episode seed is truthful, optional, and does not withhold the
      current episode's satisfaction.

### Gate B: Canon, Character, Location, and Continuity

Check:

- [ ] Character appearance, age, clothing, personality, relationships, voice,
      body language, and skill are consistent with individual canon.
- [ ] Children, elders, villagers, and the Family Dog are represented with
      safety and dignity; no one becomes a spectacle, villain, or plot device.
- [ ] Location architecture, materials, routes, work zones, atmosphere, and
      social use match documentation.
- [ ] Objects preserve material, period, ownership, position, orientation,
      amount, wetness, damage, and progress across cuts.
- [ ] Time of day, season, weather, light, mud, rain, smoke, food, fire,
      plants, animals, and clothing progress logically.
- [ ] Travel, task duration, and transitions are geographically and practically
      credible.
- [ ] No modern technology, brand, contemporary transport, generic “village”
      substitute, stereotype, or unsupported cultural assertion appears.
- [ ] Explicit ties to earlier or later episodes preserve chronology,
      relationship state, season, and released-story facts.

### Gate C: Picture, Google Flow, and Visual Rhythm

Check:

- [ ] Each Flow shot remains a faithful continuation of approved storyboard and
      input references.
- [ ] No identity drift, extra limb, unwanted person or animal, altered prop,
      warped object, flicker, text, logo, modern detail, or background drift is
      visible.
- [ ] Camera scale, side, height, screen direction, movement, and focal action
      support the scene rather than advertise the tool.
- [ ] Action is readable, physically credible, and has a clean cut or hold
      point.
- [ ] Wide, medium, close, and detail shots provide purposeful variation.
- [ ] Format supports the target viewer: central vertical readability and
      caption safety for Shorts; readable subject and geography for long form.
- [ ] The sequence remains understandable with sound muted.

Reject a beautiful but inaccurate output. Regenerate, use a more stable visual,
or redesign the shot; do not hide a contradiction in a rapid cut or blur.

### Gate D: Sound, Education, and Accessibility

Check:

- [ ] Dialogue is intelligible, appropriately voiced, and supported by the
      shown action.
- [ ] Natural sound and ambience match place, weather, time, and distance.
- [ ] ASMR appears at meaningful action points and does not interrupt emotional
      information or become constant texture.
- [ ] Music is restrained and earned; silence retains room tone or purposeful
      visual presence.
- [ ] No generated or added sound invents essential dialogue, canon, plot, or
      historical fact.
- [ ] Educational meaning arises through observed skill, choice, and care—not
      a lecture, quiz, or forced moral.
- [ ] Captions accurately time speech, names, culturally specific language, and
      essential sound cues, and remain readable in the final platform context.

### Gate E: Packaging and YouTube Promise

Check:

- [ ] Thumbnail is an actual, period-safe episode moment with mobile-readable
      focus.
- [ ] Title and description accurately name the visible action, person, object,
      place, season, or feeling.
- [ ] Thumbnail, title, description, and opening make the same promise.
- [ ] Chapters describe real stages without spoiling the resolution.
- [ ] Playlist order, previous/next links, cards, end screens, and Part labels
      describe actual continuity and do not imply false chronology.
- [ ] Captions, language fields, credits, rights, audience setting, and links
      are complete.
- [ ] No public text uses danger, urgency, trend bait, fabricated outcomes, or
      a modern framing that the episode cannot fulfill.

Release authorization itself remains under
`08_Production/14_Quality_Control.md`; upload, scheduling, public verification,
and correction procedures remain under `08_Production/15_Publishing_Workflow.md`.

## Post-Release Learning Workflow

Post-release review begins only after the public version is verified. Review at
a planned, documented interval with enough data to avoid reacting to normal
early variation. Do not refresh compulsively or change creative direction
because of a single comment or early fluctuation.

### 1. Verify the public object

Confirm the public URL, title, thumbnail, description, captions, chapters,
playlist, cards, end screens, and playback at beginning, middle, and end. Log
any discrepancy as a correction issue, not as an analytics insight.

### 2. Read audience evidence in context

Use relevant available YouTube signals together:

* impressions and click-through rate;
* opening retention and later retention or completion;
* traffic sources and search terms;
* returning viewers and playlist continuation where meaningful;
* caption, language, accessibility, and playback feedback;
* comments that show delight, confusion, expectation mismatch, or a factual
  question; and
* performance relative to comparable format, topic, season, and release
  conditions—not an unrelated viral upload.

Do not infer a motive from one metric. Analytics can show what viewers did, not
always why they did it.

### 3. Diagnose before acting

Use these evidence patterns carefully:

| Evidence pattern | Likely question | Safe response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low impressions | Is topic or packaging clarity limited? | Check title, thumbnail, metadata, audience fit, and delivery; do not invent bigger stakes. |
| Strong clicks, early exits | Did packaging or hook promise a different episode? | Compare thumbnail, title, first minute, and source brief; repair future promise alignment. |
| Low early retention, later satisfaction | Is orientation too slow or unclear? | Bring real task, place, or condition forward; preserve meaningful sensory arrival. |
| Drop during repeated work | Does the sequence repeat without new action or feeling? | Compress repetition while retaining visible craft and process. |
| Drop at a quiet pause | Is the pause uninhabited or poorly placed? | Check whether image, sound, or emotional recognition gives it purpose; do not remove all silence automatically. |
| Strong completion, weak discovery | Is the experience satisfying but hard to identify? | Improve truthful title, thumbnail, description, or playlist placement. |
| Repeated factual confusion | Is a visual, caption, context line, or sequence unclear? | Correct public information where appropriate and clarify future episode construction. |
| Requests for danger, conflict, or melodrama | Does the request fit series doctrine? | Treat it as preference data, not a mandate; preserve calm truthful stakes. |

### 4. Classify the lesson

Record each lesson as one of:

* **Correction:** a public factual, accessibility, technical, or packaging issue
  requiring the Publishing Workflow.
* **Episode-process improvement:** a specific improvement to hook clarity,
  scene purpose, pacing, continuity record, sound, captions, or handoff.
* **Research or canon question:** an ambiguity requiring evidence and the
  authority that owns it.
* **Future creative observation:** a nonbinding pattern to test within canon.

Do not label a lesson “the audience wants more drama” without specific,
repeatable evidence and a doctrine review. Do not modify historical facts,
character identity, or series values merely because a tactic performed well.

### 5. Close the loop

For each confirmed lesson, record the evidence, decision, owner, affected
future stage, and whether a current public correction is required. Update the
appropriate process record; retain the release archive unchanged except for a
documented correction event.

## Cross-Episode and Season Review

Review a release as part of a continuing world, especially when it connects to
nearby episodes.

Before treating an episode as part of a sequence, verify:

* the weather, season, time of day, clothing, plant/field state, and animal
  routine match any explicit continuation;
* objects, repairs, food, letters, school work, or tasks retain their recorded
  status;
* character knowledge, apologies, memories, and relationship feelings do not
  reset or advance without a shown reason;
* release order, playlist order, metadata, cards, and end screens do not create
  a false chronological claim; and
* the next episode can be understood without viewers having seen the prior one,
  unless it is honestly and clearly part of a documented sequence.

Seasonal learning should improve how weather affects action, sound, light, and
pace. It must not turn season into a decorative trend or permit a contradiction
with the established timeline.

## Google Flow Review Rules

Google Flow requires a dedicated review because plausible motion can conceal a
world error. For every selected generated shot:

1. Compare the first frame with approved character, location, prop, and
   continuity references.
2. Check the single intended action, camera behaviour, and end state.
3. Inspect for identity, anatomy, prop, architecture, period, weather, and
   screen-direction drift.
4. Review it in sequence with adjacent shots and sound.
5. Record the exact candidate, prompt, input references, settings available,
   selection reason, and revision.

If a clip fails, classify it: input-frame failure, motion failure, identity
drift, prop failure, location/period drift, camera failure, or timing failure.
Make one deliberate correction, log it, and re-review. Do not reroll blindly,
extend a flawed clip, or allow the generator to resolve a missing story
decision.

## Positive and Negative Review Examples

### Positive: Honest packaging and process

An episode opens with rain affecting Mother's cooking fire. Its thumbnail shows
the actual damp firewood, the title names rainy-day cooking, and the description
describes the family finding a patient way to finish the meal. Retention shows
viewers staying through the process, and comments mention the rain sound and
shared care. The lesson is to preserve the clear condition-and-task opening,
not to add a larger storm.

### Positive: Useful pacing revision

Reviewers find that three similar sorting shots repeat the same information.
They retain the first skilled action, one child observation, and the prepared
result. The episode becomes clearer while keeping its calm work rhythm. The
continuity ledger confirms that the basket contents still progress visibly.

### Negative: Metrics replace doctrine

A quiet repair receives fewer clicks than a more dramatic topic. The team adds
“emergency,” a frightened-dog thumbnail, and urgent music to the next repair.
This is a review failure: packaging overpromises, the dog is misused, and a
small practical story is falsely framed as danger.

### Negative: Beautiful generated drift

A Flow clip has graceful motion but changes a documented clay kitchen into a
generic modern interior. It must be rejected even if viewer metrics might not
notice the difference. Canon continuity is part of the viewer's long-term
trust.

## Edge Cases

* **A factual or accessibility error after release:** Follow the Publishing
  Workflow's correction or pause procedure, record the public impact, and
  include the cause in the review lesson. Do not silently alter the record.
* **Conflicting reviewer feedback:** Return to the approved baseline and the
  authority that owns the disputed fact. Do not average incompatible opinions.
* **A change after picture or sound lock:** Treat the replacement as a new
  revision and recheck adjacent scenes, captions, package promise, and release
  state.
* **Very little analytics data:** Record qualitative observations but defer
  broad conclusions. Compare only when a meaningful group of comparable
  releases exists.
* **A viral result from misleading traffic:** Check whether the incoming
  audience received the promised episode. A high click count with poor
  expectation match is a warning, not a template.
* **A valuable viewer correction:** Verify it against research and canon before
  changing public language or future work. Gratitude does not remove the need
  for evidence.

## Review Record Template

```text
Episode ID / revision:
Review gate or post-release interval:
Reviewer and role:
Evidence reviewed:
Finding level:
Timestamp or public field:
Observed issue or lesson:
Relevant canon / episode / production reference:
Required correction or proposed improvement:
Owner:
Dependencies to recheck:
Decision and approver:
Completion / verification date:
```

Use a concise evidence-based record. “Feels wrong” may begin a review, but it
must become a stated issue with supporting evidence before it drives a change.

## Canon Authority

This manual is the permanent Episode System authority for episode review and
post-release learning. It may require correction, hold a stage, or identify a
need for escalation. It does not authorize a reviewer, production tool,
analytics result, or public response to revise specific canon, Story Engine
doctrine, or the Production release process.

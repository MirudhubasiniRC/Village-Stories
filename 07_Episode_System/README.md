# Episode System

## Purpose

This folder permanently defines how a Village Stories premise becomes a watchable Short or long-form episode.

It governs the physical episode experience: first image, hook, scene order, pacing, sound placement, visual rhythm, ending, truthful continuation, review, and platform readiness.

`06_Story_Engine/` defines what makes a Village Stories story emotionally and canonically correct.

`07_Episode_System/` defines how that story is organised so an audience can understand, feel, and remember it.

---

# Episode System Philosophy

An episode is not a collection of attractive shots.

It is a complete viewer experience with:

* A clear opening promise.
* A practical and emotional centre.
* Deliberate visual and sound rhythm.
* A satisfying resting point.
* A truthful reason to remember or continue the story.

Village Stories must earn attention through recognition, care, curiosity, and craft.

It must never depend on false danger, chaotic editing, misleading titles, or an incomplete ending used only to force a next view.

```text

↓
Select episode type
↓
Choose Short or long-form delivery
↓
Design opening promise and hook
↓
Build scene structure and pacing
↓
Plan education, sound, silence, and visual rhythm
↓
Design emotionally complete ending
↓
Check continuity and quality
↓
Hand off to 08_Production/
↓
Review completed release
```

No production stage may silently change the episode's emotional promise or canon state.

# Core Episode Rules

Every episode must:

* Have one primary practical activity, emotional question, or relationship movement.
* Establish its place and time quickly.
* Use scenes that visibly progress action or feeling.
* Respect the established season, location, objects, and character behaviour.
* Give natural sound, silence, and music a specific function.
* End on an earned emotional image.
* Remain understandable to a new viewer.

Connected episodes may continue a larger process, memory, repair, or family feeling, but each part must resolve its own immediate experience.

---

# Short-Form Rule

A Short must create immediate orientation without becoming rushed.

It needs:

* A truthful first image.
* One clear episode question.
* A visible action or change.
* A small emotional payoff.
* A closing image that remains meaningful even if the viewer never sees another part.

---

# Long-Form Rule

A long-form episode earns additional duration through:

* More observable process.
* A richer seasonal or location atmosphere.
* A gentle supporting thread.
* More room for family interaction and silence.

It must not add filler, repeated conflict, or unrelated subplots merely to increase runtime.

---

# Google Flow Considerations

Episode planning must be specific enough for Google Flow production:

* Every shot has a clear location, time, weather, character state, object state, and action.
* Scene transitions preserve clothing, props, light direction, and emotional progression.
* Camera rhythm must be planned before generation, not invented after inconsistent images exist.
* A shot may be beautiful but still rejected if it breaks location, character, or time continuity.

The Episode System plans the continuity chain. `08_Production/07_Google_Flow_Workflow.md` executes and validates it.

---

# Quality Standard

Before production handoff, a director or AI must be able to answer:

* What does the first image promise?
* What does the viewer understand by the end of the first scene?
* What changes between opening and ending?
* Which visual and sound moments carry emotion?
* What must remain identical across shots?
* Why does the final image feel complete?
* If there is another part, what genuinely continues?

If these answers are unclear, the episode is not ready.

# Openings

## Authority

This manual governs the complete opening sequence of a Village Stories episode: from first frame through the first fully established scene. It sets production standards for visual entry, sound entry, orientation, title use, location establishment, character arrival, and the handoff to the episode body.

`07_Episode_System/04_Hook_System.md` defines the viewer promise made in the first moments. This document defines how that promise is staged and delivered as an opening sequence. `06_Story_Engine/` remains the authority for story doctrine, character truth, conflict, and emotional boundaries.

---

## Guiding Principle

**Enter through the most truthful image of this episode's living moment; orient quickly; then allow the story to begin without interruption.**

An opening is not a reusable village introduction. It is the first scene of this episode. It may contain beauty, ritual, weather, and sound, but every element must serve the day's specific subject.

The audience should feel invited into a real place, not held at the gate while the production announces itself.

---

## 1. Purpose

The opening sequence must:

1. establish attention with an episode-specific event or condition;
2. identify the immediate place, time, and emotional temperature;
3. introduce the central character or group through action;
4. confirm the story's practical or relational direction;
5. establish the appropriate sensory world; and
6. transition directly into Scene 1 without repeating setup.

It does not need to explain history, summarize the series, introduce every family member, or present a fixed brand ritual.

---

## 2. Opening Philosophy

Village Stories opens from inside life. A kitchen opens when a pot is checked. A school morning opens when a bag is missing. A rainy day opens when a family responds to rain. A memory episode opens when an object changes a character's attention.

The best opening image has three properties:

- it is **specific** to the episode;
- it is **legible** without explanation; and
- it contains a natural next action.

Generic beauty is insufficient. A sunrise becomes a valid opening only if sunrise affects the episode: a journey must start early, Father begins a repair before the heat, or a child prepares for school while the house wakes.

---

## 3. Opening Sequence Anatomy

Build the opening as one continuous chain of understanding.

### 3.1 First frame

The first frame is the immediate visual or sonic anchor. It must be clear at the delivery format's viewing size, especially on a phone.

Prefer a readable object, face, gesture, weather condition, or movement over a distant wide shot with many equal details.

### 3.2 Recognition

Within the next beat, the viewer understands what they are looking at. A close-up of wet fabric becomes a laundry problem when the rain and line are seen; an old key becomes meaningful when Father recognizes it.

### 3.3 Orientation

Give only the context necessary to follow the opening: relevant location, active character, time condition, and immediate situation.

### 3.4 Intent

Show a response beginning. A person gathers tools, opens the gate, calls a child, reaches for a basket, or chooses to investigate.

### 3.5 Entry to Scene 1

The opening ends at the point the episode's first scene takes full control. This is usually the first purposeful action, not a title card or a second establishing montage.

---

## 4. The First Frame Standard

Every first frame must be reviewed as a still image and as moving footage.

### It must be

- visually comprehensible on a small screen;
- consistent with the title and thumbnail promise;
- free of distracting overlays, logos, and unmotivated text;
- composed around one primary focal point;
- lit and colored to preserve the location's established identity;
- sufficiently active or emotionally charged to make the next moment relevant.

### It must not be

- black screen before a logo;
- a generic aerial or landscape shot unrelated to the episode;
- an unexplained extreme close-up that reads as abstract texture;
- a future payoff used as a false preview;
- a high-intensity reaction taken out of context;
- a repeated channel template chosen by habit.

The first frame is not required to be fast. It is required to make a viewer curious about the immediate next frame.

---

## 5. Opening Types

Select an opening type based on episode function.

### 5.1 Action opening

Begin with a character already doing the episode's core work.

Use for cooking, repair, gardening, travel, cleaning, building, or care tasks.

Example: Father presses a hand against a loose bicycle brake, hears the rub, and brings the bicycle under the shade.

### 5.2 Condition opening

Begin with a change in the world that demands a response.

Use for rain, heat, wind, an empty container, a missed school item, or a seasonal shift.

Example: the first drops of monsoon rain darken the courtyard as drying grain is still spread outside.

### 5.3 Arrival opening

Begin with a person, animal, object, message, or delivery arriving.

Use when arrival starts the episode rather than merely decorating it.

Example: the postman's bicycle bell makes the Family Dog lift its head at the gate.

### 5.4 Relationship opening

Begin with an interaction that carries the episode's feeling.

Use for reconciliation, shared preparation, a gift, a small misunderstanding, or a parent-child moment.

Example: Younger Daughter hides a hand-drawn picture while waiting for Elder Brother to return.

### 5.5 Object-memory opening

Begin when an object calls up a practical or emotional next action.

Use only when the object is genuinely active in the episode.

Example: Mother finds an old spice tin with a handwritten label and decides to remake a remembered dish.

### 5.6 Place-process opening

Begin with a sensory location detail that directly begins a process.

Use when the sensory event is inseparable from the story.

Example: tea begins to boil at the tea stall as a neighbor arrives to ask about a missing seed packet.

---

## 6. Decision Framework

Before boards, the director and episode writer answer:

1. What episode event starts the day or changes its direction?
2. What can the viewer see before anyone explains it?
3. Which character has the clearest first response?
4. Which location makes the event easiest to understand?
5. Is atmosphere part of the event or merely decoration?
6. What has the title and thumbnail led the viewer to expect?
7. Which first action carries directly into Scene 1?

Choose the opening that reveals the real center earliest with the least narration.

If the episode could begin with any landscape, it has not yet found its opening. If a wide location shot is needed, place it after a specific anchor or make it itself carry the condition, such as visible rain approaching the family courtyard.

---

## 7. Titles, Branding, and Intro Cards

The opening sequence takes priority over branding.

### Title card rules

- Do not place a title card before the viewer has seen the active episode condition.
- Keep title display brief and visually quiet.
- Place it over continuing relevant action or at a natural visual transition.
- Do not cover essential facial performance, object detail, subtitles, or process information.
- Omit it entirely when its inclusion would interrupt a short episode.

### Channel identity

Channel identity is carried primarily through craft: canon-consistent characters, village sound, truthful pacing, and familiar emotional tone. A fixed logo animation is optional and must never delay the episode.

### Metadata alignment

The first opening sequence must honor the packaging. If the title is about “Father's Old Stool,” do not begin with unrelated breakfast imagery. If the thumbnail shows monsoon laundry, the first sequence must reach that condition immediately.

---

## 8. Shorts Opening Execution

In a Short, the opening is compressed into the hook and first scene. There is no separate introduction.

### Short requirements

- show the active condition in the first 1–3 seconds;
- identify the essential character, place, or object by 3–7 seconds;
- begin the response before the viewer feels paused in setup;
- use titles only when they clarify rather than delay;
- let captions support comprehension, never manufacture suspense;
- preserve a single clear opening question or action.

### Short positive example

First frame: rain strikes a row of drying clothes. Second beat: Elder Brother sees it from the doorway. Third beat: he takes the basket and runs outside. The audience understands the task, place, character, and motion without a spoken explanation.

### Short negative example

First frame: channel logo. Then five scenic shots. Then text reading “Will they save everything?” before a routine laundry collection begins.

### Short opening timing

| Time | Required result |
| --- | --- |
| 0–1 second | A readable active image or sound |
| 1–4 seconds | Condition or subject clear |
| 4–7 seconds | Character response begins |
| 7–10 seconds | Opening has handed into the main action |

If a Short requires more than 10 seconds to start its story, rebuild the opening.

---

## 9. Long-Form Opening Execution

Long-form episodes have more room for location, sound, and emotional preparation. Use that room to deepen context, not to postpone the episode.

### Long-form requirements

- begin with a specific active anchor in the first seconds;
- establish relevant place and time in the first opening sequence;
- introduce the central character through purposeful behavior;
- include atmosphere only when it advances tone, season, or task;
- let the opening become Scene 1 by roughly 10–25 seconds;
- move the title card after the viewer has a reason to continue.

### Long-form positive example

At early dawn, Father lays tools beside an old bench. He tests one leg; it wobbles. A rooster calls in the distance. He looks toward the waking house, then begins to clear the bench. The viewer has atmosphere, place, character, task, and direction in one sequence.

### Long-form negative example

Thirty seconds of sunrise, birds, roofs, tea steam, and dogs walking, followed by Father entering frame to announce that he will look at a bench. The imagery may be attractive, but it is not an episode opening.

---

## 10. Scene 1 Handoff

The opening must not reset the story.

Use one of these handoff patterns:

### Direct continuation

The final opening action is the first Scene 1 action.

Example: Mother sees the empty container, takes the garden basket, and Scene 1 begins as she gathers ingredients.

### Motivated widen

The opening shows the specific need, then widens to the full scene where action continues.

Example: close-up rain on grain; cut wide to the family crossing the courtyard to cover it.

### Sound bridge

The opening sound introduces Scene 1 before the image cuts.

Example: a bicycle bell is heard over Father repairing a gate; cut to the postman arriving.

### Object handoff

The opening object becomes the active scene prop.

Example: an old recipe note in Mother's hands cuts to the kitchen ingredients she selects from it.

Do not use a handoff that repeats the same information in dialogue. The audience does not need to be told “It is raining” after the opening already showed rain and the family responding.

---

## 11. Visual Staging Standards

Opening composition should prioritize recognition.

### Frame design

- place the active object or person where it can be read immediately;
- use foreground only when it guides attention;
- keep background activity subordinate to the opening event;
- preserve established geography for recurring locations;
- avoid camera moves that hide the meaningful action.

### Camera movement

Use movement to reveal, follow, or connect. A slow pan can reveal clothes caught by rain; a follow can stay with a child carrying a school bag; a static frame can let a character's hesitation register.

Do not add a push-in, whip pan, or orbit simply to signal importance. Importance must come from the event.

### Cut order

Cut from condition to response, from object to owner, from sound source to listener, or from close action to relevant wider context. Avoid unrelated cutaways in the opening's first critical beats.

---

## 12. Sound Entry and Silence

The first sound should belong to the first image or prepare its arrival.

Appropriate entries:

- rain on tile before the wet courtyard is revealed;
- a kettle beginning to whistle under a kitchen close-up;
- a bicycle bell before the gate comes into view;
- a bird call that establishes dawn while Father begins an early task;
- room tone and fabric movement during a quiet relationship opening.

Avoid:

- dramatic stingers that imply danger;
- music starting before the environment is heard;
- voiceover that describes the image instead of letting it be seen;
- universal bird-and-rooster ambience regardless of location or time;
- complete silence caused by missing sound design rather than intention.

Use music after the opening has found its natural sound identity. A score may enter gently to support a transition, but it must not cover the first proof of place.

---

## 13. Season, Character, and Location

### Season

Opening conditions must belong to the current seasonal plan. Monsoon rain, harvest preparation, summer water collection, school reopening, or festival cleaning can shape the opening's urgency and palette. Do not use rain, fog, flowering plants, or festival dressing as generic visual enhancement.

### Character

Open with the character who naturally recognizes or owns the episode condition. A child may lead a school or play episode; Mother may lead a food or household decision; Father may lead repair or memory through objects; the Family Dog may lead only when its behavior matters to the episode.

Character entrance must preserve voice, body language, capability, relationships, and established wardrobe.

### Location

Use location as practical context. The kitchen establishes ingredients and family routine. The courtyard establishes weather and shared work. The school gate establishes departures and arrivals. The tea stall establishes community exchange. Do not select a location merely because it is visually attractive.

---

## 14. Continuity Requirements

The opening is binding continuity for the rest of the episode.

Confirm before final export:

- time of day is consistent with later shots;
- weather changes only when progression is shown;
- clothing, accessories, wetness, dirt, and carried props match;
- objects remain in the positions established by the opening;
- a discovered item is not discovered again;
- character knowledge and emotional state carry forward;
- travel between opening and Scene 1 is plausible;
- screen direction and entrance/exit geography remain readable.

An opening may use a later time only if the edit clearly establishes a time shift. Never use a visually stronger morning or rain shot from another condition just because it looks better.

---

## 15. Google Flow Handoff

Generation handoff begins after the opening is boarded and timed. Google Flow should receive precise production direction for each clip rather than a broad request for “a beautiful opening.”

### Required opening-shot packet

- episode ID, format, and opening type;
- shot number and target duration;
- exact first frame and final frame;
- camera framing, angle, motion, and screen direction;
- approved character and location references;
- wardrobe, props, weather, season, and time of day;
- action beat, facial performance, and energy level;
- required natural sound or intentional silence;
- continuity state from the prior episode, where applicable;
- next-shot connection;
- negative constraints.

### Example prompt direction

> Shot 01, 2.5 seconds, vertical Short. Close medium shot in the family courtyard during the first monsoon rain. Wet clothes on a low line begin to darken. Elder Brother appears at the doorway in his established school clothes, notices the rain, and turns toward the basket beside him. Natural rain on tile, no music. Calm quick recognition, no panic. Preserve the courtyard layout and rain direction for Shot 02. No thunder flash, no dramatic expressions, no additional characters.

### Flow acceptance criteria

Approve only clips that:

- begin on the planned readable anchor;
- preserve approved character and location canon;
- show the requested action in the specified order;
- allow the intended cut duration;
- retain the right emotional scale;
- do not add unplanned accidents, visitors, weather events, or props;
- connect to the next generated shot without a continuity break.

If Flow produces a beautiful but generic village image, reject it for the opening. The correct image is the one that begins this episode.

## 16. Workflow

### 16.1 Episode brief

The writer states the opening condition, viewpoint character, location, desired first sound, and Scene 1 handoff.

### 16.2 Opening options

Develop two or three truthful alternatives. Each must be based on a real beat from the approved episode, not a decorative montage.

### 16.3 Director selection

Select the option with the clearest specific anchor and strongest direct handoff. Confirm title/thumbnail alignment.

### 16.4 Storyboard and timing

Board the opening as a connected sequence. Mark exact first frame, visual reveal order, sound entry, title-card point, and Scene 1 in point.

### 16.5 Flow packet

Package generation instructions shot by shot with continuity state and negative constraints.

### 16.6 Assembly

Build the opening without music or title overlays first. Confirm visual logic and natural sound. Add branding only if it does not interrupt the sequence.

### 16.7 Review

Watch the first 10 seconds with no prior episode context. A reviewer should identify the active condition and response without explanation. Then review against the title and thumbnail.

## 17. Positive and Negative Examples

### Cooking episode

**Positive:** Mother opens a spice tin and finds it empty. She smells the last grains, looks toward the garden, and takes a basket outside. The opening establishes the missing ingredient and begins the solution.

**Negative:** slow steam shots, a long title, and narration about traditional cooking before the missing spice is shown.

### School episode

**Positive:** Younger Daughter reaches for her school bag, pauses because a notebook is missing, and searches the table while a parent notices.

**Negative:** a busy school montage followed by a dramatic line that a notebook has “vanished forever.”

### Memory episode

**Positive:** Father finds a carved toy while clearing a shelf. The mark makes him stop, and he brings it to the courtyard where the family gathers.

**Negative:** a tearful close-up and music swell before viewers know what object or memory is involved.

### Dog-led episode

**Positive:** The Family Dog waits at the gate, ears lifting at a familiar bicycle bell. The dog's anticipation leads naturally to the visitor and the episode's purpose.

**Negative:** the dog barks intensely at an unseen source to imply threat, then the episode becomes an ordinary neighbor visit.

---

## 18. Edge Cases

### An episode with no conflict

Open on a choice or beginning process: selecting seeds, setting out cups, preparing a shared meal, or beginning a seasonal clean. A peaceful episode still needs an active first intention.

### An episode with delayed central character

Use the relevant object, place, or incoming condition until the character enters. Do not introduce an unrelated secondary character only to fill the first seconds.

### Continuing storyline

Show enough of the continuing task to orient a new viewer. Avoid a recap unless the prior event cannot be understood from the present action.

### Educational opening

Show the observable question before the teaching point. Let viewers see the seed, leaf, tool, or technique that the episode will explain.

### Dialogue-free opening

Stage cause and response clearly using action, eyeline, sound, and prop placement. Use captions only for information that cannot be visualized.

---

## 19. Quality-Control Checklist

Before lock, confirm:

- The opening is specific to this episode.
- The first frame is readable at mobile size.
- The first sound belongs to the image and setting.
- The active condition arrives before any branding.
- The central character enters through meaningful behavior.
- Atmosphere supports the event rather than delaying it.
- The title card, if used, does not obstruct the story.
- The title and thumbnail accurately match the first sequence.
- Scene 1 begins through a direct, motivated handoff.
- Canon, season, location, props, and wardrobe are continuous.
- Google Flow clips meet exact first/last-frame needs.
- No false danger, exaggerated emotion, or unsupported promise has entered the edit.

### Review questions

- Would a new viewer know what has started to matter?
- Would a returning viewer recognize the world without seeing a repeated template?
- Does the opening invite attention at the same emotional scale as the rest of the episode?
- If the first two shots were removed, would the story become clearer? If so, remove or replace them.

## Final Production Reminder

Do not open with “the village.” Open with what is happening in the village today. Show the true beginning, let sound establish the place, orient the viewer with confidence, and step directly into the work, feeling, or discovery that the episode will fulfill.

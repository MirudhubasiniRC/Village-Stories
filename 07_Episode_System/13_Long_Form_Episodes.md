# Long-Form Episodes

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories builds long-form YouTube episodes that
feel complete, patient, and worth the viewer's time. It translates approved
story doctrine into a sustained viewing experience: what receives more time,
how scenes connect, how a light supporting thread may serve the main story,
and how the finished episode remains clear on YouTube.

Use it after the premise has passed Story Engine review and before a long-form
script, beat sheet, storyboard, or edit is locked. It governs episode
organisation, not the factual content of the village or the technical
production workflow.

## Guiding Principle

Longer duration must reveal more truthful life, not merely more footage.

The viewer should leave feeling that they spent meaningful time with this
family in this village—not that a short story was stretched to meet a runtime.

## What Long Form Is For

Long form is appropriate when the episode needs room for at least one of these
things:

* A practical process whose meaningful stages cannot be understood in a Short.
* A change in weather, light, or time that materially changes the family's
  response.
* A relationship feeling that needs preparation, recognition, and quiet rest.
* A place or community activity whose geography and social rhythm help tell
  the story.
* A gentle continuing task, such as cultivation, repair, cooking, or festival
  preparation, that has more than one honest stage.

It is not a larger version of a Short by default. A premise with one action
and one emotional turn may be stronger as a Short even if more material is
available. Choose long form only when the additional observation changes
understanding, feeling, or continuity.

## Runtime and Viewer Contract

The normal target is five to ten minutes. Runtime is an outcome of the story,
not a quota. An episode may be shorter when its resolution arrives honestly,
and a longer episode requires a documented reason for each additional section.

A long-form viewer contract has four parts:

1. The opening promptly shows a real point of interest.
2. The episode establishes a practical centre that can carry the duration.
3. The middle provides visible progress, variation, and emotional movement.
4. The ending gives the completed moment time to settle before any optional
   connection to another episode.

Do not use a long opening, a repeated montage, a delayed answer, or an
unrelated secondary problem to manufacture watch time. Calm pacing is not
permission to withhold the episode's actual subject.

The following order applies whenever a long-form decision conflicts with a
more specific source:

Specific canon always wins. This manual cannot add a character ability,
location feature, object, cultural practice, timeline event, weather state, or
relationship fact because a longer episode would benefit from it. Escalate a
missing fact before planning it; never solve the gap with a convenient scene.

## Long-Form Story Architecture

Use one main thread and, only when justified, one light supporting thread.

### Main Thread

The main thread is the sentence that makes the episode necessary:

> Because [ordinary condition], the family must [practical response], and
> through that work [an honest feeling or understanding becomes visible].

It must remain visible from opening through ending. Examples include cooking
through changing rain, preparing a child for school, completing a careful
repair, tending a seasonal garden task, or contributing to a documented
festival preparation.

### Supporting Thread

A supporting thread is allowed only when it:

* grows from the same day, location, people, or practical activity;
* deepens the main thread's feeling or reveals a character relationship;
* has a modest, separately understandable movement;
* does not compete with the main resolution; and
* resolves, pauses naturally, or becomes an explicitly recorded future thread.

Suitable pairings include:

* A cooking task alongside a sibling learning to share one useful kitchen
  responsibility.
* Festival preparation alongside Father and Mother's quiet memory prompted by
  an established object, place, recipe, or song.
* Garden work alongside appropriate animal care that affects the same routine.

An unrelated errand, sudden visitor, new disagreement, or second obstacle is
not a supporting thread. It is filler unless it changes the central work in a
documented, necessary way.

## Default Beat Plan

Long-form episodes should normally follow this progression. The timing is
flexible; the cause and effect are not.

1. **Truthful opening:** Show a sensory condition, purposeful action, or
   meaningful object that belongs to the actual episode.
2. **Orientation and need:** Establish who is present, where they are, what
   needs attention, and why it matters today.
3. **Preparation:** Show tools, materials, routes, roles, and practical
   competence. This is where the household becomes specific.
4. **First work movement:** Let viewers see the task begin and understand its
   rhythm through selected, meaningful stages.
5. **Gentle turn:** Introduce the small obstacle, discovery, adjustment, or
   relationship recognition that changes the work.
6. **Response and supporting movement:** Follow the useful adaptation. A
   supporting thread may appear here only if it helps the same emotional
   centre.
7. **Resolution:** Show a believable result that follows from what the
   audience has already seen.
8. **Emotional rest:** Give the family, village soundscape, and final image
   room to show what changed.
9. **Optional continuation seed:** If a real process continues, leave one
   gentle, truthful next need after today's feeling is resolved.

The episode should be understandable with the sound muted: the viewer must
still see the condition, work, change, response, and result.

## Pacing and Scene Decisions

Long form earns its length through selected observation.

### Hold when the viewer needs to notice

Give time to:

* a location, weather state, route, or time of day that changes the task;
* skilled hands, tools, materials, food, water, fire, plants, or animal care;
* a character making a practical choice;
* a quiet recognition between family members;
* a transition in rain, light, sound, or household rhythm; and
* the final release after the task is settled.

### Compress when repetition adds nothing

Compress repeated walking, carrying, sorting, chopping, similar repair steps,
or routine preparation after the viewer understands the action and its purpose.
Retain an arrival and result so characters do not appear to teleport and
objects do not change state without cause.

### Test every scene

For each scene, identify its primary job:

* advance the task;
* establish or re-establish place and time;
* reveal character through action;
* make a seasonal or continuity condition visible;
* deepen the emotional centre; or
* prepare or deliver a payoff.

Combine or remove scenes with no job. Do not retain a beautiful image solely
because it is beautiful.

## Character, Location, Season, and Continuity

Long form creates more opportunities for drift, so it requires more deliberate
continuity planning.

### Characters

Give each important character a practical reason to be present. Their
contribution must follow their documented personality, skill, relationship,
voice, body language, and age. Do not make everyone solve the main problem or
give a child, elder, or animal an implausible task to distribute screen time.

The Family Dog may accompany routine, notice familiar movement, rest near work,
or provide a gentle transition. It must not become a threat, rescue device, or
source of exaggerated comedy.

### Locations and routes

Use documented location zones, entries, paths, distances, and ordinary uses.
When the episode moves location, establish the reason, route, and arrival
before new action. A long-form journey may be compressed, but it must still
feel geographically possible.

### Season, weather, and time

Season is a working condition, not decoration. Track how rain, heat, mud,
shade, daylight, crop state, clothing, fire, animals, and sound affect each
stage. If time passes, show a credible transition in light, work progress, or
household routine. Do not let weather change merely for visual variety.

### Continuity record

Before storyboard lock, record the starting and ending state for every scene:

* character presence, costume, condition, carried items, and emotion;
* object owner, position, orientation, amount, wetness, damage, and progress;
* location zone, route, background conditions, and access;
* time of day, light, weather, and seasonal state;
* completed task, remaining task, and any honest future seed; and
* sound source, ambience, silence, or music decision.

If a later episode continues the task, hand over only confirmed facts. A
possible idea is not canon and must not be described as a promise.

## Google Flow and Visual Rhythm

Long-form planning must make the episode producible as a chain of simple,
continuous shots. Google Flow receives the approved shot contract; it does not
invent transitions, performance, or canon.

For every Flow shot, provide the locked storyboard purpose, start state,
dominant action, camera behaviour, intended end state, references, and adjacent
cut notes. Prefer one action, one camera intention, and one stable place per
clip. Break cooking, repair, walking, multi-person interaction, and emotional
recognition into separate, editable shots.

Use visual rhythm deliberately:

* wide frames orient geography, weather, and community scale;
* medium frames show work and relationships;
* close and detail frames reveal hands, materials, objects, and recognition;
* atmosphere shots must change story state or support a transition; and
* no generated text, logo, modern object, unapproved voice, or decorative
  spectacle may enter the sequence.

Review every selected clip against the preceding and following scene. A good
individual clip fails if it reverses screen direction, changes a prop hand,
alters costume or weather, shifts location architecture, or breaks the
emotional progression.

## Sound, Silence, and Educational Integration

Natural sound gives long form its sense of time. Plan an opening sound cue, one
or more process-led ASMR moments, transitions, and a final sound state. Use
music sparingly for an earned emotional turn; use silence or low ambience for
concentration, reflection, apology, memory, or rest. Sound must never inflate
a small obstacle into danger or cover information the audience needs.

Education belongs inside visible action. A longer episode can show a process in
more detail, but it must not pause for a lecture, quiz, forced moral, or
unverified cultural claim. Let the viewer learn through observed skill,
seasonal reasoning, care, and consequences.

## YouTube Design

Long form should use 16:9 composition unless an approved format decision says
otherwise. Keep the focal action readable at mobile scale and do not rely on a
tiny background detail for essential story information.

The title, thumbnail, first moments, description, chapters, cards, end screen,
and playlist must make one honest promise. Chapters may identify visible stages
without spoiling the resolution. End screens should offer only genuinely
related episodes or playlists and must not interrupt the final resting image.

Do not use false urgency, threat language, misleading Part numbers, delayed
reveals, or packaging that calls ordinary weather or hardship a crisis.

## Quality Gates and Handoffs

### Gate 1: Long-form suitability

Before scripting, confirm that the main thread has enough genuine process and
emotional breathing room; that any supporting thread is necessary; and that
the intended runtime is proportionate. Otherwise redesign as a Short or a
smaller episode.

**Handoff:** approved one-sentence thread, runtime rationale, canon references,
season/location choice, continuity starting state, and proposed final image.

### Gate 2: Beat and storyboard lock

Before generation, confirm that each scene has a job, practical cause precedes
response, the main thread remains visible, routes and time are plausible, and
the ending completes today's feeling.

**Handoff:** locked beat sheet, shot list, continuity ledger, sound plan,
Google Flow shot contracts, and explicit supporting-thread status.

### Gate 3: Sequence review

Before picture lock, watch the whole cut for forward movement. Check that
observation is meaningful rather than repetitive, the central activity remains
clear, process has not replaced story, and every transition preserves facts.

**Handoff:** selected clip versions, defect log, revised cut, and updated
continuity record to picture, sound, and quality review.

### Gate 4: Release readiness

Before publishing, verify the final master, truthful package promise, captions,
chapters, playlist placement, end-screen destinations, and any continuation
language against the final cut. Use `08_Production/14_Quality_Control.md` and
`08_Production/15_Publishing_Workflow.md` for the release decision.

## Positive and Negative Patterns

### Positive: Rain-adjusted cooking

Mother begins preparing a meal; rain makes the firewood damp; the family
protects the ingredients and finds a patient, documented alternative. The
episode shows enough preparation, fire management, sound, and shared work to
make the meal and quiet ending meaningful. A small sibling contribution
deepens the same care story.

### Positive: Repair with a memory

Father examines a familiar object, begins a careful repair, and Mother recalls
a modest shared memory prompted by the object. The memory clarifies why the
repair matters but does not displace the practical action. The repaired object
returns to ordinary use, ending on settled recognition.

### Negative: Three unrelated problems

An episode begins with a repair, interrupts it for a school disagreement, then
adds a visitor's separate request. Even if every scene is pleasant, no single
thread earns the duration. Select one centre and move the others to future
episodes.

### Negative: Scenic repetition

Repeated birds, rain, cooking, and walking shots may look calm but do not build
understanding after their first use. Keep only the images that orient, change
the task, deepen feeling, or release the ending.

## Edge Cases

* **A process continues across days:** Close the current day's practical and
  emotional unit. Record the precise remaining work, weather, object, and
  relationship state; do not imply a release date or outcome not approved.
* **The result is modest or incomplete:** End with useful progress, care, or a
  realistic plan, not forced perfection or artificial sadness.
* **A scene cannot be generated consistently:** Simplify it into smaller
  actions, use a stable detail or atmosphere shot, or revise the board. Never
  accept identity or period drift for a more dramatic movement.
* **A supporting thread becomes stronger than the main thread:** Reframe the
  episode around it only through a new brief and re-review; otherwise remove
  or defer it.
* **A release order differs from story order:** Use standalone packaging and
  accurate playlist sequencing. Do not create a false chronology with
  “tomorrow,” “Part 2,” or continuation language.

# YouTube Shorts

## Purpose

This manual defines how Village Stories creates, reviews, and hands off a complete YouTube Short. It governs episode-level vertical story design, hook honesty, runtime discipline, framing priorities, captions, ending design, connected releases, and Shorts-specific quality gates.

It does not replace the broader narrative rules in `06_Story_Engine/`, the complete episode structure in this folder, or the technical upload, metadata, thumbnail, caption, rights, and export procedures in `08_Production/`. A Short is a complete Village Stories episode in a compressed form, not a promotional fragment or a long-form episode cut down at the last minute.

---

## Philosophy

A Short earns attention by showing a real moment worth noticing immediately.

The first seconds should reveal a living question, practical task, sensory change, or character attention that the viewer can understand without being deceived or shouted at. The episode then carries one coherent line of action to an earned emotional rest. It may invite the viewer to return to the world, but it must not withhold its own resolution to force the next view.

Vertical delivery is not a smaller screen for horizontal storytelling. It changes what can be read: face, hands, object, weather, and the central action must work together at phone scale.

---

## Scope and Authority

Apply this authority order:

1. Canon, World, Character, Object, Location, Culture, and Research documents define what the Short may show.
2. Series Style Lock defines visual, audio, camera, and tonal identity.
3. Story Engine defines emotional truth, gentle conflict, character fit, seasons, locations, and prohibited patterns.
4. Episode System documents define hook, structure, pacing, openings, endings, audio, and visual rhythm.
5. This manual defines the format-specific application for an individual YouTube Short.
6. Production manuals execute storyboard, image generation, Google Flow animation, editing, metadata, publishing, and review.

Platform conventions never outrank canon, child safety, period authenticity, dignity, or a truthful ending.

---

## Guiding Principle

Make one ordinary Village Stories moment immediately clear, fully felt, and honestly complete.

---

## What a Village Stories Short Is

A valid Short has:

* one central practical action, sensory change, gentle question, or relationship movement;
* an immediate visible point of interest;
* enough orientation to understand who, where, and what matters;
* a progression from need or attention to adjustment, discovery, or completion;
* a final emotional or visual beat that resolves the Short’s own promise;
* vertical framing and captions that protect essential action;
* a truthful relation to any longer episode or connected Short.

A Short is not:

* a trailer that ends before the activity begins;
* a montage of unrelated “village aesthetic” images;
* a false emergency, mystery, rescue, or cliffhanger;
* a long-form scene arbitrarily cropped into 9:16;
* an excuse to make children, the Family Dog, weather, or ordinary work feel unsafe;
* a vehicle for modern trends, meme editing, aggressive music, or clickbait packaging.

## Short-Form Decision Matrix

| Candidate idea | Make a Short when | Use long-form instead when | Reject or redesign when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| One practical task | beginning, adjustment, and completion fit one clear line | task needs several locations, stages, or relationships | the task has no visible change or ending |
| Small discovery | discovery leads to a simple action or understanding | it opens a larger family or community sequence | it relies on a fake reveal or unexplained mystery |
| Seasonal condition | one weather effect changes an ordinary routine | the episode needs the full day, route, or seasonal cycle | weather becomes danger spectacle |
| Character learning | one safe, age-appropriate action shows learning | growth depends on multiple lessons or supporting scenes | child must carry adult responsibility or peril |
| Location moment | the location itself offers a single lived action | geography, travel, or community context needs time | location is used only as an attractive background |
| Connected event | each release has its own mini-resolution | one continuous story needs breathing room | every part ends by withholding the actual outcome |

If compression removes the practical logic, character reason, or emotional landing, the idea is not Short-ready.

## Short Structure

The timing below is a planning guide, not a mandate to fill every second. Follow `05_Pacing_Timeline.md` for the episode pacing baseline and adapt it to the actual approved runtime.

| Phase | Viewer needs to receive | Typical Short treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Visible hook | a real action, condition, object, or attention point | first image or first few seconds: rain reaches a school paper, a vessel is missing, a craft begins, the Dog waits by a familiar gate |
| Orientation and need | who is involved, where they are, and why it matters now | one compact location or character cue; no long explanation |
| Process | the practical adjustment, care, learning, or attempt | clear cause-and-effect action with selected sensory detail |
| Gentle turn | a workable discovery, response, or change in understanding | let the character notice; do not inflate it into crisis |
| Resolution and rest | completed action and earned feeling | a final practical state, look, sound decay, or peaceful image |

The intended arc is:

```text
truthful attention → understandable action → gentle change → completed emotional beat
```

Do not add a separate introduction, branded bumper, recap, or end-card sequence that consumes the episode’s short runtime unless Production has an approved delivery requirement that preserves the story.

## Hook System for Shorts

### Valid Hooks

Use a hook the viewer can see or hear truthfully:

* rain begins to affect a real household or school task;
* a hand starts a specific cooking, repair, garden, or craft action;
* a child notices a genuine change in a plant, object, or animal routine;
* Father or Mother begins a measured practical adjustment;
* a familiar location presents a distinctive seasonal or time-of-day condition;
* the Family Dog’s calm, plausible behaviour reveals a routine need.

### Invalid Hooks

Do not use:

* a reaction before the audience can know what caused it;
* an isolated cry, angry face, growl, thunder crash, or broken object as bait;
* captions that imply danger, secret information, or a shocking outcome;
* a misleading before/after claim;
* a title card that asks viewers to wait for the story;
* a full-screen graphic, loud stinger, or rapid montage that hides the actual episode subject.

The hook must be fulfilled by the opening and developed by the rest of the Short. Consult `04_Hook_System.md` for the series-wide honesty rule.

## Workflow

### 1. Approve the One-Sentence Promise

Write:

`In [established location and condition], [character] [practical action or noticing] and [small adjustment], ending with [visible or emotional completion].`

Example:

`In the established courtyard during early monsoon rain, Younger Daughter protects her school paper with Mother’s help, ending ready to leave with the paper dry.`

If the sentence needs multiple “and then” clauses, choose one part for the Short or plan a long-form episode.

### 2. Confirm Story Fit

Check character role, location, route, object, season, and safety against canonical documents. The Short format never permits an invented place, unsupported task, or exaggerated behaviour for speed.

### 3. Create the Vertical Beat Sheet

For each beat, record:

```text
time range / visual purpose / framing / audio priority / caption need / continuity state
```

Keep one dominant subject or action per beat. If two actions compete in a vertical frame, separate them or simplify the story.

### 4. Lock 9:16 Framing Before Generation

Storyboard for vertical delivery from the start:

* centre the essential face, hand, tool, food, animal, or weather action;
* preserve space where platform overlays and captions could obstruct the image;
* use close, medium, and detail frames when they clarify the action;
* use a vertical wide only when it gives necessary geography, scale, or weather;
* protect the final frame from unwanted cropping and end-screen obstruction.

Do not generate a horizontal master on the assumption that post-production can always crop it successfully.

### 5. Define Google Flow Shot Contracts

Use `08_Production/07_Google_Flow_Workflow.md` for current tool controls and generation procedure. For Shorts, every shot contract must include:

* 9:16 delivery ratio;
* central story action and safe margins;
* one focal subject and one dominant motion;
* documented start and end state;
* restrained camera behaviour;
* caption-sensitive empty space only when it does not remove the action;
* clip length required for a clean cut, hold, or final frame.

Generate the smallest truthful movement. A steady close shot of hands completing a task is usually more useful than a sweeping camera move through a vertical frame.

### 6. Build Picture and Sound Together

First check that the Short works muted: action, reaction, and resolution must remain intelligible from picture and captions. Then add natural sound, selected ASMR, narration where necessary, and music only with a stated purpose.

The opening may use a truthful sensory sound, but never a loud stinger. Follow `10_Music_And_Silence.md` for the audio decision and `09_ASMR_Placement.md` for tactile sound placement.

### 7. Add Captions and Text Safely

Use captions for approved spoken language and essential sound information. Keep them:

* accurate to the locked audio;
* readable at phone scale;
* clear of face, hands, key prop, and the central action;
* brief enough to be read at natural pace;
* consistent with approved names and cultural terminology.

Generated footage must contain no text, subtitles, logos, or watermarks. Add readable text in post-production. Use `08_Production/13_YouTube_Metadata.md` and `11_Post_Production.md` for full accessibility and public-copy process.

### 8. Test Completion and Delivery

Review the Short at normal playback, muted playback, phone-sized playback, and with captions enabled. Confirm that it ends a complete beat before title, metadata, thumbnail, and upload handoff.

## Visual and Audio Delivery Rules

### Visual

* Keep meaningful action central and large enough to read on a phone.
* Preserve the same camera restraint, hand-painted identity, early-1990s rural Tamil Nadu specificity, and continuity standards as long-form episodes.
* Use cut speed to follow attention, not current Shorts trends.
* Include at least one memorable, truthful image that can represent the actual episode—never a frame from an event not delivered.
* Avoid tiny background plot information, crowded vertical staging, rapid re-frames, and camera spectacle.

### Audio

* Begin with recognisable natural sound when it supports the hook; image must still work without it.
* Let one or two tactile or environmental sounds have space.
* Maintain clear narration without burying action and ambience.
* Use music as support, never as a retention device or emotional command.
* Avoid abrupt volume changes that punish headphone listeners.
* Preserve a final natural decay, quiet image, or gentle cue exit where the emotional beat needs rest.

## Character, Location, and Seasonal Applications

### Character Applications

* **Mother:** a Short may follow one contained household decision, food process, or act of guidance. Do not reduce her to hurried labour or make her distress the hook.
* **Father:** use a focused repair, route, garden, field, or animal-care beat that shows measured competence. Avoid heroic action framing.
* **Elder Brother:** use an age-appropriate school, memory, outdoor-learning, or helpful action. A minor mistake must lead to calm correction, not shame.
* **Younger Daughter:** use safe curiosity, a small observation, and supported participation. Never make her lost, frightened, or isolated to improve retention.
* **Family Dog:** use calm companionship, routine care, shade, shelter, waiting, or a familiar response. Never use growling, threat, injury, disappearance, or chase as a hook.

### Location Applications

Choose a Short location because it contains the whole action: Kitchen for a cooking detail, Courtyard for shared household action or rain, Thinnai for an arrival or departure, Garden for observation, School for a contained learning beat, or a documented village space for a real errand or skill.

Do not use a location merely to add a dramatic wide shot. Maintain credible travel and entry logic even when the format allows only a brief bridge.

### Seasonal Applications

Season must visibly change the activity:

* **Summer:** shade, water, early timing, dry material, and animal comfort.
* **Monsoon:** wet surfaces, protected items, adjusted routes, sheltered work, and rain sound.
* **Post-monsoon:** renewed growth, damp paths, cleaning, airing, or cautious return outdoors.
* **Harvest:** one respectful role in a real cooperative task, not a labour montage.
* **Documented festivals:** preparation or a quiet shared moment; no invented crowd spectacle or generic cultural shorthand.

Do not title a Short by season if season is not genuinely visible in its task, sound, light, and continuity.

## Continuity and Connected Shorts

Every Short must retain its own continuity ledger:

* character identity, costume, age, relationships, and active role;
* location zone, route, and fixed visual anchors;
* object owner, hand, condition, quantity, and final state;
* time, weather, season, ground condition, and light;
* camera side, screen direction, and vertical crop;
* sound bed, music cue, ASMR moment, narration, and final audio state;
* episode promise, ending, and any legitimate connection to another release.

### Connected Shorts

Use a sequence only when one real event naturally continues beyond a single satisfying Short. Each Short must:

1. identify its own activity or moment;
2. remain understandable for a viewer who has not seen the previous release;
3. provide its own mini-resolution;
4. describe its connection truthfully in public metadata;
5. preserve time, prop, costume, weather, location, and emotional continuity.

Never label unrelated uploads “Part 2,” split the only resolution into another Short, or finish every part with a manufactured cliffhanger. Use long form if the story requires sustained continuity to be satisfying.

## Positive and Negative Examples

### Positive: The Dry School Paper

The Short opens with rain reaching the courtyard beside Younger Daughter’s paper. Mother sees the condition, folds a dry cloth around the paper, and Elder Brother holds the bag open. The final image shows the protected paper in the bag and the child ready at the thinnai.

**Why it works:** the hook is visible, each person has a fitting role, rain changes the task, and the Short reaches a complete emotional and practical ending.

### Negative: “Will She Make It?”

The Short begins with a frightened close-up, uses rapid rain cuts and text asking whether the child will reach school, then ends before the paper is protected.

**Why it fails:** it creates false danger, withholds the actual resolution, and treats a child’s ordinary preparation as retention bait.

### Positive: Dog’s Water Bowl

A close vertical opening shows the Family Dog looking toward an empty documented water bowl in summer shade. Younger Daughter notices; Mother supervises the refill. The dog settles near the bowl as the final sound of water fades.

**Why it works:** animal care, child curiosity, summer condition, and gentle closure are all visible without making the dog an emotional emergency.

### Negative: Dog Alarm Hook

A growl, fast zoom, and caption about a “strange visitor” lead to the Dog rushing toward the gate.

**Why it fails:** it breaks the Dog’s canon, creates threatening suspense, and misrepresents the series.

## Edge Cases and Corrections

| Edge case | Required correction |
| --- | --- |
| The idea has a strong image but no ending | Add a practical completion or use it as a long-form scene, not a standalone Short |
| The action needs multiple locations | Reduce it to one location and task, show a truthful bridge, or make a longer episode |
| The opening only works with narration | Strengthen the first visual action or condition |
| Caption-safe space conflicts with story action | Protect the action first; reposition or reduce captions in post-production |
| A Flow clip is horizontal or crops hands/props in vertical | Regenerate a 9:16 shot; do not accept a broken crop |
| A Short is complete but its ending cannot support an end screen | Preserve the final rest and coordinate a separate delivery layout with Production |
| Metrics suggest faster editing or more shock | Review retention alongside promise accuracy; do not change canon or honest pacing to chase a metric |
| A series continuation no longer makes sense | Publish each release as self-contained and revise public linking; do not force a sequence |

## Quality Gates

### Story Gate

* Is the first visible beat an honest promise?
* Does one clear activity, question, or feeling progress to completion?
* Are conflict and emotional scale gentle, safe, and canon-consistent?

### Vertical Picture Gate

* Is the key action readable in 9:16 at phone scale?
* Are face, hands, story prop, captions, and interface-sensitive areas properly managed?
* Is every crop, cut, and camera movement motivated by attention?

### Sound and Caption Gate

* Does the Short work muted?
* Do natural sound, ASMR, narration, music, and silence have purposeful space?
* Are captions accurate, legible, and clear of essential picture?

### Continuity Gate

* Do character, object, location, season, weather, route, camera, and audio states match the episode ledger?
* Is any connection to another Short real, clear, and self-contained?

### Release Handoff Gate

* Is there an honest thumbnail-worthy frame?
* Do title, description, playlist, captions, rights, and export requirements have a complete Production handoff?
* Has the final Short been reviewed in the actual vertical delivery format?
